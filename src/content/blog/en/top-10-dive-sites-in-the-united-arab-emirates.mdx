The UAE is not the first destination most divers think of for a dedicated dive holiday, yet the Emirates offer a genuinely varied underwater world split between two very different seas. On the Gulf of Oman side — reached from Fujairah — rocky pinnacles, reef walls, and a cluster of notable wrecks provide the best diving in the country, with clear water, strong fish life, and reliable year-round access. The Arabian Gulf side, accessible from Dubai and Abu Dhabi, is murkier and shallower, but hosts its own wreck dives and sandy reef systems. Water temperatures in the Gulf of Oman range from around 22°C in winter to 33°C in summer, making October through May the most comfortable diving season. The country's dive infrastructure is well developed, particularly around Fujairah, and the ease of logistics makes it an accessible destination for residents of the region and visiting divers flying through Dubai. ## 1. Martini Rock, Fujairah

Martini Rock is a submerged pinnacle sitting roughly nine kilometres offshore from Fujairah, rising from a sandy seabed at around 28 metres to within two metres of the surface. It is the most celebrated dive site in the UAE, known for its exceptional fish life and the reliable presence of large pelagics on the reef edge. The rock is encrusted in soft corals and hosts resident Napoleon wrasse, big-eyed trevally in rotating schools, and barracuda that hang in loose aggregations on the current-facing side. Whale sharks are occasionally reported here between November and March. The crossing from Fujairah harbour takes about 30 minutes, and conditions depend heavily on current state — the site is exposed to Gulf of Oman swells that can make the surface rough. Advanced Open Water recommended; Open Water divers can dive it in calm conditions with a guide.

## 2. Inchcape 1 and Inchcape 2, Fujairah

The Inchcape wrecks are two cargo vessels sunk near each other off Fujairah, forming one of the most productive wreck-diving pairs in the Gulf of Oman. Inchcape 1 lies at 18–28 metres and is covered in prolific soft corals that have made it look more reef than wreck after years of colonisation. Inchcape 2 sits in 25–32 metres and is more intact structurally, with accessible cargo holds and a bridge that can be penetrated. Both wrecks consistently produce lionfish, moray eels in the gaps in the hull plating, and schools of goatfish working the sandy seabed around the hulls. Current can be significant between the wrecks; experienced divers plan their dive to work with it. Advanced Open Water for Inchcape 2; Open Water comfortable on Inchcape 1 in calm conditions.

## 3. Dibba Rock, Fujairah

Dibba Rock is a large submerged rock formation off the coast near the town of Dibba, forming the northern tip of Fujairah's best dive corridor. The site descends to around 25 metres on the deep side, with a shallower plateau at 12–15 metres on the sheltered east face. Turtles — mainly green turtles — are resident and reliably encountered; the population here is among the most accessible in the Emirates. The rock is also home to banded sea snakes, which cruise the reef edges in search of small fish. Schools of chub and sergeant majors occupy the shallows, and bluefin trevally hunt in the passing current. The site is close to shore and can be dived on days when offshore sites like Martini Rock are inaccessible due to weather. Open Water divers are comfortable here in normal conditions.

## 4. Snoopy Island (Sharm Al Arab), Fujairah

Snoopy Island is a small rocky outcrop adjacent to Sandy Beach Hotel north of Fujairah city, named for its resemblance — from the beach — to the cartoon beagle. It is the most accessible shore-entry dive in the UAE, swimmable from the beach in calm conditions. The reef around the island descends to 14 metres on the north side, with a mix of rocky reef, sandy corridors, and dense soft coral growth. Sea turtles are a near-certainty, and the area has a long-standing population of resident hawksbill turtles comfortable with divers. Blue-spotted stingrays rest on the sand, and the rubble zones host a variety of nudibranch species. The site is suitable for newly certified divers and is widely used by dive centres as a training site. Open Water; excellent for skills development.

## 5. Car Cemetery, Fujairah

The Car Cemetery is an artificial reef created by sinking decommissioned vehicles off Fujairah, sitting in 16–22 metres of water. The vehicles — cars, trucks, and at least one armoured vehicle — have been substantially colonised and now function as genuine reef structure, with soft corals, encrusting sponges, and communities of reef fish using the frames as habitat. The site is popular for photography because the juxtaposition of recognisable vehicle shapes with marine growth gives unusual compositions. Scorpionfish are common and require attention when placing hands near structures. The surrounding sandy seabed hosts garden eels in dense colonies. Open Water; the depth and the artificial structure make it an interesting change from natural reef dives.

## 6. Cement Wreck, Fujairah Offshore

A small cargo vessel carrying cement cargo when it sank, the Cement Wreck lies at 18–24 metres offshore from Fujairah and is a classic Middle Eastern dive — not dramatic in size but consistently productive in marine life. The hull is largely intact with reasonable penetration opportunities into the holds, where the original cement cargo has set into a solid mass that forms part of the current structural substrate. Schools of batfish are a fixture, and the stern section with its rudder and propeller is encrusted in colourful life. Cleaner shrimps work from coral heads at the bow. The dive is suitable for recently certified divers who want their first wreck experience in a manageable environment. Open Water.

## 7. Rashid Wreck, Dubai

Dubai's diving sits in the shallower, greener waters of the Arabian Gulf rather than the Gulf of Oman, and the Rashid wreck is the best the emirate offers. A former tugboat sunk deliberately as an artificial reef, it lies in 24 metres with its superstructure reaching to around 15 metres. The visibility in Arabian Gulf waters averages 5–8 metres, noticeably reduced compared to Fujairah, but the wreck is heavily colonised and the fish life is dense precisely because of the enclosed, nutrient-rich environment. Lionfish are extremely abundant here — as throughout UAE waters — and large grouper use the internal compartments. Dubai-based operators run regular trips; the dive is usually conducted as a two-tank outing. Advanced Open Water recommended; comfortable for Open Water divers in good visibility windows.

## 8. Sandy Beach Reef, Fujairah

The reef immediately off Sandy Beach at Sharm Al Arab extends south from Snoopy Island across a series of rocky ridges interspersed with sandy channels, reaching depths of 18 metres at the outer edge. This is a productive macro dive site: the sandy channels between ridges host cuttlefish eggs attached to coral heads in spring, flying gurnards on the open sand, and sand gobies co-habiting with snapping shrimps in small burrows. The rocky ridges themselves harbour lionfish at nearly every overhang and substantial populations of moray eels. The site is shore-dived with good access and is popular with underwater photographers who prefer the leisurely pace a shore entry allows over a boat-dive schedule. Open Water.

## 9. Hole in the Wall, Khor Fakkan

Khor Fakkan, within the exclave of Sharjah on the Gulf of Oman coast, has its own dive scene centred on rocky headlands and a pair of submerged arches known as the Hole in the Wall. The arches sit at around 8–12 metres and are large enough to swim through comfortably; the walls are covered in tube worms, small encrusting corals, and a resident school of glassy sweepers. Outside the arches, the reef extends to a sandy slope at 22 metres with scattered boulders that concentrate resting nurse sharks and blue-spotted stingrays. The site is dived from the Khor Fakkan corniche, which has parking and reasonable facilities. It is a short drive from Dubai compared to Fujairah proper, making it popular with city-based divers. Open Water.

## 10. Shark Island, Fujairah Offshore

Shark Island is a rocky outcrop sitting further offshore than most Fujairah sites, requiring a longer boat ride but rewarding the effort with less diver pressure and stronger current-fed marine life. The name reflects the site's history of grey reef shark sightings, though populations have thinned compared to decades past. What remains is a rocky pinnacle descending to 28 metres, swept by currents that pile up schooling fish on the upstream side — large snapper, kingfish, and rainbow runners cycling in the water column. The coral coverage is respectable for the Gulf of Oman, and the deeper rubble zones host cleaner wrasse stations attended by large reef fish. The offshore exposure means this site is not dived in rough weather. Advanced Open Water; experience with current drift recommended.

## Planning Your UAE Dive Trip

Fujairah is the unambiguous base for UAE diving, roughly 90 minutes by road from Dubai and 45 minutes from Dubai International Airport along the new mountain road. The best diving season runs from October through May, when water temperatures are comfortable — 24–27°C — and visibility in the Gulf of Oman peaks at 20 metres or more. Summer diving is possible but the heat above and below the water surface (up to 33°C) limits comfort and many operators reduce schedules. Several established Fujairah dive centres run daily two-tank boat trips to Martini Rock, the Inchcape wrecks, and offshore pinnacles; booking ahead during UAE national holidays is advisable as demand spikes sharply.
