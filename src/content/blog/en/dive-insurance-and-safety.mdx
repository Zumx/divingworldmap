## Why Standard Travel Insurance Is Not Enough

Most divers travel with some form of insurance. Fewer of them have checked whether their policy covers the specific medical events that diving produces. Decompression sickness, arterial gas embolism, and pulmonary barotrauma are not covered by standard travel insurance policies — and treating them requires a hyperbaric chamber, a piece of specialist medical equipment that is expensive to operate and is found only at specific locations worldwide.

A single session in a hyperbaric chamber for decompression sickness treatment runs to several thousand dollars in a private facility. A serious case requiring multiple treatment sessions, transport to a hyperbaric facility, and hospitalisation in a country with high medical costs can reach tens of thousands of dollars with surprising speed. In the United States, air medical evacuation alone — without treatment — commonly runs to five figures. Standard travel insurance policies generally exclude "hazardous activities" unless specifically endorsed, and even endorsements rarely cover hyperbaric treatment as a specific line item.

The geographical reality compounds the financial one. Recompression chambers are not uniformly distributed across popular dive destinations. In the Indo-Pacific, some of the most spectacular diving — remote atolls in the Tuamotus, the outer islands of Indonesia, parts of Micronesia — sits hours from the nearest chamber by boat and flight. In the Red Sea, Sharm el-Sheikh and Hurghada have dedicated hyperbaric facilities, but liveaboard itineraries into the southern Red Sea push divers further from those resources. Knowing where the nearest chamber is before you enter the water is part of responsible dive planning.

Diving-specific insurance products exist precisely to fill this gap, and the major providers — Divers Alert Network (DAN), DAN Europe, DiveAssure, and others — have built their coverage specifically around the medical events that divers actually experience.

## DAN: The Specialist

DAN, the Divers Alert Network, was founded in 1980 at Duke University as a non-profit organisation providing emergency assistance and research for diving medicine. It operates a 24-hour emergency hotline staffed by diving medicine physicians who can advise on dive-related injuries and coordinate evacuation and treatment. This hotline is available to non-DAN members in genuine emergencies, but coverage for costs requires membership or an insurance plan.

DAN membership at the base level provides access to the emergency assistance line and some degree of accidental death and dismemberment coverage. Insurance plans above the base membership level provide coverage for hyperbaric chamber treatment, hospitalisation related to dive injuries, medical evacuation, and in some plans, trip cancellation and travel inconvenience. DAN operates regional branches in the Americas, Europe, Africa and Southeast Asia, Japan, and the Asia-Pacific, each providing coverage calibrated to regional medical systems and evacuation networks. The DAN emergency hotline number — for DAN Americas it is +1-919-684-9111 — should be saved in your phone before you leave home.

DAN also operates a dive accident and injury database that has been the basis for much of what is known about recreational diving injury epidemiology. Their Project Dive Exploration logged millions of recreational dives and identified the factors most strongly associated with decompression sickness: rapid ascent, omitted safety stops, reverse dive profiles (deeper dives after shallower ones on the same day), and physical exertion immediately after surfacing.

## DAN Europe and Other Providers

DAN Europe, headquartered in Malta, covers European divers and offers individual annual policies and group plans for dive clubs. Coverage levels range from emergency assistance and hyperbaric treatment through full travel insurance packages. European divers should note that coverage through the European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) or its UK equivalent does not extend to hyperbaric treatment in non-EU countries and does not cover medical evacuation.

DiveAssure offers competitive annual coverage including unlimited hyperbaric treatment, emergency medical evacuation, and trip interruption coverage, and is particularly strong for divers making multiple liveaboard trips per year where the cumulative value of trip-interruption coverage is significant.

The key coverage elements to verify in any diving insurance product: the coverage cap for hyperbaric treatment per incident (look for policies without a per-session cap), medical evacuation coverage and its geographic scope, whether the policy covers multiple dives per day and liveaboard operations, and whether technical diving or diving with enriched air nitrox is excluded. Some base-level policies exclude dives deeper than 40 metres or any use of mixed gas — read the fine print before you travel.

## Building a Personal Safety Routine

Insurance addresses the consequences of accidents. A safety routine is about reducing the probability of accidents in the first place. The foundation of any personal safety routine is the pre-dive check — the five-point check that should be conducted with your buddy before every dive, regardless of how many times you have dived with them before.

The acronym varies by agency. PADI uses BWRAF (Begin With Review And Friend): BCD function (inflate and deflate), Weights (type, quantity, correct placement, quick-release accessible), Releases (all clips and buckles present, functional, and correctly threaded), Air (cylinder valve open, both divers breathe from regulators, air pressure checked on both gauges), and Final check (mask, fins, computer, everything in place). SSI and BSAC use similar frameworks. The specific acronym matters less than the habit: running the check completely, every time, without shortcutting.

Beyond the pre-dive check, a personal safety routine includes conservative dive planning. This means honouring the no-decompression limits on your computer with a meaningful safety margin rather than diving to the limit on every dive, ascending at the recommended rate (typically 9 metres per minute maximum), and conducting a three-minute safety stop at 5 metres on every dive deeper than 10 metres regardless of whether the computer requires it. It also means respecting the surface interval between dives — particularly on multi-dive days aboard liveaboards — and waiting the appropriate time before flying: the standard guidance is a minimum of 12 hours after a single no-decompression dive, and 18 hours or more after repetitive dives or any dive requiring a decompression stop.

## The Buddy System and Dive Planning

The buddy system is not merely a social convention. It is a core safety mechanism. A conscious buddy can assist with equipment failure, share air in an out-of-air emergency, help an incapacitated diver to the surface, and provide a first witness account in a medical emergency. Effective buddy diving means staying close enough to assist — typically within arm's reach in poor visibility, within a few metres in clear water — and maintaining visual contact throughout the dive.

Pre-dive planning with your buddy should cover the dive profile (maximum depth, planned bottom time, turn pressure), the entry and exit points, the current and conditions, the communication signals you will both use, and the emergency procedure if you become separated. The standard separation protocol — search for one minute, then ascend and meet on the surface — should be agreed before every dive. A plan made on the surface is one less decision to make at depth.

## Surface Markers and Emergency Signals

A surface marker buoy (SMB) — a brightly coloured inflatable tube deployed from depth to mark the diver's position before surfacing — is standard equipment for drift diving and strongly recommended for any dive away from a fixed mooring. An inflated SMB visible at the surface significantly reduces the risk of being struck by a boat, and in conditions of surface chop or limited visibility between boat and diver, it can be the difference between a found and a lost diver.

The audible signal device — a whistle, preferably a pealess design that works when wet, clipped to the BCD — is the minimum standard for surface signalling. An air horn powered by BCD inflation pressure carries further and is audible in conditions where a whistle is not. A signal mirror is useful in daylight in open-ocean conditions. None of these devices weigh more than a few hundred grams and none should be left at home.

Emergency oxygen is both a skill and a piece of equipment that divemasters and instructors carry on guided dives, but that any serious recreational diver should be familiar with. Oxygen first aid for decompression sickness and arterial gas embolism — breathing high-fraction oxygen at the surface while awaiting evacuation — dramatically improves outcomes. DAN's oxygen provider course teaches the skill in a single half-day session and is available at most dive centres worldwide.

## Emergency Action Plans and Recompression Chambers

Every dive operator worth diving with will brief guests on the emergency action plan at the start of a trip: the location of the oxygen kit, the emergency contact numbers, the nearest hyperbaric facility, and the procedure for calling the coast guard or emergency services in that country. If you are diving independently rather than with a guided operation, this research falls to you.

Before travelling to any remote dive destination, look up the nearest recompression chamber, note its contact number, and save it alongside the DAN hotline. DAN maintains an online database of hyperbaric facilities worldwide, accessible through the DAN website, and this is the most reliable resource for current chamber locations and operational status. In the event of a diving emergency, call DAN first — their medical staff can advise on the appropriate treatment facility and, if necessary, coordinate the logistics of a medical evacuation that would otherwise be overwhelming to organise under stress.

The complete picture of dive safety is a system: good insurance, good training, conservative diving practices, proper equipment, and a buddy who shares your commitment to these standards. Any one element in isolation leaves gaps that the others compensate for. The combination makes diving the remarkably safe activity it is when properly practised.
