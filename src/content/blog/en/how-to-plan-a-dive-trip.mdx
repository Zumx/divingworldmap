## Starting With the Right Question

The planning of a dive trip begins not with flights or gear lists, but with an honest assessment of experience level and objectives. A diver with twenty logged dives, all in warm, calm Mediterranean waters, choosing a first international trip to the Banda Sea or Cocos Island is setting themselves up for frustration or worse. The same diver choosing Raja Ampat, Bali's Amed coast, or the Red Sea's Sharm el-Sheikh resorts will find conditions that match their skills and still deliver extraordinary diving. Matching the destination to the diver is the first planning discipline.

Define what you want from the trip. Wall diving, wreck exploration, shark encounters, manta rays, macro photography, historical shipwrecks — different destinations excel at different things. Spending a week in the Coral Triangle chasing muck critters is different from a week in the Maldives riding currents past pelagic aggregations. Neither is superior; both require different conditions to deliver the experience they promise.

## Season and Conditions

Every dive destination has a best season, a shoulder season, and a period when the diving is genuinely difficult or the target species have moved on. Understanding this before booking is the single most high-impact planning decision most divers make.

In the Indo-Pacific, monsoon patterns dominate the calendar. The western Maldives is superb for manta rays and whale sharks from May through November when the southwest monsoon drives plankton-rich upwellings. The eastern side flips when the monsoon reverses. Misreading this means choppy surface conditions, reduced visibility, and absent pelagics. In Thailand, the Similan Islands close entirely from mid-May to October; planning a trip in September leads to closed liveaboards and shuttered dive shops.

Egypt's Red Sea diving is excellent year-round but the northern sites — Ras Mohammed, Blue Hole Dahab, the northern wrecks — are accessible almost every month. The Brothers Islands, Daedalus, and St Johns far to the south require stable conditions and are best in summer. The hammerheads at Scallop Reef appear reliably from April through July.

In the Caribbean, hurricane season runs June through November and affects both sea conditions and the availability of operators. The best diving in Belize, Honduras's Bay Islands, and the Cayman Islands is typically December through April — calm seas, clear water, and the whale shark aggregations at Gladden Spit in Belize coincide with snapper full moons from March to June.

Research the target season three to four months in advance. Liveaboards on premium routes book out six to twelve months ahead; shore-based resorts at popular destinations like Komodo or the Maldives fill up during peak months.

## Certification Requirements

Some destinations and dive sites require specific certifications beyond Open Water. Deep dives — anything below eighteen metres — technically require Advanced Open Water certification under most operator standards. Liveaboards frequently require at least twenty logged dives and an Advanced certification as a minimum before they will accept a booking. This is both a liability management practice and a genuine skill threshold; the conditions in places like Barracuda Point on Sipadan, the Blue Corner at Palau, or the currents at Nusa Penida demand divers who can manage their buoyancy and position in moving water reliably.

Some sites — notably certain wreck penetrations, deep technical dives, and overhead environments — require specialty certifications. Planning a trip centred on overhead diving in the Yucatan cenotes requires either an SSI or PADI cavern certification at minimum. Diving the engine room of the SS Thistlegorm in Egypt does not require penetration certification to reach the motorcycles and trucks in the cargo holds, but going deeper into the structure does.

Check certification requirements directly with the liveaboard operator or dive resort you are considering. Many list their requirements on their websites; when in doubt, email and ask. Discovering on arrival that you are not qualified for the headline dive of your trip is the kind of disappointment that is entirely avoidable.

## Gear: What to Bring, What to Rent

The calculus of bringing your own gear versus renting varies with destination and trip length. Personal items — mask, fins, wetsuit or drysuit, dive computer, signalling devices — are almost always worth bringing. The mask especially; fit and comfort matter enormously, rental masks often leak, and spending the first dive day chasing a good seal is wasted diving time. A personal computer is worth bringing to maintain your dive log and have equipment you know is calibrated and functioning.

Regulators and buoyancy compensators are harder to justify carrying for a single short trip. Quality liveaboards and established dive resorts have properly serviced rental gear. Budget resorts and remote destinations can have ancient rental equipment of questionable reliability. Reading recent reviews of the specific operator — not just the destination in general — is the best way to assess rental quality.

For liveaboard trips, most vessels provide tanks and weights. They expect you to manage your own breathing gas allocation. The tanks are typically aluminium 12-litre cylinders. If you are accustomed to steel tanks or larger volume, carry your own weight adjustments in mind.

Nitrox is available on most modern liveaboards and at established dive resorts, usually for an additional daily fee. If you are nitrox certified, bring your card; if you are not but want to use it during the trip, some operators offer the nitrox certification course on the boat.

## Logistics and Health

Dive trips require more pre-travel health planning than most holidays. If you have not dived for more than six months, a refresher dive before travelling is sensible — muscle memory fades, and arriving at a remote destination for your first dive in two years is not the time to rediscover kit assembly or buoyancy control.

Ear health deserves specific attention. If you have had a recent ear infection, sinus blockage, or cold, reschedule if possible. Diving with congestion causes painful sinus or ear squeeze that can damage tissue and end the trip prematurely. A visit to a physician before a dive trip to confirm medical fitness is good practice for any diver with respiratory, cardiac, or ENT conditions.

Travel insurance with specific dive coverage — or a DAN membership that includes evacuation and chamber treatment costs — is not optional. Hyperbaric chamber treatment abroad can cost tens of thousands of dollars, and standard travel insurance typically excludes diving injuries. DAN memberships cover chamber treatment, medical evacuation, and 24-hour emergency medical advice globally.

## Practical Preparation

Log your planned sites ahead of time. [Open the map](/map) to research the specific dive locations at your destination, understand site layouts, and identify the spots your operator is likely to include in the itinerary. Having advance knowledge of a site's terrain, depth profile, and notable features makes the actual dives more productive.

Brief yourself on local marine life. Knowing what to look for — and what to avoid — improves every dive. Understanding that a given reef has resident pygmy seahorses requires knowing to look at seafans at specific depths, that a wreck has resident lionfish in the wheelhouse, or that particular currents carry whale sharks along certain channels at particular tide states, all improve the quality of observation. This level of preparation transforms a dive trip from a series of nice dives into something more considered and memorable.

Pack logistically: carry-on your dive computer, camera, and any irreplaceable personal items. Check airline restrictions on lithium batteries for strobes and dive lights. Confirm your accommodation has a gear rinse tank, a drying area, and that early-morning boat departures are compatible with your accommodation location.

The trip that is well-planned from destination choice through seasonal timing, gear selection, certification preparation, and health precautions consistently delivers a better result than the trip booked on impulse and figured out on arrival. The underwater world rewards preparation.
