Italy is not a country that immediately springs to mind for scuba diving, yet its coastline stretches for nearly 8,000 kilometres, and the seas on either side hold extraordinary variety. The Mediterranean is warm, clear and historically rich — wrecks from two world wars, ancient Roman harbours, volcanic underwater pinnacles, and kelp-draped rocky walls where grouper and moray eels have lived undisturbed for decades. The Adriatic to the east is shallower and greener, but the Tyrrhenian and Ligurian seas to the west deliver visibility touching 30 metres on the right day. Whether you are pulling on a 5 mm wetsuit in June or a 7 mm suit in November, Italian diving rewards patience and an eye for detail. ## 1. Portofino Marine Reserve, Ligurian Sea

The protected waters around Portofino's famous promontory offer some of the best diving in northern Italy. Walls dropping to 35 metres are carpeted with orange and yellow gorgonian fans — among the densest concentrations in the Mediterranean. Moray eels, conger eels, scorpionfish and schools of saddled bream are year-round residents. The Secco di Portofino pinnacle rises from 38 metres and reliably produces sightings of dusky grouper. Water temperature ranges from 14°C in February to 27°C in August. An Open Water certification is sufficient for the shallower sites; Advanced is recommended for the deeper walls. Best season: June to October for warmest water and strongest visibility.

## 2. Ustica Marine Reserve, Sicily

Ustica, a volcanic island 60 kilometres north of Palermo, has been a marine reserve since 1987 and the protection shows. Grouper here are massive and unbothered by divers. The signature dive is the Grotta Azzurra, where sunlight refracts through an underwater entrance and lights the cavern walls in blue and silver. Nearby, the Wall of Medina drops from 5 metres to beyond 50 metres, with sponge communities, large amberjacks and, in summer, occasional mola mola. Water temperature: 15–26°C. Intermediate certification recommended for the deeper walls. Best season: May through September.

## 3. Lake Garda, Lombardy

At 346 metres deep, Lake Garda is the largest freshwater lake in Italy and offers freshwater diving unlike anything else in the country. Visibility in the northern basin regularly exceeds 15 metres. The main draw is Tremosine Wall, a sheer cliff dropping to beyond 50 metres where char, trout and perch school in open water. Near Malcesine, a sunken Roman-era harbour with amphora fragments sits at around 18–22 metres — historical diving without saltwater logistics. Temperature is cold year-round, ranging from 8°C in winter to 20°C in summer at the surface, so a wetsuit of at least 5 mm is needed even in August. Open Water certification is fine; best season is June to September.

## 4. Capo Testa, Sardinia

The granite seascape around Capo Testa in northern Sardinia produces underwater terrain of sculpted boulders, arches and swim-throughs that mirror the dramatic land formations above water. Depths stay manageable — most dives are between 10 and 25 metres — making this an excellent destination for relatively new divers. Octopus are abundant, as are lobster in the rock crevices, and the boulders shelter schools of painted combers and rainbow wrasse. Water temperature: 16–25°C. Open Water certification. Best season: May to October; late summer visibility can exceed 25 metres.

## 5. Wreck of the Mohawk Deer, Adriatic Sea

The Mohawk Deer, a merchant vessel sunk during World War II, lies on a sandy bottom at around 32 metres off the Adriatic coast near Ancona. The structure is intact enough to allow guided penetration of the holds, where schools of bream drift through the half-light. The hull is encrusted in anemones, nudibranchs patrol the railings and large conger eels occupy the engine room. Visibility in the Adriatic is more variable than in the Tyrrhenian — plan for 8–15 metres. Advanced certification recommended. Best season: June to September when thermoclines stabilise and visibility peaks.

## 6. Wreck of the Genova, Ligurian Sea

The Genova lies off Varazze at roughly 30 metres, a small coastal freighter that has become a genuinely superb artificial reef. Soft corals have colonised the superstructure over the decades since sinking, and the wreck hosts a resident population of large scorpionfish that are nearly impossible to see against the encrusted hull. Sea horses have been reported at the bow. The site is accessible from shore with a surface swim of about 400 metres, making it ideal for divers who prefer to avoid boat fees. Advanced certification for comfortable depth management. Best season: May to October.

## 7. Lago di Lugano — Parete di Caslano, Switzerland/Italy Border

The Swiss-Italian lake of Lugano offers several excellent freshwater dives accessible from the Italian side. The Parete di Caslano is a vertical wall descending from 5 metres to beyond 40 metres, its surface colonised by freshwater sponges and zebra mussels. Large pike and perch inhabit the shallower ledges. The water here is cold — between 8°C and 18°C depending on season — and the visibility can reach an extraordinary 20 metres in spring before algal growth reduces it. A 7 mm wetsuit or drysuit is recommended outside summer months. Advanced Open Water certification for the deeper sections. Best season: April to June for peak clarity.

## 8. Porto Cesareo Marine Reserve, Puglia

Porto Cesareo, on the Ionian coast of Puglia, is one of the largest marine protected areas in Italy and a destination that remains significantly less crowded than Ustica or Portofino. The reserve encompasses posidonia meadows, sandy channels and rocky reefs running to around 30 metres. Sea turtles are reliably encountered throughout summer — the loggerhead turtle population here is among the most stable in the country. The fish life is exceptional for the Mediterranean: large barracuda, amberjacks, dentex and dusky grouper. Water temperature: 17–28°C. Open Water certification. Best season: June to September.

## 9. Capo Vaticano, Calabria

The Calabrian coastline south of Tropea is underrated and under-visited. At Capo Vaticano, a series of submerged rock formations drops to 40 metres, with walls decorated in yellow and red sponges and a consistent presence of large moray eels. The cape's position causes upwellings that occasionally bring cooler, nutrient-rich water from depth, explaining an unusually dense biomass. Divers have reported sightings of sunfish here in late summer. Water temperature: 16–27°C. Open Water comfortable to 18 metres; Advanced recommended below that. Best season: July to October.

## 10. Punta del Vapore, Sardinia

Near Carloforte on the island of San Pietro off southwestern Sardinia, Punta del Vapore centres on the remains of a 19th-century paddle steamer lying in two sections at 18–22 metres. The site is small but dense with life — nudibranchs, sea horses, and schools of juvenile fish using the hull as a nursery. An octopus reliably inhabits the boiler section. The surrounding sea floor is a mixed habitat of rocky reef and sand, which attracts flatfish and electric rays. Visibility averages 15–20 metres. Open Water certification. Best season: May to October.

## Planning Your Dive Trip to Italy

The Italian diving season runs broadly from May through October, with August delivering the warmest water (24–27°C in the south) and the greatest number of dive operations running day boats. For the best visibility, target late spring or early autumn when algal blooms are lower. Sardinia and Sicily offer the clearest Mediterranean water; the Adriatic and Ligurian coasts are more variable. Dive centres are well established across all major coastal regions, and nitrox fills are widely available at larger operations.
