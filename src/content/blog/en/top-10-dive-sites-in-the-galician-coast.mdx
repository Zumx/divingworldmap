Galicia faces the open Atlantic, and the diving here has almost nothing in common with the warm, postcard Mediterranean of Spain's eastern coast. The water is green rather than blue, the temperature sits between 13 and 19°C through the year, and the seabed is granite, sand and dense forests of kelp rather than seagrass and gorgonian fans. This is the Spain of the rías — drowned river valleys that cut deep into the coast — and of the Atlantic Islands of Galicia National Park, the Parque Nacional Marítimo-Terrestre das Illas Atlánticas, which protects the Cíes, Ons, Sálvora and Cortegada archipelagos. A 7mm wetsuit or a drysuit is the norm, visibility ranges from a murky 5 metres to a surprising 20 on a calm summer day, and the marine life is built around nutrient-rich upwelling: octopus, conger eels, spider crabs, wrasse, and the occasional sunfish drifting in from open water. The Federación Galega de Actividades Subacuáticas oversees the regional dive community, and operators such as Buceo Rías Baixas work the southern rías out of Vigo and Pontevedra.

## 1. Illas Cíes, Parque Nacional

The Cíes Islands guard the mouth of the Ría de Vigo and form the most celebrated diving in Galicia. Below the famous Praia de Rodas beach, the seabed slopes through boulder fields and kelp into 20 to 25 metres, with granite walls that shelter conger eels, lobster and large shoals of bream. Access is regulated — the national park caps daily visitor numbers and dive operators need authorisation — so the reefs remain in excellent condition. Visibility on a settled summer morning can reach 15 to 18 metres, exceptional for the Atlantic. Open Water divers can work the shallower boulder gardens; the deeper drop-offs on the western, ocean-facing side suit experienced divers comfortable with surge and cold.

## 2. Illas Ons, Ría de Pontevedra

Ons sits at the mouth of the Ría de Pontevedra and offers a gentler introduction to Galician diving than the exposed western Cíes walls. The eastern, sheltered side has sandy channels and rocky reefs at 8 to 16 metres where octopus are almost guaranteed and cuttlefish hover over the kelp in spring. The island is inhabited and reachable by ferry from Bueu, which makes a day of two dives logistically simple. Spider crabs gather in numbers on the boulder slopes, and the resident wrasse — including the large ballan wrasse — are unusually confiding. This is a fine site for a checkout dive before tackling more demanding open-Atlantic locations.

## 3. Illas Sálvora, Ría de Arousa

Sálvora marks the entrance to the Ría de Arousa, the largest of the Galician rías, and its surrounding waters are wilder and less dived than Cíes or Ons. The granite reefs drop into 25 metres or more, swept by current that brings clear water and pelagic visitors — bass, large pollack, and shoaling mackerel. The exposure means conditions must be chosen carefully; a northerly swell makes the western reefs undiveable. When it works, Sálvora rewards advanced divers with the closest thing Galicia offers to a true open-ocean wall dive, carpeted in kelp and dead man's fingers soft coral.

## 4. Cabo Home, Ría de Vigo

On the mainland side of the Ría de Vigo, the Cabo Home headland and its three lighthouses overlook a series of rocky reefs accessible by short boat trips from Cangas or Vigo. Depths run 10 to 20 metres over granite ridges and kelp, with reliable octopus, conger and the occasional electric ray on the sand. It is a working dive area for the local clubs and a sensible cold-water training ground, sheltered enough that operators such as those affiliated with the Federación Galega de Actividades Subacuáticas run regular guided dives here through the season.

## 5. O Grove and the Ría de Arousa Reefs

The peninsula of O Grove, famous above water for its shellfish, sits in the productive Ría de Arousa and gives access to a string of shallow reefs and the protected Lagoa da Bodeira nearby. Underwater, the appeal is the sheer density of life that the ría's mussel-raft aquaculture supports — the rafts, or bateas, drop ropes thick with mussels into the water, and the structure beneath them teems with juvenile fish, nudibranchs and feeding pollack. Depths are modest, mostly under 15 metres, and the sites suit Open Water divers and macro photographers.

## 6. Costa da Morte, Outer Atlantic Coast

The Costa da Morte — the Coast of Death — earned its grim name from centuries of shipwrecks along this exposed stretch north of Cape Finisterre. For divers, that history means a scattered graveyard of wrecks, from nineteenth-century sailing ships to twentieth-century steamers, lying in 15 to 40 metres. The diving is committing: the coast is fully exposed to Atlantic swell, weather windows are short, and many wrecks are broken and scattered by the relentless sea. Advanced certification, local knowledge and a settled forecast are non-negotiable. The reward is genuine exploration on a coast that sees very few divers.

## 7. Ría de Ferrol Wrecks, Northern Galicia

The Ría de Ferrol, with its long naval history, holds several wrecks in sheltered water, making it a more accessible alternative to the Costa da Morte for wreck diving. The protected nature of the ría keeps conditions calmer and visibility more predictable, and the wrecks sit within recreational depths. Marine life has colonised the metal thoroughly — anemones, hydroids and the inevitable congers occupying the dark interiors. Local clubs in Ferrol and A Coruña run trips, and the diving suits Advanced Open Water divers with an interest in structure and history.

## 8. Illa de Cortegada, Ría de Arousa

Cortegada lies at the innermost end of the Ría de Arousa and forms the fourth archipelago of the national park, famous for the largest laurel forest in Europe above water. The diving is shallow, sheltered and estuarine — low visibility, soft sediment, and a different cast of life from the rocky outer islands: flatfish, gobies, hermit crabs and seahorses in the eelgrass. It is not a spectacular dive in the conventional sense, but for naturalists and macro divers it is a quiet, biologically rich corner of Galicia that rewards patience and a slow pace.

## 9. A Coruña and the Northern Reefs

The city of A Coruña, with its Tower of Hercules Roman lighthouse, sits on the north Galician coast and serves as a base for diving the rocky reefs of the surrounding bays. The water here turns colder and clearer than the southern rías, and the reefs hold kelp forests, large pollack, and abundant invertebrate life on the granite. Conditions are more exposed than the protected southern rías but more accessible than the Costa da Morte. It is a solid base for divers exploring northern Galicia, with several clubs operating from the harbour.

## 10. Ría de Vigo Inner Reefs

The inner reaches of the Ría de Vigo, close to the city itself, offer easy, sheltered diving year-round and serve as the training and recreation ground for Vigo's large diving community. Depths are modest and visibility is variable, but the sites are reliable, the boat trips are short, and the marine life — octopus, cuttlefish, wrasse and seasonal nudibranchs — is consistently present. For divers staying in Vigo, these inner reefs make perfect early-season dives while waiting for the right weather window to reach the Cíes.

## Planning Your Trip

The Galician dive season runs from late spring through early autumn, with June to September offering the best balance of warmer water and settled Atlantic weather. Even in summer the water rarely exceeds 19°C, so a 7mm semi-dry or a drysuit is strongly recommended. Vigo and Pontevedra are the main bases for the southern rías and the Atlantic Islands national park, while A Coruña and Ferrol serve the north. National park diving requires advance authorisation and operator permits, so book the Cíes and Ons well ahead. The diving is genuinely Atlantic — cold, green and surge-prone — so Advanced Open Water and good buoyancy in current are worth having before you arrive.
