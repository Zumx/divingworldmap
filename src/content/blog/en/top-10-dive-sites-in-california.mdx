California diving is defined by one thing above all: the kelp forest. Giant kelp, the fastest-growing organism on Earth, rises from rocky reefs in towering golden columns that sway in the surge and filter sunlight into shifting cathedral light. This is cold-water diving — surface temperatures range from a bracing 10°C in the north to a relatively mild 18°C in the southern islands — and a wetsuit of seven millimetres or a drysuit is the norm. But the reward is some of the richest temperate marine life anywhere: sea lions that dive-bomb your mask, garibaldi flashing orange against the green, and the slow return of the once-vanished giant sea bass. From the Channel Islands to Monterey Bay, California offers a wild, vigorous style of diving that demands respect and repays it generously.

## The Channel Islands National Park

The Channel Islands off the coast near Santa Barbara and Ventura are the crown jewel of California diving. Anacapa, Santa Cruz, Santa Rosa and San Miguel sit in cold, nutrient-rich water that fuels extraordinarily dense kelp forests and marine life. Anacapa Island, with its protected marine reserve, is famous for towering kelp, abundant garibaldi — California's state fish — and curious harbour seals. The islands are reached by day boat from Ventura, and the diving here feels genuinely remote and wild, with visibility that can exceed 25 metres on a good day and water rarely warmer than 16°C.

## Catalina Island and the Giant Sea Bass

Santa Catalina Island, the most accessible of the Channel Islands by ferry from the mainland, is the best place in California to encounter the giant sea bass. These enormous fish, which can exceed two metres and weigh well over 200 kilograms, were fished almost to oblivion by the mid-twentieth century. Protected since 1982, they are slowly recovering, and summer aggregations off Catalina now offer reliable sightings of these gentle giants drifting up from the kelp. The island's protected dive park at Casino Point near Avalon is a superb, easy shore dive through kelp and rocky reef.

## Casino Point Dive Park, Avalon

The Casino Point Underwater Park at Avalon on Catalina is California's most popular shore dive and the state's first municipal underwater park. Entry is from steps into a protected cove, and beneath the surface lies a maze of kelp, rocky reef and a scattering of small wrecks deliberately placed for divers. The marine-reserve status means fish are plentiful and unafraid: garibaldi, calico bass, moray eels and the occasional bat ray. The sheltered conditions and easy access make it ideal for training and for divers acclimatising to California's cold, kelp-rich water.

## Monterey Bay and the Breakwater

Monterey Bay on the central coast is one of the world's great temperate marine ecosystems, fed by a deep submarine canyon that brings cold, nutrient-rich water close to shore. The Breakwater at San Carlos Beach is the iconic training and shore dive, where a resident colony of harbour seals and sea lions interacts boldly with divers in the kelp. The bay's cold water — often around 10 to 12°C — supports an astonishing density of life, from nudibranchs to the otters that float in the kelp canopy above. It is the heart of California cold-water diving.

## Point Lobos State Marine Reserve

Just south of Monterey, Point Lobos is among the most protected and pristine dive areas in California, with strictly limited daily access to preserve it. Whalers Cove and Bluefish Cove offer entry into a wonderland of kelp, granite reef and the deep edge of the Carmel Canyon. The marine life is exceptional — wolf eels, lingcod, rockfish in every colour and the occasional sea otter foraging overhead. Permits are required and limited, which keeps the site uncrowded and the diving as wild as anywhere on the coast.

## La Jolla Cove and the Underwater Park

La Jolla in San Diego is home to the La Jolla Underwater Park and Ecological Reserve, a vast protected zone that includes the famous La Jolla Cove and the submarine canyons just offshore. The cove is a celebrated shore dive and snorkel, alive with leopard sharks that gather in the warm shallows during summer, bright garibaldi and rays gliding over the sandy bottom. Just beyond, the La Jolla Canyon plunges dramatically, offering advanced divers a chance at pelagic encounters. The leopard shark aggregation from June through autumn is one of the gentlest big-fish experiences in the state.

## The Wreck of the Yukon, San Diego

Off San Diego's Mission Beach, the HMCS Yukon, a 111-metre former Canadian destroyer, was sunk in 2000 as the centrepiece of the Wreck Alley artificial reef. It lies on its side in around 30 metres and has become a thriving cold-water reef cloaked in strawberry anemones and metridium, patrolled by rockfish, lingcod and sea lions. The depth and frequent low visibility make it an advanced wreck dive, but on a clear day it is one of the most rewarding wrecks on the West Coast. Nearby lies the wreck of the Ruby E and an old kelp-cutter barge.

## Farnsworth Bank, Catalina's Back Side

On the exposed seaward side of Catalina, Farnsworth Bank is a series of pinnacles rising from deep water, famous for the rare purple hydrocoral that encrusts the rock in vivid lavender. This is an advanced, often current-swept dive in deeper, colder water, but the colour and the chance of large pelagics — including the occasional shark and big schools of fish — make it legendary among experienced California divers. The bank is a designated reserve, protecting the slow-growing hydrocoral that takes decades to form.

## Northern California and the Mendocino Coast

North of San Francisco, the Mendocino and Sonoma coasts offer rugged, cold and demanding diving in green water rich with abalone history and dense kelp. Sites around Fort Bragg, Salt Point and the Mendocino headlands feature dramatic rock arches, surge channels and abundant invertebrate life. The water is cold and the conditions exposed, suiting confident divers in drysuits, but the scenery above and below the surface is starkly beautiful and far removed from the busier southern sites.

## Anacapa Landing Cove and the Sea Lions

Back at the Channel Islands, the Landing Cove at Anacapa deserves its own mention for the experience of diving with California sea lions. The animals are abundant, playful and astonishingly agile, often circling and nipping at fins in the kelp. Combined with the towering kelp forest, the bright garibaldi and the protected marine reserve, an Anacapa sea lion dive distils everything that makes California diving special — cold, green, alive and unmistakably wild.
