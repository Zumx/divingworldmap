Lithuania sits on the eastern Baltic, a country of forests, glacial lakes, and a short, sandy coastline along the Curonian Spit. It is not a name that springs to mind for diving, and that is precisely its charm. The Lithuanian scene is small, cold-water, and freshwater-focused, built around the thousands of lakes left by retreating glaciers and a handful of flooded quarries. Divers here are a tight community, well used to drysuits and the disciplines of cold, low-visibility water, and they prize the quiet, undeveloped character of their sites over any tropical spectacle. For the curious traveller, Lithuania offers a genuinely off-the-beaten-track corner of European diving.

## Diving the Lithuanian Lakes

Lithuania has thousands of lakes, and the deepest and clearest of them form the backbone of the country's diving. Lake Tauragnas, one of the deepest lakes in the country, draws divers for its depth and relatively clear water. Lake Alaušas and the Lakajai lakes in the Labanoras forest region offer similar cold, sheltered freshwater diving. These glacial lakes are at their best after the spring thaw has settled and before the summer algae bloom clouds the water, when visibility opens up and the cool, tannin-tinted water reveals submerged trees, drop-offs, and the quiet underwater world of the northern forest.

## Lake Tauragnas

Lake Tauragnas, in the Aukštaitija highlands of northeastern Lithuania, is among the deepest lakes in the country and a natural focus for divers seeking depth in a freshwater setting. The water is cold year-round, demanding a drysuit, and the visibility, while modest by tropical standards, is good for a northern lake once the season settles. Divers descend along sloping bottoms into darker, colder water, and the sense of exploring a deep glacial lake far from any sea gives the diving a distinct, contemplative character.

## The Labanoras Lakes and Lakajai

The Labanoras Regional Park, a vast expanse of forest and water in eastern Lithuania, holds some of the country's most atmospheric lake diving. The Lakajai lakes, including the spot known as Lakajai 2, sit amid pine forest and offer cold, clear freshwater diving in a genuinely wild setting. The appeal here is as much the surroundings as the diving itself: the still water, the forested shores, and the silence make for a diving experience that is more about immersion in a landscape than about any single underwater feature.

## Flooded Quarries

Beyond the natural lakes, Lithuania's divers turn to flooded quarries for accessible, sheltered training water. The Vaišvydava quarry near Kaunas is a well-used site, and the bathing area at Galvė Lake near Trakai offers easy water close to the historic island castle. These sites provide the controlled conditions ideal for courses and skill practice, with graduated depths and easy entries. The water is cold, as everywhere in Lithuania, but the convenience of a quarry close to the cities of Kaunas and Vilnius keeps these sites busy through the diving season.

## Galvė Lake and the Trakai Lakes

The lakes around Trakai, west of Vilnius, combine diving with one of Lithuania's most famous landscapes. Galvė Lake surrounds the red-brick island castle of Trakai, and its bathing area gives divers an accessible entry point into a historic setting. The lake is dotted with islands and offers gentle freshwater diving. The Pilaitė area near Vilnius adds another accessible option close to the capital. These sites are popular precisely because they pair easy, sheltered diving with the wider appeal of the Trakai region, making for a full day out.

## The Bridvaišis and Smaller Lakes

Lithuania's diving extends to numerous smaller lakes scattered across the country. Bridvaišis and similar lakes offer the quiet, low-traffic diving that defines the Lithuanian experience. These are not destination dives in the international sense, but for local divers and curious visitors they offer cool, clear-enough water, submerged structure, and the satisfaction of exploring lakes that see few divers. The Aiseto lake area, with its bridge and ferry crossing, adds another local site to the patchwork of freshwater options.

## The Baltic Coast and the Curonian Spit

Lithuania's short Baltic coastline along the Curonian Spit offers a different kind of diving, though the brackish, often murky Baltic is challenging. The water is cold, the visibility frequently poor, and the seabed sandy and featureless in many areas, but the Baltic does hold wrecks and historical interest for those willing to work for it. Coastal diving here is far less developed than the lake scene, and most Lithuanian divers regard the inland lakes and quarries as the heart of the country's diving rather than the sea.

## Planning a Lithuanian Dive Trip

Diving in Lithuania means cold freshwater, so a drysuit and proper thermal protection are essential. The season runs from late spring through autumn for comfortable diving, with the clearest lake water typically found in early summer before the algae bloom and again in autumn. The scene is club-based and informal, so connecting with local divers or a dive operation is the best way to access sites, understand entry points, and dive safely. Sites cluster around the population centres of Vilnius and Kaunas and in the lake-rich east, so plan your trip around those hubs.
