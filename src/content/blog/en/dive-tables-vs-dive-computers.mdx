There was a time when planning a dive meant sitting at a table with a laminated card, a pencil, and a slide rule of risk. You looked up your planned depth, found the allowable bottom time, calculated your residual nitrogen for the next dive, and committed the whole plan to memory before you ever touched the water. Today most divers glance at a wrist computer that does all of this continuously, silently, and far more accurately. The shift from tables to computers is one of the great quiet revolutions of the sport, and understanding both is still part of becoming a genuinely competent diver.

## What the tables actually were

Dive tables are printed schedules derived from decompression models, the most famous descended from the US Navy tables and the recreational versions later refined for sport diving. They answer a simple question: for a given depth, how long may you stay before mandatory decompression becomes necessary, and how long must you wait at the surface before diving again. They work by assigning each dive a letter group representing residual nitrogen, then carrying that group forward to plan the next dive.

The genius of the tables was that they reduced a fearsomely complex physiological problem to something a diver could compute with no electronics, in any conditions, with total reliability. They never run out of battery, never flood, and never fail. But they pay for that simplicity with a brutal assumption: the table treats your entire dive as if you spent every minute at your deepest point, the so-called square profile. A diver who drops to thirty metres for five minutes and then spends the rest of the dive at twelve is charged by the table as though the whole dive happened at thirty. That is safe, but it is wasteful of bottom time.

## Why the computer won

A dive computer throws away the square-profile assumption. It measures your actual depth every few seconds and feeds that real profile into a decompression algorithm running in real time, tracking the calculated nitrogen loading in multiple tissue compartments continuously. Because it credits you for every minute spent shallower than your maximum, it almost always grants more bottom time for the same level of risk, and it recalculates instantly the moment you change depth.

It also removes the largest source of error in table diving: the human. There is no looking up the wrong row, no arithmetic slip, no forgetting to account for a previous dive. The computer tracks ascent rate and warns you if you rise too fast, displays your remaining no-decompression time at a glance, logs the dive automatically, and on multi-level and repetitive dives it manages a complexity that hand calculation simply cannot match. For these reasons the computer displaced the table for everyday recreational diving within a generation.

## What the computer does not do

A computer is not magic, and treating it as an oracle is dangerous. It models your tissues using assumptions about an average diver; it knows nothing of your hydration, your body fat, how cold you are, or how hard you are working, all of which affect real decompression risk. It assumes you breathe a particular gas and will give dangerous readings if you switch gases without telling it. And it can fail. Batteries die, electronics flood, screens go blank, and a diver who has surrendered all understanding to the device is left helpless when it does.

This is the enduring argument for still learning the theory the tables embody. A diver who understands tissue loading, no-decompression limits, and ascent discipline can reason about a dive even when the screen goes dark, can sanity-check what the computer tells them, and can recognise when a plan is pushing into territory no algorithm fully captures. The table is no longer the daily tool, but the thinking behind it is permanent.

## Planning before the splash

Even in the computer era, dives are still planned in advance. Technical divers in particular plan meticulously, often using desktop software that models the entire dive, every gas, every stop, before they ever enter the water, then carry the computer as a live confirmation rather than a substitute for thought. Recreational divers plan more loosely but still set a maximum depth, a turn pressure, and a dive time, and use the computer to execute that plan rather than to improvise without limit. The healthiest relationship treats the computer as an instrument that informs decisions, not one that makes them.

## Redundancy and good practice

Serious divers rarely trust a single computer with their safety. Many carry two, or a computer plus a backup depth gauge and timer with a known table in mind, precisely because a single device is a single point of failure. The same logic that puts a spare mask in a pocket puts a backup plan behind the primary computer. Conservative settings matter too: most computers let the diver dial in extra caution, and adding conservatism costs only a little bottom time while widening the safety margin against everything the model cannot see.

Good practice also means diving your own computer. Following a buddy's device, or a guide's, ignores that your nitrogen loading is your own, shaped by your exact profile. Two divers on the same dive can legitimately have different remaining times, and each must respect their own.

## The honest verdict

The computer is unambiguously the better daily tool, and there is no nostalgia worth the lost bottom time and added risk of going back to tables for routine diving. But the table represents a body of understanding that no diver should skip. Learn how decompression works, learn how the tables encode it, then dive a computer with the comprehension to use it wisely and the humility to plan for its failure. The technology changed; the responsibility did not.
