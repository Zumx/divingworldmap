Vanuatu's diving reputation rests on a single extraordinary wreck — the SS President Coolidge — and the wreck more than justifies whatever hyperbole has been built around it. But limiting Vanuatu to one site would be a significant injustice; the archipelago of 83 islands stretches across 1,300 kilometres of the Coral Sea, with reef systems, walls, and underwater volcanic features that receive only a fraction of the diver attention they deserve. The island of Espiritu Santo in the north anchors the country's dive industry; Port Vila on Efate is the gateway for international arrivals and has its own cluster of excellent sites. Water temperatures run between 24°C in the austral winter and 29°C in summer, and visibility in open water regularly exceeds 30 metres. Vanuatu sits in the South Pacific cyclone zone, with the most active season from December through March. [Open the map](/map) to explore dive sites across Vanuatu.

## 1. SS President Coolidge, Espiritu Santo

The SS President Coolidge is one of the most dived wrecks on earth, and the superlatives attached to it are deserved. The former American luxury liner, converted to a troop transport in World War II, struck two friendly-fire mines entering the port of Luganville in October 1942 and sank in shallow enough water that her captain beached her on the reef to allow the troops aboard to disembark. Of the 5,000 soldiers on board, only two died. The wreck now lies on the reef slope with her bow at around 21 metres and the stern extending to 70 metres. The sheer scale — 199 metres long — is immediately apparent from the first dive. Penetration of the upper decks is conducted without overhead training; the deeper engine rooms and lower cargo holds are for technical divers. The wreck is home to enormous grouper, batfish in large schools, and a remarkable 'The Lady' mosaic in the first-class smoking room. Advanced Open Water for the upper decks; technical certification for the deep stern.

## 2. Million Dollar Point, Espiritu Santo

Adjacent to the Coolidge, Million Dollar Point is an artificial reef created by the dumping of thousands of tonnes of US military equipment into the sea at the end of WWII. Rather than ship war surplus back to the United States, the American military drove trucks, bulldozers, motorcycles, and jeeps off the reef edge into 3–40 metres of water. The equipment extends across a wide area, and much of it has been colonised by corals into a remarkable surrealist dive. Military vehicles sit in identifiable condition among coral gardens; jeep steering wheels and truck dashboards protrude from encrustations. The shallow portions are accessible as a shore dive or snorkel. The deeper sections — 25–40 metres — attract large fish aggregations. This is a genuinely unique dive; nothing else quite like it exists. Open Water for the shallows; Advanced for the deeper equipment fields.

## 3. Mele Reef, Efate

Mele Reef lies off the coast near Port Vila on the island of Efate, providing the capital's best dive within easy boat access. The reef is a combination of wall and bommie terrain at 10–30 metres, with excellent coral coverage and a diverse fish community built around the protection the site has received from local conservation initiatives. Turtles are common — green and hawksbill both — and the night dives here are particularly productive, with Spanish dancer nudibranchs regularly sighted from October through December. Mele Reef is a good entry point for divers who want to gauge Vanuatu's reef quality before committing to the northern itinerary; the variety of depth and terrain makes it worthwhile for more than a single visit. Open Water for most of the site; Advanced for the deeper wall sections.

## 4. Cathedral, Espiritu Santo

The Cathedral, also called the Blue Hole area, is a large underwater cavern system near Luganville with a cathedral-like opening at around 15 metres giving way to a chamber that divers enter from below and surface inside in natural light. The roof of the chamber above water level is open to the sky in sections, creating light shafts that divers photograph from inside. The walls in the submerged portion are clean limestone with little coral growth — the water is too clear and the light too filtered — but the geological drama compensates. The fish in the chamber entrance are habituated to divers and include large grouper and jacks that shelter in the shade. The site is reached by a short boat transfer from Luganville dive operators. Advanced Open Water recommended.

## 5. Underwater Post Office, Efate

The Underwater Post Office at Hideaway Island off Port Vila is the world's only waterproof post office, operating from a platform at around five metres where divers and snorkellers can post waterproof postcards to be delivered internationally. The reef surrounding the post office has been developed as a marine park, with coral gardens at 5–20 metres and good fish diversity including lionfish, moray eels, and several resident turtles. This is Vanuatu's best-known novelty dive, and while the novelty is real, the reef underneath it is genuinely worth doing on its own terms. Day trips from Port Vila are easily arranged. Open Water and accessible to snorkellers; a popular introductory dive for cruise ship passengers.

## 6. Aore Island Wall, Espiritu Santo

Aore Island sits in the channel between Espiritu Santo and the mainland, and its western face has a wall dive at 10–35 metres that receives almost no diver traffic compared to the Coolidge sites. The wall is in excellent condition, with large sea fans from 20 metres, purple and white soft corals in the current-swept sections, and a resident population of white-tip reef sharks that rest on ledges during the day. Current through the Aore channel can be significant on the tidal change; timing the dive on slack or the gentle early flood produces the calmest conditions. Large potato grouper patrol the base of the wall, and schooling surgeonfish work the crest. Advanced Open Water for the wall sections.

## 7. Tutuba Island Reef, Espiritu Santo

Tutuba Island is small and forested, sitting close to the south coast of Santo, and its fringing reef on the eastern face provides a sheltered dive in 8–22 metres with hard coral gardens of exceptional density. The site is used as a training and refresher dive by Luganville operators but offers more than its training-site reputation suggests: the reef is pristine, the fish life is undisturbed, and the macro diversity — including several species of nudibranch and at least one species of pygmy seahorse — is high. The shallow sections of the reef are accessible as a snorkel; divers exploring the lower reef slope find larger residents including Napoleon wrasse and banded sea kraits. Open Water.

## 8. Malo Island Wall, Espiritu Santo

Malo Island separates the large bay east of Luganville from the open sea, and the outer reef of the island drops in a vertical wall from a crest at 3 metres to beyond 50 metres. The wall is impressive in scale and relatively underexplored; limited boat traffic from the Luganville operators means conditions are undisturbed. Large sponges anchor to the wall face from 20 metres, and the current that sweeps the outer face concentrates schooling fish in eddies behind prominent coral structures. Grey reef sharks are reported on the deeper section, and the shallower crest holds a turtle population that uses the reef for resting between feeding trips. Advanced Open Water for the deep wall; Open Water on the crest.

## 9. Havana Reef, Efate

Havana Harbour was the site of significant WWII naval activity, and the seabed in the approach channel holds scattered military debris at depths of 15–25 metres alongside natural reef structures. The mix produces an unusual dive profile: reef fish communities on the natural bommies, and conger eels and octopus in the gaps of the corroding military material. The harbour itself is protected from the open Pacific swell, making this a reliable dive in conditions that close exposed outer sites. It is accessed from Port Vila by boat or by driving to the harbour mouth. Open Water.

## 10. WWII PT Boat Wreck, Espiritu Santo

A WWII-era PT boat — a small, fast patrol torpedo boat — lies on a sandy bottom near Luganville in around 20 metres, largely intact and covered in corals that have transformed it into an elegant reef structure. The boat is small enough to be circumnavigated in a single dive and the detail is remarkable: torpedo tubes in place, the engine compartment accessible, and the hull plating largely intact. Large lionfish occupy the wheelhouse and are encountered at close range throughout the site. The wreck is a natural companion dive to the Coolidge, offering a completely different scale of WWII encounter on the same day. Open Water.

## Planning Your Vanuatu Dive Trip

Port Vila on Efate is the main international entry point for Vanuatu, with direct flights from Australia, New Zealand, and Fiji. Espiritu Santo — where the Coolidge is located — is reached by a short domestic flight to Pekoa Airport outside Luganville. The best diving season is from April to November: the austral autumn through spring brings settled weather, good visibility, and manageable currents. Cyclone season from December through March can bring significant disruption, particularly in the north. Luganville's dive operators are clustered in a small area and primarily focus on the Coolidge; booking ahead is advisable during the peak July–September period when the wreck is busiest.
