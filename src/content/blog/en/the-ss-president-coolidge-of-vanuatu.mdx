Off the shore of Espiritu Santo, the largest island of Vanuatu in the South Pacific, lies one of the greatest wreck dives in the world — and one of the most accessible. The SS President Coolidge was a 198-metre luxury ocean liner requisitioned as a troopship during the Second World War. In October 1942, carrying over five thousand soldiers, she struck two American mines while entering the harbour at Luganville, and her captain, realising she was sinking, deliberately ran her aground so the men could escape. Almost all did. The ship then slid backward off the reef and settled on her side on the sloping seabed, where she has rested ever since — vast, intact, and reachable by simply walking into the sea from the beach.

## A Liner That Went to War

Launched in 1931, the President Coolidge was a flagship of American Pacific passenger travel, a vessel of art deco lounges, swimming pools, and fine dining. When war came, she was stripped of her civilian fittings and pressed into military service, ferrying troops across the Pacific. Her loss at Luganville was not to enemy action but to a defensive minefield laid to protect the very harbour she was entering — a tragic case of friendly fire. Only two of the more than five thousand aboard died: a fireman trapped below, and a captain who returned into the wreck to rescue men and never came out. Today the ship is both a war grave and a recreational dive of extraordinary scale.

## Diving Straight From the Beach

What sets the Coolidge apart from most great wrecks is its accessibility. There is no boat ride and no liveaboard required; divers kick out from a black-sand beach at Luganville and descend along a sloping bottom directly onto the wreck. The bow sits at around 21 metres, and the ship descends along her side to the stern at roughly 70 metres, meaning the wreck spans the full range from recreational depths to deep technical territory. This rare combination — a world-class wreck reachable on foot, suited to divers of widely varying experience — has made Espiritu Santo a pilgrimage site for wreck enthusiasts.

## The Lady and the Treasures Within

The single most famous feature of the Coolidge is a porcelain bas-relief fresco known as The Lady, depicting a woman riding a unicorn, set above what was the fireplace in the first-class smoking room. Reaching her requires a penetration dive to around 40 metres, and she has become the iconic image of the wreck. But The Lady is only one of countless artefacts the ship still holds. Divers pass guns, helmets, rifles, gas masks, jeeps, a cannon on the deck, chandeliers, tiles from the swimming pool, and the ship's own fittings, much of it remarkably preserved. Penetration routes lead through cargo holds, the barber shop, the dining saloon, and the engine room for appropriately trained divers.

## A Wreck Alive With Fish

Eight decades underwater have made the Coolidge a thriving reef. The hull is encrusted with hard and soft corals, and the structure shelters a dense population of fish: schools of barracuda, batfish, lionfish, moray eels, and the famous resident grouper. A large, curious humphead Maori wrasse has long been associated with the site, approaching divers on many dives. At night the wreck transforms, and night dives reveal flashlight fish, hunting lionfish, and the eerie spectacle of the corals feeding. The marine life adds a layer of living beauty to a structure already heavy with history.

## Depth, Decompression and Discipline

The Coolidge demands respect. Because the wreck slopes so steeply, it is easy to drift deeper than intended, and the most rewarding penetrations and the deeper sections lie well below recreational limits. Many dives here incur decompression obligations, and repetitive deep diving over a multi-day trip requires careful planning, conservative profiles, and ideally nitrox. The classic itinerary is a series of progressively deeper dives over several days, building familiarity with the wreck before attempting the deeper penetrations. Experienced local guides are essential; their knowledge of the routes, the hazards, and the layout of the vast interior is what keeps divers safe in this complex overhead environment.

## Espiritu Santo Beyond the Coolidge

The Coolidge is the headline, but Espiritu Santo offers far more. Nearby lies Million Dollar Point, where American forces dumped vast quantities of military equipment — trucks, bulldozers, jeeps, and machinery — into the sea at the war's end rather than ship it home, creating a surreal underwater scrapyard now colonised by coral and fish. The island's surrounding reefs, blue holes, and the famous Champagne Beach offer diving and beauty well beyond the wreck. For travellers, Luganville is a relaxed base, and the warm tropical water — typically in the high 20s Celsius — makes for comfortable diving year-round.

## A Place of History and Respect

As both a recreational dive and a war grave, the Coolidge carries a dual character that divers are asked to honour. The artefacts are protected, and removing anything from the wreck is forbidden; the site's preservation depends on every diver treating it as the historic memorial it is. The story of the captain who died returning for his men, and the soldiers who scrambled ashore as their ship went down, is part of what makes a dive here resonate beyond the spectacle. To swim through the Coolidge is to move through a moment of the Pacific War held intact beneath the warm Vanuatu sea.
