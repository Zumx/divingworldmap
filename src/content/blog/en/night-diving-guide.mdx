## A Different Reef

The reef at night is not the same reef you dove during the day. The same physical structure — the coral heads, the sandy channels, the cleaning stations — remains, but the community of animals inhabiting it shifts entirely. Many of the conspicuous daytime fish have retreated into crevices or produced mucus sleeping bags around themselves on the reef surface. In their place, the nocturnal community has emerged: octopus hunting openly across the sand, moray eels patrolling the rubble with a purposefulness they lack in daylight, flatworms gliding across coral surfaces, and the entire reef lit in brief flashes of bioluminescence from dinoflagellates disturbed by fin movements.

Night diving is not difficult, but it requires specific preparation, slightly different equipment, and a willingness to surrender the broad panoramic orientation of daylight diving in favour of a more intimate, torchlit world. For divers who have not tried it, a guided night dive on a familiar site is one of the most memorable experiences the sport offers.

## The Transformation After Dark

Several major categories of animal become active or visible only at night.

Cephalopods are the headline performers. Octopus, which spend daylight hours hiding in dens and venturing out only cautiously, are found actively hunting across open sand flats, displaying complex colour changes as they investigate, threaten, and camouflage. Reef squid hover mid-water near the surface, attracted to dive lights and rippling with iridescent colour signals. Cuttlefish patrol reef edges. On productive muck sites like Lembeh Strait or Tulamben's black-sand slopes, finding three or four octopus species in a single night dive is realistic.

Crustaceans emerge from reef crevices at night in abundance. Spiny lobsters (Panulirus spp.) forage openly across the reef, their long antennae waving above the coral. Crabs — swimming crabs, spider crabs, decorator crabs — become conspicuously active. Mantis shrimp, typically visible only as a pair of eyes in a burrow during daylight, sometimes move across open ground at night.

Coral itself is more visible in a meaningful sense at night. Stony corals extend their polyps fully for feeding after dark; the reef appears visually different with its polyps deployed, and the feeding tentacles of individual polyps are clearly visible under a torch. Feather stars open their arms fully in the water column, reaching filterable currents. Basket stars — a type of ophiuroid that looks like a tightly folded ball of spaghet during the day — expand into extraordinary branching structures metres across to filter-feed at night.

Bioluminescence is one of the particular pleasures of night diving. Turning off the dive torch for thirty seconds and waving a hand slowly through the water column produces flashes of blue-green light from disturbed dinoflagellates. Some bays — Vieques in Puerto Rico, Luminao Bay in Guam — are famous for the density of their bioluminescent dinoflagellate populations, but the phenomenon is present to some degree in most warm, nutrient-rich marine environments.

## Gear

The primary addition for night diving is a torch. The torch serves both navigation and illumination, but its quality matters: a torch that runs out of battery thirty minutes into a forty-five-minute dive, or produces insufficient beam width to light a subject properly, reduces the experience considerably. A primary torch in the 500 to 1000 lumen range with at least ninety minutes of burn time at full power is the minimum practical standard. A backup torch — typically smaller and clipped to the BCD — is required by most night dive operators and represents good practice regardless of requirement.

Glow sticks or LED safety markers on the tank valve or BCD serve the surface team. Looking up during a night dive, the team of divers is visible as a constellation of small light sources; the surface support can track the group's position and the captain can avoid running the boat over an ascending diver. Losing visual track of the group's position underwater because no tank glow sticks are deployed is how divers become separated at night.

A surface marker buoy (SMB) is as important at night as during the day — more so, because spotting an ascending diver from the surface without an SMB is significantly harder after dark. Deploying an SMB on ascent should be standard procedure on any night dive not ending at an established ascent line.

Dive computers work identically at night, but the increased sedentary pace of night diving sometimes results in longer bottom times at shallow depths. Tracking no-decompression limits applies equally after dark.

## Communication Underwater

Hand signals are largely useless at night because hands are invisible beyond the immediate pool of torchlight. Night diving communication relies on torch signals instead.

The standard conventions vary slightly by training agency and region, but the common core is: a slow circular motion of the torch beam means 'okay, all is well' (this replaces the resting OK signal); a rapid side-to-side motion means 'attention, come here'; a frantic repeated side-to-side signals an emergency. Many dive guides add their own supplementary signals for specific species sightings — a quick vertical flick often means 'come look at this' — and any site-specific signals should be established during the pre-dive briefing.

Torches are not pointed directly at another diver's face unless signalling; the full-power beam of a modern dive torch causes significant visual disruption and takes time to recover from. Pointing the beam at a buddy's chest or the water column beside them is the standard way to direct attention without blinding them.

## Preparation and the Pre-Dive Briefing

Night diving on an unfamiliar site is significantly harder than night diving on a site you know during the day. The standard recommendation is to dive the site at least once during daylight before attempting it at night. Knowing the topography — where the sandy channels run, where the reef wall drops away, where the anchor line is relative to the coral heads — means your torch orientation builds on existing spatial knowledge rather than trying to construct it from scratch in the dark.

The pre-dive briefing for a night dive should cover: entry and exit points and procedure, torch signal conventions, maximum depth and duration, turnaround point, lost-buddy procedure, and surface support signals. Many night dive operators use a dedicated shore or boat light to mark the return point; knowing what that light looks like and that it marks the exit is an important reference for the dive.

Entering the water in darkness takes a moment of adjustment. The first few minutes of any night dive are typically disorienting as the visual field contracts from the panoramic to the torchlit. This is normal and passes quickly. Experienced night divers often describe a narrowing of attention that they find meditative; the torch beam becomes the entire world, and what is outside it ceases to demand attention.

## Starting Out

The PADI Night Diver specialty, and equivalent programmes through SSI and other agencies, formalise night diving training into three supervised night dives with instruction on equipment, navigation, and species recognition. Taking this course with a qualified instructor on a familiar site produces confidence that carries forward into self-organised night dives.

A diver who has completed twenty daylight dives on a reef and then does their first night dive on the same reef often reports feeling like they are seeing it for the first time. That shift in perception is what makes night diving, for many practitioners, not a novelty but a permanent and essential part of what the sport offers.
