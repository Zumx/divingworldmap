Fiji has earned its reputation as the soft-coral capital of the world, and that title is not mere marketing. The country's 330 islands sit in a convergence zone where warm Pacific currents deliver nutrients year-round, supporting an extraordinary density of corals, reef fish, and pelagics. Visibility regularly reaches 30 metres or more in the outer reefs, and water temperatures hover between 25°C and 29°C across the seasons. Whether you are drawn to technicolour walls draped in gorgonian fans, adrenaline-charged drift dives through deep channels, or the intimacy of a village-run dive operation, Fiji delivers — often within the same morning.

## 1. Beqa Lagoon, Pacific Harbour

Beqa Lagoon is Fiji's most famous shark-dive destination and one of the few places in the world where you can reliably encounter bull sharks, tiger sharks, and tawny nurse sharks on a single dive. The site operates at around 30 metres on a sandy arena, and the choreography of eight or more bull sharks circling overhead is genuinely awe-inspiring rather than alarming. The surrounding lagoon walls carry dense soft-coral growth. Certification required: Advanced Open Water. Best season: year-round, though the sharks are most numerous May to October.

## 2. Rainbow Reef, Taveuni

Rainbow Reef stretches along the Somosomo Strait between Vanua Levu and Taveuni and is home to one of the most celebrated sections of reef in the Pacific. The soft corals here — predominantly orange, red, and purple Dendronephthya — form dense forests that are at their most vivid when currents pump through the strait. White Wall, a section of the reef blanketed in white soft coral, is photographed more often than any other site in Fiji. Depths range from 10 to 35 metres. Currents can be strong, so experience and good buoyancy control are essential.

## 3. Great White Wall, Taveuni

Part of Rainbow Reef but distinctive enough to stand alone, Great White Wall begins as a tunnel at 16 metres before opening onto a vertical drop covered top to bottom in white Dendronephthya soft coral. The effect is most dramatic when a slight current fans the polyps open. Divers typically begin at the tunnel entrance at around 12 metres and descend the wall to 30 metres before ascending slowly along the coral. Thermoclines can drop temperatures to 22°C at depth even in warm season. Advanced Open Water certification recommended.

## 4. Somosomo Strait, Taveuni

The channel between Taveuni and Vanua Levu funnels fast-moving water past a series of pinnacles, walls, and coral gardens. The Annie's Bommies section features towers of hard coral rising from 30 metres to within 5 metres of the surface, each encrusted with invertebrates and circled by jacks, barracuda, and the occasional hammerhead in winter months. Current timing is critical — dive operators plan entries around slack water, after which a gentle drift carries you through the site. Water temperature: 26–28°C. Best season: April to October.

## 5. Beqa Barrier Reef, Pacific Harbour

Separate from the lagoon shark dive, the outer edge of Beqa Barrier Reef drops off sharply through a series of walls and swim-throughs. The density of anthias schools here is extraordinary, and the hard coral table formations at medium depth (15–25 metres) are some of the healthiest in Fiji. Grey reef sharks patrol the outer edge, and manta rays pass through during the plankton blooms of June through September. Open Water certification is adequate for the shallower sections; the outer wall demands more experience. Entry via live-aboard or day boat from Pacific Harbour.

## 6. Namena Marine Reserve, Savusavu

The Namena Marine Reserve, protecting the barrier reef south of Savusavu, sees fewer divers than the Taveuni sites, which partly explains its pristine condition. North Save-a-Tack and South Save-a-Tack passages deliver strong but predictable drift dives past bommies teeming with barracuda and surgeonfish. Shark Alley lives up to its name with frequent white-tip reef shark sightings. Visibility averages 25 metres and can exceed 40 metres in the dry season. Advanced certification advised for passage dives; lagoon sites are suitable for Open Water divers.

## 7. Makogai Island, Lomaiviti Group

Makogai is rarely on the standard circuit, which makes it all the more rewarding. The waters surrounding this former leper colony and turtle-breeding station carry particularly healthy coral gardens and a surprising concentration of sea snakes, nudibranchs, and ribbon eels. A resident population of hawksbill turtles can be approached closely at the cleaning stations in the shallows. Depths across the best sites range from 12 to 25 metres, and the mild currents make it suitable for Open Water divers. Access requires a live-aboard or a private charter from Levuka.

## 8. Wakaya Island, Lomaiviti Group

Wakaya's reputation among serious divers rests on regular hammerhead shark sightings — not the occasional schooling aggregations seen elsewhere but small groups of great hammerheads moving along the outside wall between July and September. The wall itself is steep and beautiful, dropping from 10 metres to beyond safe recreational limits. Manta rays use the cleaning stations on the southern tip. Because Wakaya is a private island, dive access is typically through the island's own operation or a select few live-aboard operators. Water temperature: 26–28°C.

## 9. Vatu-i-Ra, Bligh Water

The channel between Viti Levu and Vanua Levu, known as Bligh Water, contains several seamounts and reef systems that receive little regular traffic. Vatu-i-Ra is the most accessible, offering drift dives past hard coral pinnacles at 15 to 35 metres with consistent current and reliably good visibility. Schools of big-eye trevally, Spanish mackerel, and barracuda are permanent residents, and the soft-coral growth on the deeper sections of the walls rivals Taveuni. Best reached by live-aboard; day-trip access from Rakiraki on Viti Levu is possible.

## 10. Yasawa Islands, Northern Fiji

The long chain of Yasawa Islands offers some of the most accessible reef diving in Fiji for visitors staying at the island resorts. Sites like Sawa-i-Lau Blue Lagoon incorporate cave swim-throughs with shafts of light filtering onto coral floors, while the outer reef passages produce reliable encounters with sea turtles, white-tip reef sharks, and colourful parrotfish. Depths suit Open Water divers throughout, rarely exceeding 18 metres on the recommended sites. Water temperature remains above 26°C year-round, and visibility is typically 20–30 metres.

## Planning your Fiji dive trip

The dry season from May to October brings calmer seas, stronger currents (ideal for drift diving), and the best shark and manta encounters. The wet season from November to April offers warmer water and lush terrestrial scenery, though some outer sites become inaccessible in heavy swells. Pacific Harbour is the main hub for shark diving, while Taveuni and Savusavu serve as bases for the northern reefs. Dive operations range from large resort-based shops to small village-run boats with a single guide who has dived those waters every day of his adult life.
