The Canary Islands sit in the Atlantic off the coast of Morocco, far enough south that the water stays diveable in a 5mm suit all year and warm enough in late summer — around 23 to 24°C — for many divers to drop to 3mm. What makes the archipelago extraordinary underwater is its geology: every island is a volcano, and that lava heritage has built arches, caves, chimneys and sheer black walls that plunge into clear blue water. Add a marine reserve at El Hierro that is regularly voted the best dive destination in Europe, resident angel sharks on the sandy bottoms, and visibility that routinely passes 30 metres, and the case for the Canaries as a serious year-round dive region writes itself.

## 1. El Bajón, El Hierro

El Bajón, in the Mar de las Calmas marine reserve off El Hierro's south-west coast, is the most celebrated dive in the Canary Islands. It is a twin seamount rising from deep water to within 8 metres of the surface, its flanks washed by nutrient-rich currents that draw enormous schools of barracuda, bastard grunts and amberjack. Groupers the size of armchairs hang in the current shadow. Depths run from 8 metres on the summit to well past 40 on the flanks, so the site suits Advanced Open Water divers who can manage current and depth. Visibility is frequently 30 metres or more. The water is calmest and clearest from June to November.

## 2. La Restinga and Punta Restinga, El Hierro

The village of La Restinga is the gateway to the Mar de las Calmas, and several superb dives sit within minutes of its small harbour. The volcanic seabed is studded with caves and lava tongues that shelter trumpetfish, moray eels and large dusky groupers grown tame inside the reserve. Manta rays and occasional whale sharks pass through in late summer. Most dives here run 12 to 25 metres over dramatic black rock, accessible to Open Water divers, with fishing banned throughout the reserve so fish life is exceptional. Water temperature peaks near 24°C in September and October.

## 3. Veril de las Aguas, Gran Canaria

Off the southern resort coast of Gran Canaria, near Pasito Blanco and Arguineguín, the Veril is a long reef edge dropping from around 18 metres to beyond 40. Angel sharks rest on the sand at the base of the reef in winter and spring — the Canaries are one of the last reliable strongholds for this critically endangered species. Stingrays, electric rays and butterfly rays share the sand, while the reef wall holds slipper lobsters and octopus. Visibility runs 15 to 30 metres. The southern coast is sheltered and diveable year-round, with the angel shark season strongest from November to May.

## 4. El Cabrón Marine Reserve, Gran Canaria

El Cabrón, near Arinaga on Gran Canaria's east coast, is one of the best shore dives in the Atlantic. A protected reserve since the 1990s, it is a maze of arches, gullies, caves and a famous swim-through called The Cathedral, all within 6 to 25 metres. Cuttlefish, trumpetfish, anglerfish, big shoals of roncadores and the occasional angel shark inhabit the rock. Entry is from the shore over a short rocky scramble, so a guided first visit helps. Conditions suit Open Water divers in calm weather; the site is exposed to easterly swell so check the forecast. Visibility is typically 15 to 25 metres.

## 5. Radazul and Tabaiba, Tenerife

The east coast of Tenerife around Radazul and Tabaiba offers easy shore and boat diving over volcanic reef and sand. The standout features are the wrecks — a sunken tugboat at Tabaiba sits upright in about 20 metres and is now smothered in life, and several smaller artificial reefs lie nearby. Angel sharks, stingrays and the bizarre, sand-dwelling garden eels are reliable on the flats. Depths run 8 to 30 metres, suiting all levels, and the leeward east coast is sheltered most of the year. Water temperature ranges from 19°C in late winter to 24°C in autumn.

## 6. El Puertito and the Turtles, Tenerife

In the south-west of Tenerife, near Costa Adeje, the small cove of El Puertito is famous for resident green turtles that graze the shallow seagrass and rock, often within snorkelling depth. Beyond the cove, boat dives reach reef and sand at 12 to 25 metres where eagle rays, trumpetfish and triggerfish are common. This is some of the easiest, most rewarding diving in the Canaries for newer divers and a guaranteed turtle encounter for underwater photographers. The water is calm and clear here for most of the year, with visibility often 20 metres or more.

## 7. Las Cuevas de los Cerebros, Lanzarote

Lanzarote's diving is defined by black lava and dramatic structure, and the brain-coral caves near Puerto del Carmen are a fine example. A series of caverns and arches at 12 to 30 metres hold angel sharks on the surrounding sand, large shoals of bream and roving barracuda. The Museo Atlántico, Europe's first underwater sculpture museum, sits nearby off Playa Blanca in around 14 metres — three hundred concrete human figures now colonised by marine life, accessible to Open Water divers. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres and the water stays around 19 to 23°C through the year.

## 8. The Blue Hole, Lanzarote

Also off Puerto del Carmen, the Blue Hole is a vertical lava chimney that divers enter at around 28 metres and follow up through the rock to exit at about 12 metres. It is a short, atmospheric formation rather than a deep technical cave, but the chimney effect and the shafts of light make it a memorable Advanced-level dive. The surrounding reef holds angel sharks, octopus and abundant nudibranchs for macro photographers. The leeward south coast of Lanzarote is sheltered and diveable almost every day of the year, with visibility regularly exceeding 25 metres.

## 9. Baja de Montaña Amarilla, Tenerife

On Tenerife's south coast near Montaña Amarilla, this site combines a striking yellow volcanic landscape above water with caves, arches and a sandy plateau below. Angel sharks and stingrays settle on the sand at 15 to 22 metres, while the rock holds moray eels, lobster and shoaling fish. It is a sheltered, current-light site that works well for Open Water divers and for shore entry. Visibility commonly reaches 20 to 30 metres. Late summer and autumn bring the warmest water, around 23 to 24°C, and the calmest seas.

## 10. Los Roques and the Northern Banks, La Palma

La Palma sees fewer divers, which is part of its appeal. The volcanic banks off the island's western and southern coasts drop steeply into deep blue water and attract pelagic fish — tuna, amberjack and barracuda — alongside the resident groupers and morays. The 2021 Cumbre Vieja eruption added new lava deltas to the coastline, and dive operators from Puerto Naos report fresh, fascinating geology underwater. Sites range from 12 to 40 metres, suiting all levels. Visibility is excellent, frequently 30 metres, and the western coast is sheltered for most of the year.

## Explore on the map

The Canary Islands reward island-hopping: El Hierro and La Palma for wild, current-swept reserves and big fish; Gran Canaria and Tenerife for accessible shore diving, wrecks and angel sharks; Lanzarote and Fuerteventura for lava structure and the underwater museum. Water stays diveable all year, with September and October the warmest and clearest.
