## Why a Light Belongs on Every Dive

The instinct to reach for a torch only when the sun goes down is one of the easiest habits to break once you have experienced what a dive light does in broad daylight. Even at 15 metres on a clear tropical reef at noon, you are surrounded by shadow. Coral heads cast deep pockets of darkness, overhangs hide invertebrate life, and the vibrant orange and red pigments of sponges and sea fans are filtered out entirely by the water column above. A beam of white LED light restores the full visible spectrum instantly, and suddenly what looked like a brown encrusted rock reveals itself as a scarlet sea whip crawling with tiny gobies.

The argument for carrying at least a small backup light on every dive is not about emergencies alone, though emergencies matter. It is about seeing the reef as it actually is.

## Primary Lights: Power, Burn Time and Beam

A primary dive light is the main tool — carried on a goodman handle, clipped to a D-ring, or hand-held — with enough output and battery endurance to carry a dive from start to finish. Most divers working in recreational depths between 20 and 40 metres find that 1,000 to 3,000 lumens is more than adequate. Technical divers penetrating wrecks or cave systems frequently carry 5,000 lumens or above, and canister-style lights running external battery packs can push past 20,000 lumens for extreme visibility in silty or turbid water.

Beam angle is as important as raw output. A narrow spot beam of around 8 to 12 degrees throws light a long distance and is ideal for signalling across open water or peering into a swim-through, but it creates a harsh hotspot that does little for macro photography or general reef orientation. A flood beam of 60 degrees or more spreads light widely, illuminating a broader scene with less contrast. Many experienced divers carry one of each: a narrow primary for penetration dives and a wide-angle light clipped as a backup for reef work.

Burn time deserves serious attention. Manufacturers quote maximum lumens at a particular burn time, but many lights step down their output after the first few minutes on maximum to protect the battery. A light rated at 1,500 lumens for 75 minutes may deliver that output for only 15 minutes before stepping to 800 lumens. Read the full specification curve, not just the headline figure.

## Backup Lights: Small, Reliable, Always There

A backup light has one job: to get you safely to the surface if your primary fails. In technical diving this is axiomatic — cave and wreck penetrations are conducted on a strict redundancy rule of three lights minimum, as losing visibility in a silty passage with no natural light is a genuine life-threatening situation. Even recreational divers benefit from a small clip-on backup. A canister the size of two fingers that clips to a BCD D-ring weighs almost nothing, costs under fifty euros, and has saved countless dives from ending early.

The best backup lights are simple to operate — often a single twist or push with a gloved hand — and run on common batteries like AA or 18650 cells that are easily replaced at a remote dive destination. Outputs of 150 to 300 lumens are sufficient for a controlled ascent from recreational depth.

## Night Diving: When the Light Becomes Central

Night diving shifts the torch from accessory to essential navigation tool, wildlife spotlight, and communication device simultaneously. On a well-organised night dive, every diver carries a primary and a backup, and each person wears a small chemical lightstick or LED marker on their tank valve so the divemaster can account for the group at a glance.

The technique of night diving with a light takes a little practice. Pointing it directly at a companion's face is the underwater equivalent of shining a torch in someone's eyes on land — it destroys their night vision and is universally considered bad manners. Instead, divers signal by moving the beam in a slow circle to indicate 'I'm okay', a rapid side-to-side sweep signals 'attention', and pointing the beam upward from under the chin is a reliable way to illuminate your own face during a face-to-face conversation.

Nocturnal reef life rewards the torch-equipped diver handsomely. Octopus emerge from crevices to hunt. Spanish dancer nudibranchs, which can exceed 35 centimetres, come out at night on many Indo-Pacific reefs. Basket stars unfurl from their daytime hiding places and spread filamentous arms across the current. Parrotfish sleep in mucous cocoons on the reef surface. None of this is visible without a light.

## Wreck and Overhead Environments

Any dive that involves passing under an overhead — a wreck hull, a cavern ceiling, a swim-through longer than about ten metres — requires a primary light. Without a torch in these environments you are navigating by touch, which is how divers become disoriented and, in extreme cases, fail to find the exit. The recreational threshold is defined as being within natural light and within direct vertical access to the surface, and if your dive deviates from that definition, your light is not optional.

Wrecks are particularly demanding on batteries and beam quality because silt is almost always present. Hovering above the silt line and moving slowly extends visibility dramatically, but even so, the best wreck lights are those with a focused beam strong enough to cut through particulate matter. On the SS Thistlegorm in the northern Red Sea at 32 metres, or inside the cargo holds of the Yolanda off Ras Mohammed, the difference between a 2,000-lumen focused beam and a cheap consumer torch is stark.

## Choosing and Maintaining Your Light

Material choices matter in dive lights. Anodised aluminium bodies are strong, conduct heat well, and withstand pressure reliably. High-quality plastics — particularly Delrin and ABS — are lighter and resist corrosion at the O-ring surfaces. Both work; the concern is manufacturing quality rather than material alone.

O-ring maintenance is the single most common point of failure. After every dive, rinse the light thoroughly in fresh water and allow it to dry before storage. Before every trip, inspect the O-rings for grit, hair, or flat spots, and apply a thin film of silicone grease. Do not use petroleum-based lubricants, which degrade rubber over time. Carry spare O-rings in your kit bag — they weigh a gram and can rescue a torch flooded on day two of a ten-day liveaboard.

Battery management is the other discipline. Lithium cells hold their voltage longer than alkaline in cold water and tolerate wider temperature swings, which matters for diving in Norway, the Farne Islands, or British Columbia where water temperatures hover around 8 to 12 degrees Celsius. Rechargeable 18650 lithium-ion cells are now the standard in quality primary lights, and carrying two fully charged spares adds almost no weight.

## Underwater Photography and Video

Photographers quickly discover that ambient light is the enemy of accurate colour, and a dive light or video light is the fastest way to solve the problem. A wide-angle video light mounted on an arm to one side of the camera housing creates dimensional light that separates subjects from the background. Using it directly on-axis produces the flat, backscatter-heavy images that mark amateur footage.

Colour temperature matters for still photographers using manual white balance. Most quality LED lights output around 5,500 to 6,500 Kelvin, close to daylight, which pairs well with RAW capture and custom white balance in post. Video divers working in shallow water sometimes prefer a warmer 4,500 K light to counteract the blue shift of shallow tropical water. ## Regulations and Etiquette

Several marine protected areas and dive sites have specific guidance about lights. The manta ray cleaning stations at Hanifaru Bay in the Maldives, for example, have prohibited underwater flashes and restricted bright lights during peak aggregations because the animals show clear avoidance behaviour. Similar guidance applies at some turtle nesting beaches. Responsible use means being aware of wildlife behaviour and dimming or angling your torch away from sensitive animals when the encounter calls for it.

Among divers, shining your light directly into another diver's camera housing when they are shooting is considered particularly inconsiderate. If you want to assist a photographer, ask before the dive and learn the correct positions for supplementary lighting — the effort is almost always appreciated.
