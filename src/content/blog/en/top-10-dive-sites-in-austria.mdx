Landlocked Austria has no sea, but it has lakes — dozens of them, formed by glaciers and fed by snowmelt from the Alps, with visibility that regularly exceeds 15 metres and water so clear that divers sometimes struggle to judge distance. The Salzkammergut lake district in Upper Austria and the Tyrolean mountain lakes represent two distinct diving environments: the former are relatively warm, shallow and rich in fish life; the latter are deep, cold and dramatically scenic, with sheer limestone walls descending into blue-black water beyond recreational limits. Austria's lake diving is a serious pursuit, with established club infrastructure, marked entry points and — at a handful of sites — genuine wrecks. Water temperatures in summer peak at around 24°C on the surface but drop quickly with depth; a 5mm wetsuit with a hood is the minimum for comfortable diving, and a 7mm suit or semi-dry is advisable below 10 metres.

## 1. Attersee, Upper Austria

Attersee is the largest of the Salzkammergut lakes and the centre of Austrian freshwater diving. Maximum depth in the dive zones reaches around 60 metres, though most recreational diving takes place above 30 metres along the western and southern shores. Visibility regularly hits 15 to 20 metres in summer. The lake holds pike, perch, roach and — in the deeper zones — landlocked char. Several dive clubs operate here, and the marked Weissenbacheinlauf entry point near the southern end provides access to gentle slopes and structure. Water temperature at 5 metres in July and August can reach 22°C. Open Water certification is sufficient for the shallower zones; the deeper walls benefit from Advanced training.

## 2. Plansee, Tyrol

Plansee is a dramatic mountain lake in Tyrol at roughly 1,000 metres elevation, hemmed in by forested ridges and with maximum depths exceeding 75 metres. The water is cold — rarely above 18°C at the surface in midsummer, and dropping to under 6°C below the thermocline — but the clarity is exceptional. Underwater terrain includes steep rock walls, fallen timber and boulder fields. The lake's northern shore has established entry points with easy shore access. Diving here is a different experience from the warmer Salzkammergut lakes: quiet, austere, and requiring thermal protection that accounts for rapid temperature change with depth. A 7mm wetsuit is the realistic minimum; many local divers use drysuits.

## 3. Blindsee, Tyrol

Blindsee sits at around 1,000 metres elevation in the Fernpass area and is visually one of the most striking alpine lakes in Austria — bright turquoise water against limestone cliffs. Diving is done from the northern shore; the site descends steeply from the shoreline, with boulders, crevices and submerged timber providing structure and habitat. Depths reach beyond 30 metres. Water temperature at the surface in summer hovers around 16–18°C; a drysuit or heavy wetsuit is strongly recommended. Visibility can reach 20 metres or more. The site is popular with local clubs and the Fernpass road makes it accessible as a day trip from Innsbruck. Advanced certification is recommended given the cold and depth.

## 4. Hechtsee, Tyrol

Hechtsee is a small, warm and shallow lake near Kufstein that offers some of the most beginner-friendly diving in Austria. Depths rarely exceed 20 metres, and the surface temperature can reach 24°C in summer, making a 3mm wetsuit comfortable from June through August. The lake is named for its pike population — large pike are a frequent sight for divers along the weed margins. Visibility is moderate, typically 5 to 10 metres, lower than the deeper mountain lakes but acceptable for recreational diving. The easy shore entry and calm conditions make Hechtsee a natural choice for try-dive sessions, Open Water student training and casual recreational diving close to the Inn Valley.

## 5. Attersee — Wrack Dixie, Upper Austria

Among the few genuine wrecks in Austrian lake diving, the Dixie lies at roughly 36 metres in Attersee, accessible from shore near the Steinbach end of the lake. The wooden motorboat sank decades ago and has settled largely intact into the sediment. Visibility at this depth is typically 12 to 18 metres. The dive involves a descent past the thermocline — water temperature drops noticeably at around 15 metres — and the wreck requires solid buoyancy control to avoid disturbing the sediment that has accumulated around the hull. Advanced Open Water certification and experience with cold, deeper dives is expected. This is one of the more technically interesting wreck dives available in central European freshwater.

## 6. Nöhmer, Attersee, Upper Austria

Nöhmer is a popular and well-marked dive site on the eastern shore of Attersee, offering a rocky slope descending to around 40 metres in excellent visibility. Perch, pike and char appear regularly; the bottom is clean gravel and rock with good light penetration. The site is accessible from a small parking area and has become a standard reference dive for Austrian divers testing new equipment or checking buoyancy before deeper excursions. During summer weekends it is genuinely busy, with multiple groups in the water simultaneously — buoyancy discipline and situational awareness matter more here than at remote sites. Open Water divers are comfortable to around 18 metres; deeper sections reward Advanced certification.

## 7. Ofen, Upper Austria

Ofen is a dive site within the Attersee system named for an underwater rock formation resembling an oven. It sits at around 12 to 25 metres on a rocky reef system and is well regarded for its fish life — particularly large pike that hover motionless in the thermocline and move with sudden acceleration. Visibility is typical Attersee: 12 to 18 metres in summer. The site is reached by boat from club pontoons on the lake's western shore. It presents no particular technical challenge and suits Open Water divers comfortable with cool freshwater conditions. It is often paired as a second dive with deeper sites along the same shore.

## 8. Schwarze Brücke, Salzkammergut

Schwarze Brücke — 'Black Bridge' — is a freshwater entry point in the Salzkammergut region with access to a boulder-strewn slope descending into deeper water. The site sees less traffic than the main Attersee entry points and rewards divers who prefer quieter conditions. Fish life includes the usual Salzkammergut mix of pike, perch and roach; the bottom terrain is interesting at recreational depths of 15 to 30 metres. Water temperature follows the regional pattern: warm at the surface from June through August, significantly cooler below 15 metres year-round. A 5mm wetsuit covers summer diving; autumn and spring diving below the thermocline warrants heavier exposure protection.

## 9. Steinwand, Attersee

Steinwand is a vertical rock wall on the southern end of Attersee that drops from the shallows to around 50 metres before shelving out. The wall is covered in freshwater sponge, algae and encrusting organisms unusual in alpine lakes. Visibility along the wall typically reaches 15 metres or more. The depth and the vertical nature of the diving require controlled ascent rates and awareness of gas planning — Steinwand is the kind of site where inexperienced divers can end up deeper than intended without noticing. Advanced certification is standard for diving here. In winter, when the tourists depart, the lake becomes very quiet and visibility often improves further.

## 10. Unterwasserwald, Upper Austria

Unterwasserwald — 'Underwater Forest' — refers to sites where forest was deliberately flooded during dam construction, leaving standing trunks on the lake bottom as diver attractions. Several Austrian lakes have such features. The surreal quality of swimming through submerged tree crowns in clear water makes these sites memorable and photographically compelling. Depths in the forest zones typically range from 10 to 20 metres, making them accessible to Open Water divers. The wood provides habitat for perch and other fish. Exact locations vary; local clubs and dive operators can direct divers to the active sites where the timber is still structurally intact.

## Planning Your Trip

Austrian lake diving runs from May through October; July and August offer the warmest surface water and longest daylight hours, but also the most diver traffic at popular sites. The Salzkammergut lakes are served by dive clubs in towns like Seewalchen, Unterach and Steinbach am Attersee. Tyrolean lakes are accessible from Innsbruck or Kufstein. Equipment rental is available through clubs and commercial dive centres, though most visitors bring their own thermal protection. A 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is the practical choice for comfortable all-day diving in alpine lakes regardless of season.
