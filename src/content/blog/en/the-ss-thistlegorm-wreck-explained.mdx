On the night of 6 October 1941, a British merchant ship lay at anchor in the Strait of Gubal at the northern entrance to the Red Sea, waiting to round the Suez Canal. She was the SS Thistlegorm, a steam freighter carrying war materiel to the Eighth Army in North Africa. Two German Heinkel bombers, hunting for a troopship reported in the area, found her instead. A pair of bombs struck the after holds, the ammunition stowed there detonated, and the ship was torn open and sank within minutes. Today she lies upright in around 30 metres of clear water, and she is, by common consent, the most famous wreck dive in the world.

## How the Thistlegorm Came to Rest Here

Built in Sunderland in 1940, the Thistlegorm was on her fourth voyage when she was lost. The convoy route to Alexandria through the Mediterranean was effectively closed by Axis air and naval power, so Allied supply ships took the long way: down the Atlantic, around the Cape of Good Hope, up through the Red Sea, and finally through the Suez Canal. A collision in the canal ahead of her forced the Thistlegorm to anchor and wait at Sha'ab Ali, near Shag Rock, a notorious reef that has claimed several ships over the years.

That delay was fatal. The waiting ship made a static target, and the bombs that hit holds four and five ignited the tank and aircraft ammunition stored aboard. The explosion was so violent that it threw a railway locomotive, carried as deck cargo, dozens of metres clear of the hull. Nine men died. The wreck lay undiscovered by the diving public until Jacques Cousteau located and filmed her in the early 1950s, then largely forgotten again until the 1990s, when the rise of Red Sea tourism turned her into a pilgrimage.

## Descending onto the Wreck

The Thistlegorm sits upright on a sandy bottom, her bow intact and her stern section torn apart by the blast. The deck lies at around 16 metres, the seabed at roughly 30. Most operators run the dive over two descents: an exterior orientation tour of the hull, the gun deck and the scattered debris field, followed by a penetration of the cargo holds where the real treasure lies. Currents in the Strait of Gubal can be strong and unpredictable, and the mooring lines are often the only reliable way down and back in poor conditions, so this is not a beginner site.

Visibility is typically good, often 15 to 25 metres, though the heavy traffic the wreck now receives can stir up silt inside the holds. Marine life has colonised the steel thoroughly: glassfish swarm the interior in shimmering clouds, batfish hover over the deck, and barracuda and trevally patrol the structure. Lionfish and moray eels have taken up residence in the machinery.

## The Cargo: A Frozen Moment of 1941

What makes the Thistlegorm extraordinary is her cargo, preserved almost exactly as it was loaded. The holds still contain rows of BSA and Norton motorcycles, Bedford and Morris trucks, Universal Carrier armoured vehicles, Wellington bomber wings, aircraft tyres, rubber boots, rifles, and crates of ammunition. To swim through the holds is to drift over a perfectly stocked military warehouse from the Second World War, dim and silt-hung, every object instantly recognisable.

On the seabed beside the hull lie the two steam locomotives blown from the deck, along with their coal tenders and water carriers, destined for the Egyptian railways. Near the stern, the ship's anti-aircraft gun and heavy machine gun still point uselessly skyward, a poignant reminder of how little defence she had against the attack that found her. Divers are asked to look and not touch; decades of souvenir-taking have already stripped much of the smaller material away.

## Conservation and the Pressures of Fame

Fame has been hard on the Thistlegorm. She receives huge numbers of divers, and the cumulative effects of fin damage, exhaled bubbles eroding the steel from inside, careless penetration, and outright theft have all taken a toll. Conservation projects have surveyed the wreck and installed mooring buoys to stop anchors being dropped into her, and responsible operators brief divers firmly on buoyancy and the no-touch rule. The wreck is a war grave in spirit if not in formal designation, and the men who died aboard deserve that respect.

## Diving Her from the Egyptian Coast

The Thistlegorm is most often dived as part of a northern Red Sea liveaboard itinerary out of Hurghada or Sharm el-Sheikh, frequently combined with the reefs of Ras Mohammed and the wrecks of Abu Nuhas. Day boats also make the long run from Sharm and Hurghada when conditions allow, though the crossing is exposed and weather dependent. Some divers base themselves further south on the Egyptian coast, at house reefs and resorts such as the Hausriff Zabargad Beach Resort or the dive bases around Wadi Lahmy and Lahami Bay, and travel north for the wreck as a highlight of a longer trip.

For divers building a southern Egyptian itinerary, operators along the Marsa Alam coast, including the Wadi Lahmy Azur Housereef, Barakuda Lahami Bay, and the well-known Red Sea Diving Safari camp at Wadi Lahami, offer reef and dugong dives that pair naturally with a northern wreck excursion. Site names you will hear locally include Hamada, a wreck near Abu Ghusun, and Yatouche, reflecting the region's depth of dive options beyond the Thistlegorm itself.

## Planning a Thistlegorm Dive

An Advanced Open Water certification with some wreck and deep experience is the practical minimum; a wreck specialty and good air consumption help you make the most of the limited bottom time at depth. A torch is essential for the holds, and a wreck reel or line awareness matters if you penetrate beyond the natural light. Nitrox extends your no-decompression time across the repetitive dives a liveaboard demands. The best months are the warmer, calmer windows of spring and autumn, when the Strait of Gubal is least likely to blow the day boats off the site.
