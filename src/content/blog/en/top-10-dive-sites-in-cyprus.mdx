Cyprus occupies the northeastern Mediterranean where the sea is warm, clear and, in the summer months, almost bath-like. Water temperatures range from 17°C in winter to 29°C in August, and visibility regularly exceeds 25 metres, making the island one of the most accessible and comfortable diving destinations in Europe. The island's rocky coastline, limestone cliffs and scattered offshore reefs provide a variety of terrain, and the waters are relatively undisturbed by major industrial fishing. But the real reason Cyprus appears on serious divers' itineraries is a single wreck: the MV Zenobia, a Swedish ferry that sank on her maiden voyage in 1980 and is widely considered one of the top wreck dives in the world. Around that centrepiece sits a supporting cast of reefs, caves and secondary wrecks that make a week-long diving trip here genuinely rewarding.

## 1. Zenobia Shipwreck, Larnaca

The Zenobia sank in June 1980 in a still-disputed accident less than a kilometre off Larnaca, on her way from Sweden to Syria. The roll-on-roll-off ferry, 178 metres long, lies on her port side in 16 to 42 metres of water, largely intact with 104 trucks still secured in the hold. The scale is extraordinary: the vehicle decks can be penetrated by recreational divers with a torch, the trucks remain in the rows they were loaded in, and the fish population — grouper, barracuda, amberjack, lionfish and enormous schools of small fish — has made the wreck one of the richest artificial reefs in the Mediterranean. The bow reaches to 16 metres, the keel to 42 metres. Advanced Open Water is recommended; Open Water divers can access the shallower sections with a guide. Visibility is consistently 20 to 30 metres.

## 2. Zenobia — The Trucks

Diving the Zenobia's interior, specifically the truck decks, is a separate experience from the exterior circuit and warrants treatment as its own dive. The main vehicle deck is accessible through large openings in the hull and divers can navigate between rows of lorries in the eerie light — torchlight catching paint, chrome fittings and silt that still covers the cabs of vehicles loaded 45 years ago. This is not full wreck penetration in the technical sense, as ambient light reaches most of the accessible interior, but it requires confidence in enclosed spaces and controlled buoyancy. Local dive operators at the Larnaca Marina classify this as an Advanced dive and provide detailed orientation before entry. Multiple dives are needed to cover the full wreck properly; most divers return to Zenobia repeatedly.

## 3. Kerynia Wreck, Kyrenia Harbour, Northern Cyprus

The ancient Greek merchant vessel that sank near Kyrenia around 300 BC is one of the oldest shipwrecks ever excavated. The original timbers were recovered in the 1960s and are displayed in the castle museum — what divers find today are the replicated remains and the archaeological heritage of the site. Kyrenia harbour, in the north of the island accessible from the Turkish Cypriot administration side, has modern recreational diving in the harbour approaches and the surrounding coastline, with rocky reefs at 10 to 20 metres. The historical significance of the area and the castle setting make Kyrenia a rewarding destination even beyond the diving.

## 4. Lady Thetis Wreck, off Larnaca

The Lady Thetis is a small dredging vessel deliberately sunk in 1994 as an artificial reef near the Zenobia site off Larnaca. It lies in 13 metres of water and provides a much more approachable dive than the deeper parts of the Zenobia — visibility is similar at 20 to 25 metres, fish life is good with resident barracuda and grouper, and the small scale of the vessel makes it fully explorable in a single dive. The site is often used as a second dive after the Zenobia, giving an additional hour of bottom time without the deeper gas consumption. Open Water divers are completely comfortable here, and the site works well for night dives.

## 5. LEF1 Tugboat, Larnaca

The LEF1 is a small tugboat intentionally sunk near Larnaca as an artificial reef in relatively shallow water — around 10 to 15 metres. Like the Lady Thetis, it serves the function of extending diving around the Larnaca hub beyond the Zenobia and into more accessible territory for Open Water divers or those logging additional dives. The wreck has colonised rapidly with fish; scorpionfish are common on the hull surfaces and octopus frequently occupy the engine space. It is a short swim from shore and suitable for night diving. Visibility averages 15 to 20 metres. Local dive centres include this in beginner-level programs and as a camera dive for photographers who want a compact subject they can circle repeatedly.

## 6. Elpida Wreck, Paphos

The Elpida is a small cargo vessel sunk as an artificial reef off Paphos on the island's western coast. It lies in 25 to 30 metres and offers a Paphos-side wreck alternative for divers who are basing themselves on the western coast rather than Larnaca. The Paphos coastline has a number of rocky reef dives in the 8 to 20-metre range with good fish life — grouper, bream and moray eels — and the Elpida adds a wreck element to the package. Visibility on the western coast is similar to Larnaca at 15 to 25 metres. Advanced Open Water is recommended. Paphos dive operators include this in multi-day programs.

## 7. St. George's Island, Paphos

St. George's Island, a small rocky island off the Paphos coast, is one of the premier reef dives in western Cyprus. Dive circuits run around the island's base at 8 to 20 metres over rock reef with excellent fish populations, including large moray eels, grouper, damselfish and seasonal pelagic visitors. Deeper sections on the seaward face drop to 25 to 30 metres with sea fans and encrusting organisms. Loggerhead sea turtles are sighted here regularly, particularly from July through September when they feed on the jellyfish populations. Visibility averages 20 to 25 metres in summer. The site is accessible from shore or by short boat trip; Open Water certification is sufficient for the shallower circuits.

## 8. Akrotiri — The Barge

The British Sovereign Base Area at Akrotiri on the southern coast has several dive sites available to civilian divers by arrangement, including a metal barge sitting in 18 metres of water off Akrotiri Bay. The barge has become an excellent artificial reef, thoroughly colonised by fish and invertebrates, with resident octopus, grouper and a permanent school of barracuda hovering above the structure. The military base context means access procedures differ from civilian dive centres; advance arrangement through operators in Limassol is necessary. The broader Akrotiri Bay reef is well preserved and offers rocky reef diving at 10 to 25 metres with above-average fish biomass for the Cyprus coast.

## 9. MAV Achaios, Limassol

The Achaios is a modern cargo vessel sunk as an artificial reef off Limassol in the early 2000s. It lies on a sandy bottom in 25 to 35 metres of water and has developed into a well-populated wreck with resident fish that include large conger eels in the holds and schools of chromis on the superstructure. The water clarity off Limassol is variable — the port area turbidity affects the western side — but the eastern exposure generally offers 15 to 20 metres of visibility. Advanced Open Water is recommended. Limassol is the island's commercial port city and has several dive operators capable of running daily trips to the Achaios.

## 10. Constandis Reef, Northern Limassol Coast

Constandis reef is a natural rocky reef system north of Limassol at depths of 5 to 20 metres, used by local dive centres as a training area and for casual recreational diving. The reef has a good density of fish life by Cypriot reef standards — moray eels, damselfish, sea bream and the occasional octopus — and visibility typically runs 15 to 20 metres. Shore access is possible at several points; boat trips offer more flexibility in coverage. This is a site that rewards patient macro observation rather than topographic drama: the crevices and ledges throughout the rocky structure yield nudibranchs, shrimp and small cryptic fish that make it worth multiple visits with a camera. Open Water certification sufficient.

## Planning Your Trip

Cyprus diving runs year-round; water temperature bottoms out at 17°C in February and March, when a 5mm wetsuit is necessary, and peaks at 28–29°C in August when a 3mm is more than adequate. The Zenobia is the non-negotiable dive, and Larnaca is the base for accessing it — multiple dives are standard and necessary to understand the scale of the wreck. Paphos suits divers wanting reefs and secondary wrecks on the western coast. Most operators serve both novices and Advanced divers, and the island's compact size means crossing from Larnaca to Paphos takes less than 90 minutes by road.
