Croatia's Adriatic coast runs for nearly 6,000 kilometres once its 1,200 islands are counted, and the diving covers everything from Second World War wrecks and submerged Roman amphorae to vertical walls carpeted in red coral and shallow reefs where octopus, grouper and sea bream treat divers as furniture. The Adriatic is relatively shallow for a European sea — average depths of 170 metres — and the thin, warm, clear water of the southern islands reaches visibility of 30 to 40 metres in midsummer. Water temperature ranges from 13°C in winter to 28°C in August. A 3mm wetsuit is comfortable from June through September; spring and autumn warrant a 5mm. The dive industry is well established along the Dalmatian coast, with operators on every major island and the mainland towns of Split, Dubrovnik and Zadar.

## 1. Vis Island

Vis was a Yugoslav military island closed to foreign visitors until 1989, which meant its underwater environment received decades of inadvertent protection. The diving around Vis is among the finest in the Adriatic: clear water, intact reef systems, several wrecks and the dramatic submarine caves of Bisevo nearby. The main dive sites run along the island's edges at 15 to 40 metres, with walls supporting colonies of red coral (Corallium rubrum), white gorgonians and large grouper. The Blue Cave at Bisevo, while primarily a tourist attraction, is accessible by dive and the colour effects in the enclosed space are extraordinary. Vis is served by ferry from Split; dive operators on the island run multi-day programs and boat trips to outlying sites.

## 2. Premuda Island

Premuda, in the northern Dalmatian archipelago, is most famous for a naval battle fought in June 1918 when an Austro-Hungarian destroyer squadron ambushed an Italian flotilla. The wreck of the Italian destroyer Espero lies in around 28 metres and is one of the most interesting war wrecks in the Adriatic. The island itself is tiny and sparsely inhabited — a quality that keeps dive traffic on the lower end. The surrounding reefs are clean and little-dived, with walls supporting dense populations of sea fans, scorpionfish, octopus and damselfish. Visibility is typically excellent at 20 to 30 metres. Premuda is reached by ferry from Zadar; local accommodation is limited, making liveaboard approaches or day trips from Zadar the more practical option.

## 3. Baron Gautsch Wreck, off Rovinj

The Baron Gautsch was an Austrian passenger steamer sunk by a mine in August 1914 at the very outset of World War One, with heavy loss of life. The wreck lies in 28 to 40 metres off the coast of Rovinj in Istria and is one of the most historically significant dives in the Adriatic. The hull is substantially intact with the upper structure recognisable, and resident marine life includes large conger eels, schools of sea bream and occasional amberjack passing through. Visibility typically runs 15 to 25 metres. The depth requires Advanced Open Water certification and good gas management — the deeper sections of the wreck reach 40 metres and leave limited bottom time. Rovinj dive operators run day trips to the site, typically timed to avoid the worst boat traffic.

## 4. Peltastis, off Rovinj

The Peltastis is an Austrian cargo steamer that sank in 1904 near the Baron Gautsch site off Rovinj. The wreck lies in 32 to 42 metres and is in a more deteriorated state than the Gautsch, but the cargo hold is accessible and sections of the hull are intact enough to explore with care. The two wrecks are often combined in a single boat trip by operators from Rovinj — the Gautsch as the shallower, main dive and the Peltastis as the deeper second. Advanced Open Water is required; the depth demands careful gas planning. Red scorpionfish sit camouflaged on the wreck structure; large conger eels occupy the holds. Visibility averages 15 to 20 metres.

## 5. Susac Island Wall

Susac is a remote uninhabited island in southern Dalmatia, accessible only by boat from Hvar or Korcula. The wall on the island's southern and western faces descends from 5 metres to depths well beyond recreational limits, and the quality of the wall diving is exceptional — red coral, large sea fans, gorgonians and dense schools of damselfish on the shallower sections, giving way to sea fans and sponge communities at 30 to 40 metres. Visibility frequently reaches 30 metres in summer. Loggerhead sea turtles, though uncommon, have been documented here. The remoteness means the reefs see limited damage; the wall is in better condition than sites closer to tourist infrastructure. Liveaboard trips or long day-boat runs from Hvar town make Susac accessible.

## 6. Kornati Archipelago

The Kornati National Park encompasses 89 islands, islets and reefs in the northern Dalmatian island group and contains some of the most dramatic underwater topography in the Mediterranean. The seaward walls of the outer Kornati islands drop vertically from the surface — in places to 70 metres and more — with exposed faces colonised by red coral and gorgonians at depth. The current-swept channels between islands concentrate large fish: grouper, dentex and saddled sea bream in numbers that have diminished elsewhere in the Adriatic. Dive operators in Murter and Šibenik run Kornati liveaboard programs; the park's remoteness limits day-trip access from the mainland. Summer is the primary season; Advanced certification is recommended for the deeper wall sections.

## 7. Vrnik Cave, Korcula Island

Korcula Island in southern Dalmatia has a collection of submarine caves and overhangs along its northern coast, of which the Vrnik Cave is the most regularly dived. The cave entrance sits at around 6 metres, descends through a series of chambers to around 25 metres, and exits into open water — a full swim-through with natural light visible throughout. The cave interior supports lobster, moray eels and the crimson colours of coralline algae that do not require deep water where no direct sunlight reaches. Visibility in the cave is limited but usually acceptable at 8 to 12 metres. The site requires a torch and comfort in enclosed spaces; it does not require full cave certification but benefits from Advanced training and good buoyancy. Operators in Korcula town run regular trips.

## 8. Palagruza Island, Central Adriatic

Palagruza is the most remote island in the Adriatic, sitting almost equidistant between Croatia and Italy in waters 100 to 170 metres deep. Dive sites around the island include walls and pinnacles in excellent visibility — often exceeding 30 metres — with large grouper, dentex and amberjack that see very few divers per year. The crossing from the mainland requires several hours by fast boat and is weather-dependent; most dive trips to Palagruza are run by liveaboards based in Split or Dubrovnik. Depths on the main sites run 15 to 40 metres. The isolation and the quality of the fish life make Palagruza the destination for divers who have exhausted the more accessible Croatian sites.

## 9. El Hawi Star Wreck

The El Hawi Star is a more recent wreck in Croatian waters — a cargo vessel that sank in the 1980s and now lies in around 35 to 50 metres in the southern Dalmatian area. The wreck is large enough that the hold sections can be explored in the upper portions, while the engine room and deeper sections require technical preparation. Marine colonisation is well advanced: the hull is encrusted with mussels, soft corals and sponges, and large conger eels have established themselves throughout the structure. Grouper and sea bream are common around the wreck's superstructure. Advanced Open Water certification is the minimum; the full depth requires technical qualifications. Operators in Dubrovnik or Split occasionally include this site in extended itineraries.

## 10. Plavi Jadran — Brac Island Reefs

Brac Island, the largest central Dalmatian island, has reef systems on its southern face that receive the full exposure of the open Adriatic — cleaner water, better visibility and more pelagic species than the sheltered inner channels. Sites along the southern coast drop to 30 to 40 metres over reef structures with gorgonians, scorpionfish and regular sightings of dentex. The island is well connected by ferry to Split; dive operators based in Bol on the south coast run daily trips to these sites. Visibility averages 20 to 25 metres in summer. The southern face is more exposed to swell, so the season runs primarily June through September. Advanced certification is recommended.

## Planning Your Trip

The primary Croatian dive season runs from June through September, with July and August the warmest and busiest months. Visibility is highest in midsummer. The shoulder months of May and October see fewer divers, lower prices and cooler but still manageable water. A 3mm suit is sufficient from June through September; a 5mm is more comfortable in May, October and for multiple daily dives. Split is the main hub for access to central and southern Dalmatia; Zadar serves the north. Rovinj covers the Istrian wreck sites. Most operators offer guided dive programs suitable for Open Water graduates; the deep wrecks and remote island walls generally require Advanced certification.
