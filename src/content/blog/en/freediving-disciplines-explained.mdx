A competitive freediver lies face-down in a pool, motionless, eyes closed, while a judge watches the second hand crawl past eleven minutes. There is no thrashing, no struggle visible from the surface — just a body suspended in deliberate calm, the heart rate slowing, the diver descending into a mental state most people will never visit. This is static apnea, the simplest of the freediving disciplines to describe and among the hardest to master. It is one of eight recognised competitive disciplines, each testing a different facet of the human capacity to function on a single breath.

## The Two Governing Bodies

Before the disciplines themselves, it helps to know that competitive freediving is organised by two main federations, and their rules differ in small but important ways. AIDA International governs the broadest set of disciplines and is the body most amateur athletes encounter first. CMAS, the world underwater federation, runs its own competitions with parallel disciplines and slightly different equipment rules. Both ratify world records, which is why you sometimes see two record figures for what looks like the same achievement. Understanding which federation a record belongs to is part of reading the sport accurately.

## Static Apnea

Static apnea, abbreviated STA, is the pure breath-hold discipline. The athlete floats face-down in a pool, holds the breath, and the clock runs until they surface. No distance, no depth — only time. The current men's record exceeds eleven minutes and the women's record exceeds nine, figures that sound impossible until you understand the preparation: a long sequence of breathe-up, often a period of relaxation lasting many minutes, and a tolerance for the carbon dioxide buildup that drives the urge to breathe. STA is the discipline where the mental game is most exposed, because there is nothing to do but lie still and manage the rising discomfort.

## Dynamic Apnea, With and Without Fins

Dynamic disciplines measure horizontal distance travelled underwater on a single breath in a pool. Dynamic apnea with fins (DYN) allows a monofin or bifins, and elite athletes cover well over 250 metres — six lengths of an Olympic pool and more. Dynamic without fins (DNF) strips away the propulsion aids, leaving the diver to swim a modified breaststroke; distances are correspondingly shorter but the discipline is prized for its purity. Both reward efficient technique above raw lung volume, because every wasted movement burns oxygen. A skilled DYN athlete glides almost without apparent effort, the monofin undulating in long slow strokes.

## Constant Weight

Constant weight (CWT) is the flagship depth discipline and the image most people hold of freediving: a diver descending along a guide line to a tagged depth and returning, using fins or a monofin, without pulling on the line and without dropping any ballast. The constant in the name means the diver must surface with the same weight they descended with. There is a no-fins variant (CNF) that is considered the most demanding depth discipline of all, because the diver swims down and back using only their own limbs — no fins, no line-pulling. The greatest CNF practitioners reach beyond 100 metres, a depth at which the lungs are compressed to a fraction of their surface volume.

## Free Immersion

Free immersion (FIM) allows the diver to pull themselves down and back up the guide line hand over hand, without fins. It is often the first depth discipline a freediver learns properly, because it lets them descend in a slow, controlled way while equalising the ears, and it removes the complication of finning technique. Despite being a teaching discipline, FIM is also contested at the elite level, where athletes pull to depths past 120 metres. The rhythmic hand-over-hand descent has a meditative quality that many freedivers find addictive.

## Variable Weight and No-Limits

The two heaviest disciplines are no longer part of mainstream competition but remain part of the sport's lore and record books. Variable weight (VWT) uses a weighted sled to carry the diver down quickly, after which they swim or pull back to the surface under their own power. No-limits (NLT) is the extreme: the diver rides a weighted sled to depth and ascends using a lift bag or balloon inflated from a tank. No-limits produced the deepest dives in freediving history — Herbert Nitsch reached 214 metres in a record dive and later attempted 253 metres in 2012, a dive that ended in a severe decompression injury. Both disciplines carry risks that placed them outside the safety framework AIDA applies to its competitive events, which is why they are no longer actively ratified by the main federations.

## What the Disciplines Have in Common

Across all of them, the limiting factors are the same trio: oxygen economy, carbon dioxide tolerance, and equalisation. The body's response to immersion and breath-holding — the slowing heart, the shunting of blood toward the core, the contractions of the diaphragm that signal rising carbon dioxide — is shared by every discipline. What differs is the demand each one places on that physiology. Depth disciplines add the challenge of equalising the ears and sinuses against increasing pressure, and of managing the lung compression that occurs as the diver descends. Pool disciplines remove the depth variable and concentrate the challenge on efficiency and mental control. A freediver who trains seriously usually works across several disciplines, because the underlying capacities transfer.

Safety underpins the entire sport. Every competitive dive is supervised by safety divers, every training session follows the one-up-one-down rule where buddies never both submerge at once, and shallow water blackout — a loss of consciousness near the surface caused by falling oxygen — is the hazard that all the rules exist to prevent. Freediving is breathtaking to watch and deeply rewarding to practise, but it is never a solo activity.
