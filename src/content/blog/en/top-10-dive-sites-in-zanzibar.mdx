The Zanzibar archipelago lies off the Tanzanian coast in the warm western Indian Ocean, and its diving spans gentle coral gardens, dramatic walls, and some of the most pristine reefs in East Africa. The main island of Unguja — the one most people mean by Zanzibar — is fringed by reefs and anchored by the famous Mnemba Atoll off its northeastern tip, while the sister island of Pemba to the north offers steeper, wilder wall diving for the experienced. Water temperatures are tropical, ranging from around 25°C in the cooler months to 29°C in the warm season, and visibility frequently reaches 20 to 30 metres. The reefs support hard and soft corals, reef sharks, turtles, moray eels, vast schools of reef fish and seasonal pelagic visitors. Two monsoon seasons shape the diving year — the calmer kaskazi from December to March and the windier kusi from June to September — and the dive bases cluster around Nungwi, Kendwa and Matemwe in the north and Stone Town as the historic gateway.

## 1. Mnemba Atoll, Northeast Unguja

Mnemba Atoll is the crown jewel of Zanzibar diving — a small private island encircled by a horseshoe reef and a marine conservation area that has kept the coral and fish life in excellent condition. The reef slopes gently from the shallows to around 25 to 30 metres, and the dives circle the atoll depending on current and wind. Bottlenose dolphins frequent the surrounding water, green turtles graze the reef, and the fish life is dense — schooling fusiliers, snapper, barracuda and reef sharks on the deeper edges. Most sites suit Open Water divers, and the gentle conditions make Mnemba ideal for relaxed reef diving and macro photography alike.

## 2. Kichwani, Mnemba Atoll

Kichwani is one of the signature sites on the southern side of Mnemba Atoll, a gently sloping reef and wall that descends into the blue. The coral here is healthy and varied — table corals, soft corals and gorgonians — and the site is known for reliable turtle sightings and large schools of reef fish. Reef sharks patrol the deeper sections, and the visibility is typically excellent. The site works for a range of certification levels depending on depth, and the current can pick up enough to make it a pleasant drift dive when conditions align.

## 3. Wattabomi, Mnemba Atoll

Wattabomi, on the eastern side of Mnemba, is a coral garden famous for its density of marine life and its suitability for both diving and snorkelling. The reef is shallow enough that much of the action happens within recreational depths accessible to all levels, and the diversity is striking — moray eels, lionfish, scorpionfish, octopus, and clouds of anthias and damselfish over the coral. Turtles are common. It is a relaxed, fish-rich dive that showcases why the Mnemba conservation area is regarded as the best-protected reef in the Zanzibar archipelago.

## 4. Leven Bank, Open Water North of Unguja

Leven Bank is a submerged seamount in open water to the north of Unguja, and it offers the most demanding and rewarding diving in the area for experienced divers. The bank rises from deep water and is swept by strong currents that bring nutrients and attract big pelagics — large groupers, napoleon wrasse, kingfish, barracuda, rays and, with luck, larger sharks. The current and depth make this an advanced dive requiring good experience and careful planning, but the concentration of large marine life is exceptional. It is dived as a boat trip when sea conditions allow.

## 5. Hunga Reef, North Unguja

Hunga Reef, off the northern coast near Nungwi, is a wall and slope dive with healthy coral and abundant reef life. The topography offers overhangs and ledges where groupers and moray eels shelter, and the reef is patrolled by schools of snapper and fusiliers. Visibility is generally good, and the site suits divers from Open Water upward depending on the chosen depth. The northern dive bases at Nungwi and Kendwa run regular trips here, and it serves as a solid alternative when wind or current closes the Mnemba sites.

## 6. Misali Island, Pemba

Misali Island, off the western coast of Pemba, sits within a protected marine conservation area and offers some of the most pristine coral in the entire archipelago. The reefs slope and drop into clear water teeming with reef fish, turtles and healthy hard and soft coral, and the protected status has kept fishing pressure low. The diving ranges from gentle reef suitable for all levels to deeper walls, and the relative remoteness of Pemba means far fewer divers than the busy Unguja sites. Misali rewards those willing to travel north for wilder, less-crowded diving.

## 7. Njao Gap, Pemba

Njao Gap, on the western side of Pemba, is a dramatic channel dive where the reef is cut by a gap that funnels current and concentrates marine life. The walls drop steeply, draped in soft corals and gorgonians, and the current brings in pelagics and large schools of fish. This is wall and drift diving of a quality that makes Pemba a destination for serious divers, with steep dramatic topography quite different from the gentle slopes of Mnemba. The site suits Advanced divers comfortable with current and deeper profiles.

## 8. Manta Point, Pemba

Manta Point, off Pemba, is named for the manta rays that visit the area, drawn by the nutrient-rich water flowing through the deep channels of the Pemba Channel. The diving here combines dramatic wall topography with the chance of large pelagic encounters, and the steep reefs are richly covered in coral. Manta sightings are seasonal and never guaranteed, but the site is excellent regardless, with reef sharks, large groupers and dense fish life. The depth and current make it an advanced dive, and it underlines Pemba's reputation as the archipelago's wilder, more challenging diving island.

## 9. Boribu Reef, Northeast Unguja

Boribu Reef, in the waters off northeastern Unguja near Matemwe, is a slope and coral garden dive accessible from the eastern dive bases. The reef supports a healthy population of reef fish, moray eels, octopus and the occasional reef shark, with coral in good condition. Conditions are generally gentle, making it suitable for a range of certification levels and a good choice for relaxed reef diving and training. The Matemwe bases, close to Mnemba, often combine Boribu with the atoll sites for a varied day of diving.

## 10. Tumbatu Island, Northwest Unguja

Tumbatu Island, off the northwestern coast of Unguja, offers reef and wall diving with healthy coral and abundant marine life in an area less frequented than Mnemba. The reefs here support good fish life, turtles and the occasional larger visitor, and the diving ranges from gentle slopes to deeper sections. The site provides a quieter alternative to the busy northeastern sites, and the dive bases at Nungwi and Kendwa run trips here depending on conditions. It rounds out a Zanzibar diving itinerary with a less-crowded, scenic reef experience.

## Planning Your Trip

Zanzibar's prime dive season runs from roughly June to October and again from December to March, avoiding the long rains of April and May when many operators close. The kaskazi monsoon from December to March brings the calmest seas and best visibility; the kusi from June to September is windier but still very diveable. Water is warm year-round, so a 3mm wetsuit suffices for most divers. The northern bases at Nungwi, Kendwa and Matemwe serve Mnemba and the Unguja sites, while Pemba requires a separate trip and rewards experienced divers with wilder walls. The Mnemba and Misali conservation areas charge marine park fees. Open Water certification covers most Unguja sites; Pemba's walls and Leven Bank call for Advanced experience.
