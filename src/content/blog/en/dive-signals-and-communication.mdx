## Why Underwater Communication Matters

Scuba diving removes two of the four senses that humans routinely use to communicate: speech is nearly impossible (the regulator is in your mouth, and any attempt to speak produces only bubbles) and body odour is irrelevant underwater. What remains is touch, vision, and to a limited extent hearing. The dive hand signal system is an efficient visual language designed to convey the most critical information — OK, problem, ascend, descend, out of air, share air — quickly, with one hand, at any orientation.

The practical result is that communication quality directly affects dive safety. Two divers who enter the water without a shared vocabulary of signals, or who have never practised the signals under actual dive conditions, are less able to assist each other in an emergency than two divers who have reviewed and used these conventions consistently. Reviewing signals at the dive briefing is not a formality — it is a genuine communication calibration.

## The Core Hand Signals

OK signals are the most-used exchanges underwater. At distance, the standard signal is a circle made with the thumb and index finger, remaining fingers extended. At the surface from a distance, the hand-on-head signal — both hands clasped together above the head to form a visible arch — indicates OK to a boat or shore observer. Do not confuse these two: a round 'O' made at the surface from a distance can be misread as the waving distress signal.

The 'not OK' signal is a flat hand, palm down, rocking from side to side — the 'so-so' motion. This prompts a more specific question from the buddy. Pointing to the ear or sinuses while making the 'not OK' signal indicates equalisation pain. Pointing to the head can indicate nitrogen narcosis. Pointing to the stomach indicates nausea. Pointing to the regulator or making the hand-across-throat 'cutoff' signal means out of air.

The out-of-air signal — hand flat, moving back and forth across the throat in the classic 'cutoff' gesture — is the most important emergency signal. It should be followed immediately by the share-air signal: open hand held toward the buddy, requesting their alternate air source. The two signals are often combined into a single fluid action. Practising this exchange until it is automatic is worth doing in a pool before a multi-dive trip.

Ascend and descend signals are typically a thumbs-up for ascend (not a positive 'good job' signal — thumbs-up always means up, go to the surface) and a thumbs-down for descend. The positive acknowledgement signal — the OK circle — avoids the ambiguity that thumbs-up creates in land-based culture, and this is why OK is the circular gesture rather than a thumbs-up. Divers who confuse these in an emergency are dangerous.

## Directional and Marine Life Signals

Beyond the safety-critical exchanges, a working signal vocabulary enriches the dive by allowing guide and buddy communication about what is being seen. The common directional signal — pointing with the index finger — is simple, but its interpretation depends on context. A guide pointing down toward the substrate is directing attention downward; a guide pointing ahead means 'follow me in that direction'. When precision matters, divers establish directional intent with multiple signals in sequence.

Marine life signals have no single universal standard, but conventions exist within dive communities and are typically briefed before dives at sites known for specific species. A flattened, undulating hand indicates a ray or shark. Two fingers pointing at the eyes and then forward indicates 'look over there'. A fist rotating forward mimes a turtle swimming. Pinching the thumb and finger together while holding other fingers out mimes a shrimp or nudibranch — something small. A guide who has spotted a pygmy seahorse at a cleaning station will typically combine the small-object signal with a specific point to direct the group's attention.

## Torch Signals for Night and Low-Visibility Diving

Night diving and low-visibility conditions require an adapted signal system because hand signals become difficult to see at distance and impossible in darkness. The primary tool is the dive torch (flashlight), and a specific torch signal vocabulary supplements hand signals when conditions demand it.

The OK torch signal is a circle of light drawn in the water column — moving the torch in a circular motion. This is visible at considerable distance in dark water. An emergency or attention signal is a rapid side-to-side sweep — like a lighthouse beam — which is immediately attention-grabbing. Pointing the torch beam at an object and then making the circle signal directs attention to that object.

For close-range communication at night, illuminate your own hand signals rather than shining the torch directly in a buddy's face (which destroys their night vision). The convention is to shine the light downward or to the side to create ambient illumination for your gestures, not to beam it directly at the person you are signalling.

## Surface Signals

On the surface, communication shifts to visible and audible signals designed for range. The OK signal at the surface — hand or hands on top of the head in an arch — is readable from a considerable distance and from a dive boat. The distress signal is a single arm waved back and forth above the head, which reads as distinctly different from the OK arch. If only one arm is functional (due to injury), a vigorous wave with a single arm is the distress signal.

The whistle clipped to the BCD should be accessible without looking — find it by touch and blow without delay when a boat is approaching that may not have seen you. Three short blasts followed by three long blasts followed by three short blasts is the international Morse code distress signal (SOS), recognisable to any properly trained boat crew.

The surface marker buoy (SMB) is both a safety device and a communication device. Deploying an SMB signals 'diver below, ascending here' to the boat. A red SMB is the standard. Some dive operations use an orange SMB to signal 'diver needs assistance, send the zodiac'. Confirming the convention used by your specific operation is part of the pre-dive briefing.

## Learning and Practising Signals

Reading a list of signals is a poor substitute for practising them. In the course of an Open Water certification, signal practice is typically done in confined water conditions — a pool or shallow bay — where there is time and visibility for deliberate repetition. The risk is that signals learned in a pool but rarely used in open water become uncertain under stress.

A useful habit is a five-minute signal review with a new dive buddy before every dive. Even if both divers are certified and experienced, confirming that they share the same conventions — particularly for emergency signals and for the specific marine life or conditions likely to be encountered on that dive — removes ambiguity and builds the shared communication baseline that makes buddy diving genuinely safer than diving alone.
