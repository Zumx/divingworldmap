For most divers, a reef begins as a blur of colour and movement — fish, light, and a confusion of shapes on the bottom that all read simply as coral. But spend a few dives learning to separate the staghorns from the brain corals and the soft leather corals from the sea fans, and the reef resolves into something far richer: a community of distinct organisms, each with its own way of building, feeding and competing for space. Identifying corals is the slow pleasure that keeps experienced divers looking down as keenly as they once looked up for sharks.

## Hard Versus Soft Coral

The first division to grasp is between hard and soft corals. Hard corals, the reef builders, secrete a rigid skeleton of calcium carbonate and form the permanent architecture of the reef. Their polyps sit in tiny stony cups called corallites, and the pattern of those cups is the single most useful clue to identification. Soft corals lack a massive skeleton; they are supported by tiny embedded spicules and water pressure, and they sway with the current. They build nothing permanent but add colour and movement, and they often dominate where currents are strong.

Telling them apart underwater is usually easy: if it is rigid and you could knock on it, it is a hard coral; if it bends and sways, it is soft. The exception worth knowing is fire coral, which looks superficially like a hard coral but is a hydrozoan, not a true coral, and delivers a painful sting. It is typically mustard-yellow with white tips and best admired without contact.

## The Branching Corals: Acropora and Friends

The genus Acropora is the most species-rich and recognisable of all reef corals. These are the staghorns and tables — fast-growing branching colonies that form the framework of healthy Indo-Pacific reefs and the back reef of the Caribbean. Staghorn Acropora throws out long cylindrical branches like antlers; table Acropora spreads into broad horizontal plates that maximise light capture. Look for the distinctive axial corallite at the tip of each branch, a feature unique to the genus.

Acropora grows fast — some species extend more than ten centimetres a year — which makes it the engine of reef recovery but also the most vulnerable to bleaching and storm damage. Pocillopora, the cauliflower or lace coral, is another common branching form, knobbly and compact, often pink or brown, and a favourite refuge for small crabs and damselfish.

## The Massive and Mounding Corals

Where branching corals are sprinters, the massive corals are the marathon runners of the reef. Porites forms enormous boulders and mounds, some in the Pacific over eight metres across and many centuries old; the growth bands in a Porites skeleton record climate history like tree rings. Brain corals — Diploria, Platygyra and relatives — are unmistakable, their surfaces folded into the meandering ridges and valleys that give them their name, each valley a row of polyps sharing connected mouths.

Other mounding forms include the honeycomb-patterned Favia and Favites, where each polyp sits in its own neat cup, and the star corals Montastraea that build much of the Caribbean's framework. These slow, solid corals are the reef's ballast — less spectacular than the staghorns but far more resistant to bleaching, and the colonies most likely to survive a bad year and reseed the reef.

## Plate, Foliose and Encrusting Forms

Not all hard corals branch or mound. Plate and foliose corals such as Turbinaria and Echinopora spread in thin sheets, whorls and ruffled layers, angling themselves to catch light on deeper or shaded slopes. Encrusting corals coat the rock in a thin living veneer, often the first to colonise bare substrate. Among the most beautiful are the mushroom corals, Fungia, which are unusual in being free-living single polyps that lie unattached on the sand and can right themselves if flipped — one of the few corals that move.

Learning to notice these growth forms matters because the same species can look different at different depths and light levels, while the corallite structure stays constant. When in doubt, look closely at the cups.

## The Soft Corals and Gorgonians

Soft corals bring the reef to life with movement. The leather corals, Sarcophyton and Lobophytum, form thick, mushroom-shaped or lobed mats with a tough surface, retracting their polyps into a smooth leather skin when disturbed. Dendronephthya, the carnation or tree coral, is the showstopper — translucent, brightly coloured trees of pink, red and orange that inflate with water and bloom in current-swept channels, especially in the Red Sea and Indonesia.

The gorgonians, or sea fans and sea whips, are a separate group, supported by a flexible protein skeleton. They orient their flat fans across the prevailing current to filter plankton, and on Caribbean walls and Indo-Pacific drop-offs they grow several metres tall. The tiny pygmy seahorse lives exclusively on certain gorgonians, matching their colour and polyp texture so precisely it was discovered only by accident.

## Watching Coral Behaviour

Corals are animals, and patient divers can watch them behave. Many extend their feeding tentacles only at night, transforming a drab daytime boulder into a fuzzy, flower-covered surface after dark; a night dive over the same reef you saw by day is often unrecognisable. During mass spawning events, triggered on particular nights after the full moon in late spring or early summer on the Great Barrier Reef and across the Caribbean, entire reefs release clouds of eggs and sperm in a synchronised slow-motion blizzard.

Corals also compete. Watch the boundary where two colonies meet and you may see one deploying sweeper tentacles or digestive filaments to attack and overgrow its neighbour. The reef is not a static garden but a slow, ceaseless contest for light and space, played out over years.
