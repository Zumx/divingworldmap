Venezuela holds one of the most significant marine territories in the Caribbean, including Los Roques — a federal dependencies archipelago of 350 islands and atolls that ranks among the least disturbed reef systems in the entire region. The country's offshore diving has suffered from political instability and reduced tourism over the past decade, but the reefs themselves have benefited from decreased fishing pressure and visitor numbers, and the dive sites that remain accessible are in exceptional condition. Los Roques receives a small number of dedicated dive visitors, and the combination of intact reefs, clear Caribbean water, and the absence of mass tourism gives it a quality that over-developed Caribbean destinations have long since lost. Water temperatures across Venezuela's Caribbean coast and offshore islands range from 24°C in winter to 29°C in summer. [Open the map](/map) to explore Venezuela's dive sites.

## 1. Los Roques Archipelago, Federal Dependencies

Los Roques is the centrepiece of Venezuelan diving — an atoll system sitting 160 kilometres north of Caracas in the southern Caribbean, designated as a national park in 1972 and covering over 200,000 hectares of ocean, reef, and lagoon. The outer barrier reef on the windward eastern edge drops from a coral crest at 3 metres to a wall extending to beyond 60 metres, with coral coverage that reflects decades of protected status. Inside the lagoon, turtles are common and spotted eagle rays cruise the sandy flats in pairs. The fish populations on the outer reef are notably larger and more abundant than on comparable Caribbean reefs with heavier fishing pressure. Small seaplanes and charter flights connect Los Roques to Caracas; the archipelago has no road access and limited accommodation, all of which contributes to the uncrowded quality of the diving. Advanced Open Water for the outer wall; Open Water throughout the interior.

## 2. Gran Roque, Los Roques

Gran Roque is the only inhabited island in the Los Roques archipelago and the operational base for diving the park. The fishing village has been supplemented by a collection of posadas — guesthouses — that cater primarily to divers and kite surfers, and several dive operations run daily two-tank boat trips across the park. The reef immediately surrounding Gran Roque at 8–20 metres has the ease of a local site — frequently dived, well-known to guides — combined with the fish populations that protected status generates. Nassau grouper, yellowfin parrotfish, and large schools of Creole wrasse are constant. The night diving in the channels around Gran Roque is productive, with octopus, free-swimming moray eels, and sleeping parrotfish wrapped in mucus cocoons. Open Water.

## 3. Bequevé Pass, Los Roques

Bequevé Pass is a tidal channel cutting through the outer barrier reef that creates a drift dive of exceptional quality on the incoming tide. The current through the pass reaches several knots at peak flow, driving a spectacular aggregation of jacks, barracuda, and grey reef sharks that orient to the current and feed on the passing baitfish. Eagle rays ride the incoming current into the lagoon, and the concentration of predators during peak flow is the kind of scene rarely witnessed outside remote blue-water destinations. The dive requires timing precision and a reliable boat crew; entering at the wrong tidal stage produces either no current or currents too strong to manage safely. Advanced certification; drift diving experience essential.

## 4. Cayo de Agua, Los Roques

Cayo de Agua is a remote cay in the southwest of the Los Roques atoll, reachable by a longer boat ride from Gran Roque but offering some of the most undisturbed diving in the park. The outer reef on the western face slopes from 5 metres to a rubble zone at 30 metres, with coral heads of exceptional size — massive star corals of the kind that require centuries of growth — interspersed with healthy branching Acroporas. Nurse sharks rest in groups under overhangs, and the turtle population here is high enough that green turtles are encountered almost every dive. The isolation means weather has a greater impact; the crossing back to Gran Roque in a northerly swell can be rough. Open Water.

## 5. Isla Margarita, Nueva Esparta

Isla Margarita is Venezuela's main tourist island and the entry point for divers exploring the eastern Venezuelan coast. The diving around Margarita itself is largely centred on the Macanao Peninsula on the western end, where rocky reefs at 10–25 metres shelter concentrations of fish and macro life rarely found on the more visited eastern beaches. Gorgonian sea fans are common below 18 metres on the exposed faces, and the presence of Atlantic spadefish in large schools is notable. Visibility averages 10–15 metres — adequate but not exceptional — and the proximity to the mainland introduces some sediment load. Margarita is the most accessible Caribbean diving in Venezuela for international visitors arriving by commercial flight; charter connections to Los Roques operate from the island's airport. Open Water.

## 6. Mochima National Park, Anzoátegui

Mochima is a coastal national park east of Barcelona on Venezuela's mainland coast, featuring a complex of islands, mangrove channels, and submerged rocky reefs at 5–20 metres. The diving here is the most accessible in mainland Venezuela and caters to a predominantly domestic market. The rocky reefs shelter interesting fish communities including large moray eels, porcupinefish, and the schooling fish that characterise Caribbean rocky reef environments. Visibility runs 8–15 metres depending on season; the best conditions occur from February through May when rainfall and runoff are minimal. Mochima's importance is as much geological and ecological as purely diving-focused — the park encompasses some of Venezuela's most dramatic coastal scenery. Open Water.

## 7. Morrocoy National Park, Falcón

Morrocoy is a coastal lagoon system in western Venezuela, composed of shallow cays surrounded by coral reefs at 6–18 metres. The park was declared a national park in 1974 and the reefs reflect the mixed quality of a long-protected but heavily visited site: fish populations are good, but some coral areas show pressure from anchoring and snorkelling activity. The better reefs are on the outer cays — Cayo Sombrero and Cayo Peraza — where access is more restricted and conditions more pristine. Eagle rays and nurse sharks are common on the sandy flats between cays, and the seagrass beds around the sheltered interior support green turtles. Morrocoy is primarily a domestic destination and has good infrastructure for day-trip access by small boat from the town of Tucacas. Open Water.

## 8. Los Monjes Archipelago, Federal Dependencies

Los Monjes is a remote federal dependency north of the Guajira Peninsula, rarely visited by dive tourists and virtually unknown outside Venezuelan scientific and fishing communities. The diving around these rocky outcrops — which jut from the southern Caribbean in three groups — has been described by the few divers who have made the trip as remarkably pristine, with large fish populations and healthy deep-reef structure. The logistical challenge is significant: access requires a charter boat or liveaboard passage of several hours from Punto Fijo or Maracaibo. For those with the resources to reach it, the remoteness translates directly into the kind of diving that no amount of marine park designation can produce once regular access begins. Advanced certification; liveaboard logistics required.

## 9. Chichiriviche de la Costa, Vargas

Chichiriviche de la Costa is a small fishing village on Venezuela's central Caribbean coast west of Caracas, accessible by road and with a cluster of rocky reef sites at 10–20 metres that have been developed as a dive destination by small local operators. The reefs are not as spectacular as Los Roques, but the fish life is genuine and the marine park designation in the area has helped preserve coral coverage that might otherwise have been damaged. The water clarity here is decent — 12–18 metres visibility — and the sites are accessible for day trips from Caracas. Resident barracuda, pairs of parrotfish, and hawksbill turtles are the main attractions. Open Water; primarily a domestic dive destination.

## 10. Los Frailes, Nueva Esparta

Los Frailes are a group of small, rocky uninhabited islands between Isla Margarita and the mainland, rising steeply from the seabed and providing vertical wall diving at 15–35 metres on their exposed northern faces. The current through the Los Frailes passage concentrates marine life; this is one of the more reliable sites in eastern Venezuela for large pelagic sightings — amberjack, wahoo, and the occasional whale shark reported during late summer. The water around the islands is among the clearest in the Margarita region, and the deep wall sections have black coral trees and large sponges characteristic of current-fed Caribbean walls. Advanced Open Water for the deep wall; intermediate divers can explore the shallower eastern faces in calmer conditions.

## Planning Your Venezuela Dive Trip

Los Roques is the target for international dive visitors, and the practicalities require more planning than most Caribbean destinations. Small charter flights from Caracas and Margarita operate several times daily; accommodation in the posadas on Gran Roque must be booked well in advance during peak season (December–April, and July–August). Dive operators on Gran Roque are professional and equipped; most offer two-tank morning boat trips across the park. Travel insurance with evacuation cover is strongly advisable. For mainland diving, Mochima and Morrocoy are accessible on self-organised itineraries from Caracas or other Venezuelan cities. The current security situation in Venezuela requires current-information research before travel; conditions change and regional safety varies significantly.
