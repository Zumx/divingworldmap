## The Emergency That Should Never Happen

Running completely out of breathing gas underwater is preventable with attentive gas management, but the diving world is not a place of perfection. Equipment can malfunction. Gauges fail. A diver can become separated from their buddy, cover more distance than planned, or encounter a current that doubles their consumption in minutes. Out-of-air emergencies happen to experienced divers as well as beginners, and the difference between a survivable incident and a fatality is almost always whether the affected diver and their buddy have practised the response until it is automatic.

The procedures themselves are not complicated. What makes them difficult is the psychological environment in which they must be executed: at depth, under time pressure, with a cognitive system degraded by stress and, in some cases, with a diver whose instinct is to bolt to the surface. Every element of training for out-of-air emergencies is designed to insert a trained response between panic and action.

## Signalling an Out-of-Air Emergency

The international hand signal for out-of-air is the flat hand drawn horizontally across the throat in a slashing motion, sometimes called the 'cut throat' signal. Every certified diver knows it. The problem is that a diver who has just discovered they cannot breathe may not immediately think to use it — their first impulse is to inhale harder, then to act. The training habit of checking your gauge every few minutes and signalling 'low on air' (tapping the gauge or pointing to it with a thumb-and-forefinger 'O') before the situation becomes critical is the single most important preventive step.

But when the emergency has arrived — when the regulator gives its last breath or produces nothing at all — the sequence is: signal your buddy immediately with the out-of-air sign, approach them, and establish the air-sharing arrangement. All of this must happen quickly. A diver in good physical condition has roughly a minute to work with before the absence of gas becomes physiologically critical. In practice, the time available is shorter because stress has usually already driven up oxygen consumption before the tank empties.

## Octopus Donation

The standard taught procedure in all major recreational training systems involves the donor diver offering their alternate second stage — commonly called an octopus — to the out-of-air diver. The octopus is the extra regulator that hangs from the donor's BCD, typically secured in the triangle formed by the chin and the two sides of the chest. It should always be yellow or brightly coloured and always clipped or stowed where it can be found instantly by the donor and, equally importantly, by someone else reaching for it.

The out-of-air diver takes the offered octopus, inserts it, clears it if necessary, and breathes. The pair then face each other, establish eye contact, and confirm both divers are stable and breathing. The donor controls the ascent by holding the out-of-air diver's BCD or arm, maintaining physical contact throughout. The ascent rate should not exceed 9 metres per minute — the same standard rate as any ascent — and a 3-minute safety stop at 5 metres should be performed if depth and gas permit.

The physical positioning during the ascent matters. The two divers should face each other, because this allows the donor to watch the recipient's face and to manage the ascent rate together. The hose of the donated regulator must remain free — not wrapped, not pulled taut — so neither diver accidentally dislodges the second stage during ascent. Practising this in a pool or calm shallow water until the positioning feels natural is essential, because performing it for the first time at 20 metres under stress is considerably harder.

## Primary Donation

Some training agencies — particularly those with technical diving lineages, such as PADI's TecRec programmes and DSAT, as well as GUE — teach that the donor offers their primary second stage rather than the alternate. The logic is that the primary is the regulator the donor knows performs reliably; it has been in the diver's mouth for the entire dive. The donor switches to the alternate (which they have tested at the beginning of the dive) and the out-of-air diver receives the best-performing regulator.

This approach requires the donor to be comfortable and practised with their alternate second stage and requires that the alternate is positioned accessibly. It is standard practice in technical diving and makes good sense for recreational divers who want a more robust procedure, but it requires more rehearsal to execute smoothly under stress.

## Controlled Emergency Swimming Ascent

If there is no buddy available — if the out-of-air diver is alone, or if separation from the buddy has occurred before the emergency — the alternative is a Controlled Emergency Swimming Ascent (CESA). The diver ascends under their own power at a controlled rate, continuously exhaling throughout the ascent to allow expanding gas to escape the lungs, preventing pulmonary overexpansion injury. The exhalation should be a continuous slow release, not a violent expulsion, and the ascent rate should be as controlled as possible while ascending as directly as possible to the surface.

CESA is a last resort. It carries meaningful risks: the possibility of arterial gas embolism if the ascent is too fast or if exhalation stops, decompression illness if depth and bottom time required a planned stop, and the hazard of surfacing in an exposed location far from the boat. The risk of decompression illness from omitting a safety stop is generally much lower than the risk of drowning from running out of gas at depth, so the CESA is the correct choice when no alternative exists.

Training for CESA involves practising an ascent from 9 metres while exhaling continuously — a short, manageable exercise that builds the muscle memory of continuous exhalation while ascending. The psychological barrier of swimming up while exhaling without receiving a new breath is real and worth confronting in training before encountering it in an emergency.

## Drills and Repetition

The evidence from diving incident analysis consistently shows that divers who have practised emergency procedures recently and repeatedly perform them more effectively under stress than those who last rehearsed them during their open-water certification years ago. The procedures degrade in memory and in physical comfort over time.

A useful practice is to run a buddy air-sharing ascent at the end of every few dives in conditions where it is safe to do so — at the end of a dive at 10 metres, running the ascent to 5 metres sharing air. It takes five minutes, reinforces positioning and communication, and ensures both divers know where each other's alternate second stage is kept. Different BCD manufacturers position octopus retainers differently. Confirming your buddy's equipment configuration at the surface before the dive — including finding and testing their alternate — is a two-minute investment that matters.

## Equipment Considerations

The alternate second stage must be maintained to the same standard as the primary. It is often neglected because it is rarely used — which is precisely why it needs attention. Service intervals should apply to the entire regulator system, including the octopus. A first stage that is serviced but whose alternate second stage has been untouched for four years is not a fully reliable system.

For technical divers, the solution is a fully redundant gas supply with an independent regulator on a separate valve — either a pony cylinder or a fully independent set of doubles. In overhead environment diving (cave, wreck penetration) and at decompression depths, this redundancy is not optional. The rule of thirds for gas planning in those environments exists precisely because the planned emergency response is to ascend on the buddy's gas, and that requires both divers to have sufficient gas remaining for a shared ascent from the furthest point of penetration.

For recreational divers the relevant lesson is simpler: check your alternate regularly, confirm your buddy's alternate at the start of every dive, and practise the drill. The mechanics of air sharing are not difficult. Performing them automatically, without hesitation, in the middle of a stressful situation is a skill, and like all skills it requires practice.
