## The Honest Case for Solo Diving

The buddy system is the most successful risk-reduction principle in recreational diving. A significant proportion of diver fatalities involve a single diver separated from their buddy, with no one present to assist during the critical period of a developing emergency. Nothing in this article challenges that record. And yet solo diving is a legitimate, practised activity with its own training pathway, its own equipment requirements, and a community of divers who pursue it thoughtfully.

The argument for solo diving begins not with adrenalin or independence for its own sake, but with an honest assessment of how recreational buddy diving actually operates. On a typical resort dive, two divers of different experience levels and air consumption rates are paired by a dive centre and told to stay together. In practice, one runs low on air significantly earlier than the other. One is faster. One wants to look at nudibranchs for twenty minutes while the other is bored. The result is often two divers who are nominally buddied but functionally each making their own decisions, in sight of each other but not genuinely monitoring or prepared to assist. This is sometimes described, accurately, as 'two solo divers pretending to be a team.'

The case for formal solo diver training is that it produces a diver who has explicitly acknowledged the absence of a buddy as a real possibility, equipped themselves accordingly, and practised the emergency procedures that the buddy system normally distributes between two people.

## What Self-Reliant Diving Actually Requires

The PADI Self-Reliant Diver course and the SDI Solo Diver certification are the main recreational training pathways. They focus on three components: equipment redundancy, gas planning, and skills.

Equipment redundancy for solo diving means carrying every piece of life-support equipment twice. The primary regulator has a backup — not an octopus, which relies on a second person, but a fully independent spare regulator on a separate valve or a pony cylinder. A separate gas supply is the minimum standard: this is typically a pony bottle — a small cylinder (2 to 4 litres) carried alongside the main cylinder with its own independent first stage and second stage. The pony provides enough gas for an emergency ascent plus a safety stop from recreational depths, with margin. Some solo divers use sidemount configuration with two equal cylinders as their redundancy solution.

Navigation equipment is equally important, because a solo diver who becomes lost or disoriented has no buddy to help them work out position. A compass, the ability to use it accurately, and surface marker buoy (DSMB) deployment are all required skills. A lift bag or DSMB that can be deployed from depth allows the diver to signal their position on ascent, and a safety sausage at the surface ensures visibility even in mild chop.

## Gas Management for Solo Diving

Gas planning for solo diving uses a more conservative formula than the recreational rule-of-thumb for buddy pairs, because there is no one to share gas with in an emergency. The standard solo gas plan uses a rule of thirds or even a 'turn at half' approach: use half the starting gas on the outward portion of the dive, reserve the entire second half for the return and any emergency. For a 12-litre cylinder starting at 200 bar, that means turning around at 100 bar and surfacing with at least 50 bar in the main cylinder plus whatever the pony bottle holds.

In practice, many experienced solo divers are very conservative with gas precisely because the pony bottle is their emergency gas, not their reserve. The main cylinder reserve is maintained above 50 bar; the pony is untouched unless a regulator failure or other emergency forces its use. Arriving at the surface with 80 bar in the main cylinder and a full pony is a successful gas plan, even though it means leaving gas in the water.

## Psychological Preparation

The mental component of solo diving is discussed less than the equipment, but it is at least as important. When diving with a buddy, uncertainty and mild anxiety about a situation — an unfamiliar site, increasing current, unexpected depth — are partially managed by the social dynamics of the pair. You look at your buddy, they look unconcerned, you calibrate your response accordingly. Alone, there is no external reference. Your own assessment of the situation is the only assessment available.

This requires a level of self-knowledge and self-discipline that not all divers have developed. The ability to make a calm, independent decision to abort a dive — to turn around because the current has increased, to ascend because the visibility has dropped, to call the dive because something doesn't feel right — without any social pressure to push on, is a skill. Some divers find it easier to make conservative decisions alone because there is no social dynamic to navigate. Others find that the absence of a buddy's moderating influence allows bad decisions to develop unchecked.

Self-reliant diving training addresses this by emphasising problem-solving practice, accident scenario review, and the development of explicit personal limits. A solo diver who has decided in advance that they will abort any dive where current visibility drops below 5 metres has removed that decision from the moment of stress. Pre-committing to limits is a risk management tool.

## Who Should and Should Not Solo Dive

Experience level is the primary criterion. Training agencies typically require a minimum of 100 logged dives before starting solo diver training, and instructors in practice often require significantly more. The reasoning is not arbitrary: solo diving requires a diver who can manage their own buoyancy, navigation, gas consumption, and equipment entirely without cognitive load from those fundamentals. A diver who is still actively managing buoyancy or who struggles with compass navigation has insufficient attention available to manage the solo context.

Physical fitness is a secondary consideration. A solo diver who has a medical episode at depth — a cardiac event, severe ear barotrauma, extreme vertigo — has no immediate help available. A pre-dive medical screening and reasonable cardiovascular fitness are not guarantees but they reduce the probability of the scenarios most likely to produce a fatality in a solo context.

Certain environments are simply not appropriate for solo diving regardless of experience level. Cave diving, closed-circuit rebreather diving, and night dives in conditions of strong current or poor visibility carry risk profiles that exceed what individual redundancy can manage. The philosophy of solo diving is not that a diver can handle any situation alone — it is that a trained, equipped, experienced diver can handle the specific situations that arise on a planned open-water recreational dive where the primary risk is a buddy not being present to help.

## Integration with Conventional Diving

Most divers who hold solo diver certifications do not dive solo exclusively. The training and equipment requirements make them more capable diving partners when they do dive with a buddy, because they carry their own independent gas supply, are more competent at navigation and gas management, and have explicitly thought through emergency procedures. A dive team where both divers have pony bottles, are competent navigators, and have practised emergency procedures recently is considerably safer than one where neither member has thought much beyond their basic certification.

The solo certification is best understood as an advanced self-sufficiency qualification that benefits all diving contexts, not exclusively solo ones. The mindset it instils — independent decision-making, explicit planning, personal redundancy — is applicable in any diving environment.
