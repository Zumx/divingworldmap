Every breath you take underwater is delivered at the pressure of the surrounding water, which means at thirty metres you are breathing gas more than four times denser than at the surface. Your body absorbs the extra nitrogen in that gas and dissolves it into blood and tissue. Nothing about that is harmful while you stay deep. The danger arrives on the way up, when the pressure drops and the dissolved gas tries to come back out of solution. Do it slowly and the nitrogen leaves harmlessly through your lungs. Do it too fast and it forms bubbles in your tissues and blood, and that is decompression sickness. The decompression stop is the simple, powerful tool that prevents it.

## Pressure, gas, and the shaken bottle

The classic analogy is a bottle of sparkling water. Under the cap the liquid is pressurised and the gas stays dissolved and invisible. Crack the cap slowly and the gas eases out in a gentle fizz. Tear it open and it erupts. Your tissues behave the same way. At depth, nitrogen is held in solution by pressure. As you ascend, that holding pressure falls, and how violently the gas comes out depends entirely on how fast you reduce it. A controlled ascent with stops is the slow twist of the cap.

This is why a recreational dive that never goes into formal decompression still demands a controlled rate of ascent, typically no faster than around nine to ten metres per minute on modern training, far slower than the old rates. Speed is the enemy. The deeper and longer you have been, the more nitrogen you have loaded, and the more your ascent must be paced.

## Fast tissues and slow tissues

Your body is not one uniform sponge. Decompression theory models it as a set of compartments, each absorbing and releasing gas at its own rate. Blood-rich tissues such as the brain and spinal cord saturate quickly and also clear quickly. Fatty, poorly perfused tissues such as joints and slow muscle take far longer to load up and far longer to off-gas. A short deep dive may load the fast tissues; a long dive loads the slow ones too.

The practical consequence is that the same depth can call for very different ascent strategies depending on how long you stayed. The decompression stop gives the slow compartments the time they need at a reduced pressure where they can release gas without exceeding the threshold at which bubbles form. The whole edifice of dive planning, from tables to computer algorithms, is an attempt to track these compartments and keep every one of them within safe limits throughout the ascent.

## The safety stop versus the deco stop

These two are often confused. A safety stop is a precaution. On a normal recreational dive that stays within no-decompression limits, you are technically free to ascend straight to the surface, but every agency now teaches a three-minute pause at around five metres. It is not mandatory in the mathematical sense; it is cheap insurance that lets the fast tissues shed a little extra gas and slows your final ascent through the zone where bubbles are most likely to grow.

A decompression stop is mandatory. Once a dive exceeds the no-decompression limit for its depth, your computer or table commits you to one or more stops at prescribed depths before you may surface, and skipping them genuinely risks injury. Recreational training is built around staying out of mandatory decompression entirely, while technical divers plan deliberate, sometimes lengthy decompression as the core of the dive.

## Why deeper stops entered the picture

For decades, decompression schedules concentrated their stops in shallow water, on the logic that the biggest pressure changes happen near the surface. More recent thinking, drawn from bubble-model research, suggested that pausing briefly at greater depth, a so-called deep stop, might control bubble formation before it begins rather than after. The idea was attractive, and many divers adopted it, but later controlled studies produced mixed and even unfavourable results, and the consensus has swung back toward emphasising well-managed shallow stops. The honest summary is that decompression science is still evolving, and reasonable experts disagree at the margins.

## How your computer thinks

A modern dive computer runs a decompression algorithm, commonly a variant of the Bühlmann model with gradient factors, continuously through the dive. It samples depth every few seconds, updates the calculated gas loading in each tissue compartment, and tells you in real time how long you can remain at your current depth before incurring a stop, or how long any stop must last. Gradient factors let a diver choose how conservative the algorithm behaves, trading a little extra stop time for a wider safety margin.

Because the computer adapts to your actual profile rather than the worst-case rectangular profile a table assumes, it usually grants more bottom time for the same risk, and it recalculates instantly if you change depth. That responsiveness is precisely why computers displaced tables for everyday diving, though understanding the theory behind the numbers remains essential.

## The factors that load the dice

Decompression risk is not governed by depth and time alone. Cold slows circulation and impairs off-gassing, so a cold diver clears nitrogen less efficiently. Dehydration thickens blood and is widely regarded as a contributing factor. Exertion at depth raises gas uptake, while heavy exertion during ascent can encourage bubble formation. Age, body fat, fatigue, and a recent flight all nudge the odds. Repetitive diving over several days steadily raises residual nitrogen, which is why multi-day liveaboard trips build in conservative profiles and surface intervals.

None of these factors is captured precisely by any algorithm, which is why margins exist. Conservative ascents, honest surface intervals, good hydration, and staying warm are not fussiness; they are how a careful diver compensates for everything the model cannot see.

## Building the habit

The decompression stop rewards patience above all. A diver who treats the final ascent as the most important part of the dive, who hovers calmly at the safety stop watching the reef rather than racing the clock, is building exactly the discipline that keeps the sport remarkably safe. The physics is unforgiving but entirely predictable, and respecting it costs only a few quiet minutes hanging in the blue.
