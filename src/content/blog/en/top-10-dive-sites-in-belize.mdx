Belize sits at the southern end of the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, the second-longest barrier reef system in the world, running nearly 300 kilometres along the Caribbean coast. Three offshore atolls — Lighthouse Reef, Turneffe Atoll and Glover's Reef — extend the diving another 80 kilometres eastward into open ocean, and between the barrier reef and the mainland lies a shallow lagoon dotted with cayes, mangroves and patch reefs. The result is an extraordinary range of diving environments compressed into a small, accessible country. Water temperatures throughout the year average 26–28°C, making a 3mm wetsuit comfortable at recreational depths. Visibility in the atoll sites routinely reaches 30 metres or more. Belize City and the cayes of Ambergris Caye and Caye Caulker serve as the main bases, with Placencia offering access to the southern sites.

## 1. Great Blue Hole, Lighthouse Reef

The Great Blue Hole is a collapsed karst sinkhole roughly 300 metres in diameter and 125 metres deep, sitting at the centre of Lighthouse Reef Atoll. It gained global recognition through Jacques Cousteau's 1971 expedition. The standard recreational dive descends to around 40 metres along a vertical wall to examine enormous stalactites — speleothem formations that prove the cavern formed during Pleistocene glaciation when sea levels were much lower. Caribbean reef sharks and hammerhead sharks are frequently seen at depth. The dive requires Advanced Open Water certification at minimum. Visibility is typically 30 metres or more, but the deep portion gets limited natural light. Most operators run liveaboard day trips from Ambergris Caye or Belize City, combining the Blue Hole with two shallower dives at Half Moon Caye and Long Caye.

## 2. Turneffe Atoll

Turneffe is the largest of Belize's three offshore atolls and encompasses a complex of mangrove cayes, shallow grass flats, and steep outer-reef walls. The Elbow, at the atoll's southern tip, is the signature dive — a current-swept point where all three sides of the atoll converge, producing reliable, predictable drift diving at around 15 to 30 metres. Eagle rays, horse-eye jacks in large schools, grouper, and sharks appear regularly at the Elbow during the stronger current phases. The atoll's western side holds calmer, shallower dives suitable for less experienced divers. A Turneffe liveaboard commitment of two to three nights gives access to the full range of sites. Advanced Open Water is recommended for the Elbow due to current.

## 3. Lighthouse Reef, Half Moon Caye

Half Moon Caye Natural Monument protects the southeastern end of Lighthouse Reef Atoll and encompasses some of the finest wall diving in the Caribbean. The wall drops from the reef flat at 5–8 metres to beyond 60 metres in a near-vertical face covered in plate corals, sea fans and sponges. White tip and Caribbean reef sharks patrol the edge; eagle rays move through in small groups; turtles are a near-certain sighting at recreational depths. The Half Moon Caye Wall itself runs for several kilometres and is most commonly dived at depths of 15 to 30 metres. Current is variable but manageable on most days, and the site suits Open Water divers with some experience. It is typically paired with the Blue Hole on liveaboard day trips.

## 4. Hol Chan Marine Reserve, Ambergris Caye

Hol Chan — 'little channel' in Mayan — is a cut through the barrier reef about 8 kilometres south of San Pedro on Ambergris Caye, creating a natural channel where tidal currents concentrate fish. The site is divided into four zones: the channel itself at 5 to 9 metres with nurse sharks, rays and large grouper; Shark Ray Alley, a shallow sandbar where nurse sharks and southern stingrays aggregate in numbers large enough to interact with snorkellers; and deeper reef sections on the outer side. It is accessible to Open Water divers and even beginners, with calm conditions on most days. The site is heavily visited but the sheer fish biomass remains impressive — this is one of the best-protected pieces of Belizean reef.

## 5. Glover's Reef Atoll

Glover's Reef is the most remote of Belize's atolls, roughly 45 kilometres offshore from the southern coast, and retains a less-visited character compared to Turneffe and Lighthouse Reef. The outer walls are pristine — undisturbed plate coral formations, barrel sponges two metres in diameter, and large pelagic visitors including hammerheads and oceanic white tips on the southeastern exposure. The inside of the atoll has over 700 patch reefs rising from a sandy floor at 8 to 15 metres — extraordinary for macro work, with flamingo tongue cowries, blennies and jawfish. Getting here requires a liveaboard or a long boat transfer. Open Water divers can access the patch reef interior; the outer walls suit Advanced certification.

## 6. Ambergris Caye — Boca Ciega

Boca Ciega is one of several shallow channel sites on the inner reef system accessible by short boat trip from San Pedro. Depths run 8 to 16 metres over sand channels and coral rubble, with nurse sharks resting in the channel mouths and large schools of grunts and snappers over the reef structures. This is a relaxed, high-fish-biomass dive with no significant current and very reliable visibility of 15 to 20 metres. It serves well as a first dive for newly certified divers or for those re-activating dormant skills. The sand channels make it easy to maintain positioning and the site is never completely calm — small patches of garden eels, conch and queen angelfish maintain the interest.

## 7. South Water Caye Marine Reserve

South Water Caye Marine Reserve in southern Belize protects a stretch of barrier reef with exceptional coral health — reduced agricultural runoff and lower visitor pressure compared to the northern sites have kept the reef in good condition. The outer wall drops from 10 metres to depth in vertical faces carpeted with healthy hard coral. Inside the reserve, seagrass beds provide turtle and manatee habitat; West Snake Caye and Tobacco Caye sit within the reserve and have resident dive operators. Water temperature averages 27°C year-round. The reserve sees a fraction of the dive traffic of Hol Chan or Turneffe, which means undisturbed encounters with wildlife. Open Water certification is sufficient for most sites.

## 8. Caye Caulker — Coral Gardens

Caye Caulker's Coral Gardens sits just off the split at the northern end of the island — a patch reef system at 8 to 16 metres accessible by short snorkel or dive from the shore. Nurse sharks are present year-round in the sand channels; large stingrays rest in the open; and the hard coral coverage, while not as impressive as the outer atolls, holds a diverse population of small reef fish, parrotfish and angelfish. This is genuinely accessible entry-level diving, and many divers first experience Belizean reefs here. Current is minimal, visibility averages 15 metres, and the proximity to town makes multiple dives in a day straightforward.

## 9. Basil Jones Underwater Caves, Northern Ambergris

North of San Pedro on Ambergris Caye, a series of underwater cave formations and swim-throughs run beneath the coastal shallows at depths of 6 to 15 metres. These are not full cave penetration dives — they are cavern-type features accessible without cave certification — but they provide the distinctive experience of following coral-encrusted passages where small reef fish shelter in large numbers. The topography is interesting in the low-visibility style typical of enclosed spaces, with light filtering through openings and illuminating the interiors. A torch is essential. This site suits confident Open Water divers; narrow passages require good buoyancy but not technical training.

## 10. The Elbow, Turneffe Atoll

Worth a second mention as a dedicated entry, the Elbow warrants being planned as a specific dive rather than just part of an atoll trip. Arriving at the point on an incoming tide, divers drop onto the reef crest at around 15 metres and let the current carry them around the point into the calmer western side, with the drop-off to the left falling away into deep blue water. The key is timing: the best drift happens on the last two hours of the incoming tide, when current speed is highest. Horse-eye jacks numbering in the hundreds school in mid-water; grey reef sharks hunt the edge; eagle rays sweep past with predictable regularity. Advanced Open Water and comfort with drift techniques are prerequisites.

## Planning Your Trip

Belize's main dive season runs November through May, when trade winds bring consistent clear conditions and reduced rainfall. June through October is hurricane season, and while diving is still possible, weather windows can be unpredictable. Ambergris Caye and Caye Caulker are served by daily water taxis from Belize City and have dozens of dive operators. Liveaboards depart from Belize City or San Pedro for the outer atolls; two- to three-night trips are the standard format for Turneffe, Lighthouse Reef and Glover's.
