The best macro subjects in the ocean are the ones most divers never see. A pygmy seahorse, barely larger than a grain of rice, clings to a single gorgonian fan in a near-perfect colour match. A nudibranch the size of a thumbnail grazes a sponge in colours no painter would dare combine. A skeleton shrimp, transparent and twig-like, sways on a hydroid stalk. Macro photography is the art of finding these animals, getting close enough to fill the frame, and lighting them so their detail and colour survive the journey through metres of water. It rewards patience more than any other discipline in diving, and it transforms a dive site you thought you knew into something inexhaustible.

## What Macro Means Underwater

In photographic terms, macro means a reproduction ratio approaching or exceeding life size — the subject is rendered on the sensor at close to its real dimensions or larger. Underwater, this almost always means a dedicated macro lens behind a flat port. On interchangeable-lens cameras, the 60 mm and 100 or 105 mm macro lenses are the workhorses: the 60 mm for slightly larger subjects and easier focusing, the 100 mm class for skittish or tiny critters that demand more working distance. Compact cameras achieve macro through a built-in close-focus mode, often boosted by a screw-on wet macro lens that can be added or removed underwater.

The defining challenge of macro is depth of field. At life-size magnification, the zone of sharp focus can be just a millimetre or two deep, which is why macro shooters stop their apertures down hard, to f/16, f/22 or beyond. Small apertures mean less light reaching the sensor, which in turn means artificial light becomes not optional but mandatory. Everything in macro flows from this triangle: tiny subjects, razor-thin focus, and a hunger for light.

## The Lighting Problem and Its Solutions

Water swallows light and colour. By ten metres, red has largely gone; deeper still, oranges and yellows fade, leaving the blue-green cast every diver knows. To restore the true colours of a nudibranch or a coral polyp, you must bring your own light close to the subject, where the water has no chance to filter it. This is the job of strobes — underwater flashguns mounted on arms either side of the housing.

The art lies in positioning. Two strobes angled inward toward the subject, pulled back and slightly behind the lens port, light the animal evenly while keeping the beams off the water column in front of the lens, which would otherwise illuminate suspended particles and produce backscatter — the snowstorm of bright dots that ruins so many beginner images. Many macro photographers favour a single strobe held high and to the side for dramatic, directional light that sculpts texture and throws shadow. Snoots, conical attachments that narrow a strobe's beam to a tight spot, are the macro specialist's signature tool: they isolate the subject in a pool of light against a black background, turning a cluttered reef into a clean studio portrait.

## Getting Close, and Getting It Sharp

Macro forces you to within centimetres of the subject, and at that distance two things matter above all: buoyancy and focus technique. A photographer who cannot hover motionless will never hold a pygmy seahorse in the frame, because the slightest drift swings the subject out of the wafer-thin focal plane. Macro is therefore a buoyancy discipline first and a camera discipline second. Many shooters use a single finger or a pointer on a bare patch of rock or sand to steady themselves, never on living coral.

Focusing at high magnification is its own craft. Autofocus often hunts at life size, so experienced macro shooters frequently set focus manually to a fixed distance and then rock their whole body gently forward and backward until the critical point — usually the eye — snaps into sharpness, firing at the instant it does. A focus light, a continuous lamp separate from the strobes, is essential: it lets the camera and the eye find the subject in dim water and switches off or is overpowered the moment the strobes fire.

## Finding the Subjects

None of the technique matters if you cannot find the animals, and this is where macro becomes a treasure hunt. The richest macro diving on earth is concentrated in the Coral Triangle, and one region stands above all others: the Lembeh Strait off Sulawesi in Indonesia, the global capital of muck diving. Its black volcanic sand looks barren until you slow down and start seeing — flamboyant cuttlefish, hairy frogfish, mimic octopus, blue-ringed octopus, Bobbit worms and dozens of nudibranch species, all on a single dive. Nearby Anilao in the Philippines rivals it, and the Indonesian sites around Ambon and Bali's Secret Bay are world-class.

Muck diving inverts the usual aesthetic of diving: instead of dramatic walls and clear blue water, you hunt over drab sand and rubble for the bizarre and the tiny. The dive guides of Lembeh and Anilao are extraordinary spotters, trained to find a 5 mm subject on a hectare of sand, and a good guide is worth more to a macro photographer than any piece of gear. Beyond the tropics, temperate macro thrives too: the nudibranchs of the British and Norwegian coasts, the seahorses of the Mediterranean, and the cold-water critters of British Columbia all reward the patient lens.

## Patience as a Technique

More than any other branch of underwater photography, macro is about slowing down. A wide-angle photographer might cover a reef in a dive; a macro shooter may spend the entire dive on two square metres, or on a single nudibranch, waiting for it to lift its rhinophores or crawl onto a clean edge. The mindset is closer to fishing or birdwatching than to sightseeing. You learn the behaviour of your subjects — that a frogfish will yawn if you wait long enough, that a shrimp will return to its anemone, that a mantis shrimp peering from its burrow will eventually emerge if you stay still.

This patience pays a second dividend: it is the gentlest way to dive. The macro photographer who hovers motionless, touches nothing living, and watches a single animal for twenty minutes leaves the reef exactly as they found it. The discipline that produces the best images also produces the lightest footprint, which is why so many macro shooters become passionate advocates for the small, overlooked creatures their lenses reveal.
