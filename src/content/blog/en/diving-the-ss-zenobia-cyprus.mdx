The MS Zenobia was a brand-new Swedish-built roll-on roll-off ferry on her maiden delivery voyage when she capsized and sank in Larnaca Bay in June 1980, taking her entire cargo to the bottom with her. No lives were lost, but the ship and the 104 articulated lorries lashed to her decks went down together. The result is one of the most extraordinary wreck dives on Earth: a 172-metre vessel lying on her port side in clear, warm water, with her freight still in place, reachable from a five-minute boat ride off a busy Cypriot resort town.

## How the Zenobia Came to Rest Here

The ferry had been built to run between Sweden and the Middle East. On her first commercial voyage problems began with the computerised ballast system, which began pumping excess water into the side tanks. By the time the ship reached Larnaca the list was severe, salvage attempts failed, and over the following days she rolled completely onto her port side and slid to the seabed. She now lies in roughly 16 to 42 metres of water, the starboard hull just 16 metres below the surface and the deepest accessible points on the seabed at around 42.

That depth profile is exactly why the wreck is so popular. A snorkeller can see the upper hull, an Open Water diver can explore the shallow superstructure, and technical divers can penetrate the engine room and lower car decks. Few wrecks offer such a complete spread of dives on a single structure.

## The Cargo Frozen in Time

The defining feature of the Zenobia is her cargo. The lorries — articulated trucks complete with trailers — are still where they were chained when she rolled, now hanging at impossible angles or tumbled into heaps where their securing chains finally gave way. Some dangle off the edge of the cargo deck, suspended over open water; others lie crushed beneath the weight of those above. Among the wreckage divers have catalogued a famous lorry near the upper car deck, eggs and other freight long since dispersed, and the eerie sight of an entire commercial fleet stopped mid-journey.

The cargo is what makes the Zenobia feel less like a shipwreck and more like a time capsule. Swimming along the line of trucks, with the hull rising on one side and the open blue on the other, is an experience that even divers who have seen hundreds of wrecks describe as unique.

## Marine Life on a Maiden Wreck

In four decades on the seabed the Zenobia has become a thriving artificial reef. The hull and superstructure are blanketed in sponges and tube worms, and the wreck supports a resident population of large fish. Enormous shoals of bream and barracuda hang in the water around the upper structure, groupers shelter in the recesses of the car decks, and moray eels occupy the dark gaps in the steelwork. Tuna and the occasional turtle pass through the open water around the wreck.

Because the site sits in the relatively nutrient-rich water of Larnaca Bay, the biological richness is unusual for the eastern Mediterranean. The combination of intact structure, abundant fish life and easy access keeps the Zenobia at or near the top of most published lists of the world's best wreck dives.

## Diving Levels and Penetration

The wreck rewards every level of diver. The shallower dives along the starboard hull and the upper superstructure are within reach of newly certified Open Water divers, often as a first taste of wreck diving. The accommodation block, bridge and upper car deck sit in the 16-to-28-metre range and form the bread-and-butter dives for most visitors.

Deeper and more committing are the penetration dives into the lower car decks, the engine room with its catwalks and machinery, and the restaurant and accommodation spaces where furniture still litters the floors. These are serious overhead environments requiring wreck-penetration training, redundant gas and a guide who knows the layout intimately. Several divers have died inside the Zenobia over the years, almost always while penetrating beyond their training, and reputable operators are strict about who they take inside.

## Operators and Logistics in Larnaca

Larnaca is the obvious base, with the wreck a short boat ride from the marina. The town has a dense cluster of dive centres competing for Zenobia business, including long-established outfits like Alpha Divers, Viking Divers and The Dive Centre, alongside Cypriot-run operations such as Σχολή καταδύσεων ΚΙΜΩΝ. Most run two-tank morning trips to the wreck, with technical and penetration packages available from the centres equipped for them, including those operating vessels like MAV Achaios.

Water temperatures climb to the mid-20s Celsius in summer and the visibility regularly exceeds 20 to 30 metres. The wreck can be dived year-round, though winter brings cooler water and occasional surface chop. Larnaca International Airport is a few minutes from the dive centres, making this one of the most accessible world-class wrecks anywhere.

## Repeat Diving and Why People Keep Coming Back

Few wrecks reward repetition the way the Zenobia does. Because the ship is so large and the dives are spread across such a range of depths, no single dive comes close to covering it, and most divers who take the site seriously plan four, six or more dives over several days. The first dives tend to be orientation runs along the starboard hull and the upper structure; later dives push into the bridge, the accommodation and, for the qualified, the car decks and engine room.

The wreck also changes with the light and the season. Early-morning dives catch shafts of sunlight slanting down the hull, while the deeper interior spaces are perpetually dark and demand torches. The marine life shifts too: the great shoals of bream are denser in the warmer months, and the resident groupers grow visibly bolder over a week of diving as they become used to the bubbles. This combination of scale, depth range and changing conditions is exactly why so many divers return to Larnaca year after year for the same single wreck.

## Beyond the Zenobia

While the Zenobia dominates Larnaca diving, Cyprus offers more for a longer trip. The waters off Kyrenia — Kerynia on the northern coast — hold the famous ancient shipwreck whose hull is preserved in a castle museum, and the island has several deliberately sunk artificial reefs and patrol boats. Day boats from Larnaca and Limassol visit other sites, and operators sometimes run combined itineraries pairing the Zenobia with reef and cavern dives. But for almost everyone who comes, the ferry and her cargo are the reason.
