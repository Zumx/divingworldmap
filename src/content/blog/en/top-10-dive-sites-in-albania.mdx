Albania occupies a stretch of the Adriatic and Ionian coastline that most European divers have never seriously considered, which is exactly why it rewards the curious. The country's decades of isolation under communist rule left its coastline largely undeveloped, and while that history brought its own difficulties, one unintended consequence was a coastline far less degraded than comparable stretches of the Italian, Greek, or Croatian coast. The Ionian side in particular — around Sazan Island, the Karaburun Peninsula, and the deep bays to the south — holds clear water, respectable marine biodiversity, and a growing number of operators willing to take divers to sites that appear on almost no international itinerary. Water temperatures range from around 14°C in winter to 26°C in summer; visibility on the Ionian side regularly exceeds 25 metres in calm conditions.

## 1. Karaburun Peninsula, Ionian Coast

The Karaburun Peninsula forms the western edge of the Karaburun-Sazan Marine Protected Area, Albania's most significant marine reserve. The underwater topography here is dramatic: vertical walls dropping to 40 metres and beyond, sea caves, and a series of archways carved by wave action over millennia. Posidonia seagrass meadows at shallower depths host seahorses, cuttlefish, and juvenile grouper. Deeper sections produce gorgonian sea fans, and moray eels are common in the crevices. Advanced certification is recommended for the deeper wall sections; the shallower caves are accessible to Open Water divers. Summer is the clear season; September and October offer warmer water and reduced swell.

## 2. Sazan Island, Karaburun-Sazan Marine Park

Sazan is a military island that was closed to all visitors for most of the twentieth century, which means its surrounding waters were effectively a no-take zone for decades. The result is visible: fish populations are notably denser than on comparable Albanian sites, and lobster are present at depths where they have been fished out elsewhere. The island's north-facing walls carry significant biological interest, with nudibranchs, sea urchins, and colourful encrusting sponges in hues of orange and yellow. Depths to 35 metres. Open Water certification sufficient for most sites; some passages require navigation skills. Permits are required to dive here; operators in Vlorë can arrange access.

## 3. Porto Palermo Bay, Southern Albania

The small bay at Porto Palermo is sheltered by a promontory bearing an eighteenth-century Ottoman fortress, and beneath the surface it holds one of the most photogenic dive environments on the Albanian coast. The bay's calm, protected water means visibility is consistently good even when the Ionian Sea is churned up by wind. Depths are modest — maximum around 18 metres in the main anchoring area — making this an excellent site for newly certified divers or those returning to diving after a break. Octopus are common on the sandy bottom; scorpionfish rest on rocky outcrops near the walls. The sheltered aspect also makes this a reliable night-dive venue.

## 4. Kakome Bay, Ionian South

Kakome is one of the most enclosed natural bays on the Albanian coast, backed by pine-covered hills and largely undeveloped. The underwater environment reflects this isolation: the bottom remains relatively undisturbed, the posidonia is healthy, and fishing pressure has been lower than on more accessible stretches of coast. Dive depths run to about 25 metres at the bay's mouth. Octopus, moray eels, and various wrasse species are the main resident fauna; in late summer, large shoals of bogue and other small pelagic fish fill the water column. Shore diving is possible from the beach, making it accessible without a dedicated dive boat.

## 5. The Vlorë Bay Wrecks, Vlorë

The waters off Vlorë hold several wrecks from different eras, including vessels from the Second World War and later Albanian military materiel. The most accessible of these sit at depths between 18 and 35 metres. Marine growth on the steel structures is well established — encrusting sponges, soft corals, and the usual Mediterranean wreck inhabitants including conger eels in the darker compartments and large scorpionfish on the outer hull. Exact positions are best confirmed with local operators, as navigational charts for these sites are not always current. Advanced Open Water certification is recommended for the deeper pieces.

## 6. Gramë Cave System, Karaburun

The sea caves along the Karaburun coast include the Gramë caves, a network of passages accessible from the sea that combine snorkelling depth at the entrance with diveable chambers inside. The caves are known for their light effects — shafts of filtered sunlight in the morning hours create striking visual conditions for photographers. Inside, the walls carry sponges and coralline algae; a population of cave-adapted crustaceans inhabits the darker recesses. Navigation skills and a dive torch are essential. The cave passages do not require technical certification but demand calm, disciplined buoyancy. Best dived in the morning when light penetration is at its maximum.

## 7. Shën Vasil Point, Near Sarandë

The rocky point south of Sarandë near the Greek border marks a transition between the calmer inner Ionian waters and more exposed open-sea conditions. The site produces stronger currents than most Albanian dive sites, which in turn brings better pelagic action: large amberjack are regularly seen, and occasional sightings of bluefin tuna and hammerhead sharks have been reported in summer. The bottom topography here — boulders and ridges rather than flat sand — suits drift diving. Certification requirement is Advanced Open Water at minimum; current reading and buoy deployment are practical necessities.

## 8. Pashaliman Naval Base Area, Vlorë

The former naval base at Pashaliman was home to the Albanian submarine fleet during the communist era, and the surrounding waters contain evidence of that history in the form of submerged infrastructure and several decommissioned vessels. Diving here requires coordination with local authorities and is best arranged through an established Vlorë operator. The artificial structures have accumulated significant marine life over the decades. Depth ranges from 15 to 40 metres depending on which elements of the site are visited. This is not a polished dive resort site — it is genuinely exploratory, with all the satisfactions and uncertainties that implies.

## 9. Livadhi Beach Wall, Near Himara

The rocky coastline near Himara drops steeply below the surface, producing short but interesting wall sections with depths to about 30 metres before the gradient eases into sandy bottom. The wall carries gorgonian fans below 20 metres, and the rocky rubble zone at the base is productive for macro life — nudibranchs, blennies, and various goby species inhabit the algae-covered rocks. Shore diving is possible at Livadhi Beach itself. The site is exposed to northwesterly swell, so conditions should be checked before committing; in calm summer weather it is an easy and rewarding dive.

## 10. Ksamil Reefs, Near Butrint

Near the Butrint archaeological site and the small island chain of Ksamil, a series of shallow reefs extends into calm, protected water. Depths rarely exceed 12 metres in the main reef areas, making this one of the most approachable dive sites in Albania and a natural choice for Discover Scuba experiences or snorkelling alongside divers. The reefs are home to sea urchins, starfish, octopus, and dense posidonia — the ecological foundation species for Mediterranean inshore environments. Water clarity in this sheltered stretch is generally excellent throughout the summer season.

## Planning a Diving Trip to Albania

The best diving season runs from May through October, with July to September offering the warmest water and best visibility on the Ionian coast. The Adriatic coast, around Durrës and Shëngjin, is shallower and less interesting for diving but more accessible from the north. Most serious diving operations are based in Vlorë, with day trips to Karaburun, Sazan, and the southern sites. Liveaboard options are limited but not unknown — ask local operators about multi-day arrangements during the summer months. Equipment hire is available in Vlorë and Sarandë; bringing your own regulators and computer is advisable. The infrastructure is still developing, which keeps prices low but requires flexible expectations.
