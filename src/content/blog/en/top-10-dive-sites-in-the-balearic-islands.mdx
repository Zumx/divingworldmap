The Balearic Islands hang in the western Mediterranean east of mainland Spain, and their diving is built on one quiet hero: posidonia. The vast meadows of this protected seagrass filter the water so effectively that the Balearics offer some of the clearest sea in the Mediterranean, with visibility frequently passing 30 metres and occasionally touching 40 off Formentera. On top of that clarity sit limestone caves and arches, the marine reserves of the Cabrera archipelago and the Freus channel, and a summer water temperature of 25 to 27°C that makes long, warm dives in a 3mm suit the norm from June to October. Each island has its own character — Mallorca for caves and a national park, Menorca for relaxed reef, Ibiza and Formentera for clarity and walls.

## 1. Cabrera Archipelago National Park, Mallorca

The Cabrera archipelago, a national park since 1991, lies south of Mallorca and is one of the best-preserved stretches of seabed in the Mediterranean. Diving is permit-controlled and reached by boat from Colònia de Sant Jordi, which keeps the sites pristine. Groupers grown large and fearless in the no-take zone investigate divers, while caves, posidonia and rocky reef run from 10 to 35 metres. Visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres. Most sites suit Open Water divers, with deeper walls for Advanced. The season runs May to October, with the warmest, calmest water in August and September.

## 2. The Cathedral, Cala Llonga, Ibiza

Off the east coast of Ibiza near Santa Eulària, La Catedral is a celebrated cavern system where light pours through openings in the rock into a domed chamber, giving divers the sense of swimming inside a flooded church. Depths run 12 to 30 metres, and the swim-throughs are wide and well-lit, suiting Open Water divers with a guide. Groupers, scorpionfish, octopus and big shoals of bream inhabit the rock. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres. The site is best in the calm months from June to September.

## 3. Don Pedro Wreck, Ibiza

The Don Pedro is the largest wreck in the Balearics — a 142-metre cargo ferry that sank off Ibiza town in 2007 and now rests between 25 and 46 metres. The upper structure is within reach of Advanced Open Water divers, while the deeper sections are technical territory. The hull is colonised by groupers, congers, barracuda and shoaling fish, and the sheer scale of the ship makes it an unforgettable dive. Visibility is usually 15 to 30 metres. Currents can run, so it is dived from local boats with experienced guides, mainly between May and October.

## 4. Freus Marine Reserve, Formentera

The Freus is the channel between Ibiza and Formentera, protected as a marine reserve, and the clarity here is the finest in the Balearics — divers routinely report 40 metres of horizontal visibility over the posidonia. Reef and sand run from 8 to 25 metres, with eagle rays, barracuda, groupers and the occasional sunfish in late summer. Sites like the Llosa de Santa Eulària and the reefs off Espalmador suit all levels. The protected seagrass is the foundation of the ecosystem and the reason the water is so blue. Dive it June to October.

## 5. Pou des Lleó and the Caves, Ibiza

On Ibiza's quieter north-east coast near Pou des Lleó, a series of caves and arches at 6 to 22 metres offers atmospheric cavern diving with abundant light and life. Octopus, moray eels, scorpionfish and nudibranchs crowd the rock, and the shallow caverns suit newer divers under guidance. The bay itself is sheltered, making it a reliable choice when stronger summer winds close the more exposed sites. Visibility runs 15 to 25 metres. The season aligns with the rest of the Balearics, May through October, with warmest water in August.

## 6. Illa des Bisbe and Northern Mallorca

Off the rugged north coast of Mallorca near Port de Pollença and Cap de Formentor, the islets and walls offer the island's most dramatic topography. Steep drop-offs descend past 35 metres into clear blue water where amberjack, barracuda and the occasional sunfish patrol. Groupers and large dentex haunt the reef edges. These are boat dives suited to Advanced divers comfortable with depth and mild current. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres. The north coast is exposed to the tramontana wind, so conditions are best in settled summer weather.

## 7. Cala en Brut and the Menorca Caves, Menorca

Menorca, a UNESCO biosphere reserve, offers gentler diving than its neighbours, much of it over reef and through limestone caves along the south coast near Cala en Brut and Cala Galdana. Depths run 8 to 25 metres, with octopus, cuttlefish, groupers and shoals of bream. The island's marine reserve on the northern coast protects an exceptional density of fish. Conditions are calm and accessible, ideal for Open Water divers and relaxed second dives. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres, and the diving runs May to October.

## 8. El Toro Marine Reserve, Mallorca

Just off the south-west coast of Mallorca near Santa Ponça, the islet of El Toro sits within a marine reserve where fish life has rebounded spectacularly. Walls and boulder fields descend from 10 to beyond 35 metres, with enormous groupers, dentex, barracuda and shoals of saddled bream that swarm divers. The reserve status makes this one of the most fish-rich dives in the Balearics. Sites range from easy reef for Open Water divers to deeper walls for Advanced. Visibility runs 20 to 30 metres. Best from May to October.

## 9. Sa Dragonera, Mallorca

The island of Sa Dragonera off Mallorca's west coast is a natural park whose surrounding waters offer caves, walls and posidonia meadows from 10 to 30 metres. The dramatic limestone topography above water continues below, with arches and swim-throughs sheltering groupers, octopus and moray eels. Eagle rays cruise the sand, and the clarity off this western point is excellent. Boat access is from Sant Elm or Port d'Andratx. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility frequently passes 30 metres, with the calmest conditions from June to September.

## 10. Punta Gavina and the Formentera Walls

Off the southern tip of Formentera, the walls and reefs around Punta Gavina and Cap de Barbaria drop into deep, exceptionally clear water. Posidonia gives way to rock and sand at 12 to 30 metres, where barracuda, amberjack and groupers gather, and the visibility — among the best in the entire Mediterranean — makes the diving feel weightless and infinite. These are boat dives from La Savina, suited to Open Water and Advanced divers. The water reaches 26 to 27°C in late summer. Dive June through October for the calmest seas and warmest water.

## Explore on the map

The Balearics reward a multi-island trip: Mallorca for the Cabrera national park, El Toro and northern walls; Menorca for relaxed reef and caves; Ibiza for the Cathedral cavern and the Don Pedro wreck; Formentera for the clearest water in the Mediterranean. The diving season runs May to October, peaking in warmth and calm in August and September.
