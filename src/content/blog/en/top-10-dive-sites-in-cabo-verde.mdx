Cabo Verde rises from the Atlantic some 570 kilometres off the coast of West Africa, a volcanic archipelago of ten islands where the sea is warm, the visibility good and the diving built on lava geology rather than coral reef. The water here is a meeting point of currents and temperatures — cooler upwelling than the tropics yet warm by Atlantic standards, ranging from around 22°C in winter to 27°C in late summer — and the marine life reflects an Atlantic-African character: large schools of jacks and barracuda, moray eels, rays, turtles, and seasonal pelagic visitors including whale sharks and humpback whales. The diving is concentrated on the eastern islands of Sal and Boa Vista, where the dive industry is most developed, with São Vicente and Santiago offering further options. The islands' volcanic origins show underwater as caves, arches, swim-throughs and dramatic rock formations, and a handful of wrecks add structure to the dives.

## 1. Buracona, Sal

Buracona, on the northwestern coast of Sal, is one of the archipelago's signature sites — a series of volcanic caves and the famous Blue Eye, a sinkhole where sunlight enters through an underwater opening and floods the cavern with intense blue light around midday. The diving combines dramatic cavern topography with the marine life of the surrounding reef, and the play of light through the rock is the great attraction. The site requires good buoyancy and is best dived when the sun is high. It is among the most photographed dives in Cabo Verde and showcases the islands' volcanic character.

## 2. Santa Maria Bay and the Lemon Sharks, Sal

The waters off Santa Maria on Sal's southern coast host one of Cabo Verde's most remarkable seasonal phenomena — an aggregation of lemon sharks that gather in the shallows, typically between October and March. Divers and snorkellers can observe these large sharks in clear, shallow water in numbers rarely seen elsewhere in the Atlantic. Beyond the sharks, the bay offers reef diving with rays, turtles and abundant fish life. The shallow, sheltered conditions make much of the diving accessible to a range of levels, and the seasonal shark aggregation draws divers specifically to Sal.

## 3. The Boris Wreck, Sal

The Boris is a wreck off Sal that has become an artificial reef, colonised by marine life and offering structure and interest for wreck enthusiasts. Lying within recreational depth, the wreck attracts schools of fish, moray eels sheltering in the metal, and the occasional larger visitor. The clear Atlantic water and the wreck's accessibility make it a popular dive from the Santa Maria bases. Wrecks like the Boris add variety to a Cabo Verde itinerary otherwise dominated by volcanic reef and cave diving, and they suit divers from Open Water upward depending on conditions.

## 4. Cavala and the Reefs off Sal

Cavala and the various reef sites off Sal offer classic Cabo Verde reef diving over volcanic rock, with the islands' typical mix of large schools of jacks, barracuda and grunts, moray eels in the crevices, and rays and turtles passing through. The volcanic topography creates ledges, overhangs and small swim-throughs that shelter fish and add interest. Visibility is generally good, and the sites suit a range of certification levels. These reefs form the backbone of everyday diving from the Santa Maria operators and showcase the Atlantic-African character of the archipelago's marine life.

## 5. Ponta do Sino, Sal

Ponta do Sino, near the southern tip of Sal, offers reef and rock diving with the volcanic topography characteristic of the islands. The site supports good fish life, including schooling species and the moray eels typical of the archipelago, with rays and turtles seen regularly. The conditions are generally manageable, and the proximity to Santa Maria makes it an accessible dive. Like much of Sal's diving, it combines the warm, clear Atlantic water with the dramatic underwater geology that gives Cabo Verde diving its distinctive character.

## 6. Baía das Gatas and the Reefs off São Vicente

São Vicente, in the northwestern Barlavento group, offers a different diving scene from the busy eastern islands, with reefs and volcanic formations around the island's coast. The waters here can be subject to stronger upwelling and cooler temperatures, but they support rich marine life including large schools, rays and the occasional pelagic visitor. The diving sees less traffic than Sal or Boa Vista, and São Vicente's cultural hub of Mindelo provides a lively base. The reefs reward divers exploring beyond the main tourist islands with quieter, scenic Atlantic diving.

## 7. Boa Vista Reefs and Turtles

Boa Vista, east of Sal, is famous above water as a major nesting site for loggerhead turtles, and the surrounding waters offer reef diving where turtles, rays and abundant fish life are common. The volcanic reefs and the clear Atlantic water provide good conditions, and the island's developing dive industry offers access to a range of sites. Boa Vista combines its turtle-nesting fame with genuine reef diving, and the relative quiet compared to Sal appeals to divers seeking a more relaxed island base while still enjoying warm-water Atlantic diving.

## 8. Whale Shark and Pelagic Encounters, Eastern Islands

The waters around the eastern Cabo Verde islands attract seasonal pelagic visitors, including whale sharks during certain periods, drawn by the productive Atlantic water. These encounters are never guaranteed and depend on timing and luck, but the possibility of sharing the water with the ocean's largest fish adds an extra dimension to diving here. Operators on Sal and Boa Vista watch for these visitors, and the open Atlantic position of the archipelago means larger pelagic species pass through. The mid-ocean setting gives Cabo Verde a genuine big-animal potential.

## 9. Humpback Whale Waters, Boa Vista and Sal

Between roughly March and May, humpback whales pass through Cabo Verde's waters on their migration, and the eastern islands become a base for whale watching and the occasional in-water encounter. While these are surface-based experiences rather than scuba dives, the seasonal presence of humpbacks underlines the rich marine ecosystem of the archipelago. Divers visiting in spring may combine reef diving with whale watching, and the songs of the humpbacks can sometimes be heard underwater. It is a reminder that Cabo Verde sits on a genuine oceanic crossroads.

## 10. Santiago Reefs

Santiago, the largest and most populous island, offers reef and volcanic diving around its coast, with sites accessible from the capital Praia and other coastal towns. The diving here reflects the archipelago's typical character — volcanic rock formations, large schools of fish, moray eels, rays and turtles — and the island's cultural and historical interest above water, including the UNESCO-listed Cidade Velha, makes it a rewarding base for a combined diving and exploration trip. Santiago sees less dive tourism than Sal, offering quieter sites for divers exploring the southern Sotavento islands.

## Planning Your Trip

Cabo Verde offers year-round diving, with water temperatures from around 22°C in winter to 27°C in late summer, so a 3mm to 5mm wetsuit suits most of the year. The seasonal highlights shape the calendar: lemon sharks off Sal from October to March, humpback whales in spring, and pelagic visitors through the warmer months. Sal and Boa Vista have the most developed dive industries and are served by international flights; São Vicente and Santiago offer quieter alternatives. The Atlantic position means conditions can be exposed, and some sites are weather-dependent. Open Water certification covers many sites; the caves at Buracona and deeper or current-swept dives reward Advanced experience and good buoyancy.
