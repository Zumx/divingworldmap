Poland is not the first country that comes to mind when planning a diving holiday, but the country has a surprisingly rich underwater landscape that rewards the divers who explore it. The Baltic Sea coastline in the north holds dozens of shipwrecks from two world wars and centuries of trade, lying in cold green water where visibility varies but historical interest is constant. Inland, a collection of lakes stretches across the northeast and western lake districts, several of which have been developed into excellent dive sites with flooded forests, submerged vehicles and stable conditions that make them useful training grounds and interesting sport dives. The deepest lake in Poland, Hancza in the Suwalki region near the Lithuanian border, plunges to 108 metres — a technically demanding environment that draws technical divers from across Europe. Water temperatures in the lakes run from 4°C at depth in winter to around 20°C at the surface in summer; the Baltic sits persistently cold at 6–15°C depending on depth and season. A 7mm wetsuit is the minimum for comfortable lake diving; a drysuit is strongly recommended for Baltic wrecks and winter lake diving. ## 1. Hancza Lake, Suwalki Region

Hancza is the centrepiece of Polish inland diving. At 108 metres, it is the deepest lake in the Polish lowlands and one of the deepest in Central Europe. The water is exceptionally clear by Polish standards — visibility in winter can reach 15 metres — because the lake receives little agricultural runoff and sits in a protected nature area. The upper 30 metres are accessible to recreational divers on standard equipment, with submerged boulders, crayfish and various freshwater fish visible in the clear water. Below 40 metres, the lake becomes a technical environment where divers on trimix or rebreathers explore the steep walls and silty bottom. A DIVE DEVIL dive centre operates at the lakeside. Water temperature drops sharply with depth; below the thermocline it sits around 4–5°C year-round. Advanced Open Water for the upper section; technical training required for anything below 30 metres.

## 2. Baltic Wrecks, Pomeranian Bay and Gulf of Gdansk

Poland's Baltic coast holds a remarkable collection of shipwrecks, most concentrated in the areas around Gdynia, Gdansk and further west toward Swinoujscie. The wrecks span centuries — wooden sailing ships, nineteenth-century steamers and WWII vessels lying in depths from 20 to over 60 metres. Many are protected as historical monuments, meaning penetration is restricted, but the exterior structures, covered in Baltic mussels, hydroids and soft corals, are rewarding for divers who specialise in wreck exploration. Visibility in the Baltic averages 5 to 10 metres in summer, occasionally stretching to 15 metres after prolonged settled weather. Temperatures at depth sit at 6–8°C year-round. Drysuit and wreck specialty training are effectively prerequisites for serious Baltic wreck diving; dive centres in Gdynia and Gdansk run guided boat trips during the summer season from June through September.

## 3. Jezioro Moryn, near Chojna

Moryn is a small lake near the town of Chojna in northwestern Poland, accessible from two entry points — Moryn village on the western shore and Przyjezierze on the eastern side — both of which are established dive sites with underwater features. The Moryn side holds a submerged wooden jetty structure and a sloping reef that drops to around 25 metres before hitting the silty bottom. The Przyjezierze entry is shallower, with a gradual slope covered in freshwater vegetation and a resident population of perch, tench and eels that tolerate divers at close range. Both sites have well-maintained entry steps and facilities for changing; they are popular with dive clubs from Berlin and the surrounding region, and the proximity to Germany means that weekend visits are common. Open Water certification is sufficient for both entries.

## 4. Jezioro Drawsko, Stare Drawsko and Zodiak

Lake Drawsko in Pomerania is one of the largest and most dive-developed lakes in Poland. At the Stare Drawsko site, named for the village of Kleofas on the western shore, the lake bottom holds submerged wooden structures — remnants of old boat landings and a flooded area — that have colonised with freshwater mussels and algae, providing interesting relief in an otherwise flat lake bed at 15 to 20 metres. The Zodiak site, accessible from the eastern shore, has a slightly different character: steeper drop-off, cleaner visibility and a resident colony of freshwater crayfish along the rocky slope. Both are used extensively for training dives and by recreational divers wanting calm, predictable conditions. Summer visibility reaches 6 to 10 metres; winter cold water improves it further. Open Water certification for both sites.

## 5. Jezioro Jelenin, near Zielona Gora

Jelenin is a former quarry lake near Zielona Gora in western Poland that has been deliberately developed as a dive site. The T-Punkt entry offers access to a sloping wall that drops to around 30 metres, with various sunken objects placed for diver interest — vehicle parts, a small boat, a submerged cross — and consistent freshwater fish life including catfish of considerable size. The Badeanstalt entry point accesses a shallower area with a sandy bottom and gentle slope, suitable for training and Open Water certification dives. The water is cleaner than most natural lakes in the region because the quarry bedrock is granite rather than limestone, and algae growth is limited. This is a competent year-round dive site; in winter, ice diving is occasionally organised from the Badeanstalt entry with proper safety protocols.

## 6. Jezioro Bielin, Lubusz Region

Bielin is a circular lake of glacial origin in western Poland, notable among Polish divers for its unusually good visibility — in winter, it can exceed 10 metres, exceptional for Polish inland water. The lake has a single well-organised entry point with parking and basic facilities. The slope drops gradually to about 12 metres before flattening onto a muddy basin at 18 metres; the interesting zone is the slope itself, where boulders and submerged tree stumps provide habitat for freshwater perch, pike and crayfish. The site is used extensively as a training lake by dive clubs from Zielona Gora and neighbouring cities. Open Water students can complete all four open-water training dives here in summer conditions. Night diving at Bielin produces good encounters with freshwater crayfish and European catfish that hunt in the shallows after dark.

## 7. Jezioro Insko, Insko

The lake at Insko in the Pomeranian lake district was the site of a German military facility during WWII, and the flooded remnants of that history are distributed across the lake bottom. Two main entry points — Damm Nord and Damm Süd — access different sections of the lake. The south entry sits near a submerged dam structure and the remnants of a flooded roadway that runs across the lake bed at 8 to 12 metres, easily navigable even in average visibility. The north entry leads to a broader area with scattered debris and a deeper zone around 22 metres where visibility is often better than in the shallower, more vegetated sections. A third entry at the Westspitze offers a longer shallow dive along the northwestern shore, best in summer when warm surface water brings out the fish life. Open Water for all three entries.

## 8. Jezioro Czarnoglowy Duze, Pomerania

This lake in western Pomerania holds one of Poland's more unusual underwater features: a flooded forest. The Unterwasserwald — German for 'underwater forest' — consists of the preserved trunks and root systems of trees that were submerged when the lake level rose, either naturally or through water management works, in the mid-twentieth century. The skeletal remains of the trees rise from the lake bed at 6 to 10 metres, draped in freshwater algae and colonised by perch and roach that use the vertical structures as habitat. The overall effect is eerie and photogenic in clear winter conditions. Entry is from a well-marked point on the southern shore, and the submerged forest is within 100 metres of the entry. Open Water divers can navigate the site comfortably, though neutral buoyancy is important to avoid stirring the silt between the root systems.

## 9. Jezioro Insko Westspitze

The Westspitze — western point — of Insko Lake offers the longest uninterrupted shore dive on the lake, following the northwestern shoreline through freshwater vegetation and over a gradual sandy slope. In late spring and early summer, the visibility peaks before algae bloom cuts it back, and encounters with pike — some exceeding a metre — are common in the weed beds. The dive is best done with a boat cover or on a calm day when swimmer traffic is low. Depths along the shore do not exceed 8 metres for the first 200 metres from entry, making this appropriate for all certification levels including students in open-water training. The site has less infrastructure than the Damm entries but offers more of a natural lake experience.

## 10. Jezioro Drawsko, Zodiak

The Zodiak site on the eastern shore of Lake Drawsko deserves its own entry beyond the general Drawsko description because the diving character is meaningfully different from the Kleofas side. The drop-off from the shore is sharper, reaching 20 metres within 80 metres of the entry point, and the bottom substrate shifts from silt to the sort of mixed gravel and clay that supports cleaner water and better visibility. Several large catfish — local divers estimate some individuals at well over 15 kg — have taken up residence under the slope, and they are reliably encountered on most dives. The site has a small wooden pier, basic changing facilities and organised entry steps. It is popular with diving schools from Szczecin and operates through the summer season from May through September.

## Planning Your Poland Dive Trip

Poland's dive season varies sharply between the lake and Baltic environments. Lakes are best from April through October, with peak conditions in May and June before summer algae blooms reduce visibility. The Baltic season for wreck diving runs June through September, constrained by weather and sea state. Dive centres exist in all major cities and at the established lake sites; the scene is well-organised, with PADI and SSI instruction available throughout the country. A drysuit is strongly recommended for Baltic diving and for any lake diving outside the summer months.
