French diving is more varied than most visiting divers expect. The Mediterranean coast offers warm, clear water above rocky reefs and seamounts from May through October, while Brittany's Atlantic sites deliver dramatic kelp walls and wreck diving in cold, green water with significant tidal ranges. Corsica sits midway between the two climates, with underwater scenery that rewards both wide-angle and macro photography. France was also an early leader in underwater cave exploration, and the karst systems of the Lot and Ardèche rivers contain some of the most challenging freshwater cave dives in Europe. The country's dive culture is well-organised, shaped by the FFESSM federation and a network of clubs that maintain many of the best access points.

## 1. Marseille Calanques, Provence

The calanques — limestone fjords carved into the coast between Marseille and Cassis — shelter sites of real quality within a short boat ride of the city. The Grand Congloué island, about 15 km offshore, was the site of two Roman amphora wrecks discovered in the 1950s and remains historically significant. The rock walls carry red gorgonian fans at 25–40 metres, and the clarity in calm weather touches 20–25 metres. Scorpionfish lurk on every ledge, and moray eels are common in the rocky substrate. Best season: May to October. Open Water divers can access the shallower sites; the deeper walls need Advanced.

## 2. Port-Cros National Park, Var

Port-Cros became France's first marine national park in 1963, and decades of protection have made it noticeably richer than the surrounding coast. The park covers the island and a generous buffer of water where anchoring and fishing are prohibited. La Gabinière seamount, rising from 40 metres to the surface on the island's southern tip, carries gorgonians on every face and hosts large grouper that have lost most of their fear of divers. Visibility regularly reaches 25 metres in summer. Entry to the marine zone requires a permit purchased from the park, and dive operators in Hyères and Le Lavandou run regular trips.

## 3. Le Donator Wreck, Corsica

The Donator, a cargo vessel sunk in 1945, lies at between 30 and 45 metres off the coast near Propriano in southern Corsica. The hull is largely intact and heavily colonised by red gorgonians, sponges, and soft corals, with massive grouper sheltering in the holds. The depth and the penetration possibilities mean this dive requires at least Advanced Open Water certification and ideally some wreck diving experience. Visibility in the clear Corsican water is typically 15–25 metres. Dive operators in Propriano and Porto-Vecchio run regular excursions to the wreck in summer.

## 4. Scandola Nature Reserve, Corsica

The Scandola peninsula, a UNESCO World Heritage Site accessible only by sea, offers some of the most dramatic volcanic scenery in the Mediterranean. The underwater topography mirrors the surface — jagged black rock formations dropping through caves, arches, and gullies to sandy floors at 25–35 metres. Moray eels, octopus, and sea bream are ubiquitous. The reserve prohibits anchoring and restricts diver numbers, which keeps the marine life relatively bold. Water temperature peaks around 24°C in August and drops to 14°C in February. Best diving season: June to September.

## 5. Sec de la Baume, Provence

This isolated seamount rising from 40 metres to 14 metres below the surface, located offshore between Hyères and Toulon, concentrates pelagic life the way a coral head does in warmer oceans. Large schools of barracuda hang above the summit through the summer, and the gorgonian growth on the deeper flanks is exceptional. The site is exposed to swell and current, which can limit access to calm weather windows. Advanced certification is appropriate, and a rescue diver or divemaster guiding the group is standard practice given the offshore nature of the dive.

## 6. Basse du Jas, Port-Cros

A shallower site within the Port-Cros marine park, Basse du Jas is known for its accessibility and the diversity it packs into a 5–25 metre depth range. Nudibranchs of a dozen species have been recorded on a single dive, sea horses shelter in the posidonia meadows, and electric rays rest on the sandy patches between the rocks. This is an excellent site for Open Water divers wanting their first experience in a genuinely protected Mediterranean environment, and for underwater photographers working macro. The park's protection is immediately visible in the size and approachability of the fish.

## 7. Pointe du Raz Wreck Dives, Brittany

The headland at Pointe du Raz, at the tip of Cap Sizun in Finistère, generates some of the strongest tidal currents in Brittany, and over centuries those currents have left a graveyard of wrecks along the sea floor. Several lie at accessible recreational depths of 18–35 metres, encrusted with anemones, urchins, and the cold-water sponges that make Atlantic wreck diving so visually different from the Mediterranean. Water temperature ranges from 8°C in winter to 17°C in August, so a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is necessary year-round. Tidal windows are short; local operators time departures precisely.

## 8. Île de Sein, Brittany

The shallow reefs and wrecks surrounding this remote island 8 km off the Pointe du Raz are among the most visited dive destinations on the Atlantic coast of France. The remains of multiple vessels are scattered across the sandy bottom at 12–22 metres, and the cold, nutrient-rich water supports dense kelp forests that shelter lobster, edible crab, and wolf fish. Seals occasionally appear at the western anchorages. Visibility is variable — 5–15 metres depending on tidal state — and the exposed position means diving is limited to settled weather. Best season: April to September.

## 9. Sète Sea Bed, Languedoc

The waters off Sète, on the Languedoc coast between Montpellier and Spain, contain several artificial reefs and a couple of deliberately sunk vessels that have grown into productive dive sites over the years. The Jules Verne reef system, dropped in the early 2000s, now supports a dense community of sea bass, bream, conger eels, and octopus. Depths on the artificial sites range from 15 to 28 metres, making them straightforward for Open Water divers. The coast is less visited than Provence or Corsica, so boats tend to be less crowded during the peak summer months.

## 10. Cerbère-Banyuls Marine Reserve, Roussillon

The most southerly dive destination on the French Mediterranean coast sits just above the Spanish border and was one of the first marine reserves established in France. The rocky headlands and islands around Banyuls-sur-Mer shelter sites where red gorgonians start at 15 metres and continue well beyond 40 metres down the rock faces. Large grouper, painted comber, and scorpionfish are common throughout the reserve. The water temperature here benefits from occasional warm influxes from further south, reaching 25°C in August. Banyuls has a resident dive research station that offers guided dives during summer.

## Planning your French dive trip

Mediterranean sites along the Var coast, Corsica, and Languedoc dive best from May to October, with visibility and water temperature peaking in July and August. Brittany's season runs from April through September, though cold-water specialists dive year-round. For Corsica's best sites, early June or September avoids the worst of the summer crowds without compromising conditions. French dive operators almost universally offer guided dives as a prerequisite for unfamiliar sites within marine reserves.
