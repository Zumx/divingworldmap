## What Nitrox Actually Is

Standard compressed air contains approximately 21 percent oxygen and 79 percent nitrogen. Enriched air nitrox — almost always called EANx or simply nitrox — is any breathing gas mixture with an oxygen fraction above that baseline. In recreational diving, two blends dominate: EAN32, with 32 percent oxygen, and EAN36, with 36 percent. A handful of specialised operations go higher, but anything above 40 percent oxygen requires technical training and equipment beyond the recreational norm.

The distinction matters because every extra percentage point of oxygen comes at the cost of nitrogen. Less nitrogen means slower saturation of your tissues on every dive, which translates directly into more allowable bottom time before your dive computer or tables demand you surface or slow your ascent. That single benefit is what drives the widespread adoption of nitrox across the recreational diving world.

## Why Less Nitrogen Changes Everything

The mechanism behind extended bottom time is straightforward once you understand decompression theory. Nitrogen dissolved in your blood and tissues is inert — your body neither uses it nor processes it, so it accumulates with every minute you spend at depth. At some point, the dissolved load exceeds what you can safely off-gas during a normal ascent, and a mandatory decompression stop becomes unavoidable. Recreational diving is defined by the absence of mandatory stops, so the goal is always to stay within the no-decompression limit.

On air at 30 metres, a no-decompression limit of around 20 minutes is typical, depending on your dive computer's algorithm. Switch to EAN32, and that same algorithm may grant you 30 minutes or more at the same depth — a substantial increase that matters enormously on a reef wall, a wreck, or anywhere the interesting features are concentrated in the 25 to 35 metre range.

A second benefit, harder to quantify but frequently mentioned by regular nitrox divers, is reduced post-dive fatigue. Whether this is physiological — a genuine result of lower nitrogen loading — or partly psychological remains debated, but experienced divers on multiple-dive liveaboard trips often prefer nitrox specifically because they feel sharper on the fourth dive of the day.

## The Oxygen Toxicity Trade-Off

Nothing in diving comes without a trade-off, and nitrox is no exception. Oxygen under pressure becomes toxic, and the higher your fraction of oxygen, the shallower the depth at which toxicity becomes a concern.

The critical number is the partial pressure of oxygen, expressed in bar. Most recreational and agency guidelines set a maximum operating depth at a partial pressure of 1.4 bar, with 1.6 bar as an absolute ceiling for brief exposures. For EAN32, the maximum operating depth works out to around 34 metres. For EAN36, it drops to approximately 28 metres.

Central nervous system oxygen toxicity at these thresholds can manifest without much warning. Symptoms may include visual disturbances, ear ringing, nausea, twitching, and, most dangerously, a convulsion. Underwater, an oxygen convulsion is almost always fatal because the regulator is lost. This is not a theoretical hazard — it is a real physiological limit that must be respected.

The implication is clear: nitrox extends your bottom time at moderate depths but restricts your maximum depth. Using EAN36 on a dive to 35 metres is not just inadvisable; it exceeds the maximum operating depth and exposes you to serious risk. Before every nitrox dive, you must calculate or confirm your maximum operating depth and set it on your dive computer.

## Analysing and Labelling Your Cylinder

Because oxygen toxicity limits are non-negotiable, every nitrox diver is expected to analyse their own cylinder before diving, even when a trusted fill station has labelled the tank. The analysis takes about 30 seconds with a handheld oxygen analyser: you purge a small amount of gas past the sensor and read the percentage.

Once confirmed, the mix fraction is written on a tank band or label and initialled by the diver. This is not bureaucratic fussiness — it protects you from a transposition error, a mislabelled valve, or a fill that was blended slightly off-spec. A diver who does not know their actual oxygen fraction cannot set a correct maximum operating depth.

Your dive computer also needs to know the mix. Modern dive computers have nitrox modes that let you input the oxygen percentage; the computer then adjusts its decompression algorithm and will alarm if you exceed the maximum operating depth. Some computers handle multiple gas switches, which matters for technical divers using nitrox as a bottom gas and a richer decompression mix.

## Getting Certified for Nitrox

PADI, SSI, BSAC, NAUI and every major training agency offer enriched air certifications, and almost all of them can be completed without any additional open-water dives. The course is primarily academic: you study the theory of gas laws, oxygen toxicity, no-decompression extension, and how to analyse and label cylinders. There is usually a knowledge review or exam, followed by a practical cylinder analysis session.

The entire process can be completed in a single day, and the card you earn is one of the most universally recognised specialty certifications in recreational diving. Many liveaboards and dive centres in nitrox-rich destinations — the Red Sea, the Coral Triangle, the Maldives — either assume you have it or offer it as a pre-departure online course.

It is worth completing the certification before a dedicated dive trip rather than scrambling to do it on arrival. Having the academic foundation means you understand what you are doing when you set your computer, not just following instructions.

## Where Nitrox Makes the Most Difference

The greatest gains from nitrox come in the 18 to 30 metre range, where recreational bottom times are meaningful but nitrogen loading accumulates quickly. On a shallow reef at 10 metres, the no-decompression limit on air is already very long — probably beyond your gas supply — so nitrox adds nothing. At 40 metres, EAN32 is past its maximum operating depth, so you should not be using it at all.

The sweet spot is that mid-water band: the 22 metre plateau on a coral wall, the 28 metre deck of a wreck, the 25 metre cleaning station where reef mantas circle. These are exactly the depths where an extra ten or fifteen minutes transforms a rushed glimpse into a genuinely satisfying dive.

Multiple-dive days amplify the advantage further. On a liveaboard doing four dives a day in the 25 to 30 metre range, cumulative nitrogen loading on air builds quickly. Nitrox creates more breathing room between dives and compresses the interval you need before the next descent.

## Practical Logistics

Nitrox fills cost more than air fills — how much depends on the destination and whether the centre blends partial pressure or uses a membrane system. In established dive destinations, the premium is usually modest: a few euros or dollars per fill. Factor it into your daily budget rather than treating it as an optional luxury.

Not every dive centre offers nitrox everywhere in the world. Remote locations, developing dive destinations, and smaller inland sites may have no membrane or cascade system for enriched air production. If your travel plans depend on nitrox, check availability before you book.

Tank markings vary slightly by region, but a green and yellow band on the cylinder shoulder has become the near-universal indicator that a tank contains enriched air rather than air. Never assume a tank is air just because it lacks a nitrox band — always analyse.
