## How You Choose to Look

A dive site is not a fixed experience. Two divers entering the same water can have completely different encounters depending on where they direct their attention. One diver hovers at five metres on a black-sand slope, moving thirty centimetres at a time, spending fifteen minutes on a single patch of sediment cataloguing the animals hiding in it. Another diver is twenty metres away, positioned at the corner of a reef, watching a school of several hundred bumphead parrotfish roll through the blue water above the wall.

This is the macro-versus-wide-angle divide, and it shapes not just what you look for on a dive, but the gear you carry, the destinations you prioritise, and the skills you develop as an underwater observer.

## The Macro World

Macro diving means getting close to small subjects — very close, often within a few centimetres. In photography terms, it means using a lens or wet-mount dioptre that renders a small subject large on the sensor. In pure observational terms, it means training your eye to notice things that most divers walk (or fin) past without seeing: a pair of painted frogfish on an orange sponge, a ribbon eel showing just its head from a sandy burrow, a ghost pipefish hovering vertically among feather stars, a bobtail squid barely larger than a thumbnail buried in sediment with only its eyes exposed.

The skills macro diving demands are different from standard reef navigation. Buoyancy must be precise enough to hold a position within centimetres of a substrate without touching it, for extended periods. This is harder than it sounds; even a small breath changes your depth by a centimetre or two. Fin technique must avoid disturbing sediment. A macro diver who stirs up the bottom silt not only obscures their subject but damages the benthic environment and ruins the visibility for everyone behind them.

The rewards are disproportionate to the apparent humbleness of the subjects. Nudibranch diversity on a single muck slope in Lembeh Strait, Indonesia, can rival or exceed the total species count of an entire tropical reef. The Coral Triangle — spanning Indonesia, the Philippines, and Papua New Guinea — hosts the highest density of small cryptic species in the world. Anilao in the Philippines, Tulamben in Bali, and Mabul in Malaysian Borneo are pilgrimage destinations for macro divers precisely because the species density and rarity of their benthic communities is unmatched.

Preferred macro habitats include muck slopes (soft, often black volcanic sediment with rubble), seafan gardens where pygmy seahorses shelter, deep sponge walls hosting crinoids and shrimp commensals, shallow rubble zones full of cryptic scorpionfish and flatheads, and the underside of coral heads where small crabs and cowries live. The density of macro subjects is often inversely related to the visual appeal of the site from above: the glamorous-looking coral garden often has fewer unique macro finds than the featureless-looking muck slope nearby.

## The Wide-Angle World

Wide-angle diving operates at the other extreme: big animals, big reefs, big formations, and the open water column. The focus is on subjects that require distance for proper perspective — a manta ray with a three-metre wingspan needs to be far enough away for the frame to show the full span, the context of the water around it, and perhaps the reef below. A school of hammerhead sharks circling a seamount needs an angle wide enough to show the school's scale against the water column.

Wide-angle subjects include large pelagics (sharks, rays, turtles, whale sharks), dense schools of fish, dramatic reef topography (walls, pinnacles, arches, caverns), wrecks, and open-water encounters in the blue. The destinations that define wide-angle diving are those where these subjects and formations are reliably present: Palau's Blue Corner for shark aggregations and Napoleon wrasse in extraordinary density, the Coral Sea's Osprey Reef for hammerheads in the blue water, Socorro Islands in Mexico for manta rays of unusual size and curiosity, the Similan Islands for the famous Elephant Head rock formation and its schooling jacks.

The physical skills wide-angle diving demands differ from macro. Current management becomes critical — reading and using current to position yourself at the cleaning station, the corner, or the school without fighting the water. Many prime wide-angle sites are high-current environments; Blue Corner, German Channel in Palau, and Richelieu Rock in Thailand all require divers comfortable with strong water movement. Reef hook deployment — clipping yourself to a rock spur in a legally permissible way to hold position in current — is a learned technique specific to wide-angle drift diving on exposed sites.

## Gear Considerations

In photography, the lens choice dictates everything else. A macro lens for an underwater housing — a 60mm, 100mm, or 105mm prime, or a wet-mount dioptre on a compact camera — renders small subjects large and requires close approach. A wide-angle rectilinear lens — a 16-35mm or 10-17mm fisheye — is used close to large subjects or scenes, and requires proximity even for wide shots because water absorbs light and colour; shooting wide from three metres away in water gives a flat, blue-tinted image, while shooting a manta ray from fifty centimetres with a fisheye produces the intimacy and colour richness that characterises the best underwater wide-angle work.

Lighting differs too. Macro subjects require a single small strobe or twin strobes positioned very close to the subject, or a video light for stationary subjects. Wide-angle work with strobes requires powerful units positioned to light the foreground subject while allowing the background — reef, water column, ambient light — to expose correctly on the sensor. The dual-strobe, wide-angle setup used at Blue Corner or on a manta ray requires significantly more hardware than a single small macro strobe.

Divers who do not photograph at all experience the macro/wide-angle distinction differently, but the observational mode still applies. Where do you look? How do you fin? How long do you spend at a single location? Macro divers in the non-photographic sense are naturalists who fin slowly and look carefully. Wide-angle divers are more spatial in their movement, covering more ground and placing themselves for encounters with mobile or large animals.

## Choosing Destinations

Muck diving destinations — Lembeh, Anilao, Ambon Bay, Milne Bay in Papua New Guinea, Mabul — are the macro pilgrimages. The Coral Triangle generally is macro-rich by default; even on reef systems there, the density of unusual invertebrates and small fish is extraordinary.

Wide-angle destinations are built around topography, current, and the pelagic species those conditions attract. Palau, the Red Sea's outer reefs, the Maldives' current channels, the Similan Islands and Burma Banks, Cocos Island and the Revillagigedo Archipelago are where wide-angle photography and observation come into their own.

Many serious divers do both, and the choice between them on any given dive is partly site-driven and partly a decision about how you want to use your attention. [Open the map](/map) to browse dive sites by region and find destinations that match your preferred mode of seeing the underwater world.

The most rewarding approach, particularly for divers building long-term experience, is to develop genuine proficiency in both. A diver who can find a mimic octopus on a muck slope and then hold position in the current at a shark cleaning station is seeing the reef in its full dimension — from the smallest cryptic invertebrate to the largest pelagic visitor.
