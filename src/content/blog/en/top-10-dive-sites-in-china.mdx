China's dive industry is concentrated almost entirely in three distinct areas: the tropical waters around Hainan Island in the south, the subtropical reefs of Weizhou Island in the Beibu Gulf, and Wuzhizhou Island off the Hainan coast. Beyond these established sites, China has a vast coastline extending thousands of kilometres to the north, but the cooler, murkier waters of the Yellow Sea, Bohai Bay, and East China Sea support limited recreational dive development. The story of Chinese diving is largely the story of Hainan: the island province has positioned itself as China's dive gateway, with Sanya in the south as the operational hub and a growing number of liveaboard operations extending to the remote reefs of the South China Sea. Water temperatures in the tropical south range from 24°C in winter to 30°C in summer; visibility varies considerably between sites.

## 1. Yalong Bay, Sanya, Hainan

Yalong Bay is the most accessible reef system on Hainan's southern coast, a crescent-shaped bay backed by the resort hotels that have made Sanya China's domestic beach tourism capital. The diving here is not pristine — the bay has suffered from the development pressure of a rapidly expanding resort zone — but the coral heads in the outer bay retain reasonable biological diversity, and the site serves as an introduction to tropical diving for the enormous domestic Chinese dive market. Depths to around 15 metres. Fish populations are lower than on the more remote Hainan sites but still include the characteristic species of the northern South China Sea: various grouper, parrotfish, and abundant reef fish of the tropical Indo-Pacific assemblage.

## 2. Wuzhizhou Island (Five Finger Island), Northeast of Sanya

Wuzhizhou is the star of Hainan diving — a small island protected as a national marine park about 30 kilometres northeast of Sanya, accessible by a 15-minute speedboat ride. The island's protected status means its reefs have recovered significantly from earlier degradation, and diving here reveals the kind of coral health that is genuinely impressive by Chinese standards. Depths range from 5 to around 25 metres on the accessible sites. The north side of the island has the best hard coral coverage; the south side sees stronger currents and larger fish aggregations, with schools of snapper and trevally common. Sea turtles are regularly sighted. Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper, current-exposed southern sites.

## 3. Sanya Bay Wrecks, Southwest of Sanya

Several deliberate artificial reef sinkings have been carried out in Sanya Bay to provide dive sites and reduce pressure on the natural reefs. The wrecks, ranging from small patrol vessels to larger steel hulls, sit in depths of 18 to 30 metres and have accumulated meaningful coral and fish growth. They function as genuine dive attractions in their own right rather than purely as crowd-management tools. The most established of these wrecks carries good sponge coverage and resident populations of grouper and moray eels. Open Water divers can access the shallowest sections; Advanced is recommended for the deeper hulls and hold penetrations.

## 4. Weizhou Island, Beibu Gulf, Guangxi

Weizhou Island in the Beibu Gulf is technically outside the tropics but benefits from the warm northern extension of the South China Sea. The island is a volcanic remnant with hard basalt rock foundations, and the reefs that have established themselves on this substrate have a different character from Hainan's coral-reef topography. Depth range is modest — the accessible dive sites run to around 20 metres. The island is known for its whale shark and manta ray sightings in certain seasons (typically late summer), which have made it something of a bucket-list destination for Chinese divers. The visibility here is generally poorer than at Hainan sites due to the more turbid Beibu Gulf water, but on calm days it can reach 10 to 15 metres.

## 3. Yongle Atoll (Xuande Atoll Area), Paracel Islands

The remote atolls of the South China Sea — claimed and administered by China — are accessible from Hainan to liveaboard divers with the appropriate permits. The Paracel Islands include reef systems at Yongle Atoll and surrounding structures that have seen minimal recreational diving pressure and retain coral and fish populations rarely seen on the mainland-accessible sites. Depth ranges from the surface to dramatic outer wall drops. Large pelagic fish — manta rays, various shark species, and schools of barracuda — are reported on these outer atoll dives. Access is complex and requires coordination through established Chinese liveaboard operators, but for those able to arrange it, the diving is genuinely world-class for the South China Sea region.

## 5. Meisha, Shenzhen, Guangdong

For divers based in the Pearl River Delta, Meisha near Shenzhen provides the closest accessible marine diving. The site is not tropical in the Hainan sense — water temperatures can drop to 17°C in winter — and visibility is affected by the turbid coastal water of the South China Sea's northern margin. However, the rocky reef structures at the eastern end of Dapeng Bay support a reasonable fish population, including some interesting cold-temperate species not seen further south. This is a site for local divers and those wanting to explore what recreational diving means in the context of China's most densely developed coastal region.

## 6. Sanya Coral Reef National Marine Nature Reserve

The formal protected area encompassing much of the reef system south and east of Sanya represents the best-managed coral environment in mainland Chinese waters. Within the reserve boundaries, coral health is noticeably better than on unprotected reefs, and enforcement of no-anchor and no-take rules has made a measurable difference. The accessible dive sites within the reserve include the Luhuitou area — named for the deer head rock formation above the surface — where depths of 8 to 20 metres carry hard coral in reasonable health and the resident fish population includes large Napoleon wrasse. Open Water appropriate.

## 7. Dongdao (East Island), Xisha (Paracel) Group

One of the outer Paracel islands accessible to liveaboard operations, Dongdao has a significant seabird nesting colony above the surface and a reef system below that has seen very little dive pressure. The remoteness translates directly to fish density: grouper populations are at levels rarely seen in the more frequently visited South China Sea reefs. The topography includes both shallow reef flats and steeper walls on the leeward sides of the island. Conditions can be challenging in the summer typhoon season; the most reliable window for liveaboard access is March through May and October through December.

## 8. Zhongye Island, Spratly Group

The deep south of China's claimed maritime territory, in the Spratly Islands, hosts reef systems that are essentially unexplored for recreational diving. The few dive reports from these areas describe pristine coral, extraordinary fish density, and visibility exceeding 40 metres. Access is logistically and politically complicated and is not a standard tourist dive operation. For the purposes of recreational diving, these sites exist more as a theoretical frontier than a practical destination; they are mentioned here because they represent the outer limit of what Chinese-administered waters offer to diving exploration.

## 9. Haikou Bay Reefs, Northern Hainan

The reefs off Haikou, Hainan's provincial capital on the north coast, represent a different character from the southern resort sites. The water is slightly cooler in winter and occasionally turbid from mainland river runoff, but the less-visited reef systems north of the island have seen lower diver traffic and support fish populations including humphead wrasse and bumphead parrotfish at sites where these species have been locally extirpated further south. Conditions require checking; dive operations based in Haikou are small but knowledgeable.

## 10. Lingshui Reef Area, Southeast Hainan

The southeast coast of Hainan between Lingshui Bay and the Qinghai area has reef systems that are less developed for tourism than the Sanya coast and consequently less impacted. Shore diving is possible at some points; boat trips to the outer reef sections show coral health that is noticeably better than the busier resort-adjacent sites. This is increasingly a target for Chinese dive operators looking to develop new sites away from the Sanya bottleneck.

## Planning a Diving Trip to China

Hainan diving is at its best from October through May, when the Northeast Monsoon has eased and the rainy season has not yet begun. July and August bring typhoon risk that can shut down operations for days at a time. The Sanya dive industry is large, professionally run, and geared primarily toward the domestic Chinese market — English-language services exist but are not universal. Nitrox is available from the larger operators. Liveaboard operations to the Paracel Islands run on set schedules and require advance booking.
