The reef you know by day becomes a different planet after sunset. The fish that schooled in the open have wedged themselves into crevices; the corals have unfurled their feeding tentacles; hunters that hid all day are out on the prowl. And somewhere in the dark, if you turn off your torch and stay still, the water itself begins to answer your movements with sparks of cold blue light. Night diving is where the ocean reveals its strangest face, and bioluminescence is the show that no daytime dive can match.

## What Bioluminescence Actually Is

Bioluminescence is light produced by living organisms through a chemical reaction. A molecule called luciferin reacts with oxygen, catalysed by an enzyme called luciferase, and the energy released is emitted almost entirely as light with virtually no heat — which is why it is called cold light. The result is overwhelmingly blue-green, the wavelength that travels furthest through seawater, although some deep-sea animals produce red light invisible to most of their neighbours.

It is astonishingly common. The majority of animals in the open ocean, by some estimates more than three quarters of them, can produce light. In the deep sea, where sunlight never reaches, it is the dominant form of illumination. Organisms use it for everything imaginable: to attract mates, to lure prey, to startle or blind predators, to communicate, and to camouflage themselves against the faint light from above by matching it from below, a trick called counter-illumination.

## The Sparks in the Water: Dinoflagellates

The most accessible bioluminescence for divers and swimmers comes from dinoflagellates, single-celled plankton that flash when the water around them is disturbed. A wave of the hand, a kick of the fin, a fish darting past — each triggers a burst of blue sparks. The leading hypothesis is that the flash is a burglar alarm: it startles the small grazers trying to eat the plankton, and may attract the larger predators that eat those grazers in turn.

Certain places concentrate these organisms into permanent displays. The bioluminescent bays of Puerto Rico, especially Mosquito Bay on Vieques, glow so brightly that every fish leaves a comet trail. Luminous Lagoon in Jamaica, the waters around the Maldives at certain times of year, and Toyama Bay in Japan with its firefly squid are all famous. For a diver, the magic needs no special site: on a dark night, far from artificial light, simply switching off the torch and moving an arm through the water is often enough.

## The Creatures That Glow

Beyond the plankton, a whole cast of larger animals carries its own light. Comb jellies, or ctenophores, drift through the water rippling with rainbow light along rows of beating cilia — much of that shimmer is the scattering of your torch, but many also produce true bioluminescence in blue when touched. Some jellyfish glow. Certain brittle stars detach a glowing arm to distract a predator while the rest of the animal escapes.

The deep sea is where light becomes a way of life. The anglerfish dangles a glowing lure, lit by symbiotic bacteria, in front of its jaws. The flashlight fish of the Indo-Pacific carries pouches of luminous bacteria beneath its eyes and blinks them on and off by rolling them out of sight, schooling at sites like Banda in Indonesia where night divers gather specifically to watch their flickering. Lanternfish, hatchetfish and countless others wear rows of light organs along their bellies.

## Blackwater Diving: The Frontier

The most extreme way to meet these animals is blackwater diving — drifting at night in open ocean over very deep water, suspended beneath a downline hung with lights, watching the greatest migration on Earth pass by. Every night, untold billions of small animals rise from the deep toward the surface to feed in the dark, then sink again before dawn, in what is called the diel vertical migration. A blackwater diver hangs in the middle of it.

What rises is a parade of the bizarre: transparent larval fish, juvenile cephalopods, pelagic snails that swim with wing-like feet, siphonophores that string themselves out for metres, and larval crustaceans riding on jellyfish. Many are juveniles whose adult forms live in the depths and are almost never seen. Blackwater diving has become a global pursuit, with hotspots off Kona in Hawaii, Anilao in the Philippines, and Palm Beach in Florida, and it has even turned up species new to science.

## Diving at Night Safely

Night diving demands discipline more than special bravery. The single most important rule is to dive a site you already know by day, so the topography holds no surprises in the dark. Carry a primary torch and at least one backup, and agree clear light signals with your buddy before you descend — a slow circle for OK, a fast back-and-forth for attention. Stay close together; in the dark, a buddy a few metres away can vanish completely.

Move slowly and deliberately. Disorientation is the main hazard, so keep track of your depth and your bubbles, which always rise toward the surface. To experience bioluminescence properly, the group cups their torches against their bodies or switches them off for a minute, letting the eyes adjust until the living sparks become visible. Mind your buoyancy near the bottom, because silt stirred up in the dark can blind the whole group, and never shine a torch directly into another diver's eyes or hold a beam too long on light-sensitive creatures.

## Why the Dark Is Worth It

Many divers resist the first night dive and never look back after it. The reef at night is not merely the day reef with the lights off; it is a genuine shift change, a different community of animals living a different life. Decorator crabs and basket stars emerge, parrotfish sleep wrapped in cocoons of mucus, hunting morays and lionfish use a diver's torch beam as a spotlight for their prey, and the corals themselves come alive with feeding polyps.

And then there is the light. To hover weightless in true darkness, switch everything off, and watch your own movement trail blue fire through the water is one of the few experiences that genuinely cannot be photographed or described into someone who has not felt it. It is the closest most people will come to swimming through the night sky.
