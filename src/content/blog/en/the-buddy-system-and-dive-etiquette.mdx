## Why the Buddy System Exists

Scuba diving presents a category of potential problems that a lone diver cannot solve. An out-of-air emergency 20 metres down is fatal without a buddy and manageable with one. A regulator malfunction that requires a controlled alternate-air-source ascent is an emergency protocol that specifically requires two people. Entanglement in fishing line at depth, a cramp during a long kick against current, losing your orientation in reduced visibility — all of these scenarios have survivable outcomes when a buddy is present and unmanageable alone.

The buddy system is not merely a certification-agency policy. It is the minimum organisational unit of safe diving, derived from decades of incident analysis showing that a disproportionate number of fatalities involve divers who were alone at the moment of their emergency. Solo diving exists as a specialty — it requires additional training, equipment redundancy, and a deliberate acknowledgment of the changed risk profile — but it is not the default for good reason.

The practical argument for buddy diving is also simpler than the safety one: diving is more enjoyable with a companion. Pointing out a nudibranch, signalling excitement at a shark passing through, surfacing to share what you just saw — these are fundamental parts of the experience.

## Pre-Dive Checks: The BWRAF System

A buddy system only functions if both divers have working equipment and both divers know how the other's equipment works. The pre-dive check is the mechanism for ensuring both conditions are met.

The most widely taught pre-dive check sequence is BWRAF, given various mnemonics across agencies — 'Bruce Willis Ruins All Films' being among the more memorable. The letters stand for BCD, Weights, Releases, Air, and Final check.

BCD: inflate and deflate to confirm the BC is functioning and properly fitted. Check that the inflator hose is connected. Note where the alternate inflator is on your buddy's kit.

Weights: confirm the weight system is correctly loaded and that the quick-release mechanism is accessible and operable. Know how to release your buddy's weights in an emergency — weight belt buckles, integrated weight pockets, and ditchable trim weights all release differently. You must be able to operate your buddy's system without fumbling.

Releases: run through all fastening points — shoulder clips, cummerbund, chest strap. Confirm each is secure, and confirm you know how to release each one. An unconscious diver on the surface needs their BCD deflated and possibly their equipment removed; knowing how to do this quickly is not a detail.

Air: open the cylinder valve fully, then back a quarter turn. Confirm adequate pressure for the planned dive. Breathe from your buddy's regulator to confirm it delivers gas properly. Note where the alternate second stage — the octopus — is mounted on your buddy's equipment.

Final check: masks, fins, computers activated and set, dive flag deployed if required, entry conditions reviewed one last time.

This sequence takes two to three minutes with a familiar buddy. It is not bureaucratic overhead — it is the physical act of making your equipment known to another person who may need to operate it in an emergency.

## Staying Together Underwater

Buddy separation accounts for a significant fraction of diving incidents. Two divers enter the water together and surface 40 minutes later having had essentially solo dives — they drifted apart in the first five minutes and neither made a sustained effort to close the gap. In good conditions, this often produces no consequence beyond a missed experience. In reduced visibility, strong current, or at night, the same separation can develop into a genuine emergency for one or both divers.

The standard to aim for is staying within arm's reach at all times in confined spaces and within visual range — generally accepted as no more than a few metres in good conditions — in open water. In current, leading and following positions are reversed from instinct: the stronger swimmer leads slightly upcurrent, reducing the risk of the less powerful swimmer being swept away.

When one diver wants to ascend — low on gas, cold, or simply finished — the dive ends for both. This is not a courtesy; it is a protocol. A buddy who surfaces to wait while the other continues the dive has ceased to function as a buddy for the duration.

## Signal Vocabulary

Hand signals form a basic communication language underwater. The universally understood core signals are: OK (thumb and forefinger forming a circle, or flat hand rocked side to side at the surface), not OK (flat palm facing down, rocked side to side), and ascend or descend (thumb up, thumb down). The distinction between thumb-up as 'ascend' and the colloquial 'thumbs up' meaning approval causes occasional confusion for new divers — in diving, a thumb up means the dive is ending.

Additional standard signals include: something wrong / problem (flat hand rocked down), out of air (horizontal knife hand drawn across the throat), equalisation problem (finger pointing to ear), and the request for buddy air (pointing to regulator, then pointing at buddy's alternate). Many dive centres add local signals for specific marine life common to their sites.

Torch signals matter at night or in poor visibility. A slow circle with a torch beam means OK. Rapid side-to-side movement is an attention-getting distress signal. Pointing a torch beam directly at a buddy's eyes is rude and temporarily blinding.

Slates — small white boards with a pencil attached — extend communication beyond signal vocabulary for anything complex: depth changes, navigation decisions, pointing out a location on an underwater map. Dive computers with digital consoles can also show depth and time to a buddy at a glance.

## Etiquette on the Reef and on the Boat

Good dive etiquette extends beyond the buddy relationship. On the reef, the principles are those of responsible diving: hover above, touch nothing, keep fins well clear of the substrate. Underwater photography etiquette adds a dimension — photographers who stop abruptly in a passage, block other divers' views, or subject a marine animal to prolonged harassment for a shot create frustration and sometimes damage. Taking a photograph should never take priority over the experience of other divers around you or the welfare of the subject.

On a dive boat, etiquette is partly about safety and partly about shared space. Gearing up efficiently without spreading equipment across communal areas, briefing the boat crew if you have any medical condition they should know about, and following the crew's instructions on entry and exit timing are basic requirements. Taking the longest surface interval before complaining about the wait is not.

## Buddy Briefings Before the Dive

The pre-dive buddy check is often reduced to a mechanical equipment inspection, but the most useful buddy briefings go further. Before entering the water at an unfamiliar site, a thorough buddy discussion should cover the dive plan: maximum depth, planned route, turn pressure for the ascent, and what to do if separated. Agreeing in advance that separation means surfacing independently if the buddy cannot be found within one minute removes ambiguity from a situation that produces stress and poor decisions.

Dive guide briefings at reputable operations will cover all of this, but they address the whole group. A buddy discussion is the opportunity to agree on personal thresholds: a diver who has had a recent ear squeeze will turn shallower than planned; a diver early in their post-certification career may want to agree a shallower maximum depth than the site's stated maximum. These are conversations between two people that protect both of them.

A final dimension of buddy briefing is sharing relevant health information. If one diver had a cold last week and is not sure their sinuses will equalize cleanly, or if one diver is on medication with any potential diving interaction, the buddy should know. This is not oversharing — it is providing your safety partner with the context they need to recognise when something is going wrong.
