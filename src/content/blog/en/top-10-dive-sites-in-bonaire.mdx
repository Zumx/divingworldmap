Bonaire may be the purest shore-diving destination on the planet. The island — a flat, arid Dutch special municipality off the Venezuelan coast — established one of the world's first marine parks in 1979, and the decades of protection that followed have produced a reef system that is measurably healthier than most Caribbean alternatives. The west coast is sheltered by the island itself from the trade winds that constantly push in from the east, creating a calm lee with dive access directly from the road: stone cairns mark each site at the water's edge, tanks can be strapped to a pickup truck, and a diver can do four or five sites in a day without ever boarding a boat. Water temperature holds between 26°C and 29°C throughout the year. Visibility typically runs 25 to 40 metres on the better sites.

## 1. Bari Reef, Kralendijk Waterfront

Directly in front of the Sand Dollar Condominium resort on the Kralendijk waterfront, Bari Reef is one of the most dived sites in Bonaire and yet consistently rewards repeated visits. The reef structure here is classic double-reef: a shallow upper reef crest at around 6 metres gives way to a sand terrace at 12 metres, and the main reef wall drops from there to beyond 40 metres. Hard coral coverage is excellent by Caribbean standards. Seahorses have been found in the sea grass on the sand terrace; flying gurnards appear on the sandy patches between coral heads. The proximity to accommodation makes early-morning and night dives easy. Open Water certification sufficient for most sections.

## 2. Salt Pier, South of Kralendijk

The Salt Pier is an active commercial loading facility — cruise ships and cargo vessels tie up here — and diving it requires checking that no ship is in port. When the pier is free, the columns supporting the structure are among the most photographed subjects in Bonaire's waters. Each pillar is encrusted from the waterline to the sandy bottom (around 9 metres) with layers of corals, sponges, and tunicates in every colour, and the fish density under the pier is extraordinary: large schools of French grunt, blue-striped grunt, and smallmouth grunt pack the spaces between the columns, and spotted eagle rays cruise the sandy bottom nearby. Open Water level; depth is shallow throughout.

## 3. 1000 Steps, North Bonaire

The name is an exaggeration — there are 67 steps cut into the limestone cliff — but the site itself lives up to the drama. A long-established classic on the north shore, 1000 Steps is a shore-entry wall dive that begins just below the surface and drops to depths exceeding 40 metres. The upper reef sections carry excellent hard coral coverage with brain corals and star corals dominating. Larger marine life is more common here than on the busier southern sites: hawksbill turtles are reliably sighted, and tarpon patrol the reef edge. The steps entry is not difficult but requires carrying a tank up the same steps after the dive — a fact that selects for motivated divers. Advanced recommended for the deeper wall.

## 4. Klein Bonaire, Northwest Side

Klein Bonaire is an uninhabited small island directly opposite Kralendijk, accessible by water taxi in about five minutes. The island has no fresh water and no human habitation, and diving the full perimeter of its western and northern sides is a full-day liveaboard-style project accessible from Bonaire. Sites including No Name Beach, Forest, and Knife are the most consistently excellent, with near-pristine coral, very high fish density, and the clearest water around Bonaire. No shore diving here — boat access only. Sea turtles, hawksbill and green, are more commonly seen on Klein Bonaire than anywhere else in the island group. Advanced Open Water for deeper sections; intermediate sites suit Open Water.

## 5. Hilma Hooker Wreck, South Shore

The Hilma Hooker is a freighter with a chequered past — it was found carrying a hidden cargo of 25,000 kilograms of marijuana in 1984 and, while the legal proceedings were underway, sank at its anchor due to poor maintenance. Bonaire authorities eventually allowed it to settle permanently as an artificial reef. The wreck is now one of the Caribbean's best: it lies on its starboard side in 15 to 30 metres of water, intact and accessible, with the hull sides and superstructure well colonised. Large schools of horse-eye jack aggregate above the wreck, and the deeper port-side sections carry significant soft coral and sponge growth. Advanced Open Water recommended for full exploration.

## 6. Something Special, Near Lac Bay

Something Special is a muck-diving site — one of the few on Bonaire — where the sandy, seagrass-dominated bottom between depths of 5 and 18 metres rewards the patient macro photographer. Seahorses are reliably found here; so are flying gurnards, frogfish in several colour morphs, and a variety of nudibranchs. The site is less visually dramatic than the wall dives of the west coast but offers a distinct style of diving. Night dives here are productive, with octopus actively hunting on the sand and various crustaceans emerging from the seagrass. Open Water certification sufficient; calm, settled buoyancy is more important than certification level.

## 7. Karpata, North Shore

Karpata is considered one of Bonaire's premier wall dives — the reef is among the healthiest on the island, the fish life is prolific, and the topography is varied enough to sustain multiple dives without repetition. Large brain corals mark the upper reef crest at around 6 metres; the wall then plunges past 40 metres with black coral and deep-water gorgonians on the deeper sections. Green turtles are resident. Tarpon up to 1.5 metres in length are commonly encountered in the afternoon. The shore entry requires some navigation over a rocky intertidal zone; booties are essential. Advanced recommended for the wall sections below 25 metres.

## 8. Lac Bay East, Windward Side

The eastern coast of Bonaire faces the open Caribbean and is rarely dived due to the consistent trade wind swell, but Lac Bay — a large, sheltered lagoon on the windward side — is the exception. The bay's seagrass meadows and mangrove fringe support a distinct community: juvenile fish of numerous species, stingrays resting on the sandy bottom, and the occasional seahorse in the seagrass. This is snorkelling territory as much as scuba territory, with depths rarely exceeding 5 metres in the main areas of interest. The windsurfing community has made Lac Bay famous; divers are a minority here but welcome.

## 9. Oil Slick Leap, Central West Shore

Oil Slick Leap is a classic wall site on the central section of the west shore, known for the dramatic shore entry from the concrete quay — you leap in and descend immediately to the wall. The reef begins just below the surface and the wall drops quickly to recreational limits and beyond. Soft corals and sponges dominate the upper sections; black coral appears below 25 metres. Schools of Creole wrasse are a constant companion on the descent; eagle rays are sometimes seen in the blue water away from the wall. A second entry point a short swim away allows a different angle on the same site.

## 10. Town Pier, Kralendijk

The Town Pier is a working pier in the centre of Kralendijk, and diving under it requires a permit and is restricted to night diving to avoid interference with boat traffic. The permit is worth obtaining: the pier pillars are more heavily encrusted than even Salt Pier, and the concentration of marine life under the structure at night is extraordinary. Frogfish in multiple colour variations — yellow, orange, red, black — are reliably found; so are flamingo tongue cowries, Christmas tree worms, and the full range of small Caribbean reef fish. A guided night dive here with a knowledgeable local divemaster is one of Bonaire's signature experiences.

## Planning a Diving Trip to Bonaire

Bonaire dives well all year, with no pronounced bad season. October and November bring brief periods of increased wind, but the leeward shore remains diveable throughout. The marine park fee is mandatory for all divers and snorkellers and funds conservation and enforcement. Equipment rentals are widely available, but Bonaire regulars typically bring their own gear. Many divers hire a vehicle and spend mornings doing two or three self-guided shore dives, with boat trips to Klein Bonaire for afternoon or full-day excursions. Nitrox is available from all major operators.
