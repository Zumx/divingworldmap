On 8 May 1902, the volcano Mont Pelée erupted with catastrophic violence, destroying the city of Saint-Pierre in minutes and killing an estimated 30,000 people. The eruption simultaneously created one of the world's most remarkable wreck dive collections: in the harbour of Saint-Pierre, at least fifteen ships were sunk by the eruption's shock wave, pyroclastic flows, and the tsunami that followed. That single geological event made Martinique famous in wreck diving circles long before the island developed its reef diving infrastructure. Today, Saint-Pierre wrecks and the broader reef system across the island — protected partly by Martinique Regional Natural Park — attract divers who come for the history and stay for the coral.

## 1. The Roraima, Saint-Pierre

The Roraima is the most visited of Saint-Pierre's wrecks: a Canadian steamship of 123 metres resting on its starboard side at 50 to 55 metres, a challenging dive requiring Advanced certification and ideally Nitrox. The shallower structures — the bridge wing and upper deck railings — sit at around 40 metres, still accessible to confident Advanced divers. Coral and sponge encrustation over 120-plus years has transformed every surface. The Roraima's great advantage over deeper Saint-Pierre wrecks is that photographs capture it at the edge of recreational depth limits. Large grouper and barracuda school around the superstructure. Current at this exposed anchorage site can be significant; dive on a calm day with a reliable operator.

## 2. The Tamaya, Saint-Pierre

One of the more accessible Saint-Pierre wrecks, the Tamaya — a small French sailing schooner of around 30 metres — rests at 15 to 18 metres and suits Open Water divers comfortably. The shallow depth allows long bottom times, which is useful given the density of marine life here: seahorses hide in the rigging remnants, frogfish are regularly found on the hull, and the wreck's decomposed wooden structure has become an artificial reef supporting a population of cleaner wrasse and chromis. Sand-eel colonies surround the wreck perimeter. Excellent for macro photography.

## 3. The Gabrielle, Saint-Pierre

The Gabrielle was a three-masted French sailing vessel that went down in 1902 and now rests at 48 to 55 metres. As with the Roraima, the shallower extremities of the wreck — mast stumps and anchor chains — extend to around 30 to 35 metres and are reachable by Advanced divers. The ship's iron hull has fused with coral growth over more than a century into an almost organic-looking structure. Large schools of horse-eye jacks circulate around the wreck. The site requires a competent operator with solid mooring logistics; drifting off an unmoored Saint-Pierre wreck in uncertain current is a significant safety issue.

## 4. Diamond Rock (Rocher du Diamant)

Diamond Rock, the 176-metre basalt monolith that rises from the sea at Martinique's southwestern tip, was famously commissioned as a warship by the British Royal Navy in 1804 — giving it the distinction of being one of the few rocks in history to hold a naval commission. Underwater, the site lives up to the drama: walls drop from the surface to beyond 50 metres on the western face, with the upper sections at 10 to 25 metres accessible to Open Water divers. Large sea fans, black coral at depth, and a resident population of turtles and nurse sharks make Diamond Rock one of Martinique's finest reef dives. Current can be strong on the exposed faces; calm periods in early morning are best.

## 5. The Tamaya Night Dive, Saint-Pierre

The same Tamaya wreck transforms after dark into one of Martinique's best night dive experiences. Octopus emerge from the hull to hunt across the surrounding sand, lobster explore the open deck freely, and the bioluminescence of the plankton-rich Saint-Pierre Bay is often notable on the surface descent. Moray eels hunt more actively at night, threading through the wreck's decomposed structure. The shallow depth — 15 to 18 metres — makes this an ideal night dive for divers who are competent but haven't done extensive night diving. Operators in Saint-Pierre run regular night dive departures in summer months.

## 6. Cap Enragé, Atlantic Coast

On Martinique's wilder Atlantic coast, Cap Enragé offers a different character entirely from the sheltered Caribbean side. The exposed rocky cape creates a wall and boulder terrain from five to 30 metres, swept by Atlantic swell and current that concentrates pelagic species. Amberjack, tuna, and large barracuda are common; eagle rays pass through on the current. The site demands more diving experience than the calm Caribbean sites — surge on the shallower sections requires attentive buoyancy, and drift experience is useful on the outer point. Advanced Open Water certification is appropriate. Best from November through May when Atlantic conditions are comparatively predictable.

## 7. Rocher Gros Raisin, Le Marin

Off the southern tip of Martinique near Le Marin, Rocher Gros Raisin is a submerged pinnacle rising from 30 metres to six metres below the surface. The current funnelling around the pinnacle feeds dense growths of yellow and orange sea fans, and the site hosts one of the island's better populations of nurse sharks resting on the sandy base. Hawksbill turtles feed on the sponge growth on the pinnacle's upper sections. The site is accessible to competent Open Water divers on the upper pinnacle and to Advanced divers on the deeper sections. Best in calm conditions.

## 8. Anse Dufour Reef

The small fishing bay of Anse Dufour on Martinique's leeward coast hosts an easy reef dive at eight to 20 metres, known particularly for reliable turtle encounters and a photogenic coral garden that provides clear and colourful foregrounds for wide-angle photography. Local fishermen and dive operators share an understanding that has kept this reef in reasonable health despite its proximity to shore. Nurse sharks rest under coral overhangs at the reef's outer edge. This is a good site for less experienced divers and a pleasant afternoon dive complementing a more demanding morning site. Visibility typically runs to 15 metres.

## 9. Morne Rouge Reef, Near Saint-Pierre

A few kilometres south of Saint-Pierre on the Caribbean coast, Morne Rouge Reef is a natural reef system at eight to 22 metres that benefits from its proximity to the nutrient-rich waters around the historic anchorage. The site has seen progressively improving coral health over the past decade. Large brain corals and sea fans dominate the deeper sections; the shallower zone is a fish nursery of juveniles and cleaning stations. Groups of spotted drum — a rarely seen species — have been recorded here on multiple occasions. Open Water level for most of the site. Combines well with a Saint-Pierre wreck in a two-dive day.

## 10. La Rose des Vents, Le François

Off the Atlantic coast town of Le François, La Rose des Vents is a drift dive along a reef ridge at 12 to 25 metres that takes divers through schools of creole wrasse, dense populations of surgeonfish, and occasional encounters with large tarpon. The drift is gentle to moderate depending on tide; the Atlantic exposure can introduce swell on the entry and exit. Visibility on the Atlantic side of Martinique tends to be lower than the Caribbean side, typically eight to 12 metres, but the fish biomass compensates. A good site for divers who want Atlantic conditions without the full exposure of Cap Enragé.

## Planning Your Martinique Dive Trip

Martinique is diveable year-round as a French overseas territory with reliable air connections and infrastructure. The Caribbean side — home to Saint-Pierre and most reef sites — is calmest from November through April during the dry season. Water temperature is tropical: 26–29°C year-round. The most important planning note for Martinique is this: the Saint-Pierre wrecks require experienced, well-briefed operators who know the sites. Mooring reliability, current assessment, and depth management matter more here than at most destinations. Dive operators are concentrated in Saint-Pierre itself, with additional centres in Le Marin and Les Trois-Îlets. Nitrox is available from the main Saint-Pierre operators.
