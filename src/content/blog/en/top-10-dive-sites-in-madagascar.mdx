Madagascar sits at the crossroads of Indian Ocean currents, and this position shapes its underwater world as profoundly as its famous isolation has shaped the terrestrial one. The island's northwestern coast, centred on Nosy Be and its satellite islands, hosts the country's most developed dive infrastructure, a relatively compact reef system, and marine life of high quality and density. Further south, the Mitsio Archipelago offers wilder, less-visited diving. Throughout, the combination of warm water — rarely below 24°C — strong currents on the outer reefs, and extraordinary biodiversity makes Madagascar a destination that rewards well-prepared divers. Whale sharks, manta rays, humpback whales, and coelacanths (in very deep water) are among the headline species, but the real pleasure is in the reef complexity and the profusion of smaller life.

## 1. Nosy Tanikely Marine Reserve

Nosy Tanikely, a small island 12 kilometres southwest of Nosy Be, is the epicentre of Malagasy diving. The marine reserve encircles the island, protecting a reef that slopes from the surface to 30 metres on the northern face and deeper on the western side. Hard coral coverage is excellent, with impressive table Acropora and brain coral dominating the upper sections. Green turtles are almost guaranteed — this is one of their nesting beaches and they feed on the reef year-round. Hawksbill turtles are less common but present. The southeast face of the island is particularly good for macro subjects: nudibranchs, flatworms, and pipefish inhabit the rubble zone. Open Water level and above.

## 2. Nosy Sakatia Wall

Nosy Sakatia, a forested island west of Nosy Be, is known primarily for its eastern wall — a vertical reef face descending from five metres to beyond 40 metres, with dramatic gorgonian sea fans appearing below 20 metres and deepwater sponges carpeting the lower sections. Whale sharks pass through the blue water off the wall seasonally, most reliably from October through December. The wall face itself hosts schools of batfish, surgeonfish, and anthias in dense formations. Current can be strong on the exposed sections; divers comfortable with moderate flow will get the most from the site. Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper sections.

## 3. The Mitsio Islands, Outer Archipelago

The Mitsio group, around 50 kilometres northeast of Nosy Be, are accessible only by liveaboard or organised day trip from a fast boat — the journey takes two to three hours depending on conditions. The reward for the effort is largely undisturbed reef diving with low traffic and high marine life density. The underwater topography is dramatic: volcanic rock formations, caves, and chimneys descend to sand at 30 to 40 metres. Large schools of barracuda and jack concentrate around the pinnacles. Grouper and Napoleon wrasse of notable size patrol the deeper reef. Strong current is the norm on exposed sites; this is experienced-diver territory. Best from April through November.

## 4. Coral Gardens, Nosy Be

The sheltered eastern side of Nosy Be itself hosts a series of coral garden dives at five to 18 metres that are ideal for Open Water divers, refreshers, and photographers working the abundant macro life. Sea horses are reliably found in the sea grass adjacent to the reef. Juvenile fish in extraordinary variety inhabit the coral heads, and the site's protection from swell means visibility is consistent — eight to 15 metres even during transitional months. These sites lack the drama of Tanikely's walls but compensate with intimacy and accessibility. Multiple operators based in Hellville and Ambatoloaka run daily departures to these gardens.

## 5. The Cratera, Nosy Tanikely

On the southern side of Nosy Tanikely, a volcanic crater formation creates a natural amphitheatre at 15 to 25 metres. The walls of the crater support extraordinary soft coral growth — orange and yellow dendronephthya densely covering the rock faces — while the sandy floor hosts garden eels and rays. The site is effectively a bowl sheltered from the prevailing current, making conditions inside calm even when the outer reef is running. Cuttlefish are frequently seen here, particularly in the morning. Advanced Open Water divers can explore the crater's deeper sections and the exit channel to the north.

## 6. Shark Point, Nosy Be

A submerged pinnacle off the northwestern tip of Nosy Be, Shark Point is correctly named: white-tip reef sharks, nurse sharks, and occasional grey reef sharks are reliable finds on the deeper sections at 20 to 35 metres. The pinnacle's current-exposed summit at eight metres is swept clean of sediment, exposing hard substrate that supports low-profile encrusting corals and a remarkable density of blue-ringed octopus (small but unmistakable with their blue iridescent rings). Eagle rays pass through in pairs or small groups. Advanced Open Water certification is the minimum given the depth and current exposure; divers without drift experience should wait for a calmer day.

## 7. Grand Bleu Reef, Nosy Be

A broad coral plateau extending from eight to 20 metres southwest of Nosy Be, Grand Bleu offers a contrast to the more vertical topography of the northern sites. The plateau is carpeted in a mosaic of hard coral types including large Porites bommies that create the local reef architecture. Resident hawksbill turtles, octopus, and a variety of moray species inhabit the coral heads. The site transitions at its outer edge to a gentle slope descending to 30 metres and below, with larger grouper holding in the deeper sections. Visibility here averages 12 to 18 metres. Open Water level for the plateau; Advanced for the slope.

## 8. Manon Point, Nosy Sakatia

The northern tip of Sakatia presents a different character from the island's famous wall — a rocky point with substantial current exposure and the associated congregation of large pelagics. Manta rays are the primary attraction from September through January, when current-driven plankton blooms bring them to the point's filtering grounds. Rides in clear water past a manta ray doing slow barrel rolls through a krill concentration are genuinely exceptional. Outside manta season the point still produces eagle rays, trevally, and large barracuda. This is an Advanced Open Water site at minimum due to the unpredictable current.

## 9. Nosy Iranja Reef

Two small islands connected by a white sand bar at low tide, Nosy Iranja is 60 kilometres southwest of Nosy Be and requires a day trip by speedboat. The surrounding reef is among the most pristine in the Nosy Be region by virtue of its distance from regular dive traffic. Hard coral coverage is high, turtles are numerous (the beach is a major nesting site), and the outer reef edges receive enough current to support impressive coral growth to 30 metres. Dolphin pods regularly accompany the boat crossing. The dive itself is relatively straightforward — a reef slope from five to 25 metres — and suits Open Water divers in good sea conditions.

## 10. Lokobe Reserve Fringing Reef

On the southeastern tip of Nosy Be, the Lokobe Nature Reserve extends into the water, protecting a fringing reef that has seen comparatively little dive traffic. The site is primarily a macro and night dive destination: frogfish are reliably present in two or three colour morphs, decorator crabs use bits of algae and sponge as camouflage, and the sea grass beds adjacent to the reef shelter leaf fish and juvenile seahorses. Night dives reveal hunting cone snails, flatworms, and occasional small cuttlefish. Maximum depth is around 15 metres. This is a legitimate and rewarding site for Open Water and intermediate divers who prefer methodical searching to wall dives.

## Planning Your Madagascar Dive Trip

The primary season runs from May through November when the southeast trade winds are calmer and water visibility peaks — often exceeding 20 metres on the outer reefs. December through March is cyclone season; conditions can be difficult and some operators close. Water temperature ranges from 24°C in the austral winter (July–August) to 29°C in summer. Most visitors fly into Nosy Be via Antananarivo. The town of Hellville on Nosy Be's south coast and the resort area at Ambatoloaka both host multiple PADI-certified dive centres. Liveaboards are the best way to reach the Mitsio Archipelago. Nitrox is available from the main operators.
