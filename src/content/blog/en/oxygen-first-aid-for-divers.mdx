## The First Tool in Diving Emergency Response

When a diver surfaces in distress — unconscious, showing signs of decompression illness, or having suffered a near-drowning — the single most important piece of first-aid equipment available on the boat or the beach is an oxygen kit. Not a recompression chamber, which may be hours away. Not a physician, who may not be present. Oxygen: high-flow, high-concentration, administered by someone trained to deliver it correctly while evacuation is organised.

Emergency oxygen has transformed the outcome of diving incidents. The physiological rationale is well established: breathing 100% oxygen at the surface accelerates the elimination of nitrogen bubbles from the tissues, reduces the size of existing bubbles, and addresses the hypoxia that frequently accompanies decompression illness and near-drowning. In cases of arterial gas embolism, the immediate administration of normobaric oxygen — oxygen at normal atmospheric pressure — is the standard first-aid response while recompression therapy is arranged.

Every diver has an interest in oxygen first aid. A diving incident can happen at any site, to any diver, and the people most likely to be present are other divers. Waiting for emergency medical services to arrive without delivering oxygen first is, in the current evidence-based consensus, a significant missed opportunity for a potentially reversible injury.

## What Decompression Illness Actually Is

The term 'decompression illness' (DCI) encompasses two distinct but related conditions: decompression sickness (DCS) and arterial gas embolism (AGE). Both involve gas coming out of solution in the body or entering the circulatory system as bubbles, with potentially serious consequences for any tissue they affect.

DCS occurs when a diver ascends faster than dissolved nitrogen can be safely transported out of the tissues via the bloodstream and eliminated through the lungs. Nitrogen comes out of solution and forms bubbles in the tissues or bloodstream. Symptoms range from itchy skin and joint pain (Type I DCS) to neurological deficits, confusion, paralysis and respiratory distress (Type II DCS). The classic 'bends' — severe joint pain — is the most widely recognised presentation, but spinal cord involvement, producing weakness or paralysis in the legs, is among the most common serious presentations and the one most dramatically benefited by prompt oxygen administration and recompression.

AGE occurs when pulmonary overexpansion injury — caused by ascending too rapidly while breath-holding, or by ascending with a blocked airway — allows gas to enter the pulmonary veins and from there into the systemic arterial circulation. Bubbles in the arterial circulation can reach the brain and coronary arteries within seconds. Stroke-like symptoms, loss of consciousness shortly after surfacing, and cardiac arrest are all possible presentations. AGE is a true emergency requiring the fastest possible evacuation to a recompression facility, with oxygen administered continuously throughout.

Both conditions are treated by recompression — most commonly in a hyperbaric oxygen chamber, where the patient breathes 100% oxygen at elevated pressure, which drives nitrogen back into solution and provides the tissues with abundant oxygen. Emergency oxygen at the surface cannot replicate this, but it reduces the bubble burden and maintains oxygenation until recompression is available.

## Oxygen Equipment

The standard diving-emergency oxygen kit consists of a cylinder of medical or aviator's oxygen — never industrial oxygen, which is not certified for breathing — with a pressure regulator, a delivery system, and appropriate masks. Cylinders are commonly available in sizes ranging from 400 litres to 1800 litres. Larger cylinders are heavier but provide more duration for extended rescue operations or during long boat returns to shore.

The delivery system is the critical component for first aid. High-flow demand valves, which deliver oxygen only when the patient inhales, achieve near-100% inspired oxygen concentration in a breathing patient. They are efficient with oxygen and the preferred delivery method for a conscious, cooperative patient. Non-rebreather masks — a mask with a reservoir bag and one-way valves — can deliver 60 to 80% oxygen at flow rates of 10 to 15 litres per minute and are used for patients who cannot or will not use a demand valve, or when the demand valve is unavailable. For unconscious patients not breathing, a bag-valve-mask resuscitator with an oxygen inlet allows ventilation with high-concentration oxygen during resuscitation.

Cylinders must be inspected and tested on a schedule specific to the jurisdiction and cylinder type. The regulator should be maintained and tested regularly, and all divers using the kit should be familiar with it before an emergency. A kit discovered to have a leaking seal or a regulator that cannot be opened during an actual incident is worse than useless — it creates false assurance.

## The DAN Oxygen Provider Course

The standard training for emergency oxygen administration in diving is the Oxygen First Aid for Scuba Diving Injuries course offered by Divers Alert Network (DAN), or the equivalent offered by other agencies including PADI (Emergency Oxygen Provider) and DAN Europe. These courses typically run for a single day and cover recognition of DCI symptoms, assembly and use of the oxygen equipment, delivery techniques, and integration with the Emergency Action Plan.

The training is not medically demanding. It does not require a clinical background. It is designed to be completed by any recreational diver and to produce a capable oxygen provider who can act confidently in the first critical minutes of a diving emergency. Many dive operators around the world require their divemasters and instructors to hold current oxygen provider certification, and it is a prerequisite for several advanced diving certifications.

Recertification is recommended every two years. Skills that are not practised regularly — particularly assembling unfamiliar equipment under stress — degrade more quickly than divers typically appreciate. A one-day refresher reactivates those skills and updates knowledge of current protocols.

## On the Boat and at the Dive Site

An oxygen kit is a reasonable expectation at any organised dive operation. Reputable dive centres, liveaboards and dive clubs maintain kits on their boats and keep them accessible. On many liveaboards — vessels operating at distance from medical facilities in places like the Maldives, Raja Ampat, or the remote atolls of Micronesia — the oxygen kit may represent the only emergency medical resource available for hours.

Before diving from an unfamiliar boat or with an unfamiliar operator, it is entirely appropriate to ask where the oxygen kit is, whether it is charged, and whether crew members are trained to use it. An operator who is irritated by this question is sending a meaningful signal about their safety culture. An operator who answers it promptly and confidently is demonstrating that they take emergency preparedness seriously.

## Oxygen in Near-Drowning and Other Diving Incidents

Decompression illness is the most discussed application of diving emergency oxygen, but it is not the only one. Near-drowning — submersion with recovery after initial unconscious or with water inhalation — produces hypoxia and pulmonary damage that is treated in part with supplemental oxygen. A diver rescued from the water after an unexplained unconsciousness or cardiac event at depth should receive oxygen while the cause is investigated and during evacuation.

Carbon monoxide poisoning from contaminated compressed air — which still occurs when compressors are incorrectly maintained or positioned near exhaust sources — produces symptoms that can be confused with DCS or simple fatigue. The treatment is also supplemental oxygen, which accelerates the elimination of carboxyhaemoglobin. Any diver who develops unusual symptoms during or after a dive from a compressor whose air quality is uncertain should be considered for oxygen administration and medical evaluation.

The underlying principle is simple: oxygen rarely makes things worse and frequently makes things better. Administered at high concentration and high flow through appropriate equipment by a trained provider, it is the safest and most universally available intervention in the diving emergency toolkit.
