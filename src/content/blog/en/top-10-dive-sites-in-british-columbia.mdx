Jacques Cousteau once called the waters of British Columbia the second-best cold-water diving in the world, and divers who brave the chill understand why. The emerald-green water of the Pacific Northwest is dense with plankton and nutrients, fuelling a riot of invertebrate life: giant Pacific octopus, the largest of their kind on Earth; wolf eels with their oddly soulful faces; plumose anemones carpeting the rock in white; and forests of bull kelp. This is serious cold-water diving — temperatures hover around 8 to 12°C year-round, drysuits are essential, and the strong tidal currents of Howe Sound and the surrounding fjords demand careful dive planning. The reward is a marine landscape of staggering richness, much of it accessible from shore around Vancouver.

## Whytecliff Park, West Vancouver

Whytecliff Park in West Vancouver is one of the most popular shore dives in Canada and a designated marine protected area — the first saltwater no-take reserve in the country. The protected status has made it a haven for the giant Pacific octopus and the resident wolf eels that peer from their rocky dens, allowing close, respectful encounters. The site offers walls, a wreck and abundant rockfish, lingcod and invertebrate life, all reachable from a shore entry. Its protected status and easy access make it the ideal introduction to BC's emerald world.

## Porteau Cove, Howe Sound

Porteau Cove, on the shore of Howe Sound along the scenic Sea to Sky Highway, is a provincial marine park built specifically for divers. Several vessels have been deliberately sunk here as artificial reefs, including the Centennial II and the GB Church, sitting in accessible depths and now thickly encrusted with plumose anemones and patrolled by lingcod and rockfish. The combination of easy shore access, marked underwater trails and multiple wrecks makes Porteau Cove a perfect training and recreational site, especially for divers building cold-water confidence.

## Whytecliff and the White Lady, Repulse Rock

Around Whytecliff and the outer reaches of Howe Sound lies Repulse Rock, home to the dramatic formation known as the White Lady — a wall draped in white plumose anemones that glow against the dark water. This is more advanced diving, with current and depth demanding good planning, but the density of anemone life and the chance of large lingcod, rockfish and the ever-present curiosity of harbour seals reward the effort. The walls here drop into deep, dark water that captures the wild character of Howe Sound diving.

## Kelvin Grove, Howe Sound

Kelvin Grove, on the western shore of Howe Sound, is a beautiful wall and slope dive accessible from shore via a forest trail. The reef descends steeply through zones of life — kelp and rockfish in the shallows, then a wall draped in anemones, sponges and the burrows of octopus and wolf eels deeper down. The clarity here is often excellent in the colder months, when the plankton bloom subsides, and the site rewards careful, slow exploration of the rich overhanging walls.

## Ansell Point, Howe Sound

Ansell Point offers one of the finest wall dives in Howe Sound, a near-vertical face plunging into deep water and cloaked in invertebrate life. Cloud sponges, some centuries old and extremely fragile, grow on the deeper sections, alongside crimson anemones, nudibranchs and the dens of octopus. It is an advanced dive requiring good buoyancy to avoid damaging the delicate sponge gardens, but it showcases the otherworldly richness of the Pacific Northwest deep wall environment.

## The Wreck of the HMCS Annapolis, Halkett Bay

The HMCS Annapolis, a former Canadian destroyer escort, was sunk in Halkett Bay Marine Park off Gambier Island in 2015 as an artificial reef. Now resting upright, it has rapidly colonised with marine life and offers a superb wreck dive in the emerald water of Howe Sound. Divers can explore the superstructure and decks, with trained wreck divers penetrating the interior, while the surrounding waters teem with rockfish and the curious harbour seals of the sound. The depth and current make it an advanced dive.

## Nakwakto Rapids and the Gooseneck Barnacles

For the truly experienced, the Nakwakto Rapids in the central BC coast are among the fastest tidal rapids in the world, and the only place where the rare red gooseneck barnacle thrives in dense colonies on the aptly named Tremble Island. Diving here is strictly for expert divers on slack tide, as the currents are ferocious, but the experience of a reef supercharged by nutrient-laden tidal flow — anemones, soft corals and dense invertebrate life — is unmatched. This is the pinnacle of BC's current-fed diving.

## God's Pocket and the Northern Vancouver Island Reefs

Off the northern tip of Vancouver Island, the waters around God's Pocket Marine Provincial Park and the Browning Pass are legendary among cold-water divers. The tidal currents that funnel through the pass nourish walls so densely carpeted with soft corals, anemones, sponges and feather stars that the rock is invisible beneath them. Reached by liveaboard or remote lodge, this is some of the richest temperate wall diving on Earth, and the reason Cousteau's praise endures. Wolf eels and octopus are common, and the water is famously clear in winter.

## Quadra Island and the Discovery Passage

The waters around Quadra Island and Campbell River in the Discovery Passage offer dramatic drift dives through current-swept channels rich with life. Sites like Steep Island and Copper Cliffs feature walls and pinnacles covered in invertebrates, frequented by lingcod, rockfish and the occasional seal or sea lion. The strong currents demand slack-tide planning, but the drift dives here, gliding past walls of colour, are among the most exhilarating in the province.

## Saltery Bay and the Mermaid Statue

Saltery Bay Provincial Park on the Sunshine Coast is a gentle, scenic shore dive famous for its underwater bronze mermaid statue, Emily Carr, resting in around 18 metres of water. Beyond the novelty, the site offers healthy rockfish, lingcod, octopus dens and anemone-covered rock in calm, accessible conditions. It is a relaxed, beautiful dive that contrasts with the more demanding current sites, ideal for divers easing into BC's cold green waters.
