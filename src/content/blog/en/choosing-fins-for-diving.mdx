## What Fins Are Actually Doing

Fins convert leg movement into forward thrust by pushing water backwards. Everything else — blade shape, stiffness, channel structures, split designs, material choice — is a variation on that basic mechanism, aimed at increasing efficiency, reducing fatigue, or adapting propulsion for specific kicking styles and diving contexts. A fin that works brilliantly for a powerful open-circuit diver in a strong current may be exhausting for a recreational diver doing three shallow reef dives per day. Understanding what you want from your fins is the necessary first step before evaluating any of the technical claims manufacturers make.

The two primary variables that determine how a fin performs are blade stiffness and blade area. Stiffer, larger blades move more water per stroke and generate more thrust, but they require more muscular effort and are harder to stop once set in motion — they resist fine-tuned control. Softer, smaller blades are more forgiving, easier on joints, and better suited to the flutter kick that most recreational divers use. The right balance depends on your leg strength, your kicking style, and the diving you typically do.

## Blade Styles: Split, Paddle, and Channel

Paddle fins are the original design — a solid or near-solid blade, wider at the tip, that generates thrust in both the down-kick and the recovery stroke. They are the most intuitive to use and the most versatile. Blade stiffness varies enormously across paddle fin models: the Mares Avanti Quattro and its imitators use a channel-blade design that stiffens under load; the Cressi Palau family is softer and suited to recreational diving in light-to-moderate conditions. Longer, stiffer paddle fins like the Scubapro Seawing Nova produce significant thrust but are better suited to divers with above-average leg strength.

Split fins, popularised by products like the TUSA SF-22 Liberator and similar designs, use a longitudinal split down the blade to allow each half to flex independently, creating a propeller-like vortex effect. They are exceptionally easy to kick — the effort required is noticeably lower than a comparable paddle fin — and are well-suited to divers with joint issues or those who prioritise low-effort, extended diving over raw thrust. The tradeoff is reduced backwards propulsion: split fins do not perform well with the reverse-kick, sculling, and helicopter-turn techniques that technical and cave divers rely on for precise positioning.

Blade channelling — raised ridges or structural features that stiffen the centre of the blade while allowing edge flexibility — appears on many modern designs and attempts to combine efficiency with control. The logic is sound, and several well-regarded fins use this approach effectively.

## Foot Pocket: Full Foot versus Open Heel

Full-foot fins are designed for barefoot or thin-sock use in warm water. They are lighter, more compact for travel, and the direct connection between foot and blade improves feedback. Most are intended for snorkelling or warm tropical diving where a 3mm wetsuit is the heaviest exposure protection used.

Open-heel fins use an adjustable heel strap and are designed for use over dive boots. In cold water, where a 7mm wetsuit and 5mm boots are standard, open-heel fins are effectively mandatory. The spring heel strap — a loop of bungee or formed elastomer that replaces the traditional buckle strap — is almost universally superior to the older buckle design: it adjusts automatically, never slips, and is much faster to put on and remove. Any diver buying open-heel fins should be looking at spring-strap models as a default.

Boot fit is critical. A full-foot fin that is slightly too large will move on your foot; one that is slightly too small will cause cramping within 30 minutes. Try fin sizes over the dive boots you plan to wear. The fit should be firm but not constrictive, and the heel of your boot should sit fully against the fin heel pocket.

## Propulsion Techniques and Fin Choice

The flutter kick — alternating up-down leg movements — is what most recreational divers use and what most consumer fins are designed to accommodate. The frog kick, used extensively by cave divers and technical divers, involves a simultaneous outward-and-back cycling motion that generates thrust without disturbing the bottom sediment and is highly efficient for horizontal travel. The modified flutter (also called the bent-knee flutter) uses short, fast movements from the knee down and works well in tight spaces.

Fins optimised for the frog kick are typically shorter and stiffer than standard paddle fins, with a blade shape that generates consistent thrust even through the unusual angle of the frog kick stroke. Some divers find that the same fins work adequately for both flutter and frog kick; others note that fins designed specifically for one technique feel wrong in the other. If you are seriously pursuing cave or technical diving training, your instructor's fin choice and recommendation are worth taking seriously — they have worked with the technique across many different setups and will have specific preferences.

## Weight and Travel

Full-foot fins for warm-water travel are significantly lighter and smaller than open-heel fins. Several manufacturers produce short-blade travel fins — the Mares Plana Avanti Tre Short is a well-known example — that fit in carry-on luggage. The thrust they produce is inferior to a full-size blade, but for a week of holiday diving on a shallow reef in warm water, the convenience usually outweighs the performance compromise.

If you travel frequently with open-heel fins, consider that most airline checked baggage limits are tight enough that heavy fins create a problem when combined with a regulator, BCD, and wetsuit. Spring straps are slightly bulkier than buckle straps when packed but are compact enough to fit in a standard dive bag.

## Durability and Maintenance

Fins require minimal maintenance but are not indestructible. Rubber heel straps crack with UV exposure and age; replace them before they snap mid-dive. Spring straps are more durable but can lose tension over time. Blade cracks, usually near the blade tip or along channelling, are worth inspecting regularly on older fins — a fin blade that flexes cleanly through a known crack will eventually fail under load.

Rinse fins in fresh water after diving, particularly if they have been in the sea for extended periods. Salt crystals that are allowed to dry between sessions accelerate degradation of rubber components. Hang or lay flat for storage rather than stacking heavy objects on top.
