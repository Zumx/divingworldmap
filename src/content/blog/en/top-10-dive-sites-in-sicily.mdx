Sicily is the largest island in the Mediterranean and one of its most rewarding dive regions, ringed by volcanic islands, ancient shipwrecks and Italy's first marine reserve. The sea here carries layer upon layer of history: Phoenician, Greek, Roman and Second World War wrecks lie scattered across the seabed, while the volcanic archipelagos of the Aeolians and Egadi offer dramatic walls, caves and thermal vents. Ustica, a volcanic cone north of Palermo, became Italy's first marine protected area in 1986 and remains one of the Mediterranean's great dives. Water reaches 26 to 27°C in August, visibility frequently passes 30 metres, and the season runs comfortably from May to October.

## 1. Secca della Colombara, Ustica

The Secca della Colombara, off the volcanic island of Ustica, is the jewel of Italy's first marine reserve. A seamount rising from beyond 40 metres to within 18 of the surface, its terraced rock is smothered in yellow and orange gorgonians and circled by groupers, amberjack, barracuda and dense shoals of damselfish. Decades of protection have made the fish here extraordinarily abundant and tame. Depth and the offshore position make it an Advanced dive. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres. Boats run from the island's small harbour, with the best conditions from June to October.

## 2. Grotta dei Gamberi, Ustica

Also off Ustica, the Grotta dei Gamberi — the shrimp cave — is a tunnel at around 40 metres whose ceiling and walls are carpeted in tiny red shrimp that glitter in dive lights, alongside lobster and slipper lobster tucked into the rock. The dive descends through clear blue water past gorgonian-clad walls to the cave entrance, making it a memorable deep dive within the marine reserve. It suits Advanced and experienced divers comfortable at depth. Visibility is excellent, often 30 metres or more, and the site is dived in the calm summer months.

## 3. Roman Amphora Wreck, Capo Graziano, Filicudi

Off Filicudi in the Aeolian Islands, the seabed at Capo Graziano holds the scattered cargo of an ancient Roman ship — rows of amphorae left where they spilled when the vessel went down two thousand years ago. The site lies at 25 to 40 metres and is protected as an underwater archaeological zone. Diving among the amphorae, colonised now by sponges and small fish, is a direct touch of antiquity. It suits Advanced divers; permits and guided access apply. Visibility is typically 25 to 30 metres, best from June to September.

## 4. Punta del Corvo, Panarea, Aeolian Islands

Panarea, the smallest of the inhabited Aeolian Islands, sits atop an active volcanic system, and the diving reflects it. At Punta del Corvo and the nearby reefs, walls drop past 35 metres while underwater fumaroles release streams of warm gas bubbles from the seabed — a surreal volcanic seascape. Groupers, barracuda and amberjack patrol the deeper rock. The thermal vents make this some of the Mediterranean's most unusual diving. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres, and the season runs May to October.

## 5. Secca del Toro, Egadi Islands

The Egadi Islands off Sicily's west coast hold the largest marine protected area in Europe, and the Secca del Toro off Favignana is among its finest dives. A reef rising to around 15 metres and dropping past 40, it is washed by current that draws amberjack, barracuda and dentex, with groupers resident in the deeper crevices. Red gorgonians fan from the walls. The site suits Advanced divers; shallower reefs in the reserve welcome all levels. Visibility is typically 25 to 30 metres, with the calmest, warmest conditions in late summer.

## 6. Grotta del Cammello, Levanzo, Egadi Islands

Off Levanzo in the Egadi reserve, the Grotta del Cammello is a large, light-filled cavern at 8 to 20 metres where shafts of sunlight pierce the blue and illuminate the resident shoals of cardinalfish and bream. The shallow, well-lit cavern suits Open Water divers under guidance and is a favourite for underwater photographers. Octopus, moray eels and lobster inhabit the surrounding rock. The Egadi marine reserve protects the whole area, keeping fish life rich. Visibility runs 20 to 30 metres. Dive June through September for the calmest seas.

## 7. The Wrecks of Capo Murro di Porco, Siracusa

Off the headland of Capo Murro di Porco near Siracusa, on Sicily's south-east coast, lies a marine reserve dense with reef, caves and the scattered remains of vessels from antiquity through the Second World War. The headland was a key landing point in the 1943 Allied invasion, and divers can explore wartime debris alongside reef at 10 to 30 metres. Groupers, octopus and shoaling fish are abundant in the protected water. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres, best in summer.

## 8. Capo Gallo, Palermo

The Capo Gallo marine reserve, just north of Palermo near Mondello, offers accessible diving over reef, caves and posidonia from the shore and by boat. Depths run 6 to 30 metres, with octopus, scorpionfish, moray eels and groupers grown bold in the protected water. The dramatic limestone headland is riddled with caverns that suit guided cavern diving. Its proximity to Palermo makes it one of the most convenient quality dives in Sicily. Visibility runs 15 to 25 metres. The reserve is dived from spring through autumn, warmest in August.

## 9. Lo Scoglio del Medico, Ustica

Returning to Ustica, the Scoglio del Medico is a volcanic pinnacle off the island's west coast, riddled with tunnels, arches and swim-throughs carved by the sea. Light pours through the openings as divers thread the passages at 10 to 30 metres, surrounded by groupers, barracuda and dense shoals of bream. The dramatic topography and protected fish life make it one of Ustica's signature dives. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres, with the best conditions in the calm summer months.

## 10. Cala Junco and the Reefs of Vulcano

Off the island of Vulcano in the Aeolians, the diving combines black volcanic rock, sulphurous thermal vents and clear blue water. Reefs and walls run from 10 to 35 metres, with bubbling fumaroles, groupers, octopus and shoaling fish, all beneath the steaming crater of the island's active volcano. The surreal landscape and warm volcanic water make it a memorable dive. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility is typically 25 to 30 metres. Boats run from Vulcano and Lipari, with the season from May to October.

## Explore on the map

Sicily rewards island-hopping as much as the mainland coast: Ustica for Italy's first marine reserve and gorgonian-clad seamounts; the Aeolian Islands for amphora wrecks and volcanic vents; the Egadi reserve for Europe's largest protected area; and the south-east around Siracusa for caves and wartime wrecks. The season runs roughly May to October, warmest in August.
