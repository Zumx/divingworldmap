The Red Sea is one of the world's most iconic diving destinations — a narrow body of water squeezed between Africa and the Arabian Peninsula, fed by no significant rivers, and consequently carrying extraordinary clarity and salinity. Visibility of 20 to 30 metres is routine, water temperatures range from 21 °C in the northern reaches in winter to 28 °C in summer, and the combination of tropical reef, historic wrecks and genuinely large pelagic encounters makes it exceptional value for the travelling diver. The Egyptian coastline between Hurghada and Marsa Alam sees the heaviest dive tourism traffic; the deeper south and the far offshore sites attract the liveaboard crowd.

## 1. SS Thistlegorm, Northern Red Sea

The SS Thistlegorm is the most famous wreck in the Red Sea and among the top five wreck dives in the world. A British armed merchant navy vessel sunk by German bombers in October 1941, she lies on her side at 15 to 30 metres in the Strait of Gubal. Her holds remain packed with the cargo she was carrying at the time of her sinking: motorcycles, trucks, Lee-Enfield rifles, boots, aircraft components and railway wagons. The experience of dropping into Hold 2 among the motorcycles is unlike anything else in recreational diving. Best reached by liveaboard; day trips from Sharm el-Sheikh exist but involve a very early start.

## 2. Ras Mohammed, Sinai

Ras Mohammed National Park sits at the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula and protects arguably the finest coral reef system in the northern Red Sea. The Shark Reef and Yolanda Reef sites offer a wall that plunges from the surface into the blue, encrusted with enormous sea fans and soft corals. The Yolanda is a wreck — a cargo ship carrying bathroom fittings, the toilets and basins of which are now scattered across the slope at 25 to 55 metres. Grey reef, white-tip and hammerhead sharks are regular at Shark Reef. Visibility commonly exceeds 25 metres.

## 3. Brothers Islands, Egyptian Red Sea

The Brothers (El Akhawein) are two small limestone pinnacles rising from open ocean 60 kilometres offshore, accessible only by liveaboard. Big Brother carries a lighthouse and two additional wrecks — the Numidia, which sank in 1901, and the Aida II, a more recent dive boat casualty. The walls on both islands plunge beyond 100 metres and are among the most coral-encrusted vertical surfaces in the Red Sea. Oceanic white-tip sharks circle the outer walls year-round, and thresher sharks appear in summer. Advanced Open Water is the minimum certification most liveaboards require.

## 4. Elphinstone Reef, Marsa Alam

Elphinstone is a narrow table reef about three kilometres offshore whose walls plunge vertically to beyond 70 metres. The north plateau, reached after descending the wall to 25–30 metres, is where the encounters happen: oceanic white-tip sharks appear with high regularity, and grey reef sharks patrol the shelf edge. Hammerheads are possible during summer. The southern saddle at about 35 metres produces strong surge at any wind and is better suited to experienced divers. Day access is available from Marsa Alam, though a liveaboard provides better timing.

## 5. Daedalus Reef

Daedalus (Abu el-Kizan) is a remote atoll 80 kilometres east of Marsa Alam, reachable only by liveaboard, with a lighthouse that has operated continuously since 1863. The circular reef wall offers some of the most consistent hammerhead and thresher shark encounters in the northern Red Sea — they appear in their highest numbers during summer, from June through September. Manta rays and whale sharks pass during the same season. Visibility is superb, commonly 30 metres or more. A 3 mm wetsuit is comfortable in summer; 5 mm is more appropriate in winter.

## 6. St John's Reef, Southern Red Sea

St John's is a remote reef complex accessible via liveaboard from Marsa Alam or Hamata, sitting close to the Sudanese border. The reef system includes several pristine bays and lagoons with near-virgin coral. The site called 'The Cathedral' is a swim-through cave system at 15 to 25 metres that lets in shafts of ambient light through chimney cracks. Far fewer divers reach St John's than the northern classics, and the reef shows it — coral coverage here is among the best remaining in the Egyptian Red Sea.

## 7. Blue Hole, Dahab

The Blue Hole is the most famous and most statistically dangerous dive site in Egypt — an almost circular sinkhole roughly 60 metres in diameter, opening from a shallow coral shelf at 5 metres and descending beyond 100. The site has claimed many lives, most involving technical divers attempting to find the Arch, a passage through the reef wall at 52 metres. For recreational divers who stay above 30 metres, the Blue Hole itself is a spectacular drift dive with excellent visibility and easy reef diving on the adjacent saddle. Advanced Open Water level is sufficient for a sensible dive here.

## 8. Hamada

Hamada is a reef site in the Elphinstone area that serves as a counterpoint to the shark-focused diving of the main reef. Sandy shallows at 8 to 12 metres host an extraordinary density of macro life: frogfish, robust ghost pipefish, blue-spotted ribbontail rays, and porcupinefish in the coral heads. It is suitable from Open Water level and is often used as the second dive on a day trip when surface interval time limits deeper options.

## 9. Abu Nuhas, Gulf of Suez

Abu Nuhas reef has claimed no fewer than four significant ships, all within recreational diving range. The Chrisoula K (formerly Demetrios II), the Carnatic, the Kimon M, and the Giannis D all rest in 15 to 27 metres, and all four can be visited on a single liveaboard day in good sea conditions. The Giannis D is the most intact and the most penetrated, with easy swim-throughs through the engine room. The Carnatic, sunk in 1869, is entirely overgrown with soft coral and provides context for the Thistlegorm story: Victorian-era cargo ships were already calling on these routes.

## 10. Shag Rock

Shag Rock is a pinnacle rising from deep water in the Brothers area, less frequently visited than the main islands but notable for its pristine hard coral and consistent grey reef shark presence. It sits at 20 to 35 metres on the shallow ledge and rewards patient hovering in the current. Liveaboard operations that visit Big Brother often include Shag Rock as a later-day dive to avoid surge on the main walls.

## Practical information

Liveaboards out of Hurghada or Port Ghalib serve the offshore sites; day boats reach Ras Mohammed from Sharm el-Sheikh and the nearshore wrecks from Hurghada. The northern Red Sea is diveable year-round; water temperature is at its coolest in January and February when a 5 mm wetsuit is advisable. The summer months bring whale sharks and hammerheads to the offshore sites but also bring heat and potential jellyfish in coastal areas.
