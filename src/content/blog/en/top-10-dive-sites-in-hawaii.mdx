Hawaii sits in the most isolated archipelago on the planet, almost 4,000 kilometres from the nearest continent, and that isolation is precisely what makes its underwater world so distinctive. Roughly a quarter of the reef fish you will see here exist nowhere else, the legacy of a volcanic chain so remote that few species ever colonised it. Add to that the architecture left by active and dormant volcanoes — lava tubes, arches, pinnacles and collapsed caverns — and you have diving that looks and feels unlike the Caribbean or the Indo-Pacific. The water is warm, typically 24 to 27°C, visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres in the lee of the islands, and the wildlife runs from green sea turtles loitering at cleaning stations to the manta rays that have made the Big Island world famous.

## Kona Manta Night Dive, Big Island

Nothing else in Hawaiian diving compares to the manta night dive off the Kona coast. Operators run two main sites — Garden Eel Cove near the airport and Manta Village off Keauhou — where divers kneel on a sandy bottom at around 12 metres and aim their lights upward. The lights attract plankton, the plankton attract the mantas, and reef mantas with wingspans up to four metres barrel-roll within centimetres of your mask, mouths agape. Individuals are catalogued by their belly markings, and the population here is one of the most studied in the world. It is among the few genuinely guaranteed big-animal encounters in diving, and it is suitable for relatively new divers given the shallow, current-free conditions.

## The Lava Tubes and Arches of Kona

By day, the Kona coast reveals why the Big Island is a magnet for divers. The reef here is young volcanic rock, riddled with lava tubes you can swim through, archways framing shafts of blue light, and collapsed ceilings that open into bright cathedrals. Sites such as the Suck-Em-Up cavern and the various tubes around Garden Eel Cove are signature dives. The clarity is exceptional because there is little runoff along this dry, lava-bound coast. Whitetip reef sharks rest under ledges, frogfish hide on the walls, and the endemic Hawaiian fish — saddle wrasse, milletseed butterflyfish, the state fish humuhumunukunukuapuaa — are everywhere.

## Molokini Crater, Maui

Molokini is a crescent-shaped, partially submerged volcanic crater lying between Maui and Kahoolawe. The inner wall of the crescent shelters a sloping reef in calm, clear water that often exceeds 45 metres of visibility — among the best in the islands. The back wall, by contrast, drops vertically into the open ocean and is a more advanced dive where pelagic species, the occasional whale shark, and reef sharks patrol the blue. The crater is a State Marine Life Conservation District, so the fish life is dense and unafraid. Most operators run from Maalaea or Kihei, and morning trips beat both the wind and the crowds.

## Turtle Town, Maui

Strung along Maui's south shore near Maluaka and Makena, Turtle Town is a series of lava formations and cleaning stations where green sea turtles — honu — gather in remarkable numbers. The turtles hover patiently while cleaner wrasse and tangs nibble algae from their shells, allowing exceptionally close, respectful observation. The terrain itself is a maze of lava arches and overhangs at moderate depths of 12 to 18 metres, making it an easy and rewarding dive for all levels. It is also a reliable spot for eels, octopus and the occasional reef shark.

## Cathedrals of Lanai

Off the small island of Lanai lie two of Hawaii's most celebrated cavern dives, Cathedral I and Cathedral II. These are vast lava chambers where sunlight pours through holes in the ceiling, creating the stained-glass effect that gives them their name. Inside, the swirling light, schooling baitfish and resting sharks make for genuinely theatrical diving. The sites are usually reached by boat from Maui's Lahaina side, and conditions are best in the calmer summer months when the channel crossing is gentle.

## The Sea Tiger Wreck, Oahu

Off Honolulu, the Sea Tiger is a 51-metre former passenger vessel deliberately sunk in 1999 as an artificial reef. It sits upright in around 30 metres of water and has matured into a thriving ecosystem, draped in coral and patrolled by green sea turtles, eagle rays, whitetip sharks and large schools of fish. The intact superstructure allows penetration for suitably trained divers, while the open deck and railings give less experienced divers plenty to explore. The wreck's depth and occasional current make it an Advanced Open Water dive.

## The YO-257 and San Pedro Wrecks, Waikiki

Also off Oahu's south shore, the twin wrecks of the YO-257 and San Pedro lie close together at around 25 to 30 metres, famously visited by the Atlantis submarine tours. The combination of two large hulls in one site, abundant turtles, and frequent eagle rays makes this one of Waikiki's most popular dives. The proximity of the two wrecks lets you tour both in a single dive, and the reef between them adds eels and reef fish to the mix.

## Sharks Cove, Oahu North Shore

In summer, when the legendary North Shore surf falls quiet, Sharks Cove becomes a superb shore dive. The name is misleading — there are rarely sharks — but the lava terrain is spectacular, with tubes, caverns and tide pools sheltering turtles, octopus, eels and dense reef life. It is a favourite for shore-diving training and freediving when the ocean is flat, which is essentially only from May to September. In winter the same spot is pounded by enormous waves and is strictly off-limits.

## Kauai's Tunnels Reef

On Kauai's north shore, Tunnels Reef (Makua) is named for the maze of lava tubes and caverns honeycombing the reef. In the calm summer season it offers a lush, easy dive with turtles, reef sharks and the chance of monk seals, Hawaii's critically endangered endemic pinniped. The reef structure is genuinely complex, rewarding slow, careful exploration. As with much of Kauai, north-shore diving is seasonal and dictated by swell.

## Niihau and Lehua, Forbidden Island Waters

For experienced divers, summer charters from Kauai venture to the waters off Niihau — the privately owned forbidden island — and the nearby Lehua crater. These remote, current-swept sites offer the islands' best chance of Galápagos and grey reef sharks, monk seals and dramatic walls in water of staggering clarity. The crossings are long and conditions can be demanding, so these trips suit confident, well-equipped divers and run only in the calmest summer windows.
