Lebanon occupies a stretch of eastern Mediterranean coastline barely 220 kilometres long, yet it punches above its weight as a dive destination for one compelling reason: the seafloor off its coast is scattered with history. Phoenician anchors, Roman amphorae, Byzantine trading vessels, and 20th-century ships lost to war and accident all rest in relatively accessible water. The broader marine environment is classic eastern Mediterranean — moderate visibility, seasonal thermoclines, a temperate fish community — but the archaeological and historic wreck diving gives Lebanon a character that few Mediterranean countries can match. The dive community is small but enthusiastic, centred mainly on Jounieh, Sidon, and Batroun, and operators are welcoming to visiting divers who come prepared.

## 1. The Lebanese Wreck (MV Souverain), Jounieh

The MV Souverain, often called simply the Lebanese Wreck, is the country's most celebrated dive. This 90-metre cargo vessel lies at 28 to 45 metres in the bay north of Jounieh, scuttled to create an artificial reef. The ship lies on its side with the shallowest point of the hull at around 28 metres, making full exploration an Advanced or Divemaster-level dive. Inside, the main hold and engine room are penetrable with proper configuration. The wreck is heavily colonised by sponges, nudibranchs, and sea fans, and grouper of considerable size use the deeper sections as territory. Visibility averages 12 to 18 metres. Best dived May through October.

## 2. Sidon Harbour Wrecks

The ancient harbour of Sidon (Saida) in southern Lebanon contains a concentration of wrecks and archaeological material spanning several thousand years. Phoenician anchors, Roman amphorae, and the remains of medieval harbour installations rest at depths between five and 20 metres. More recently, several 20th-century fishing vessels have settled into the sediment among the ancient remains. The harbour's protected geometry keeps visibility reasonable — typically eight to 15 metres — even after northerly winds disturb the open coast. The mixed archaeological and biological interest makes Sidon well-suited for divers with a historical inclination. Entry is managed by local operators who hold the necessary archaeological dive permits.

## 3. The Freight Wreck, Jounieh Bay

A second significant wreck in Jounieh Bay, this unnamed Lebanese freight vessel rests at 15 to 22 metres with much of its upper structure intact. The combination of accessible depth and good structural integrity makes it the more appropriate wreck introduction for divers who lack extensive wreck experience. Scorpionfish are abundant on the superstructure, and the hold floors have accumulated a remarkable density of Mediterranean fauna — spiny lobster, conger eels, and carpet of encrusting organisms. Night dives here are productive: octopus emerge onto open deck, and bioluminescent plankton can make the return ascent unexpectedly theatrical in late summer.

## 4. Batroun Rocky Reef

Batroun, north of Jounieh, offers the best rocky reef diving on the Lebanese coast. The seabed here transitions from sand to a system of large boulders and ridges at five to 22 metres, with crevices hosting spiny lobster, moray eels, and small octopus. The site lacks the drama of a wall or wreck but rewards divers who slow down and search carefully — nudibranchs, seahorses, and pipefish appear regularly to those who look. Visibility is often cleaner here than in the central bays, regularly reaching 20 metres in the summer months. Suitable for Open Water divers. A small dive club operates out of the old Batroun harbour.

## 5. The Amphora Site, Tyre (Sour)

Off the coast of Tyre in southern Lebanon, a field of Phoenician and Roman amphoras rests at eight to 16 metres on mixed sand and rubble. The concentration is not uniform but rather distributed across a wide area, consistent with the gradual breakup of a vessel rather than the sudden sinking of a tightly packed cargo. Individual amphoras have become home to small fish, gobies, and occasionally small eels. The site is historically protected, and touching or removing objects is prohibited. Visibility depends heavily on sea state; after prolonged calm weather it can reach 18 metres, while even moderate swell from the west reduces clarity to five or six metres.

## 6. Cap de l'Eau Reef, Batroun

A rocky point north of Batroun shelters a reef that catches the cleaner oceanic water coming from the north, giving it reliably better visibility than more southerly sites. The structure here includes a series of channels and swim-throughs at 10 to 20 metres, with good sea fan coverage on the exposed faces. Schools of bream and barracuda patrol the outer edge. The site is affected by current from the coastal drift, which can be gentle or require attentive navigation depending on tidal and wind conditions. Advanced Open Water divers will make the most of the outer sections. Best from June through September.

## 7. Jounieh Bay Night Reef

The rocky reef system in the shallow northern section of Jounieh Bay, dived primarily at night, reveals a side of Lebanese underwater life that daytime visits miss entirely. Octopus hunt across open sand, fireworms glow faintly along rock surfaces, and cuttlefish patrol the edges of the kelp-equivalent meadows. The depth range — four to 12 metres — makes this site approachable for Open Water divers. Local operators run dedicated night dives from the marina. Water temperature in July and August can reach 28°C, making a night dive here genuinely pleasant with minimal exposure protection required.

## 8. Tabarja Reef and Drop-Off

North of Jounieh, the reef at Tabarja descends more steeply than elsewhere on the coast, providing one of Lebanon's few genuine wall experiences. The wall face runs from about 12 metres to beyond 40, with sea fans becoming increasingly large with depth. Loggerhead turtles pass through occasionally, and pelagic species including amberjack appear on the deeper sections. The upper reef is in reasonable condition despite proximity to the resort coast, with good sponge coverage and resident populations of damselfish and chromis. Advanced certification is appropriate for the wall sections. The site is best approached from a boat; shore access is complicated by private property.

## 9. The Tire Reef (Pneu Coulé), Sidon

A collection of old vehicle tyres deliberately arranged as an artificial reef off Sidon rests at 10 to 18 metres and, despite its unglamorous origin, hosts a surprisingly rich community of Mediterranean species. Grouper, dentex, and bream hold in the tyre stacks, while the spaces between have been colonised by sponges, bryozoans, and sea urchins that in turn attract small fish and invertebrates. The site is well-suited to novice divers as a confined-area-style dive given its moderate depth and relatively protected location. Visibility here is typically eight to 14 metres.

## 10. Chekka Rocky Headland

The headland at Chekka, south of Tripoli, provides one of the more exposed and current-influenced dive sites on the Lebanese coast. The underwater topography includes large boulders and scattered rock formations descending to 25 metres, and the exposure to open water means marine life diversity exceeds most of the southern sites. Dentex, sea bream, and occasionally tuna are seen on the outer edge. The site requires planning around current conditions and is better suited to divers with drift experience. Visibility can be exceptional — up to 25 metres — when the prevailing northerly current brings cleaner Atlantic-influenced water across the basin.

## Planning Your Lebanon Dive Trip

The Lebanese diving season runs from May through October, with July and August representing peak conditions — warm water of 26–28°C, visibility often extending beyond 15 metres, and stable surface conditions. Winter storms from November through April limit diving significantly, particularly at exposed northern sites. Operators in Jounieh and Batroun are the most established; Sidon and Tyre have smaller operations that occasionally require advance notice. Equipment rental is available but variable in quality — bringing your own regulator and computer is advisable. There is no recompression chamber in Lebanon; evacuation to Cyprus or Jordan is the contingency.
