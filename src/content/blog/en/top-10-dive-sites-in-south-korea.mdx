South Korea is a diving destination that most Western divers have barely considered, and that oversight is increasingly their loss. Jeju Island, the volcanic island off the southern tip of the peninsula, provides the country's flagship diving: a unique combination of subtropical reef fauna, dramatic underwater volcanic topography, and the centuries-old haenyeo free-diving culture that intersects with the modern sport-diving scene in ways found nowhere else. Visibility ranges from 10 to 25 metres depending on season and site, with the clearest conditions occurring from October through December when cold Korean Strait water pushes surface plankton down. Water temperatures swing dramatically across the year: 14–16°C in winter (February–March) and 26–28°C in summer (July–August). A 5mm wetsuit is the minimum in summer; a drysuit or semi-dry is more appropriate from October through May. The diving infrastructure has developed rapidly in recent years, particularly on Jeju, and English-speaking guides are increasingly available. ## 1. Munseom (Moon Island), Jeju

Munseom is an uninhabited islet just off Seogwipo, the main diving town on Jeju's south coast, and it is the most dived and arguably the best site in South Korea. The island's base is volcanic rock creating a series of walls, archways, and caverns that plunge to about 30 metres. The soft coral coverage is extraordinary for a temperate site — bright orange and yellow gorgonians carpet the walls at depth, rivalling tropical sites in colour density. Schools of yellowtail amberjack orbit the island's rocky spurs. Sea turtles are regular visitors. The water clarity is typically the best in the Jeju region, averaging 15–20 metres and reaching 25 metres in the October–November window. Advanced Open Water recommended; drysuit or semi-dry from October through May. Boat access from Seogwipo, 10 minutes.

## 2. Seogwipo Coast, Jeju Island

The Seogwipo coastline on Jeju's volcanic south shore is covered with lava formations that create a complex underwater landscape of caverns, tunnels, and overhangs. The shallow sites — 5–15 metres — are accessible for Open Water divers and offer a fascinating mix of temperate and subtropical marine life: false clown anemonefish at the northern limit of their range living alongside cold-water species like kelp greenling that are more typical of Korean mainland waters. The diving here is accessible year-round, with dive centres in Seogwipo town offering multiple daily departures. Visibility averages 10–15 metres. Water temperature: 26°C in August, 15°C in February. Haenyeo divers — the traditional female free-divers — work these same waters, and watching them descend barefaced to 15 metres and beyond while you hover at depth with a tank is a genuinely humbling experience.

## 3. Haenyeo Waters, Jeju Island

The haenyeo are the UNESCO-recognised community of female breath-hold divers unique to Jeju Island and parts of the southern Korean coast, who have dived these waters for over a thousand years collecting abalone, conch, sea cucumber, and urchin. Their working areas — the coastal shallows to about 15–20 metres — overlap with sport-diving zones, and diving in the same water provides context for a cultural tradition that is slowly declining as the community ages. The reef structure in haenyeo-managed zones is notably healthy because the community enforces sustainable harvesting practices. Open Water level. The experience of sharing water with haenyeo who descend on a single breath, work the bottom for two to three minutes, then surface without drama, is unlike any other in Korean diving. Best in summer when visibility is acceptable and surface conditions calm.

## 4. Jeju Island: Marado

Marado is the southernmost inhabited island in South Korea, a small flat lava plateau about 11 kilometres south of Jeju's Moseulpo port. The surrounding waters are the most exposed on the Jeju coast and correspondingly rich in marine life due to the stronger currents that wash the island. Hammerhead sharks have been sighted here in summer; the resident grouper population is large by Korean standards. The walls and rocky spurs around the island descend to 20–25 metres. Visibility is typically the best accessible from Jeju — 20–25 metres in the autumn window. Access requires a licensed dive boat and favourable sea conditions; the crossing can be rough. Advanced Open Water strongly recommended. Best October through December for visibility; summer for water temperature.

## 5. Munseom Arch, Jeju

Separate from the main Munseom site, the Arch is a specific formation on the island's eastern face: a natural lava tunnel at about 12 metres depth that cuts completely through a section of the island's base, with sufficient width for two divers abreast. The tunnel walls are encrusted with hydroids, feather stars, and delicate encrusting corals. Nudibranch species that are uncommon elsewhere on Jeju are found here regularly on the hydroids. Swimming through the arch and emerging in the boulder field on the far side, with sunlight filtering down through the entrance behind, is one of Jeju's signature photographic experiences. Conditions inside the arch are typically calm regardless of surface swell. Open Water standard with good buoyancy control. Year-round access.

## 6. Northeast Coast, Jeju: Pyoseon

The northeast coast of Jeju, around the town of Pyoseon, offers a different character from the Seogwipo sites — shallower, with more sediment and seagrass habitat interspersed with the lava reef. The attraction here is macro life: nudibranchs in variety equal to the west coast sites, plus flatfish, pufferfish, and occasional octopus in the sandy channels. The site is more appropriate for photographers than for those seeking dramatic topography. Visibility is typically 8–12 metres, lower than the south coast due to the nutrient runoff from the east coast's agricultural zones. Open Water standard. Good introductory Jeju diving. Best season August through October when warm water and manageable visibility coincide.

## 7. Hongdo Island, South Jeolla Province

Hongdo (Red Island) is a national park island west of the Korean mainland, accessible by ferry from Mokpo. The island's coastal waters are less dived than Jeju but provide rocky reef diving at 15–25 metres with impressive concentrations of rockfish, sea bream, and large moray eels. The kelp forests here reach heights of 3–4 metres in the spring before being grazed back in summer. The island's park protection status has preserved fish populations notably larger than on the unprotected mainland coast. Accommodation on the island is limited; advance booking is essential in the summer season. Visibility averages 10–15 metres. Advanced Open Water recommended due to occasional current. Best diving May through October.

## 8. Geoje Island, South Gyeongsang Province

Geoje Island in the Korea Strait, accessible from Busan, is a popular weekend diving destination for divers from the mainland's southern cities. The rocky reefs around the island's outer headlands descend to 20–25 metres over hard substrate covered in turf algae and encrusting invertebrates. Korean spotty-belly greenling, a large endemic wrasse relative, is common here. Sea cucumbers, spiny lobster, and various crabs occupy the reef base. The site provides reliable, convenient diving without the ferry dependency of island destinations. Several dive shops in Geoje City offer day trips and equipment rental. Visibility 8–15 metres. Open Water sufficient for most sites. Best June through October.

## 9. Ulleungdo Island, East Sea

Ulleungdo is a remote volcanic island in the East Sea (Sea of Japan) roughly 130 kilometres offshore, and its isolation has produced marine life that is genuinely unusual for Korean waters. The submarine slopes of this steep volcanic island drop rapidly to great depth, and the clear East Sea water provides some of the best visibility in South Korean diving — 20–25 metres is common. Species diversity is lower than Jeju (the East Sea is colder and less species-rich than the south coast), but the populations of what lives here are large and unafraid. Conger eels of impressive size inhabit the rocky crevices. Access requires ferry from Pohang or Gangneung; the island's tourist infrastructure is modest. Best July through October. Advanced Open Water for the deeper wall sections.

## 10. Gaeyado Reef, Near Incheon

Gaeyado (Gaeya Island) in the Yellow Sea off Incheon is a dive site that demonstrates the remarkable difference between Korea's eastern and western coasts. The Yellow Sea is shallow, warm in summer, and highly productive, but it suffers from low visibility — typically 3–8 metres — due to tidal mixing of the fine-silt bottom. What it offers in exchange is a nutrient-driven density of invertebrate life: enormous concentrations of sea cucumbers, mussel beds, and soft-bottom species rarely seen in clearer waters. Cuttlefish aggregate here in numbers during the spring spawning season. This is a specialist site for divers interested in Korean temperate-water biology rather than dramatic topography. Open Water level. Best April through June for cuttlefish; July through August for warmest water.

## Planning Your South Korea Dive Trip

Jeju Island is the entry point for almost all international diving visitors. Korean Air and Asiana operate regular flights from Seoul Gimpo to Jeju, and the 50-minute crossing is trivially easy. Seogwipo on Jeju's south coast is the dive town, with multiple shops offering equipment, guided dives, and certification. Most leading Seogwipo operators are PADI or SSI affiliated, and English-speaking guides are available at the larger centres. The best dive season for visibility and reasonable water temperature runs October through December; the warmest water is July and August. January through March provides clear water but very cold temperatures that demand a drysuit. Equipment rental is widely available on Jeju. Busan on the mainland provides access to Geoje Island, and the Mokpo ferry connects to Hongdo. South Korea rewards divers who come looking for something genuinely different from the tropics — the underwater world here is dramatic, historically layered with haenyeo culture, and almost entirely uncrowded by international standards.
