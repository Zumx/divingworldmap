Egypt is where millions of people first discover the underwater world, and the concentration of outstanding diving in the Red Sea makes it unlike almost anywhere else. The northern Red Sea is warm, clear and protected from oceanic swell by its narrow width and the sheltering peninsula of Sinai. The southern reaches, accessed from Hurghada and Marsa Alam, push into deeper open water and more exposed conditions. Between them lie the ruins of the most famous shipwreck in the world, some of the most dramatic coral walls in the Indo-Pacific, and small isolated reefs in the open sea that attract hammerheads, thresher sharks and oceanic white tips. Water temperature ranges from 20°C in winter to 30°C in summer; a 3mm wetsuit covers most of the year and a 5mm is only needed for winter diving at depth. Visibility regularly exceeds 20 metres and often reaches 30 to 40 metres at the offshore sites.

## 1. SS Thistlegorm, Northern Red Sea

The Thistlegorm is a British Armed Merchant Vessel sunk by German bombers in October 1941 while lying at anchor in the Strait of Gubal. The wreck sits in 18 to 30 metres with its cargo intact: motorcycles, trucks, weapons carriers, train engines and ammunition in the hold. The scale of the wreck — 126 metres long, with the cargo exactly as it was loaded for the Western Desert campaign — makes it the most compelling wreck dive in the Red Sea and one of the finest anywhere. Resident marine life includes enormous schools of batfish, glassfish, moray eels and a permanent population of large barracuda around the mast. Dive it in the early morning or evening to avoid the worst of the tourist boat traffic; the site can be crowded at peak times from Sharm el-Sheikh. Advanced Open Water recommended.

## 2. Ras Mohammed National Park, Sinai

Ras Mohammed sits at the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula where the Gulf of Aqaba and the Gulf of Suez converge, and the current-swept walls of Shark Reef and Yolanda Reef are among the most spectacular in the northern Red Sea. Shark Reef's western wall descends from 5 metres to well beyond recreational limits; the upper 30 metres are covered in hard and soft corals and populated by Napoleon wrasse, grey reef sharks, white tip sharks, barracuda and trevally schools. Yolanda Reef is famous for its secondary attraction: the SS Yolanda, a cargo ship whose hold spilled its cargo of bathroom fittings — toilets, bidets and bathtubs — across the reef slope, creating an absurdist dive backdrop. Current can be strong; Advanced certification recommended. Best season is September through November.

## 3. Blue Hole, Dahab

The Blue Hole is a vertical submarine sinkhole in the reef flat north of Dahab, descending to around 160 metres — far beyond recreational limits. Its notoriety rests partly on its beauty and partly on its history as the site of a disproportionate number of fatalities, mostly technical divers attempting to pass through the 'Arch' — a tunnel connecting the sinkhole to open water at 55 metres. Recreational divers can dive the rim of the Blue Hole at 18 to 30 metres and enjoy exceptional visibility, healthy coral, and pelagic species including Napoleon wrasse and reef sharks passing in the open water. Shore access makes it one of the most accessible Red Sea sites. Advanced Open Water covers the recreational sections; the depth temptation is real and must be actively resisted.

## 4. Brothers Islands (Al-Akhawein)

The Brothers are two isolated limestone pillars in the central Red Sea, 60 kilometres from the coast and accessible only by liveaboard. Big Brother, the larger island, has a lighthouse and walls that descend from the surface to beyond 200 metres; a British cargo ship and a supply vessel from the 1950s sit on ledges between 28 and 60 metres. Hammerhead sharks are common here from November through January; oceanic white tips pass in the blue water; thresher sharks are occasional visitors. The walls are covered in pristine soft coral — gorgonians, sea fans and black coral at depth — in a condition that reflects the limited visitor numbers and the current that constantly refreshes the water. Liveaboard only; Advanced certification essential.

## 5. Elphinstone Reef

Elphinstone is a narrow reef plateau sitting in open water 25 kilometres north of Marsa Alam, accessible by day boat. The reef's northern and southern tips are famous for shark encounters: oceanic white tip sharks patrol here year-round, and long-finned pilot whales have been documented. The wall drops sharply on both sides in visibility that regularly reaches 30 to 40 metres. Resident marine life on the wall itself — Napoleon wrasse, schools of jacks, anthias clouds — is outstanding. The current can be powerful at the tips; the platform mooring requires experienced boat handling and the dive itself requires drift skills. Advanced certification is the minimum expectation. Best season for sharks is October through March.

## 6. Abu Nuhas Wrecks, Northern Red Sea

Abu Nuhas ('Father of Teeth') is a reef in the Strait of Gubal that caught four large commercial vessels over the course of the 20th century, creating a unique wreck park at 10 to 30 metres. The Carnatic (1869), Chrisoula K (1981), Kimon M and Giannis D all sank on this reef within a few kilometres of each other. Each wreck has a different character: the Carnatic's wooden hull has collapsed into a photogenic tangle of ironwork; the Giannis D is the most intact with recognisable cargo holds; the Kimon M sits deeper at 30 to 35 metres with a fragile hull. Advanced Open Water is recommended for the deeper wrecks; the shallower portions of Carnatic are accessible to Open Water graduates.

## 7. Daedalus Reef (Abu Kizan), Southern Red Sea

Daedalus Reef is among the most remote accessible sites in Egyptian waters — an exposed reef table 80 kilometres from the coast, ringed by vertical walls and visited only by liveaboard. Hammerhead schools, thresher sharks, tiger sharks and grey reef sharks are all documented; pelagic billfish pass in the blue water. The upper sections of the reef are in excellent health precisely because access is so difficult. The walls descend vertically to beyond recreational limits; the shallowest diving is on the reef table at 5 to 15 metres. Current exposure can be intense at the north and south points. This is advanced liveaboard diving — strong drift experience is expected, and the remoteness means any issue is far from immediate assistance. Best season is October through March.

## 8. St. Johns Reef System, Marsa Alam

St. Johns is a complex of outlying reefs 200 kilometres south of Hurghada, accessible by liveaboard from Marsa Alam. The reef system encompasses several named dive sites — Habili Ali, Habili Jiffain and others — with walls, pinnacles and a very specific attraction: grey reef and white tip sharks in significant numbers. Manta rays are seasonal visitors from October through March. The coral here is in notably good condition; the distance from the main tourist hubs means limited anchor damage and better-maintained reef structure. Dive depths run 10 to 35 metres at most sites. Liveaboards from Marsa Alam typically include St. Johns in five to seven-night itineraries covering the south. Advanced certification recommended.

## 9. Shag Rock (Sha'ab Abu Nuhas periphery)

Shag Rock is a standalone pinnacle and reef structure in the northern Red Sea near the Abu Nuhas wreck site, less visited than its famous wreck neighbours and offering a different experience: living reef rather than wreck archaeology. Walls covered in table corals, gorgonians and sea fans descend to 30 metres; Napoleon wrasse, large moray eels and schools of sweet lip grouper are common. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres. The site suits divers who want to pair a wreck dive at Abu Nuhas with a reef dive without travelling far, and it provides good photographic contrast — the colour and movement of the living reef wall after the industrial still life of the wrecks. Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper sections.

## 10. Wadi Lahmy Housereef, Southern Red Sea

Wadi Lahmy sits in the far south of Egypt's Red Sea coast, close to the Sudanese border, and the stretch of coast here sees very limited mass tourism. The housereef accessible from the Wadi Lahmy Azur Beach Resort and Red Sea Diving Safari's Wadi Lahmy base offers immediate entry from the beach into a pristine fringing reef at 5 to 25 metres. The fish biomass here is noticeably higher than the heavily visited northern sites: schools of unicornfish, surgeonfish, large grouper and Napoleon wrasse treat divers with a lack of wariness that speaks to the reduced pressure. Dolphins pass through the outer reef regularly. Water temperature averages 26°C year-round on this stretch. Open Water certification is sufficient. Getting here requires driving the coast road south from Marsa Alam.

## Planning Your Trip

Egypt's main dive hubs are Sharm el-Sheikh in the north (gateway to the Thistlegorm, Ras Mohammed and the Strait of Gubal wrecks) and Hurghada/Marsa Alam further south (access to Elphinstone, Brothers, Daedalus and St. Johns). Both cities have extensive dive operator infrastructure. October through December is widely regarded as the best all-round season: warm water, high visibility, sharks active at the southern sites, and reduced summer crowds. Winter from December through February is also excellent, especially for pelagic species, though a 5mm suit is appreciated at depth. Liveaboards are the only way to access the Brothers and Daedalus; day-boat access from Hurghada reaches Elphinstone in around two hours.
