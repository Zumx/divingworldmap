Sabah, on the northeastern tip of Malaysian Borneo, is one of Asia's great diving destinations, fusing world-class wall diving with some of the planet's richest muck. Its crown jewel, Sipadan, is the country's only oceanic island, rising from a 600-metre abyss and pulling in turtles, sharks and barracuda in numbers that have made it legendary. A short boat ride away, the silty fringes of Mabul and Kapalai hide a parade of rare critters that draws macro photographers from around the world. Water temperatures hover at a warm 27 to 30°C year-round. Diving is possible all year, with the calmest seas and best visibility from April through December.

## 1. Barracuda Point, Sipadan

Barracuda Point is the most famous dive on Sipadan and one of the most celebrated drift dives on Earth. Divers descend onto a reef shelf and ride a current along a wall that drops into the deep blue, and at the corner they meet the spectacle that gives the site its name: a vast, swirling tornado of chevron barracuda, often thousands strong, spiralling slowly in the current. Green and hawksbill turtles are everywhere, reef sharks patrol the edge, and bumphead parrotfish crunch the coral. The current can be strong, so this is best for confident divers. Sipadan requires a permit, with daily numbers strictly limited.

## 2. Turtle Tomb (Drop Off), Sipadan

The wall directly off Sipadan's island jetty, known as Drop Off, plunges almost vertically from the surface to abyssal depth, and within it lies the eerie Turtle Tomb — a labyrinth of underwater caverns where turtles that lost their way are said to have perished, their skeletons still visible inside. Cave penetration is restricted and dangerous, but the wall itself is breathtaking, smothered in coral and patrolled by turtles, white-tip reef sharks and schooling jacks. Visibility is often spectacular. The site can be reached directly from the shore-style jetty entry, making it one of the most accessible great walls anywhere.

## 3. South Point, Sipadan

South Point is Sipadan's deeper, wilder corner, a stretch of wall and current that offers the island's best chance of encountering larger pelagics. Grey reef sharks, the occasional hammerhead and even thresher sharks have been recorded here, along with the resident green turtles and schooling jacks. The current can run hard and unpredictably, so the site is for experienced divers comfortable with drift and depth. When the conditions and the big animals align, South Point delivers some of the most thrilling diving Sabah has to offer, with the bottomless blue always just beyond the reef edge.

## 4. Mabul Island Muck

Mabul, the island base for many Sipadan trips, is itself one of the world's great muck-diving destinations. Sites around the island's sandy, silty slopes and jetty pilings at 3 to 18 metres harbour an extraordinary cast of macro life: flamboyant cuttlefish, blue-ringed octopus, frogfish, mandarinfish, ghost pipefish, mimic octopus and a long list of nudibranchs and crabs. The contrast with Sipadan's adrenaline could not be sharper — here the pace is slow and the rewards are tiny and bizarre. Mabul is a favourite of underwater photographers, who happily spend whole trips combing its unglamorous-looking sand.

## 5. Kapalai

Kapalai is not quite an island but a sandbar on a shallow reef between Mabul and Sipadan, home to a stilted water resort and some of the region's finest muck and macro diving. Its shallow sandy flats and seagrass beds at 5 to 15 metres are a hunting ground for ornate ghost pipefish, harlequin shrimp, mandarinfish at dusk, leaf scorpionfish and frogfish. The artificial structures and house reef provide rich habitat, and the shallow, calm conditions make for relaxed, photographer-friendly diving. Kapalai pairs naturally with Mabul and Sipadan for a trip that spans walls, muck and everything between.

## 6. Lankayan Island

In the Sulu Sea north of Sandakan, Lankayan is a tiny, remote island fringed by coral and surrounded by sandy flats famous for muck and the seasonal arrival of whale sharks. Several small wrecks and artificial reefs around the island draw schooling fish and macro critters, including jawfish, frogfish and a wealth of nudibranchs. Whale sharks visit from around March to May. The island is part of a marine conservation area, its calm, clear water and uncrowded reefs offering a tranquil contrast to the busier southeast coast. Visibility and conditions are best in the dry season.

## 7. Si Amil and Mataking

Si Amil is a remote, current-swept island east of Mabul that offers some of the area's healthiest hard coral and best chances of pelagic action away from the Sipadan permit system. Nearby Mataking, with its resort and an artificial wreck deliberately sunk as an underwater attraction, combines easy reef diving with macro on the house reef and the famous underwater mailbox. Together these eastern islands extend Sabah's diving beyond the famous trio, with coral gardens, drop-offs and a quieter feel. They reward divers willing to travel a little further out into the Celebes Sea.

## 8. Pulau Tiga

Off Sabah's west coast near Kota Kinabalu, Pulau Tiga — known to many as Survivor Island after the television series — sits within a marine park of three small islands formed partly by mud volcanoes. The diving here is gentler and more accessible than the famous east coast, with coral-covered slopes, soft coral gardens and reefs at 5 to 25 metres hosting reef fish, nudibranchs, cuttlefish and the occasional turtle. It makes an easy day trip from the state capital and an ideal site for newer divers or those basing themselves on the more developed west side of Sabah.

## 9. Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park

Just offshore from Kota Kinabalu, the Tunku Abdul Rahman Marine Park encompasses five islands — including Gaya, Sapi and Manukan — whose fringing reefs offer convenient, sheltered diving within sight of the city. Depths run 5 to 20 metres over coral and sand, with reef fish, anemones and clownfish, nudibranchs and frequent turtles. Conditions are calm and the sites suit beginners and check-out dives. While it cannot match Sipadan's drama, the park is invaluable as an accessible introduction to Sabah's underwater world and a relaxed counterpoint to the remote east coast.

## 10. Layang-Layang (Swallow Reef)

Far out in the South China Sea, around 300 kilometres northwest of Kota Kinabalu, Layang-Layang is an isolated atoll famous for sheer walls, gin-clear water and a springtime aggregation of scalloped hammerhead sharks. The atoll's walls plunge past 2,000 metres, with visibility frequently beyond 30 metres and pristine hard coral on the rim. Reef sharks, eagle rays, tuna and the chance of hammerheads from March to May draw experienced divers willing to make the long journey by air or liveaboard. Layang-Layang is Sabah at its most remote and pelagic — a true blue-water destination.
