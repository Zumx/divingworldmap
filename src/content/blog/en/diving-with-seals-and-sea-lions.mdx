## Pinnipeds Underwater: Two Very Different Animals

The casual use of 'seal' to cover all pinnipeds obscures a distinction that matters underwater. True seals — phocids — move on land by undulating their body like a caterpillar, their rear flippers useless on shore. Sea lions and fur seals — otariids — walk on four flippers and use their powerful fore-flippers to propel themselves through the water with a flying motion that generates remarkable speed and agility. The difference is visible the moment you are in the water with them: sea lions and fur seals are acrobatic, investigative, and playful in a way that seals rarely are. Seals are more likely to hold position in the water column, staring at you with their enormous dark eyes from a respectful distance.

Both families are curious about divers in different ways, but it is the sea lions — Californian sea lions, South American sea lions, and Australian sea lions in particular — that have generated the most celebrated pinniped diving encounters. A juvenile sea lion will bite at your fins, blow bubbles at your mask, and mimic your hovering position within seconds of entering the water. The experience is less like watching wildlife and more like being intercepted by dogs that can outmanoeuvre you in three dimensions.

## Hornby Island and British Columbia: Steller Sea Lions

The waters around Hornby Island off the coast of British Columbia in Canada offer encounters with Steller sea lions — the largest of the sea lion species, with bulls reaching 2.5 metres and 700 kilograms. Stellers are considerably less playful than Californian sea lions and considerably more impressive in the water. A large bull cruising past you at 5 metres depth in visibility that extends to 15 metres in the cold Pacific water is an experience of a different order from a colony of juveniles in warmer seas.

Water temperatures around Hornby and the Gulf Islands run 8 to 12 degrees Celsius for most of the year, and a 7mm wetsuit with hood and gloves, or a drysuit, is appropriate. The diving here is genuinely cold-water diving: rich kelp forests, nudibranchs, wolf eels, and the occasional lingcod sharing the habitat with Stellers hauled out on the rocky islets. The pinnipeds are most reliably encountered from autumn through early spring, when the males haul out on favourite rocks and juveniles patrol the kelp beds.

## Isla Espiritu Santo, Baja California: Californian Sea Lions

At the other end of the temperature scale, the Los Islotes sea lion colony off Isla Espiritu Santo near La Paz in Baja California Sur is one of the most reliably joyful dive experiences in the Pacific. Water temperatures here run 20 to 28 degrees Celsius depending on season, visibility is typically 10 to 20 metres, and the resident colony of several hundred animals includes a high proportion of juveniles who have been interacting with divers for decades.

The protocol at Los Islotes is to enter the water quietly and wait. Within seconds of descent, the juveniles arrive — testing your fins, spiralling around you in corkscrew patterns, and often hovering inverted just centimetres from your mask as if checking their own reflection. Adult males on the haul-out rocks pay minimal attention to divers in the water. The females are more watchful, particularly in pupping season from May through June, when the rocks are covered in newborn pups and the adults' protective instincts are elevated.

The best diving is at 5 to 12 metres, close to the colony rocks. Deeper water around the base of the islets, dropping to around 25 metres, holds occasional larger fish and the odd sea turtle, but the sea lion interaction is entirely in the shallows.

## The Farne Islands: Grey Seals in Cold North Sea Water

The Farne Islands off the coast of Northumberland in northeast England are home to one of the largest grey seal colonies in the world, with over 6,000 animals using the islands for breeding. Grey seals are phocids, not sea lions, and their behaviour underwater reflects the difference — but they are curious animals and at the Farnes they have been diving with visitors long enough to behave in ways that regularly surprise divers.

The water here is cold — 8 to 14 degrees Celsius — and green. Visibility is typically 4 to 8 metres, occasionally better after settled weather. The seals approach divers in an investigative rather than playful way: a juvenile may circle you slowly, reducing the radius until it is close enough to sniff your hand, then dart away. Large adult males are more imposing and less curious; encounters with breeding bulls during the autumn rutting season call for additional caution.

The diving at the Farnes is managed by local charter operators out of Seahouses, and the combination of seals, anemone-covered rock walls, large common lobster, and historic wreck sites in relatively shallow water makes it one of the most distinctive temperate-water dive experiences in Britain. ## Juanita's Kelp Forest, New Zealand: NZ Fur Seals

New Zealand fur seals dive at the Leigh Marine Reserve near Goat Island, north of Auckland, in one of the most accessible marine reserves in the country. Water temperatures here sit around 15 to 19 degrees Celsius — cooler than tropical diving but manageable in a 5mm suit — and the kelp forests support a rich community of snapper, crayfish, and the resident fur seals that haul out on the rocky points.

New Zealand fur seals are smaller than sea lions but equally curious with juveniles. They are particularly interested in bubbles from open-circuit regulators and will often position their faces directly in front of your exhaust and snap at the rising stream. The behaviour is clearly play rather than aggression — there is no tension in it — and it is one of those diver experiences that requires no special preparation or skill, just the willingness to stay still and let the animals set the pace of the encounter.

## South Georgia: Antarctic Fur Seals and Leopard Seals

South Georgia Island in the South Atlantic represents the extreme of cold-water pinniped diving. The island's beaches are covered in Antarctic fur seals during the breeding season from November through January, and the males are genuinely aggressive on land. Underwater encounters require experienced guides who understand individual animal behaviour and can read the warning signs of a bull's territorial response.

Leopard seals are the other encounter available to divers willing to reach Antarctic and sub-Antarctic waters. These animals are powerful predators — up to 3.5 metres in adult females — and the famous series of interactions documented by the wildlife photographer Paul Nicklen, where a leopard seal attempted to feed him with freshly killed penguins, captures something of the quality of these encounters: extraordinary, occasionally alarming, and entirely on the animal's terms.

## How to Behave Around Pinnipeds

The playfulness of sea lions creates a temptation to interact actively — reaching out to touch, following animals as they dart away, or attempting to mimic their movements so persistently that it crosses into harassment. The appropriate posture is passive: enter the water calmly, descend to a stable position, and allow the animals to determine the distance.

Do not dive directly at a haul-out rock during breeding season, when adult males are territorial and pups are vulnerable. Do not approach a seal or sea lion that is feeding — the same animal that cheerfully nibbles your fin during a reef dive may bite hard if it feels competition for food. Bites from sea lions in particular are medically significant due to a bacteria called Mycoplasma pinnipedii and should be treated seriously.

The encounters that stay with you longest are the ones in which you held still and let the animal come to you. [Open the map](/map) and explore the temperate and cold-water dive sites where pinniped encounters have been recorded by divers — some of the best are not famous, just local knowledge.

## Certification and Conditions

There is no specific certification for diving with pinnipeds, but the conditions associated with the best encounters typically require solid diving skills. Cold water, limited visibility, current, surge near rocky haul-out sites — these are demanding conditions for divers who trained in warm tropical pools. A PADI Dry Suit specialty, BSAC cold-water experience, or equivalent preparation is worth pursuing before a first cold-water pinniped dive. The animals are worth the effort.
