Hong Kong is not the first city most divers think of when planning an underwater trip, and that reputation for unimpressive diving is largely unearned. Beneath the visibility issues of the inner harbour and the congested Pearl River estuary, the outer islands and northeastern coast of the New Territories host dive sites of genuine quality. The marine parks at Hoi Ha Wan and Yan Chau Tong protect reef systems with over 80 species of hard coral — comparable in diversity to many better-known Indo-Pacific destinations. Sai Kung Country Park provides access by junk or tender to dozens of small islands with clean water, kelp beds, and a surprisingly diverse temperate-subtropical marine community. The diving here requires tolerance for limited visibility at some sites and a pragmatic attitude toward seasonal conditions — typhoon season restricts operations from July through September, and winter brings thermoclines that challenge thermal management. But for the diver willing to engage on its own terms, Hong Kong offers accessible, interesting diving within a short journey from one of Asia's great cities. Water temperatures range from 16°C in February to 29°C in August.

## 1. Sai Kung — Outer Islands

The sprawling Sai Kung peninsula and its offshore islands constitute the majority of Hong Kong's productive dive territory. The outer islands — Sharp Island, Kiu Tsui, and the more distant Crescent and Bluff Islands — are accessible by junk charter or speedboat from the Sai Kung waterfront and offer reef diving in 5–20 metres. Visibility here, away from Pearl River turbidity, regularly reaches 10–15 metres. The reef structure is dominated by boulders with encrusting sponges and tunicates, and the fish life includes grouper, snapper, scorpionfish, and in summer months, various species of fusiliers. Octopus, cuttlefish, and nudibranch species are the macro subjects that reward careful searching. Open Water level for most sites.

## 2. Hoi Ha Wan Marine Park

The most protected and most important reef system in Hong Kong, Hoi Ha Wan (White Flower Bay) on the northern Sai Kung coastline is designated as a marine park with anchoring restrictions and fishing bans strictly enforced. The hard coral coverage here — particularly the stands of Acropora and massive Porites colonies — is exceptional for subtropical waters. Visibility in the bay is variable, typically 5–10 metres, but coral diversity exceeds 80 species. Seahorses are resident in the seagrass beds near the mangrove margins, and the protected status has allowed fish populations to recover from historical overfishing. Open Water level; the bay's protection means calm conditions year-round when typhoons are absent.

## 3. Sharp Island (Kiu Tsui Chau)

One of the closest outer islands to Sai Kung town, Sharp Island has accessible reef diving on its sheltered northern side and more exposed reef on the eastern and southern faces. The northern lagoon offers a gentle introduction — boulders descending to 12 metres with good encrusting coral and abundant reef fish including the theatrical and distinctive batfish. The southern face, when conditions permit, drops more steeply and produces encounters with larger pelagics including cobia and the occasional hammerhead reported by local divers in summer months. Open Water for the sheltered north; Advanced for the exposed southern sites.

## 4. Ninepin Islands (Ping Chau)

The dramatic hexagonal basalt columns of the Ninepin Group rise from the sea south of Clear Water Bay and continue below the waterline as a dramatic underwater cliff and boulder landscape. The basalt formations create complex topography with overhangs, crevices, and swim-throughs at 8–20 metres, and the relatively clean oceanic water exposure means visibility is among the best available in Hong Kong waters. Lionfish are resident on the rocky substrate; cephalopods — cuttlefish and octopus — are frequently encountered. The exposed location means the site requires calm weather assessment. Advanced Open Water recommended; boat access from Sai Kung.

## 5. Yan Chau Tong Marine Park, Double Haven

The Double Haven area in the northeastern New Territories corner of Hong Kong, enclosed within the Yan Chau Tong marine park, is less visited than Sai Kung but offers productive reef diving in moderately clearer water due to its more northern position away from Pearl River outflow. The park protects seagrass beds, coral communities on the surrounding islands, and the enclosed bay creates calmer conditions. Dugong have been sighted in the seagrass beds — extremely rare for Hong Kong. Macro diving for nudibranch and flatworm species is productive. Open Water level; accessible by junk charter from Tai Mei Tuk.

## 6. Tung Ping Chau Marine Park

The most remote of Hong Kong's outer islands, Tung Ping Chau lies on the boundary between Hong Kong and Daya Bay. The island's flat-topped table rock extends as shallow reef, and the clarity here, exposed to the open South China Sea rather than the Pearl River plume, can reach 15+ metres — exceptional for Hong Kong. Coral growth on the flat reef plates is notable, and the fish community is more oceanic in character, with species uncommon elsewhere in Hong Kong waters. Accessible by ferry from Ma Liu Shui on weekends; the remote location means it is never crowded. Open Water level; good for photographers.

## 7. Po Toi Islands, South of Hong Kong Island

The Po Toi archipelago south of Hong Kong Island sees reliable visits from whale sharks between April and June — not guaranteed, but regularly enough that operators run specific trips during this period. The reef around the main island and its surrounding islets descends from 5 to 25 metres with a boulder and slab structure. Year-round fish diversity is high including sweetlips, goatfish, and schools of fusiliers. The outer southeastern face of the main island produces the best pelagic sightings. Advanced Open Water for the outer face; Open Water for the sheltered areas. A one-hour boat journey from Aberdeen.

## 8. HMS Tamar Site, Lamma Channel

During the colonial period, the Royal Navy hulk HMS Tamar served as a depot ship before being scuttled, and various remnant structures from the old dockyard are submerged in the Lamma Channel at manageable depths. The site is primarily of historical interest — encrusted metal structures between 10 and 20 metres — and the fish life attracted to the artificial reef structure includes resident grouper and a reliable population of banded coral shrimp in the crevices. Visibility in the channel varies. An accessible urban dive that combines historical context with a productive fish count. Open Water level.

## 9. High Island Reservoir — Underwater Quarry

The freshwater reservoir created by damming the eastern arm of Sai Kung in the 1970s flooded a section of coastal village, leaving submerged structures on the reservoir floor at 5–15 metres. The freshwater visibility is excellent — 10–20 metres is routine — and the submerged remains of old walls, piers, and agricultural structures create unusual photographic subjects. Freshwater species including various carp and tilapia have established themselves. The site is used for training dives by local clubs. Open Water level; a distinctive experience unique to Hong Kong's engineered landscape.

## 10. Fan Lau, Lantau Island

The southwestern tip of Lantau Island at Fan Lau faces the Pearl River estuary, which creates challenging visibility conditions at many times of year. However, after extended dry periods when the river flow decreases and cleaner oceanic water pushes into the area from the southwest, visibility at Fan Lau can improve dramatically. Indo-Pacific humpback dolphins — the pink dolphins for which Hong Kong is famous — are resident in these waters. Snorkelling with dolphins is the main activity here, but the reef around the headland offers accessible diving on a mixed coral and rocky substrate with a diverse fish community. Conditions require checking; Open Water level when visibility cooperates.

## Practical Information

The best diving window in Hong Kong runs from April through June, when water temperatures are warming, visibility is improving before summer typhoon turbidity, and whale sharks visit Po Toi. October through December offers a second window with clear post-typhoon water. Avoid diving in July and August when typhoon season and Pearl River outflow both degrade conditions significantly. Most operators work out of Sai Kung or Aberdeen on Hong Kong Island; junk charters provide the most flexible access to the outer islands.
