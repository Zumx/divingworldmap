There is a particular moment, familiar to any diver who has spent time on the reef, when a rock you have been staring at turns into an eye, then into an arm, and then dissolves backward into the substrate as an octopus pours itself away. Few encounters underwater are as quietly astonishing. Cephalopods — the octopuses, cuttlefish, squid and nautiluses — are the most behaviourally sophisticated invertebrates on Earth, and learning to find and read them transforms an ordinary dive into a study in alien intelligence.

## What Makes a Cephalopod

Cephalopods are molluscs, related to snails and clams, but they long ago abandoned the protective shell for speed, intelligence and an unmatched control over their own appearance. The name means head-foot, describing a body plan in which a ring of arms surrounds the mouth and the whole animal is dominated by a large, complex brain and a pair of camera-like eyes that rival our own. They are exclusively marine, found from tropical shallows to the abyssal deep.

The group divides into a few familiar branches. Octopuses have eight arms, no internal shell, and den in holes and crevices. Cuttlefish have eight arms plus two retractable feeding tentacles and an internal buoyancy organ, the cuttlebone. Squid are the streamlined open-water hunters. The nautilus, the ancient outlier, still carries its coiled external shell and drifts up from deep water at night. All share three hearts, blue copper-based blood, and a propensity to escape in a cloud of ink.

## The Masters of Camouflage

No animal on Earth controls its appearance like a cephalopod. Their skin contains layers of specialised cells: chromatophores, pigment sacs that expand and contract under direct muscular control to flash colour patterns in fractions of a second; iridophores and leucophores beneath them that reflect and scatter light to produce blues, greens and silvery whites; and papillae that contract to change the very texture of the skin from glass-smooth to spiky to mimic algae or rock.

The strangest part is that most cephalopods are colour-blind, possessing only a single visual pigment. How they match the colour of their surroundings so precisely remains partly mysterious; recent work suggests they may sense light directly through their skin. A cuttlefish settling onto a chequerboard of sand and rubble will reproduce the pattern across its body within a heartbeat, and a hunting octopus passing over a reef runs a continuous, rippling slideshow of disguises as the background changes beneath it.

## Octopus: The Solitary Genius

The octopus is the cephalopod divers most often meet and the one that most rewards patience. Spot the tell-tale midden of broken shells outside a den, hold still, and you may watch an animal that solves problems in real time — prying open clams, unscrewing jars in captivity, and using coconut shells and rocks as portable shelter and tools. Octopuses have roughly five hundred million neurons, two thirds of them distributed through the arms, so that each arm can taste, touch and partly decide for itself.

Particular species are dive celebrities. The day octopus of Indo-Pacific reefs hunts in the open and changes appearance constantly. The mimic octopus of Indonesian muck sites impersonates lionfish, sea snakes and flatfish by changing both colour and posture. And the blue-ringed octopus, no larger than a golf ball and found from Australia to Japan, carries enough tetrodotoxin to kill a person — its electric-blue rings a warning to be admired and never touched.

## Cuttlefish: The Hypnotists

Cuttlefish are, to many divers, the most charismatic of the group. They hover with an undulating fringe of fin, regard you with a sideways, oddly knowing eye, and hunt with a performance worth crossing oceans for. The broadclub cuttlefish of the Indo-Pacific stuns prey by running pulsing bands of dark colour down its arms in a hypnotic display before striking with its two tentacles faster than the eye can follow.

The giant Australian cuttlefish gathers each winter in the cool waters of Spencer Gulf in South Australia in one of the ocean's great breeding spectacles, tens of thousands crowding the rocky reefs near Whyalla, the males flushing through frantic colour displays to win mates and the smaller males disguising themselves as females to sneak past their rivals. Flamboyant cuttlefish, a thumb-sized walker of Indonesian and Philippine sand, pulses warning colours and is one of the great prizes of muck diving.

## Squid and the Deep-Water Outliers

Squid are harder to approach, built for speed and open water, but reef squid reward the diver who moves slowly. Bigfin reef squid hover in small schools over Indo-Pacific reefs, communicating with rapid colour changes and laying clusters of finger-like egg capsules among the coral. Divers on night dives sometimes encounter them hunting in the beam of a torch, and on blackwater dives over deep ocean a whole parade of larval and juvenile squid rises with the migrating plankton.

The chambered nautilus is the living fossil of the group, little changed in hundreds of millions of years, drifting up from several hundred metres at night in places like Palau and the Philippines and retreating to the depths by day. Encountering one, with its coiled shell and dozens of simple tentacles, is to meet the ancestor-form from which all the others evolved.

## Watching Cephalopods Responsibly

Cephalopods reward calm, slow divers and punish hurried ones with a puff of ink and a vanishing act. The best approach is to settle a respectful distance away, control your buoyancy, and let curiosity do the work — many octopuses and cuttlefish will approach a still diver and even reach out an exploratory arm. Avoid cornering an animal, blocking its escape route, or forcing it from its den, which causes real stress.

Never handle them. Beyond the lethal risk of the blue-ringed octopus, all cephalopods have a sharp beak and most can deliver a bite, and the oils and lotions on human skin damage their delicate surface. Keep torch beams off their eyes on night dives. These are short-lived animals — most octopuses live barely a year or two and die after breeding — so every encounter is with an individual on a brief, intense arc of life.
