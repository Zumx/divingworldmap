Raja Ampat, the Four Kings, lies off the northwest tip of West Papua at the very heart of the Coral Triangle, and it holds the richest marine biodiversity ever documented. Surveys have counted more than 1,700 reef fish species and around three quarters of all known hard coral species within this single archipelago of more than 1,500 islands. The diving here is defined by nutrient-rich currents that flow between the Pacific and Indian Oceans, fuelling reefs so dense with life that a single dive can feel overwhelming. Water temperature stays a warm 28 to 30°C year-round, and most diving is done from liveaboards or a handful of remote eco-resorts. Visibility varies with current and plankton, from a hazy 10 metres to a clear 30.

## 1. Cape Kri, Dampier Strait

Cape Kri off Kri Island holds the world record for the most fish species recorded on a single dive — 374, counted by ichthyologist Gerald Allen. The site is a sloping reef and ridge swept by current, where schooling fish stack up in walls: snapper, fusiliers, jacks, batfish and barracuda swirl over coral while reef sharks patrol below and giant trevally hunt the edges. The current can be strong, and a reef hook is often useful. This is the quintessential Raja Ampat dive — proof of why the region is considered the planet's most biodiverse reef.

## 2. Blue Magic, Dampier Strait

Blue Magic is a submerged seamount in the Dampier Strait that rises to within around 8 metres of the surface and is one of Raja Ampat's premier manta cleaning stations. Reef mantas, and occasionally the larger oceanic manta, circle the pinnacle to be cleaned, hovering in the current while wrasse tend them. The same nutrient flow draws wobbegong sharks resting on the reef, schooling barracuda and the full Coral Triangle cast of reef fish. Current is the price of admission — divers hook in and watch the show. Manta encounters peak when plankton blooms feed the strait.

## 3. Manta Sandy, Dampier Strait

Manta Sandy is a dedicated manta cleaning station on a sandy patch surrounded by coral bommies, where divers settle behind a marked line on the sand and wait for mantas to glide in. Several mantas at once is common, queuing over the cleaning stations as cleaner wrasse pick parasites from their gills and bellies. The protocol of staying behind the line keeps the encounter calm and the mantas relaxed. Depths are shallow, around 12 to 18 metres, making for long, comfortable bottom times. It is one of the most reliable manta experiences in all of Indonesia.

## 4. Sardine Reef, Dampier Strait

Sardine Reef, named for the dense baitfish that once swarmed it, is a current-swept reef famous for explosive fish action. Walls of fusiliers and snapper part as schools of jacks and barracuda cut through, and grey reef and blacktip sharks cruise the deeper water. The reef itself is plastered in hard and soft coral and sea fans. Like most Dampier Strait sites it is current-dependent, best dived on a running tide that concentrates the predators and prey. Strong conditions make it an advanced dive, but the payoff is some of the most kinetic reef diving anywhere.

## 5. Misool's Boo Windows

In the remote south, Misool is Raja Ampat's wilderness of mushroom-shaped limestone islands, and Boo is among its signature dives. The rock here is pierced by two windows — swim-through holes near the surface, framed by soft coral, through which divers pass into clouds of glassfish. The pinnacle drops to around 30 metres and is encrusted with some of the most spectacular soft coral in the region, in vivid reds, oranges and purples. Pygmy seahorses cling to the gorgonians. Misool's reefs, protected within a private marine reserve, are among the healthiest on Earth.

## 6. Magic Mountain (Shadow Reef), Misool

Magic Mountain is a seamount off southern Misool that doubles as a cleaning station for both reef and oceanic manta rays — one of the few places they overlap. The pinnacle rises from deep water to around 7 metres, drawing mantas, schooling fish and pelagic hunters into a single dramatic site. Soft corals and fans cover the rock, and the current that brings the mantas also brings the action. Because it sits in open water away from sheltering islands, conditions can be demanding, but on a good day it is one of the most rewarding dives in Misool.

## 7. Melissa's Garden, Penemu

In the Fam Islands near Penemu, Melissa's Garden is celebrated for its pristine hard coral — a shallow reef and bommies where staghorn, table and brain corals form an unbroken carpet barely touched by bleaching or damage. The site is gentler than the current-driven Dampier sites, with depths from 5 to 25 metres, making it ideal for relaxed diving and photography of the coralscape itself. Anthias and damselfish swarm the coral heads, turtles graze the slopes, and the clear water shows off one of the most intact reef gardens in the archipelago.

## 8. Cross Wreck, near Sorido

Cross Wreck is a small Japanese-era patrol boat lying upright in around 25 metres near the Kri area, marked by a cross planted on a nearby shore. Now thickly encrusted in soft coral, sponges and hydroids, the little wreck is a magnet for macro life: ghost pipefish, frogfish, nudibranchs and pygmy seahorses on the surrounding fans. Schooling baitfish often fill the water column above it. It is an accessible, sheltered dive that offers a complete change of pace from the big-current reefs, and a favourite for muck and macro photographers.

## 9. Aljui Bay (Pearl Farm), Waigeo

Aljui Bay on the west of Waigeo is a sheltered, nutrient-rich inlet that hosts a working pearl farm and some of Raja Ampat's best muck and macro diving. The jetties and the bay's silty slopes harbour an astonishing cast of critters — flamboyant cuttlefish, mandarinfish, ornate ghost pipefish, wunderpus and a long list of nudibranchs and shrimp. The calm, current-free conditions and shallow depths make for long, methodical dives with a sharp-eyed guide. It is the place to come when the reefs have wowed you and you crave the strange and the small.

## 10. The Passage, between Waigeo and Gam

The Passage is a narrow saltwater channel — almost a marine river — separating Waigeo from Gam Island, where current rips between the islands at tidal change. Diving it feels like flying down a flooded jungle gorge: mangrove roots and limestone walls hung with soft coral, archerfish and shafts of light overhead, and a meeting of reef and estuary life found nowhere else. Depths are shallow, but the current dictates timing absolutely, and dives must be planned around slack and the running tide. It is one of Raja Ampat's most atmospheric and unusual sites.
