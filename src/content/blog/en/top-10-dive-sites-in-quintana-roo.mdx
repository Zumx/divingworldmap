The Mexican state of Quintana Roo, running down the Caribbean edge of the Yucatan Peninsula, offers a diving experience found almost nowhere else: world-class coral reefs, an underwater sculpture museum, and the otherworldly flooded cave systems known as cenotes, all within a short drive of one another. The peninsula is a vast limestone slab riddled with the longest underwater cave systems on the planet, while offshore the Mesoamerican Reef — the second-largest barrier reef on Earth — runs the length of the coast. The Caribbean water is warm year-round, 26 to 29°C, and astonishingly clear, and the diving spans gentle reef drifts, technical cave exploration and the surreal stillness of a cenote where sunbeams pierce the dark. From Cozumel to Tulum, this is one of the most varied diving regions in the world.

## Palancar Reef, Cozumel

Cozumel, the island off Playa del Carmen, is one of the planet's premier drift-diving destinations, and Palancar Reef is its crown jewel. A series of coral formations — Palancar Gardens, Caves and Bricks — stretches along the island's protected southwest shore, where strong, reliable currents carry divers effortlessly past towering coral pinnacles, swim-throughs and sand channels. The water clarity routinely exceeds 30 metres, and the reef teems with splendid toadfish found only here, eagle rays, turtles and nurse sharks. The current does the work; the diver simply floats and watches the reef glide by.

## Santa Rosa Wall, Cozumel

Santa Rosa Wall is Cozumel's most celebrated wall dive, a sheer drop-off plunging into the deep blue of the Cozumel Channel. The wall is riddled with tunnels, caves and overhangs draped in sponges and gorgonians, and the brisk current sweeps divers along the face past groupers, turtles and schooling fish. It is an advanced drift dive demanding good buoyancy and comfort with current, but the scale of the wall and the richness of life clinging to it make it unforgettable. The Cozumel reefs are part of a protected national marine park.

## The MUSA Underwater Museum, Cancun

The Museo Subacuatico de Arte off Cancun and Isla Mujeres is one of the largest underwater art installations in the world, with over 500 life-size concrete sculptures by Jason deCaires Taylor resting on the sandy seabed. Designed to grow into an artificial reef, the figures — a circle of people, a Volkswagen Beetle, a man at a desk — are slowly being colonised by coral and sponges. At a shallow depth of around eight metres, MUSA is accessible to divers and snorkellers alike, and the haunting, slowly transforming figures make it one of the most photographed dives in the Caribbean.

## Cenote Dos Ojos, Tulum

Cenote Dos Ojos near Tulum is the most famous cenote dive in the Riviera Maya, part of one of the longest underwater cave systems on Earth. The two interconnected sinkholes that give it its name — the two eyes — open into crystalline freshwater of staggering clarity, where divers follow guidelines through cavern zones lit by shafts of daylight. The Barbie Line and the Bat Cave route showcase stalactites, stalagmites and the eerie blue light of the cenote, all within recreational cavern-diving limits when guided. It is the perfect introduction to the underworld of the Yucatan.

## Cenote Dos Pisos and the Cave Systems

Cenote Dos Pisos, part of the vast Sac Actun system near Tulum, offers experienced cave divers two distinct levels of passage — the upper and lower floors that give it its name — through decorated chambers of remarkable formations. The Yucatan's cenotes are connected by hundreds of kilometres of flooded tunnels, making this the world capital of cave diving. Full cave certification is mandatory beyond the cavern zone, and the diving is as demanding as it is beautiful, with the famous haloclines where fresh and saltwater meet creating shimmering, blurred boundaries in the water.

## Cenote Angelita, Tulum

Cenote Angelita, south of Tulum, is one of the most surreal dives anywhere. At around 30 metres, a dense layer of hydrogen sulphide forms a cloudy false bottom that looks exactly like a river running through a forest, complete with tree branches and leaves protruding from the murk. Divers descend through clear freshwater, hover above the eerie cloud, and can dip below it into the salty, hazy darkness beneath. It is an advanced dive requiring good buoyancy and depth control, and an experience that genuinely defies description.

## The Gran Cenote, Tulum

The Gran Cenote, just outside Tulum, is one of the most accessible and beautiful cavern dives in the region, a network of clear pools and caverns adorned with dazzling stalactites and stalagmites. Within the cavern zone, divers and snorkellers glide through crystalline water past rock formations and the occasional freshwater turtle, with shafts of sunlight illuminating the chambers. Its easy access and gentle conditions make it a favourite first cenote experience, and a reminder of the geological wonder hidden beneath the Yucatan jungle.

## Cozumel's Columbia Reef

Columbia Reef at the southern end of Cozumel rivals Palancar for grandeur, a deep reef of enormous coral pinnacles rising from the sand, separated by canyons and swim-throughs. The deeper sections are an advanced drift dive where the towering coral heads, some the size of buildings, host turtles, eagle rays, groupers and the occasional reef shark. The shallower Columbia Shallows offer a gentler garden of coral and abundant fish, making the area suitable across a range of experience levels.

## Punta Sur and the Devil's Throat, Cozumel

At Cozumel's southern tip, Punta Sur is the island's most demanding and dramatic dive, home to the famous Devil's Throat — a deep coral tunnel that drops through the reef and exits onto the wall at depth. This is an advanced dive requiring excellent buoyancy, good gas management and comfort in current and confined space, but the journey through the cathedral-like coral passages and out onto the sponge-draped wall is among the most exhilarating in the Caribbean. It rewards experienced divers with a sense of genuine exploration.

## Akumal and the Turtle Bay

Akumal, whose name means place of the turtles in Maya, lies between Playa del Carmen and Tulum and is famed for its resident green sea turtles that graze the seagrass beds in a shallow, protected bay. While the bay itself is a snorkelling site with strict regulations to protect the turtles, the surrounding reefs offer pleasant diving among coral, rays and reef fish. Akumal makes an ideal base for combining gentle reef diving with cenote trips inland, capturing the full range of what Quintana Roo offers.
