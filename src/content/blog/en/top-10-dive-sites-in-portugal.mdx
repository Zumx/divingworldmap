Portugal's underwater geography is spread across three very different environments: the Atlantic-facing mainland coast, the mid-Atlantic island group of the Azores, and the subtropical archipelago of Madeira sitting roughly 600 kilometres off the Moroccan coast. Each has a distinct character. The mainland offers cold, productive Atlantic water with strong biodiversity at sites like the Berlengas natural reserve. Madeira brings warm, clear water and dramatic walls. The Azores are in a class of their own — deep oceanic seamounts that rise from the abyssal plain, generating upwellings that attract large pelagic animals: blue sharks, mako sharks, whale sharks, mobula rays, and in peak season, manta rays in numbers that would be extraordinary anywhere else. Water temperatures span a considerable range: 14°C in winter on the mainland, 24°C at the surface in the Azores in summer. ## 1. Princess Alice Bank, Azores

Princess Alice Bank — known in Portuguese as Banco D. João de Castro — is a seamount that rises from 2,000 metres to within 65 metres of the surface in the central Azores, between Faial and the tiny island of Formigas. The water above it concentrates pelagic life in a way that is difficult to overstate. Manta rays aggregate here in numbers that during peak season (July to September) routinely reach the hundreds — estimates of 200 or more mantas in a single dive have been recorded. Schooling mobulas join them. Blue sharks are present virtually every day. Conditions are exposed, with ocean swells and currents that can be demanding; this is liveaboard territory, accessible from Faial or Terceira. Technical divers run decompression dives to the top of the bank. Advanced Open Water with current experience is appropriate for the blue-water dives in the water column above the bank.

## 2. Azores Seamounts, Faial and Pico

The Azores archipelago sits on a tectonic triple junction where the North American, Eurasian and African plates meet, producing a series of seamounts and underwater volcanic structures in addition to the islands themselves. The dive sites around Faial and Pico include sheer volcanic walls dropping from 5 metres to beyond recreational limits, fumarole fields where warm water bubbles through the rock (particularly dramatic around Faial, which had a major eruption in 1957), and pristine hard coral gardens at mid-depths. The visibility in clear conditions exceeds 30 metres. Mobula rays, blue sharks and occasionally whale sharks are reported year-round around the deeper seamounts; loggerhead sea turtles are resident. Liveaboards operate the outer banks; shore-based diving from Horta on Faial accesses the inner sites. Best season is May through October.

## 3. Berlengas Islands, Atlantic Coast

The Berlengas are a tiny granite archipelago 15 kilometres off the fishing port of Peniche, declared a biosphere reserve and nature park. The diving is cold — 15°C in summer, dropping to 12°C in winter — and the visibility averages 8 to 15 metres, with occasional remarkable clarity after prolonged northerly winds push cleaner Atlantic water onto the site. The cold water drives extraordinary biodiversity: octopus, conger eels, moray eels, sea horses, triggerfish, grouper and enormous numbers of commercial fish species that are protected by the reserve's no-fishing zones. The underwater topography is dramatic — granite boulders the size of houses stacked at odd angles, creating swim-throughs, caves and overhangs. The main site is the Papoa Tunnel system at 10 to 20 metres. A 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is appropriate. Daily boats from Peniche run from June through September; winter access is weather-dependent.

## 4. Madeira, South Coast Walls

Madeira's volcanic origins produce steep underwater walls and dramatic drop-offs close to shore, and the water — influenced by the warm North Atlantic gyre — is significantly warmer and clearer than the Portuguese mainland. The south coast sites around Funchal and Garajau sit in a marine reserve where fishing is restricted, and the density of large fish reflects that protection. Grouper of 20 kg are not unusual; barracuda school along the shallower sections of the walls. The Garajau Christ statue at 14 metres, placed as an artificial reef, has developed a thick covering of invertebrates and is surrounded by fish. Further west, the rocky coast near Ribeira Brava and the Calheta area offers wall and boulder diving in excellent visibility — 20 to 25 metres in settled conditions. Water temperature runs from 18°C in March to 24°C in September. Open Water certification is sufficient for most Madeira sites.

## 5. Sao Miguel, Azores

Sao Miguel is the largest island in the Azores and the most accessible, with frequent flights from Lisbon. The diving is centred on the south coast, where volcanic walls and rock pinnacles drop from 5 to 35 metres in water that often reaches 22°C in summer. The underwater volcanic heritage is visible in the form of basalt formations — hexagonal columns, lava tubes and arches — that make the topography distinctive from any other diving in the region. Marine life includes resident Atlantic wrasse species, moray eels, octopus and loggerhead turtles. Visibility is good — 15 to 25 metres on the cleaner days — though Sao Miguel does not quite match the clarity of the western islands like Faial or Pico. Several dive centres operate out of Ponta Delgada, and the island's combination of above-water geothermal attractions (hot springs, crater lakes) and underwater diving makes it a good introduction to the Azores.

## 6. Formigas Reef, Azores

Formigas — the ants — is a tiny, barely-emergent rock about 45 kilometres east of Sao Miguel, and its underwater structure is a series of pinnacles and walls rising from depth to the surface. The site is remote enough that it sees relatively few divers outside of liveaboard itineraries. What it offers is consistently good pelagic activity: schooling barracuda, amberjack, blue sharks and mobula rays are regular. The hammerhead population in the Azores is centred partly around Formigas, and encounters in the 15 to 25 metre zone above the deeper pinnacles are reported by liveaboard guides. The site is weather-exposed, and conditions can deteriorate quickly. Advanced Open Water with current experience; accessible primarily by liveaboard, though some day-trip operators run out of Sao Miguel in calm conditions.

## 7. Pico Island Walls, Azores

Pico is dominated by the highest mountain in Portugal — Mount Pico rises to 2,351 metres above sea level — and the volcanic drama continues below the waterline. The west coast of Pico, around Madalena, has some of the most photogenic wall diving in the Azores. Basalt columns and lava formations give way to sheer drops that begin at 5 metres and continue past the recreational limit. Sperm whales inhabit the deep water between Pico and Faial year-round; while surface encounters are possible from cetacean-watching boats, the whales are not dive-accessible. However, seeing them from the surface before or after a dive is a routine part of the Pico diving experience. Mid-depth sites between 15 and 25 metres hold abundant reef fish and frequent manta sightings outside the Princess Alice peak months. Water is warmest from July to October.

## 8. Graciosa and Terceira, Azores

These two eastern Azores islands are less visited by divers than Faial, Pico and Sao Miguel but offer very similar quality in a less crowded setting. Graciosa has a small dive centre and access to volcanic reef structures at 10 to 30 metres. Terceira has a wider dive infrastructure based in Angra do Heroismo, including sites with resident loggerhead turtles and clear water around the headlands. Both islands benefit from the same pelagic activity as the rest of the archipelago; blue sharks and mobulas are reported at the outer sites. For divers who want the Azores experience — warm clear water, pelagics, volcanic topography — without the crowds that Faial and Pico now attract in high season, the eastern islands are worth considering.

## 9. Sesimbra, Near Lisbon

Sesimbra is the best dive site within easy reach of Lisbon — about 45 kilometres south — and it is sheltered from the prevailing northwest Atlantic swell by the Arrábida massif, which means conditions are more reliably calm than the exposed stretch of coast to the north. The diving is on rocky reefs at 5 to 25 metres, with a variety of Atlantic species: conger eels, moray eels, large grouper, various bream, and an abundance of sea urchins and starfish. A fish market operates in the port above; the marine life density below suggests the fishing pressure inside the reserve boundaries is effectively managed. Visibility averages 10 to 15 metres. Water temperature is cold by Azorean standards — 15 to 19°C in summer — requiring a 5mm minimum. A small local dive centre runs regular boat dives from the harbour.

## 10. Natural Reserve of the Arrábida, Setubal

The Arrábida natural reserve protects a stretch of coastline southwest of Setubal where the limestone cliffs of the Serra da Arrábida meet a marine park with restricted fishing. The underwater topography is varied: rocky reefs, caves, walls and sandy channels between 5 and 25 metres. What distinguishes Arrábida from other mainland sites is visibility — the turquoise clarity of the water here in summer, driven by a combination of geology and protected status, can reach 15 to 20 metres, which is exceptional for the Portuguese Atlantic coast. The reef holds substantial octopus populations, large grouper, and sea horses. The caves along the base of the underwater cliffs shelter conger eels and moray eels. Day trips run from Setubal and from the beach resorts inside the park, with several dive operators working the season from May through October.

## Planning Your Portugal Dive Trip

For mainland diving, the season runs from May through October; winter conditions are rough and cold. Madeira is diveable year-round, with best conditions from May through November. The Azores peak season is May through October, with July to September the prime window for pelagic encounters at Princess Alice Bank. Portugal's dive infrastructure is mature on the main islands and in the Azores; equipment hire and Nitrox are available from major centres. Liveaboard operators based in the Azores run week-long itineraries covering multiple islands and the outer banks. Cold-water diving on the mainland requires at least a 5mm wetsuit in summer; a drysuit is more comfortable for winter Berlengas dives.
