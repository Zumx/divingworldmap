Stretching in a long arc between Kyushu and Taiwan, the Ryukyu Islands give Okinawa some of Japan's warmest and clearest water — a subtropical zone where the Kuroshio Current sweeps nutrients and pelagics past coral that would not look out of place in the Coral Triangle. Surface temperatures climb to 29°C in late summer and rarely fall below 21°C even in February, so a 3mm wetsuit covers most of the year. The diving spans an astonishing range: hammerhead schools off remote Yonaguni, blue-tinted coral gardens around the Kerama Islands, sea-turtle pastures near Zamami and macro-rich muck on Okinawa-Honto's protected coasts. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres on the offshore reefs.

## 1. Yonaguni Monument

At Japan's far western edge, the Yonaguni Monument is the Ryukyus' most famous and most debated dive — a vast stepped sandstone formation rising from around 25 metres to within 5 metres of the surface. Whether it is a natural fracture pattern or a man-made terrace remains contested among geologists, but the right-angled platforms, the channels and the so-called turtle and stage features make for unforgettable swimming. The current here can be ferocious, so the site demands Advanced certification and a settled forecast. Winter, from December to March, also brings schooling scalloped hammerheads to the island's northern points. Water temperature sits around 22°C in that season.

## 2. Manta Way, Yonaguni

Just off Yonaguni's coast, Manta Way and the nearby cleaning stations draw reef mantas that hover over the bommies while wrasse pick them clean. Depths run 10 to 20 metres over a reef cut by surge channels, and the same Kuroshio flow that makes the monument tricky delivers the plankton the mantas feed on. Encounters are most reliable in winter and early spring. Hammerheads sometimes pass in the blue beyond the reef edge, and the island's sheer underwater topography — drop-offs and arches — gives the site real drama even between big-animal sightings.

## 3. Kerama Islands Coral Gardens

The Kerama Islands, a short ferry ride west of Naha, were designated a national park in 2014 largely for their reefs. The water here glows an almost luminous turquoise the Japanese call Kerama Blue, and visibility routinely passes 40 metres. Shallow coral gardens at 5 to 15 metres around Zamami and Aka islands support healthy hard-coral cover, anthias clouds and reef fish in extraordinary density. The gentle conditions on the leeward sites suit Open Water divers and snorkellers, while current-swept points offer more for experienced visitors. Green and hawksbill turtles are near-guaranteed.

## 4. Zamami Turtle Reefs

Zamami in the Keramas has become one of Japan's most dependable places to dive with sea turtles. Sites such as those around Ama and Furuzamami beaches hold seagrass beds and coral patches at 5 to 18 metres where green turtles graze, often unbothered by divers hovering a respectful distance away. The shallow, sheltered nature of many sites makes them ideal for newer divers and for long, relaxed bottom times. Summer brings the calmest seas; winter occasionally delivers humpback whale song carrying through the water from the migrating pods that pass the islands.

## 5. Cape Maeda and the Blue Cave

On Okinawa-Honto's west coast near Onna, Cape Maeda is the island's most popular shore dive thanks to the Blue Cave — a shallow grotto where sunlight refracting through the entrance fills the water with an electric blue glow. Entry is via a staircase down the rocky cape; depths range from the surface to around 20 metres on the reef outside. Hard coral, schooling fish and the occasional turtle populate the slope. The site can get crowded, so early starts are wise, and surge can build in onshore winds. It suits Open Water divers in calm conditions.

## 6. Sunabe Seawall

The Sunabe Seawall in Chatan is Okinawa's classic muck and macro dive, a long concrete-armoured shore beloved of underwater photographers and the local diving community. Entry points along the wall lead onto a gently sloping reef and sand at 5 to 18 metres where the macro life is the star: nudibranchs, frogfish, mandarinfish, pipefish, ghost pipefish and a parade of cephalopods. Because it is a shore dive accessible at almost any tide, it is a favourite for night diving, when mandarinfish spawn at dusk and the reef wakes with hunting octopus and crustaceans.

## 7. Ie Island Drop-offs

Ie Island, visible from the Motobu Peninsula and reached by a short ferry, offers some of northern Okinawa's most dramatic topography. Dive sites here feature vertical walls, caverns and swim-throughs dropping well past 30 metres, with strong Kuroshio current that ushers in pelagic visitors. Sharks, large schools of jacks and big groupers patrol the deeper reaches, while the shallows hold typical Ryukyu coral. The site rewards Advanced divers comfortable with current and depth. Visibility is often spectacular, regularly exceeding 30 metres on the offshore faces.

## 8. Sesoko Island

Off the Motobu Peninsula, Sesoko Island combines easy access with reefs healthy enough to draw divers from across the main island. The reef edges drop to around 25 metres, and the mix of hard coral, soft coral and sandy patches supports turtles, garden eels, lionfish and large schools of fusiliers. Conditions are generally calm on the sheltered side, making Sesoko a strong choice for relaxed reef diving and for training dives. The bridge connecting the island to the mainland means much of the diving is accessible without a boat.

## 9. Irabu and Shimoji Caves, Miyako

The Miyako island group, southwest of Okinawa-Honto, is famous for the labyrinthine cave and arch systems off Irabu and Shimoji islands. Sites such as the Antonio Gaudi formation and the W-shaped arch are a cathedral of light beams piercing through holes in the limestone, with depths from 10 to 30 metres. Visibility here is among the best in Japan, often beyond 40 metres. The complex overhead environments demand good buoyancy and ideally cavern experience, but the reward is some of the most photogenic structure diving in the country.

## 10. Kume Island and Ou Reef

Kume Island, west of the Keramas, is best known for Ou Shima — a vast reef plateau exposed at low tide that becomes a shallow diving and snorkelling playground, and for deeper sites along the island's edges where current brings in larger fish. The plateau's sheltered pools and the surrounding coral at 5 to 20 metres make it accessible, while channels off the reef can deliver eagle rays, turtles and the occasional grey reef shark. Kume's relative remoteness keeps the reefs in fine condition and the dive sites uncrowded compared with Naha's nearer options.
