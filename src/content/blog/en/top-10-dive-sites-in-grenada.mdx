Grenada is one of the Caribbean's most underrated dive destinations, and the divers who discover it tend to return repeatedly. The Spice Isle sits at the southern end of the Lesser Antilles just above the Venezuelan coast, and its relative proximity to the Atlantic creates conditions — upwellings of cooler, nutrient-rich water, modest currents, excellent visibility — that sustain richer marine life than many of its more-visited Caribbean neighbours. The island offers exceptional wreck diving, most famously the Bianca C, but also has a genuinely world-class marine sculpture park, healthy fringing reefs, and the consistent pelagic encounters that come from a productive mixed water column. Grenada sits below the hurricane belt's most destructive track, and while storms have impacted the reefs in the past, they recover well given the general health of the water. Temperatures run 26–29°C year-round; visibility is typically 20–30 metres. The island's dive operations are professional, typically small-group, and guided by people who have spent years on these specific sites.

## 1. Bianca C Wreck

The Bianca C was a 184-metre Italian ocean liner that caught fire and sank off Grenada in October 1961. She now lies in 48–52 metres of water off the southwestern coast, the largest wreck in the Caribbean accessible to recreational and technical divers. The bow section is the shallowest part of the site — around 28 metres — making it reachable on extended range recreational dives, while the midship and stern sections extend to depths requiring technical equipment. The wreck is heavily colonised by black coral, orange cup corals, and enormous barrel sponges; resident populations of large grouper, green moray eels, and schooling fish make the site biologically rich. Advanced Open Water minimum; technical certification for full wreck exploration.

## 2. Molinere Underwater Sculpture Park

Jason deCaires Taylor's installation of over 65 cement human figures on the seafloor north of St George's is one of the most distinctive dive sites in the world. The sculptures, some arranged in a circle and others posed individually on the sandy bottom between 5 and 8 metres, are now richly colonised by corals, sponges, and encrusting organisms — the transformation from stark cement to living reef is the point. Schools of fish hover around and between the figures; the shallow depth and calm conditions make the site accessible to Open Water divers, snorkellers, and even beginner freediving. Night dives here, when octopus emerge and the figures take on a different character in torch light, are particularly memorable.

## 3. Flamingo Bay

Located on the southwest coast, Flamingo Bay offers a reef slope dropping from 10 to 25 metres with consistently excellent coral coverage and an unusually high density of flamingo tongues — the bright orange spotted cowrie shells that are the site's namesake, found grazing on sea fans throughout the dive. Hawksbill turtles are resident and approachable; eagle rays pass through regularly on the outer sand at depth. The site is sheltered by the headland and generally calm, making it accessible to Open Water divers while still providing enough depth and variety for Advanced and experienced divers. Productive for both wide-angle and macro photography.

## 4. Shark Reef, Isle de Ronde

North of Grenada's main island, the satellite island of Isle de Ronde is surrounded by reef systems that see far fewer divers than the sites closer to St George's. The area known as Shark Reef on the island's southern side produces reliable nurse shark sightings, and the occasional Caribbean reef shark patrols the deeper edge. The reef structure here features large overhangs and swim-throughs between 15 and 25 metres, with healthy sea fan and black coral growth. The additional distance from the main harbour keeps visitor numbers low. Advanced Open Water for the deeper sections; boat only.

## 5. Happy Valley, Grenada Bank

On the shallow bank extending south from the main island, Happy Valley is characterised by a series of dome-shaped coral formations rising from a sandy plain at 18 metres. Each dome is thickly colonised with sea plumes, orange cup corals, and various encrusting species, and the gaps between them shelter nurse sharks, spotted eels, and vast quantities of small reef fish. Eagle rays cross the sand between domes regularly. The site's name reflects its generally pleasant, low-stress nature — moderate depth, no significant current, good visibility. Open Water to Advanced. Reliable for consistently productive dives on any conditions.

## 6. Dragon Bay Wall

On the Atlantic-facing northeastern coast of Grenada, Dragon Bay offers the island's most dramatic wall diving. The reef edge drops from around 8 metres to beyond 30 in a near-vertical face festooned with black coral starting at about 20 metres and large sea fans reaching out into the current on the upper wall. Barracuda schools aggregate at the wall's edges, and hawksbill turtles browse the sponge community. The Atlantic exposure means conditions require assessment before diving; calm weather windows on this coast are typically shorter than on the Caribbean side. Advanced Open Water; best in the dry season from January through April.

## 7. Watersports, St George's Harbour Mouth

The site at the harbour entrance south of St George's is accessible for training and discovery dives, with a shallow reef beginning at 5 metres and extending to about 18. It is the site where many Grenadian divers first see reef and where operators run referral dives for newly certified visitors. The fish life has habituated to divers over many years and is consequently bold — grouper hover close, French angelfish approach within a metre, and the resident turtle shows complete indifference to human presence. Not a dramatic dive, but reliable and productive for beginning an introduction to Grenadian marine life.

## 8. Boss Reef, Northern Coast

An exposed reef extending from the northern coast that functions as a current-swept feeding station for pelagics. The structure features a flat-topped reef at 12 metres that drops steeply on the seaward side to 30 metres and beyond. Schools of Atlantic horse-eye jacks circle the reef constantly, and large tarpon hover in the current on the deeper side. During October to January, manta rays have been reliably sighted at this site using it as a cleaning station — the resident cleaner wrasse establish cleaning territories on the reef top and the mantas circle in to be attended. Advanced Open Water for the exposed sections; the reef top suits Open Water divers.

## 9. Purple Rain, Southwest Reef Complex

Named for the extraordinary density of purple tube sponges on its reef walls, Purple Rain sits at the southern end of the main reef complex and descends from 10 to 28 metres. The sponge growth here is some of the most impressive in Grenada — single tube sponges exceeding 60 centimetres in diameter stand in clusters along the wall. The soft coral gardens on the upper slope are habitat for spotted drum juveniles, which hover in their characteristic circular motion under coral plates. French and grey angelfish are abundant. Open Water to Advanced; excellent for photographers. Generally calm and accessible.

## 10. Kick-em-Jenny Submarine Volcano

Grenada's most unusual dive environment is technically not around Grenada itself but at the Kick-em-Jenny submarine volcano 9 kilometres north of the island. This active volcano rises from the seafloor to within 160 metres of the surface, and the hydrothermal activity creates genuinely unusual conditions — warm water upwellings, occasional bubbling on the flanks, and chemical gradients that support unique biological communities. The summit is beyond recreational diving depth, but the flanks in 20–30 metres show the volcanic substrate colonised by hardy coral species. The site is periodically closed to all vessels when activity increases; operators check status before planning trips. Advanced Open Water minimum; experience in variable conditions required.

## Practical Information

Grenada's best diving conditions run from January through May, when Caribbean swells are calmest, visibility is at its best, and manta ray season peaks. The hurricane season from June through November can disrupt operations, though Grenada's geography provides some protection and dive operators typically resume within days after storms. St George's on the southwestern coast is the main base for dive operations; smaller operators work from Grand Anse and Carriacou to the north, which offers its own reef diving.
