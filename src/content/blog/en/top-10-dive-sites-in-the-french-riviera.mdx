The French Riviera, the Côte d'Azur, is where Mediterranean diving became a sport: Jacques Cousteau tested his earliest aqualungs in these waters off the Var coast. Today the coastline from Marseille to Menton offers a remarkable concentration of dive sites — France's oldest marine national park at Port-Cros, a cluster of deep wrecks off Cavalaire, and the dramatic underwater terrain of the Esterel and the Lérins islands. The water warms to 24 or 25°C by late summer, visibility commonly reaches 20 to 30 metres in settled weather, and a season that runs comfortably from April to November makes the Riviera one of the most accessible quality dive regions in the Mediterranean.

## 1. Port-Cros National Park

Port-Cros, off Hyères, was France's first marine national park, created in 1963, and remains one of the best-protected stretches of the French Mediterranean. The waters around the island teem with groupers, barracuda, dentex and dense shoals over reef and posidonia from 6 to 30 metres. Decades of protection mean the fish are abundant and unafraid, and a marked underwater trail at La Palud lets snorkellers and Open Water divers explore the shallows. Sites suit all levels. Visibility is typically 15 to 25 metres. Boats run from Hyères and Le Lavandou, with the best conditions May to October.

## 2. Le Togo Wreck, Cavalaire

The Togo is the most famous of the Cavalaire wrecks, a steam cargo ship torpedoed in 1918 that lies upright on the seabed at around 60 metres off Cap Lardier. The intact hull, holds and superstructure shelter conger eels, groupers and lobster, and the depth places it firmly in technical-diving territory. For trained divers it is one of the finest deep wrecks on the French coast. Visibility at depth is typically 15 to 25 metres. Boats run from Cavalaire-sur-Mer, with the best conditions in the calm summer months.

## 3. Le Rubis Submarine Wreck, Cap Camarat

Off Cap Camarat near Saint-Tropez, the Rubis is a French minelaying submarine scuttled in 1958 as a memorial to its wartime service. It lies upright and remarkably intact at 35 to 40 metres, the conning tower, deck gun and hull clearly recognisable, now colonised by congers, groupers and shoaling fish. The depth and the importance of the wreck make it an Advanced and technical dive. It is one of the most atmospheric wrecks on the Riviera. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres. Dived from Saint-Tropez and Cavalaire, best from June to September.

## 4. La Gabinière, Port-Cros

The rock of La Gabinière, off the south side of Port-Cros island, is the national park's signature dive — a steep wall and pinnacle dropping from the surface past 40 metres, washed by current that draws enormous groupers, barracuda, dentex and amberjack. The protected status has made the grouper population here one of the densest in France. The wall suits Advanced divers comfortable with depth and current; shallower sections welcome Open Water divers. Visibility frequently reaches 25 to 30 metres. Dived from Hyères and Le Lavandou, best in the summer months.

## 5. The Lérins Islands, Cannes

Off Cannes, the islands of Sainte-Marguerite and Saint-Honorat offer accessible diving over reef, posidonia and the occasional wreck from 8 to 30 metres. Groupers, octopus, scorpionfish and shoals of bream populate the rock, and the sheltered channels between the islands keep conditions calm. The proximity to Cannes makes these some of the most convenient dives on the eastern Riviera, ideal for Open Water divers and relaxed second dives. Visibility runs 15 to 25 metres. The diving season runs from spring through autumn, warmest in August.

## 6. The Esterel Reefs, Saint-Raphaël

The red porphyry coast of the Esterel near Saint-Raphaël continues underwater as dramatic reefs and boulder fields of the same striking rock, descending from 10 to 35 metres. The colour of the stone, combined with groupers, moray eels, octopus and shoals of bream, makes this some of the most photogenic diving on the Riviera. Sites like the Lion de Mer and the Sec du Lingousto offer reef and pinnacle terrain for all levels. Visibility frequently passes 25 metres. Boats run from Saint-Raphaël and Agay, best from May to October.

## 7. The Wrecks of the Donator and Grec, Port-Cros

Off Port-Cros, two famous wrecks lie within reach of trained divers. The Donator, a cargo ship sunk by a mine in 1945, rests at 30 to 50 metres and is among the most popular wreck dives in France, its hull rich with congers, groupers and shoaling fish. Nearby, the Grec sits at similar depth. Both suit Advanced and technical divers and are protected within the wider Port-Cros marine area. Visibility at depth is typically 20 to 30 metres. Dived from Le Lavandou and Hyères, best in the calm summer months.

## 8. Cap d'Antibes

The headland of Cap d'Antibes, between Cannes and Nice, offers reef and wall diving over granite and limestone from 8 to 35 metres. Groupers, octopus, moray eels and shoals of bream inhabit the rock, and the deeper walls hold red gorgonians. The site combines easy reef for newer divers with deeper terrain for the experienced, all within easy reach of the resort coast. Visibility runs 15 to 25 metres. Boats run from Antibes and Juan-les-Pins, with the warmest, calmest water from July to September.

## 9. The Médoc and Ramon Wrecks, Cavalaire

Cavalaire-sur-Mer sits beside a concentration of wrecks that make it one of France's premier wreck-diving bases. Alongside the Togo, the steamers Médoc and Ramon lie on the seabed at 30 to 45 metres, their broken hulls colonised by congers, groupers and dense shoals. The cluster of wrecks within a short boat ride gives Cavalaire a reputation matched by few Mediterranean ports. These are Advanced and technical dives. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres. Best dived in the settled summer months from June to September.

## 10. Les Calanques, Marseille and Cassis

At the western edge of the Riviera, the Calanques national park between Marseille and Cassis offers some of the most dramatic underwater terrain in France. Limestone cliffs plunge into deep water riddled with caves — including the famous Cosquer cave with its prehistoric paintings, now sealed and protected. Diveable reefs, walls and caverns run from 10 to 40 metres, with groupers, barracuda, lobster and red coral. Sites suit Open Water through technical divers. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres. Boats run from Marseille and Cassis, best from May to October.

## Explore on the map

The French Riviera rewards matching the base to the diving: Port-Cros and Le Lavandou for the national park and its famous wrecks; Cavalaire for the deep wreck cluster; Saint-Raphaël for the red Esterel reefs; Cannes and Antibes for accessible eastern sites; and Marseille and Cassis for the dramatic Calanques. The season runs comfortably from April to November, warmest in August.
