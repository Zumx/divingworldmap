Turkey sits at the junction of the Aegean and Mediterranean seas, and its coastline — particularly the stretch from Bodrum south through Kas and around to Antalya — offers some of the most historically layered and visually varied diving in Europe. Warm, clear water, a seabed littered with ancient amphora and Byzantine stonework, dramatic underwater topography, and a well-developed dive infrastructure make Turkey an underrated destination that rewards both beginners and advanced divers. Water temperatures peak at 28°C in August and rarely drop below 18°C in winter along the southern coast, meaning a 3–5mm wetsuit covers most of the season. ## 1. Kas, Antalya Province

Kas is the nerve centre of Turkish diving, a small town on the Lycian coast whose entire economy orients around the sea. Underwater, the landscape shifts between sheer walls dropping to 40 metres, tunnels threading through limestone headlands, and sandy gullies patrolled by octopus and nudibranchs. The most celebrated site nearby is the Uluburun Bronze Age shipwreck — the original is off-limits as an archaeological site, but nearby dive spots in the same stretch of coast yield ancient amphora visible on the seabed at 20–30 metres. The Haberli Wall plunges vertically from 5 to 40-plus metres, with grouper, scorpionfish, and sea fans lining the rock. Open Water divers handle the shallower sites well; the walls and deeper cave entrances suit Advanced. Best season runs April through November.

## 2. Antalya and the Bay

The Antalya coast offers a range of sites suited to resort divers and more experienced divers alike. Shallow reefs in the 8–18-metre range host sea bream, wrasse, and moray eels threading through Posidonia seagrass meadows. Further out, underwater ridges and isolated boulders create sheltered environments for octopus, cuttlefish, and the occasional sea turtle. The archaeological dimension is persistent along this coast — Roman anchors, pottery sherds, and occasionally more substantial structures appear at depth. Visibility in the bay averages 15–20 metres in calm summer conditions. The sheltered nature of the bay keeps currents mild, making it well suited to Open Water divers and students completing their checkout dives. Dive centres including Mediterranean Diving Center operate year-round from the harbour.

## 3. Bodrum Peninsula, Aegean Coast

The Bodrum Peninsula anchors the Aegean side of Turkish diving. Sites around Kos Channel and the islands offshore offer drift dives along rocky walls, meadows of sea grass, and sandy shallows where stingrays rest half-buried. Visibility is typically excellent in summer, reaching 20–25 metres on good days, with water temperatures of 24–26°C from June through September. The area has a rich wreck history — a Mycenaean vessel excavated offshore in the 1980s fundamentally changed archaeologists' understanding of Bronze Age trade routes. Today, the wreck dive sites available to sport divers include more recent vessels at 15–35 metres. Open Water certification covers most Bodrum sites; the deeper walls and wrecks benefit from Advanced.

## 4. Kemer and the Bee Cave (Ari Magarasi)

Kemer, south of Antalya, is home to one of Turkey's most photographed dive sites: the Ari Magarasi, or Bee Cave, a deep fissure and cavern system beginning at around 12 metres and descending into a cathedral-like chamber at roughly 28 metres. The play of light through the entrance is extraordinary on calm, sunny mornings. Inside, the walls carry sponges and soft corals, and grouper often shelter in the lower chamber. The site rewards divers with good buoyancy control — the entrance is narrow enough that poor technique stirs silt and ruins visibility for the group behind. Open Water divers can enter the outer section with a guide; the deeper passage suits Advanced or higher. Best June through October.

## 5. Kabak Bay, Near Oludeniz

Kabak is a remote bay accessible only by road and trail south of Oludeniz. The diving here is largely undeveloped — a rarity on the Lycian coast — and rewards those willing to make the effort with an undisturbed underwater landscape of boulders, arches, and sea grass at 5–25 metres. Sea turtles are regularly sighted here, as the remote coastline offers nesting habitat. The bay's isolation means fewer boats and less anchor damage, which translates to noticeably better coral and sponge cover than many busier sites. Currents are typically light. Dive operators from Oludeniz run day trips when conditions permit; the best diving is from May through October.

## 6. Bozburun Peninsula, Near Marmaris

The Bozburun Peninsula south of Marmaris offers a string of sites that reward both beginners and experienced divers. Walls, caves, and rocky reefs at 10–35 metres shelter sea bass, Mediterranean barracuda, and large moray eels. The Turkish coastal law protects a marine reserve here, and fish populations are noticeably denser inside the protected boundaries. The area is also a liveaboard hub — gulet-based diving itineraries that depart Marmaris and cruise through the Bozburun Peninsula and on toward Kas are a classic way to cover this coastline. Visibility averages 15–20 metres through the summer season. Open Water certification is sufficient for most sites.

## 7. Aksona Area, Kas

The dive sites clustered around Aksona near Kas include shallow reef systems at 6–15 metres that PADI and SSI operators use for Open Water training, alongside deeper walls and archways at 25–40 metres that reward Advanced divers. The shallow reefs here are notable for their seahorse populations — spotted seahorses cling to gorgonian fans and seagrass stems in numbers rarely seen elsewhere on the Turkish coast. Dive centres including Aksona operate small groups to reduce crowding. Water temperature in the Kas area is consistently 2–3 degrees warmer than the Aegean coast due to the more sheltered Mediterranean water mass. Best season April through November; October particularly good for clarity and marine life activity.

## 8. Kastellorizo Channel, Near Kas

The channel between Kas and the Greek island of Kastellorizo (Megisti) is a narrow, deep waterway where Aegean and Mediterranean water masses meet, creating upwellings that fuel plankton blooms and concentrate fish. The Turkish side offers wall dives to 40-plus metres with dense populations of sea bream, large amberjack cruising in open water, and occasional sightings of bluefin tuna in the channel itself. Currents can be strong in the channel, making this more appropriate for Advanced divers comfortable managing drift conditions. Day trips operate from Kas harbour; the crossing itself is a memorable boat ride through waters that have hosted trade routes since antiquity.

## 9. Fethiye Bay and Surrounds

Fethiye Bay's sheltered waters, flanked by pine-covered mountains and the ghost town of Kayakoy above the shoreline, offer a calm and accessible diving environment. Göcek's small islands create a natural maze of channels and coves where depths rarely exceed 20 metres. The seagrass beds here support loggerhead sea turtles, which nest on nearby beaches and are regularly encountered underwater in summer. An artificial reef programme has added structure to some sites, and the Fethiye wreck — a cargo vessel sunk at around 28 metres — provides the area's main wreck interest. Open Water divers are well accommodated throughout. Dive operators run from Fethiye town and from the Göcek marina complex.

## 10. Adrasan Cove, Antalya Province

Adrasan is a quiet cove south of Cirali where the Taurus Mountains meet the sea, and the diving reflects the landscape's character: dramatic, unpolished, and largely uncommercialised. Underwater archways begin at 10 metres and drop to around 25 metres, and the remote location means sites rarely see more than one or two dive boats per day. Grouper and dentex are particularly large here compared to busier stretches of the coast, a product of lower fishing pressure and the nearby Olympos-Beydaglari National Park protection. A small number of local operators run guided dives from the beach. Advanced certification is useful for the deeper archway dives; the shallower reef is accessible from Open Water level. Best from May through October.

## Planning a Turkish Diving Trip

The main diving season runs April through November, with July and August delivering the warmest water and longest daylight hours but also the heaviest tourist traffic. May, June, and October offer an ideal balance of good conditions and manageable crowd levels. Kas, Antalya, and Bodrum all have direct or one-stop flights from most European cities. Local dive centres operate at every resort town; PADI and SSI qualifications are universally recognised. Nitrox is increasingly available at better-equipped centres in Kas and Bodrum.
