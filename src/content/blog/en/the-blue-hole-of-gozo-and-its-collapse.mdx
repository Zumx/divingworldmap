On the western coast of Gozo, Malta's quieter sister island, a near-perfect rectangular pool of seawater sits hollowed into the limestone shelf at a place called Dwejra. This is the Blue Hole, one of the most famous dive sites in the Mediterranean and the entry point to a dive that runs through a natural chimney in the rock and out onto a dramatic outer reef wall. For decades the dive was overlooked by the Azure Window, a vast natural stone arch that stood just offshore — until, in March 2017, the entire structure collapsed into the sea during a storm, changing the site forever.

## The Geology of Dwejra

Dwejra is a showcase of limestone karst sculpted by the sea. The coast here is built of soft globigerina and harder coralline limestone, and over millennia the waves and rain have carved it into a complex of pools, caves, arches and collapsed sinkholes. The Blue Hole itself is a roughly ten-metre-wide shaft, around 15 metres deep, open to the sky and separated from the open sea by a wall of rock. It formed exactly as its name suggests — a collapsed cavity in the limestone that flooded.

Inland of the Blue Hole sits the Inland Sea, a lagoon connected to the Mediterranean by a long natural tunnel through the cliff, and the whole area is dotted with the geological aftermath of collapse and erosion. It is one of the most geologically dramatic stretches of coast in the Maltese islands and, before the arch fell, one of the most photographed.

## The Dive: Through the Chimney to the Wall

The classic dive begins in the sheltered Blue Hole, an ideal place to descend and check gear in calm water regardless of conditions outside. From the bottom of the hole, a natural archway or chimney in the rock — an opening in the wall separating the pool from the open sea — leads divers out onto the outer reef at around 15 to 18 metres. Passing through this gap, with the enclosed pool behind and the open blue ahead, is the signature moment of the dive.

Beyond the chimney, the reef drops away as a wall and slope into deeper water. Divers typically work along the wall to depths of 25 to 30 metres or more, exploring the contours, the coral and sponge growth, and the boulders strewn across the slope. The return is back through the chimney into the Blue Hole, where the shallow pool serves as a safe, sheltered place to complete a safety stop and exit.

## The Azure Window and Its Collapse

For generations the Azure Window dominated this coast — a colossal limestone arch, its top a flat platform high above the sea, its legs planted in the water just off Dwejra. It was Malta's most recognisable natural landmark, a fixture of postcards and a backdrop for films and television. Underwater, the arch and the reef beneath it formed part of the diving landscape, with the submerged base offering boulders, swim-throughs and rich life.

On 8 March 2017, after years of visible erosion and rockfalls, the entire arch collapsed into the sea during a heavy storm. The pillar and the span both gave way, and the landmark that had defined Dwejra was simply gone from the surface. The collapse was sudden and complete, and it made headlines around the world.

## Diving the Ruins

What the collapse left behind is itself now a dive. The fallen arch lies as an enormous field of limestone boulders on the seabed off Dwejra, some of them house-sized, scattered across the bottom in 7 to 50 metres of water. Divers can swim among and over these blocks, which are gradually being colonised by marine life and which form swim-throughs and overhangs where the rubble has stacked. It is a strange, poignant dive — moving over the remains of a landmark that millions once photographed from above, now a submerged rockfall.

The site is a reminder that the coastline is alive and changing, and that the same forces that carved the Blue Hole and the arch are still at work. Marine life has taken to the new structure quickly: groupers, moray eels, octopus and a steady cast of Mediterranean reef fish occupy the gaps between the boulders.

## Conditions, Seasons and Safety

The Blue Hole can be dived year-round, but conditions on the exposed outer wall depend entirely on the weather. When the prevailing winds are calm the site is superb, with visibility frequently exceeding 30 metres in summer and water temperatures climbing into the mid-20s Celsius by late summer. When swell wraps around the headland, the open-sea section and the entry over the rocks can become dangerous, and the dive is called off — the sheltered Blue Hole itself can look deceptively calm while the exit over the rocks is being pounded.

The entry and exit are the crux. Divers gear up on the rocky shelf and clamber down into the Blue Hole over uneven, sometimes slippery limestone, which can be awkward in surge. The dive itself is straightforward for an Open Water diver in good conditions, but the shore entry and the exposure of the outer wall mean local knowledge and a careful read of the sea state matter.

## A Wider Maltese Trip

Dwejra is one stop on a Maltese diving itinerary that is among the richest in the Mediterranean for its size. Gozo alone offers the deep cavern of the nearby reef systems, while the main island of Malta and tiny Comin between them hold a remarkable concentration of wrecks deliberately scuttled as dive sites, plus caves and reefs all around the coast. The short ferry hop between the islands makes a multi-site trip easy, and the Blue Hole, even without its arch, remains the headline dive on Gozo.
