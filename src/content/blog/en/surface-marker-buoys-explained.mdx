## The Most Important Safety Device You Carry

A diver surfacing in open water — away from a reef, in a channel, or anywhere a boat crew might not immediately see a neoprene-hooded head in chop — needs to be visible. The surface marker buoy, deployed at the end of a dive while still underwater, announces 'diver ascending here' to every boat in the vicinity and marks the exact ascent point for the pickup boat. It is a device that costs relatively little, weighs almost nothing, and has saved a statistically significant number of divers from being struck by passing vessels or from drifting away from the pickup boat unnoticed.

The terminology is worth clarifying because it is used inconsistently. A surface marker buoy (SMB) is traditionally a buoy deployed at the surface — carried down at the start of the dive and used to mark a line at a mooring or to signal position from the water. A Delayed Surface Marker Buoy (DSMB), sometimes called a delayed SMB or a 'deco buoy', is deployed from underwater while the diver is still at depth, typically during the safety stop or decompression stop, and rises to the surface on a reel of line while the diver remains below. In common usage, many divers call both devices SMBs, and the distinction is mainly technical. This article is primarily about the DSMB — the device you send up from depth.

## Types of DSMB

The most common recreational DSMB is a sealed tube of bright material — typically international orange or yellow, though surface visibility tests consistently favour orange over yellow in varied light conditions — that is inflated from the diver's regulator second stage or from a separate inflation tube. Tube SMBs range from 1 metre to 2 metres in length. Longer tubes extend higher above the water surface, are visible at greater distance, and are more stable in mild chop. A 1.5-metre tube is a practical minimum for a recreational diver; technical divers often carry 1.8 or 2-metre tubes.

Closed-bottom SMBs (also called 'fully-sealed' or 'dumbbell' types in some regions) trap air that cannot escape once the tube is sealed. They are simpler to inflate and easier for beginners to deploy, but if over-inflated — a risk when deploying from depth where the air expands as the buoy ascends — they can burst or the overpressure valve must vent. Open-bottom SMBs have an open tube at the bottom through which excess air vents as the buoy ascends, preventing over-pressure. Most technical divers prefer open-bottom designs because they are safer to deploy from depth.

A separate category is the lift bag, which serves a dual purpose: signal on the surface and lifting device for objects underwater. Lift bags are used in technical and salvage diving; they are not typically used as ascent markers in recreational diving.

## The Reel

A DSMB without a line reel is a balloon floating away from you. The reel is the other half of the system. It spools the line between the diver and the deployed buoy, allowing the diver to remain at the safety stop depth while the buoy sits on the surface above. The line length must be at least as long as the diver's maximum depth plus a metre or two — a reel with 20 metres of line is sufficient for recreational depths; technical divers carry 30 or 45-metre reels or larger.

Finger reels — compact spools held in the hand and wound with a knurled ring — are the standard for recreational DSMB use. They are small enough to pocket or clip to the BCD, and the 20-metre line length suits most recreational diving. Larger primary reels with handles and a clutch are used for deeper technical dives and for cave guideline work.

Line management during deployment is where most beginners struggle. The line must feed freely as the buoy rises without tangling, and must be held under tension but not pulled — a diver who grabs the line and tries to stop a rising buoy is likely to be dragged upward, which is the primary safety hazard of DSMB deployment. The reel should be held loosely and allowed to spin freely, with the diver maintaining their depth by working their buoyancy rather than by holding the line taut.

## Deploying a DSMB: Step by Step

Deployment procedure varies somewhat between SMB types and instructors, but the core sequence is consistent. The diver neutralises buoyancy at stop depth, takes the DSMB from its stowage position, opens the bottom of the tube, places the open end of the inflation tube into the opening, and releases a short breath of air into the tube. For an oral-inflation tube, this is a direct exhalation; for a regulator-inflated type, the regulator second stage is inserted into the inflation port. The air bubble in the tube is sufficient to make it positively buoyant; the diver releases the tube and lets it rise.

As the tube rises, the line feeds from the reel. The diver watches the reel, holds it away from their body to avoid entanglement, and manages their depth actively — the line tension should not be used for buoyancy reference. When the buoy reaches the surface, tension in the line increases. The diver locks the reel or holds the line at the correct depth and completes the stop.

The most common errors are: failing to get enough air into the tube before releasing it (a partly-filled tube may flip and spill the air before it rises), allowing line to tangle around the hand or fingers during the ascent, and ascending with the buoy rather than maintaining stop depth. In confined quarters on a busy dive boat, deploying an SMB without fouling it on other divers' lines is an additional skill.

Carrying a cutting tool — a dive knife or line cutter — is essential when using any line equipment, including SMBs. A line wrapped around a finger, a reel dropped and entangled in coral, or a line fouled in a propeller during a live-boat situation are all scenarios where a cutter resolves the problem immediately. A line cutter worn on the BCD in an easily accessible location is the minimum.

## When and Where to Deploy

The DSMB should be deployed before the ascent begins, not at the surface. The purpose is to signal position before the diver is at the surface and potentially in the path of boat traffic, not after. For a dive ending at a known mooring or in a small enclosed bay with one dive boat, the DSMB adds safety rather than being strictly essential. For a drift dive in open water, a dive with a surface interval boat pickup, or any dive in an area with boat traffic, it is mandatory.

Many experienced instructors and divemasters teach divers to deploy the DSMB at the end of every open-water dive, regardless of whether it is strictly needed, because the skill atrophies if it is never practised. A diver who only uses their DSMB in situations where it is 'really' necessary has probably not used it for several months and will fumble the deployment in exactly the scenario where smooth execution matters most.

## Signal Mirrors and Other Surface Signalling

The DSMB is the primary signalling tool, but divers diving in open water benefit from carrying complementary signalling devices. A signal mirror, available in waterproof form from most dive equipment retailers, can signal to aircraft or distant vessels by reflecting sunlight over distances exceeding five kilometres in good visibility conditions. A dive whistle — not an air-powered one, which relies on the regulator, but a mechanical pealess whistle — provides acoustic signalling in conditions where visual signals are obstructed by wave action.

The combination of a deployed DSMB, a signal mirror and a whistle covers the three main signalling channels — visual from below, visual from the side, and acoustic — and together represents a complete surface signalling kit for any open-water dive. The total weight is negligible and all three items can be carried in a single BCD pocket.
