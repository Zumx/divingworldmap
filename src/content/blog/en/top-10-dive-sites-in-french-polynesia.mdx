French Polynesia sprawls across an area of the South Pacific larger than Western Europe, and its 118 islands and atolls represent one of the most diverse and spectacular marine environments on earth. The diving here divides broadly between two types: pass diving in the Tuamotu atolls, where tidal flows through narrow channels between lagoon and ocean concentrate extraordinary quantities of sharks, rays, and schooling fish; and the volcanic island diving around Tahiti, Moorea, and Bora Bora, where underwater topography features dramatic drop-offs and endemic marine species. The Tuamotu atolls sit at the core of the Polynesian Triangle, with clear oceanic water from the undisturbed South Pacific and virtually no land-based pollution reaching even the nearest atolls. Water temperatures range from 26°C in the cooler austral winter months (July–September) to 29–30°C in summer. Visibility in the outer atoll passes can exceed 40 metres. The French Polynesian diving infrastructure is well-developed, with professional operators in all major destinations offering multilingual guiding and solid equipment.

## 1. Tiputa Pass, Rangiroa

Tiputa Pass is the larger of the two navigable passes at Rangiroa — itself one of the world's largest atolls, with a lagoon so expansive that clouds form above it. The pass runs roughly 2 kilometres between the lagoon and the open Pacific, and twice daily the tidal exchange drives hundreds of millions of litres of water through this channel. Divers drift the pass on incoming tides when the current pushes them from ocean into lagoon, hovering mid-water while the marine life assembles below and around them. Grey reef sharks in schools of dozens, bottlenose dolphins in resident pods, hammerheads at depth, and occasional tiger sharks on the deeper sections. Advanced Open Water minimum; experience with strong current drift diving is essential. The outgoing tide is for experienced drift divers only.

## 2. Fakarava South Pass (Tumakohua Pass)

The southern pass at Fakarava UNESCO Biosphere Reserve is the site of one of the most celebrated predation events in shark diving: each June and July, thousands of marbled grouper gather in the pass to spawn, and the grouper spawning aggregation attracts hundreds of grey reef sharks that hunt cooperatively in what observers describe as a controlled frenzy. Outside the spawning period, the pass holds permanent populations of grey reef sharks, Napoleon wrasse, and schooling fish of every description on the encrusted walls. The north pass at Fakarava is a calmer alternative offering excellent coral and turtle diving. Advanced Open Water for the passes; the lagoon sites suit Open Water divers.

## 3. Avatoru Pass, Rangiroa

The smaller of Rangiroa's two passes is calmer and more predictable than Tiputa, making it the standard introduction to pass diving on the atoll. Divers are taken by boat to the ocean side and drift inward, hovering at 15–20 metres while grey reef sharks circle below and schools of barracuda spiral overhead. The walls of the pass are dense with soft corals and the bottom is sandy and clean. Bottlenose dolphins often join the drift midway. Advanced Open Water recommended; the site is where many divers have their first meaningful shark encounter in a natural setting. Best on an incoming tide in the morning.

## 4. Tahiti — The Dropoff, Papeete Area

The main island of Tahiti offers wall diving on its northern coast that begins just minutes from Papeete by boat. The reef crest sits at 5–8 metres and the wall drops to 30–40 metres, with black coral, large gorgonians, and abundant soldierfish in the overhangs. The sites around the west coast near the lagoon entrance channels attract lemon sharks — Tahiti has one of the most reliable populations of lemon sharks in the Pacific, and dive operators have developed well-managed feeding dives that allow close observation without disrupting natural behaviour. Visibility on the outer reef is typically 25–35 metres. Open Water for the upper reef; Advanced for the wall.

## 5. Moorea — Tiahura Area

The lagoon fringing reefs around Moorea are excellent beginner diving, but the outer reef pass dives are what draw experienced divers. The Opunohu Bay area in the north offers multiple dive sites including a stingray cleaning station in the sand at about 10 metres where dozens of marbled and blacktip rays congregate — a signature Moorea dive that also reliably produces lemon shark encounters. Blacktip reef sharks are so common in Moorea's lagoon shallows that they become unremarkable within the first dive. The outer reef wall drops cleanly from 10 to 30 metres with good coral and turtle populations. All certification levels catered for by the island's numerous operators.

## 6. Tikehau Pass

Tikehau, a small atoll 300 kilometres northeast of Tahiti, has one of the highest fish biomass densities of any atoll in Polynesia. The single navigable pass concentrates that biomass in a relatively narrow channel. Barracuda in schools of thousands spiral in the pass on incoming tides; grey reef sharks are permanent residents; eagle rays pass through in small groups. The atoll is less visited than Rangiroa and Fakarava, so encounters feel less managed and more genuinely wild. Tikehau is also notable for its pink-sand beaches and flamingo-pink inside the lagoon — not relevant to diving but memorable for the surface intervals. Advanced Open Water for pass diving; the lagoon suits Open Water divers.

## 7. Bora Bora — Muri Muri Pass

Bora Bora's reputation as a honeymoon destination somewhat overshadows its diving, which is very good on the outer reef but limited in variety compared to the Tuamotu atolls. The Muri Muri pass on the northern side of the lagoon produces reliable lemon shark sightings and the outer reef wall is healthy with moderate coral cover and large Napoleon wrasse. The hammerhead encounters reported in the 1990s are now infrequent, but eagle rays and occasional manta rays more than compensate. The lagoon's famously turquoise water makes the surface intervals as spectacular as the dives. Open Water for lagoon diving; Advanced for the outer reef and pass.

## 8. Manihi Atoll — Blue Lagoon

Manihi is a pearl-farming atoll in the northern Tuamotu group whose lagoon supports an extraordinary density of bumphead parrotfish — schools of 50 or more animals working the coral in the early morning, the sound of their biting carrying underwater for tens of metres. The atoll's single pass produces the standard Tuamotu combination of sharks and schooling fish, but it is the bumphead parrotfish that distinguish Manihi in divers' memories. The pearl farm structures in the lagoon also attract fish aggregations and provide an unusual subject for photography. Advanced Open Water for the pass; Open Water for lagoon sites.

## 9. Tahaa — Coral Garden

Between Raiatea and Tahaa, a section of the shared lagoon known as the Coral Garden offers some of the best intact hard coral coverage in the Society Islands. This is not a high-adrenaline site — no sharks, no strong current — but the density and health of the Acropora and brain coral formations, combined with the fish communities that depend on them, make it outstanding for reef photography. Turtles graze the coral heads throughout the day, and the protected lagoon conditions mean the site is accessible when outer reef weather prevents other dives. Open Water level; excellent for new divers and snorkellers accompanying dive groups.

## 10. Gambier Archipelago — Rikitea

The remote Gambier Archipelago, accessible only by long flight or ship from Tahiti, offers some of the most undisturbed diving in French Polynesia. The main lagoon at Rikitea carries exceptional clarity — visibility in the outer areas regularly exceeds 40 metres — and the coral cover on the submerged reef crests is among the healthiest in the entire territory. Pelagic visits include hammerheads, silky sharks, and occasional whale sharks on the outer reef. The complete absence of tourist infrastructure means self-sufficiency is essential; most visits are by private yacht. Advanced Open Water; the remoteness requires careful planning and reliable equipment.

## Practical Information

French Polynesia diving is excellent year-round, with the peak season for pass diving (calm conditions and best visibility) running from April through November. The austral summer from December through March brings more rain, occasionally reducing visibility, and the swell increases on exposed outer reefs. Liveaboards connecting the Tuamotu atolls offer the most efficient way to dive Rangiroa, Fakarava, Tikehau, and Manihi in a single trip. All major islands have multiple PADI and SSI operators with solid equipment and professional guides.
