Switzerland is a landlocked country at the centre of Europe, and its diving is entirely in freshwater — glacial lakes carved by ice sheets and the rivers that connect them. That constraint is also the invitation: Swiss alpine lakes produce some of the clearest freshwater diving in the world, the deep cold preserving visibility, slowing biological growth and, in several lakes, preventing the decomposition of submerged objects that would long since have rotted in warmer water. The Verzasca river valley in Ticino, the Italian-speaking canton in the south, offers something entirely different — river diving in fast-moving water through a gorge of white granite, with visibility so good that the river looks like a swimming pool from the surface. Water temperatures in Swiss lakes range from 4°C at depth year-round to around 22°C at the surface in summer. Drysuits or thick wetsuits are the norm; a 7mm wetsuit with hood and gloves is the comfortable minimum for anything below the summer thermocline. ## 1. Verzasca River, Ticino

The Verzasca is one of the most unusual and demanding dive destinations in central Europe. The river runs through a steep granite gorge in the canton of Ticino, carving pools and channels in the white and grey rock that are visible from the surface with a clarity that makes the water almost invisible. Divers enter the pools from the sides, fin through the underwater gorge while current pushes them downstream, exit at the next pool and walk back up the canyon trail for another run. The fish population — brown trout, chubs and perch — is visible at remarkably close range. Water temperature in the river sits at 8 to 12°C even in summer, requiring a 5mm wetsuit at minimum. The diving is suitable for Open Water certified divers in the gentler pool sections; the faster gorge passages between pools require strong swimming ability and current diving experience. Best season is May through September when river level is stable.

## 2. Lake Geneva, Geneva and Vaud

Lake Geneva — Lac Léman in French — is the largest alpine lake in Europe by surface area, stretching 72 kilometres from Geneva in the west to Montreux in the east. The diving concentrates on the Geneva end, where the Wassersportzentrum and several dive clubs maintain access points and facilities. The lake shelves quickly from the shore — depths of 40 metres are accessible within a few hundred metres of the waterline near Geneva. The deep wall drops further still; technical divers have descended to over 100 metres. Visibility in the upper 10 metres is typically 8 to 12 metres in summer, improving dramatically in winter when algae die back and clarity can exceed 20 metres. The Geneva shoreline offers several good dive entries including Cologny and the area around Genthod. Resident fish include perch, roach, pike and the occasional very large catfish. Water temperature at the surface reaches 18 to 20°C in August.

## 3. Lake Lugano, Ticino and Lake Geneva Border

Lake Lugano sits across the Swiss-Italian border in the canton of Ticino, and its dive sites are among the most diverse and thoroughly developed in Switzerland. The Wassersportzentrum Zürich (WZT) connection to Lugano represents the federation-level dive club infrastructure; local clubs maintain multiple named entry points around the lake. The Parete di Caslano on the northwestern shore is a submerged rock wall dropping from 5 metres to over 35 metres with encrusting algae and freshwater sponges giving it colour. Il Deserto on the south shore is a flat sandy basin at 20 metres; Le Macchine offers a collection of submerged industrial equipment used as recreational dive props. The Relitto Milano is a small sunken boat at 22 metres that has been colonised by perch and wels catfish. The Lago di Lugano is accessible from both Swiss and Italian road networks, making it a popular destination for divers from northern Italy as well as Switzerland.

## 4. Lake Zurich, Zurich

Lake Zurich is the working lake of the city, and its dive sites reflect that — industrial history in the form of submerged structures, managed entry points with concrete steps, and a dive club scene centred on the Wassersportzentrum Zürich at Zollikon. The Steinrad site, a millstone formation at around 15 metres, is the signature feature — a large circular stone millstone lost from a boat centuries ago, now resting on the lake floor encrusted with freshwater mussels. The WZT Luftverbund area refers to a cluster of submerged objects and features used for club training and recreation. Kleiner Parkplatz Erlenbach and Schipfe are entry points on the eastern shore with rocky slopes descending to 20 metres. Visibility in Lake Zurich averages 6 to 10 metres in summer; the lake is subject to algae bloom in warm summers. Best visibility comes in late autumn and winter.

## 5. Lake Thun, Bernese Oberland

Lake Thun in the Bernese Oberland is set against a backdrop of the Bernese Alps that makes it arguably the most photogenic dive destination in Switzerland by the measure of what you see before getting in. The underwater environment reflects the glacial origin of the lake: sheer walls of smoothed rock, clear cold water and depths exceeding 200 metres in the main basin. The shoreline dive sites are in the 10 to 30 metre range; the village of Spiez on the southern shore has established dive entries. The fish life includes lake trout, perch and the endemic Gangfisch whitefish that is found only in pre-Alpine Swiss lakes. Water temperature at the surface reaches 18°C in a good summer. Visibility in clear conditions reaches 10 to 15 metres. Not as developed in terms of infrastructure as Lake Zurich or Lake Lugano, but the quality of the environment is high.

## 6. Lake Lucerne, Beckenried

The Vierwaldstättersee — Lake Lucerne — is a large, complex lake shaped by the glaciers that carved the valleys converging at Lucerne. The dive sites are distributed around its shores; the Beckenried area on the southern shore at Rütenen has two established entry points — Beckenried Rütenen Einstieg 1 and Einstieg 2 — that access a rocky slope descending from 5 to over 30 metres. The wall at Beckenried is one of the better freshwater wall dives in Switzerland, with rock encrusted with calcified algae and freshwater sponges, and the characteristic freshwater fish of pre-Alpine lakes — lake trout, char, perch — moving through in sizeable numbers. The water is typically clear: 10 to 15 metres in summer, more in winter. The Lopper site on the north shore of the Alpnachersee arm, accessible as Lopper 1, offers a different character — a shallow reef at 8 to 15 metres with more vegetation and bream.

## 7. Lake Neuchatel, Fribourg and Neuchatel

Lake Neuchatel is the largest lake entirely within Switzerland, and its dive sites along the eastern shore offer a distinctive underwater landscape: the lake floor holds a significant density of prehistoric pile dwellings — the remains of Iron Age and Bronze Age lakeside settlements — visible as stakes and debris fields at depths of 3 to 10 metres. Several protected archaeological zones prevent disturbance, but the areas around the entry points near Portalban and Estavayer-le-Lac allow divers to swim over the remnants. Visibility varies between 5 and 12 metres depending on season, with autumn typically the best. Lake Neuchatel is less commonly dived than Lake Zurich or Lake Geneva precisely because its attractions are specific; it suits divers with an interest in underwater archaeology and freshwater ecology rather than those seeking wall diving or dramatic topography.

## 8. Ermitage Dive Site, Lake Zurich Region

Ermitage is one of the more curated dive sites in the Lake Zurich area — a purpose-configured entry point with submerged objects arranged for recreational and training purposes. The Bocchiaplatz and Terlinden entry points access similar managed conditions on the lakeside. These sites are heavily used by dive clubs and training facilities in Zurich because they offer consistent conditions, known depths and reliable facilities. The objects — a bicycle, a wine barrel, a small boat — are arranged at 8 to 15 metres depth on a sandy substrate. For certified divers, the sites are pleasant if not spectacular, and night diving there is productive for freshwater shrimps and catfish. Terlinden's slope descends to around 25 metres, offering a longer dive profile for those who want to extend beyond the training area.

## 9. Pumpwerk Moos, Lake Zurich

Pumpwerk Moos is a water intake structure on the southwestern shore of Lake Zurich, near the Riff Williams entry point, that has created an interesting underwater feature at around 18 metres depth. The pipework and concrete structures are colonised by freshwater mussels and the small organisms that feed on them — diving around industrial infrastructure in a glacial lake is an acquired taste, but the density of fish using the structure for shelter makes it better than the description suggests. Large perch and pike are regular; wels catfish are occasionally found under the pipework. The Bocchiaplatz and surrounding entries on the southern shore are managed through the ZVT dive club federation, with changing rooms and equipment storage.

## 10. Riff Williams and Southern Lake Zurich Sites

The cluster of sites on the southern shore of Lake Zurich — Riff Williams, Bocchiaplatz, Terlinden and the adjacent entry points — form a cohesive dive area used by the established Zurich dive community throughout the season. Riff Williams is the most interesting of these for recreational divers: a natural reef-like structure of glacial boulders at 12 to 20 metres, providing the kind of relief that allows fish to shelter and makes navigation straightforward. The site is well-marked and consistently maintained by the local dive club infrastructure. Summer visibility is 6 to 10 metres; autumn can improve to 12 metres. Wels catfish are the most sought-after encounter for visiting divers, and the late evening dives in summer are the best time to find them active in the shallows.

## Planning Your Switzerland Dive Trip

Swiss diving is a year-round activity, though the cold demands appropriate thermal protection. Summer (June to September) offers the warmest surface water — up to 22°C — and the most comfortable conditions for wetsuits. Winter (November to March) brings the clearest visibility as algae growth slows, and several sites are suited to drysuit diving in cold, gin-clear conditions with visibility exceeding 15 metres. Lake Lugano and the Verzasca are accessible from Lugano airport; Zurich and Geneva have international airports serving their respective lake sites. Equipment hire is available at established dive centres; Nitrox is less commonly offered than in marine environments. Switzerland's dive club culture means that site access and local knowledge often comes through club affiliation rather than commercial operators.
