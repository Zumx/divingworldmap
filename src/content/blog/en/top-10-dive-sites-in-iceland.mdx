Iceland offers diving experiences that exist nowhere else on Earth. The country straddles the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the tectonic boundary between the North American and Eurasian plates, and that boundary is accessible underwater in a freshwater fissure at Thingvellir with clarity so extreme it approaches the limits of human vision. Away from the famous freshwater sites, Iceland's fjords and coastal waters hold volcanic chimneys, kelp forests, cold-water corals, and a rich community of North Atlantic marine life. Water temperatures are cold year-round — typically 2–4°C in the freshwater sites and 4–10°C at sea — making drysuits mandatory and appropriate thermal undergarments essential. But the cold is part of the character: the water clarity that Iceland's fissures deliver is a direct consequence of the near-freezing temperature that suppresses bacterial and algal activity.

## 1. Silfra Fissure, Thingvellir

Silfra is the most celebrated freshwater dive site in the world. The fissure cuts through the lava field of the Thingvellir rift valley, and the water that fills it has spent 30–100 years filtering through volcanic rock before emerging at Silfra, giving it a measured horizontal visibility of 80–100 metres. Divers drift gently from the Silfra Hall through the Cathedral — a wider, deeper section — and past the Silfra Lagoon to the exit. The crack narrows at points to squeeze both arms through simultaneously. Depth is modest — 18 metres maximum in the Cathedral — but the cold (2°C) and the mandatory drysuit make it a minimum Advanced Open Water dive. A PADI or SSI drysuit certification is required by most operators.

## 2. Davidsgja Fissure, Thingvellir

Adjacent to Silfra in the Thingvellir national park, Davidsgja is shallower and narrower than its famous neighbour — a crack descending to around 10 metres with walls of dark lava barely wide enough to pass through. The intimacy of the space and the extraordinary visibility create a completely different experience from the open grandeur of Silfra. Algae clings to the rock in green filaments that move in the slow current, and the light from the surface, filtered through 2°C water, has a quality photographers describe as irreplaceable. The site is typically dived as part of a multi-site day combined with Silfra. Advanced Open Water with drysuit certification required.

## 3. Strytan Hydrothermal Chimney, Eyjafjordur

Strytan is a geothermal chimney rising from the floor of the Eyjafjordur fjord near Akureyri in northern Iceland — the only known hydrothermal vent accessible to recreational divers anywhere in the world. The chimney rises from 65 metres to around 15 metres below the surface and emits warm (65°C at the vent) water rich in magnesium and calcium. Marine life concentrates around it: sea urchins, scallops, and crabs are abundant, and the contrast between the chimney's warm plume and the 6°C surrounding water creates a shimmering refractive effect. Access is managed strictly. Advanced certification minimum; guided dives only from Akureyri.

## 4. Kleifarvatn Lake, Reykjanes

A volcanic crater lake south of Reykjavik, Kleifarvatn sits on an active geothermal zone and periodically loses water through fissures in its bottom. The lake is dark and atmospheric — the lava shoreline, the sulfuric smell, and the black sand bottom create a feeling unlike any other dive in Iceland. Geothermal vents on the bottom emit warm water visible as shimmering columns ascending through the cold lake. Maximum depth around 97 metres but recreational dives stay in the 20–30 metre range. The silt on the bottom is easily disturbed, limiting visibility if care is not taken. Drysuit essential; best dived with a local guide.

## 5. Nesjavellir Thermal Springs, Thingvellir

At the southern end of Thingvallavatn lake, where geothermal pipes from the Nesjavellir power plant drain, a section of the lake shore is warmed significantly above ambient temperature. The contrast between cold, clear open-lake water and the warm, slightly murky thermal margin creates a distinctive transitional diving environment. Three-spined sticklebacks, the dominant fish species in the lake, are abundant in the warmer margins, and the rock substrate carries unusual bacterial mat formations associated with geothermal chemistry. This is a site for divers with genuine curiosity about geology and biology rather than conventional reef aesthetics. Drysuit recommended year-round.

## 6. Arnarfjordur, Westfjords

The deep fjords of the Westfjords peninsula see almost no recreational dive traffic, which makes them simultaneously more challenging to access and considerably more rewarding for those who make the effort. Arnarfjordur, one of the longest and deepest, has walls of cold-water corals — primarily Lophelia pertusa — beginning at 30 metres, kelp forests in the shallower sections, and a resident seal colony at the river mouth on the eastern end. Visibility in the fjord averages 10–15 metres. No commercial dive operations; access requires a personal vessel or a connection with local fishing communities. Water temperature: 4–8°C year-round.

## 7. Reykjanes Ridge Kelp Dives

The Reykjanes peninsula south of Reykjavik has accessible coastal diving at several points where volcanic rock meets the North Atlantic. Kelp (Laminaria hyperborea) forms dense underwater forests in 5–15 metres of water, and beneath the canopy the light has a green, cathedral quality. Wolffish, lumpsucker, and various flatfish are found throughout the kelp zones. The Atlantic swells can make entry and exit challenging, and the water temperature rarely exceeds 10°C even in summer. Local dive centres in Reykjavik organise guided shore dives on the Reykjanes coast for divers who want to experience Icelandic marine biodiversity alongside the more famous freshwater sites.

## 8. Thingvallavatn Open Lake Dives

Beyond the famous fissures, the open water of Thingvallavatn itself offers relaxed lake diving that provides context for the geological drama of the cracks. The lake floor is a mosaic of lava boulders and sand, and the visibility — while not matching Silfra — is still exceptional by northern European standards, typically 15–25 metres. Arctic char, the apex fish of the Icelandic freshwater system, are common sightings. The lava formations in the shallows have been compared to lunar landscapes. Water temperature 2–4°C year-round; drysuit and thermal undersuit mandatory. The lake's edge within the national park is accessible with prior registration.

## 9. Heimaey Island, Westman Islands

The Westman Islands archipelago off Iceland's south coast sits in the North Atlantic and offers seabird colony diving that is unique in Europe. Puffins, guillemots, and razorbills dive from the surface and pursuit-dive underwater, and encounters with diving seabirds at depth are a specific draw for wildlife divers. The submarine cliffs around Heimaey drop sharply through kelp to rocky walls at 20–30 metres. Visibility averages 10–15 metres. Access via ferry from Landeyjarhofn; dive infrastructure is limited, so most visitors bring their own equipment and arrange local boat access through guesthouses on the island.

## 10. Bruarfoss and Oxara River Fissures, Thingvellir

The river systems flowing into Thingvallavatn within the national park pass through sections of the rift valley where shallow fissures — 3–8 metres deep — offer easy, accessible snorkeling and beginner-level drysuit dives in the same exceptional water clarity as Silfra. The Oxara river enters the rift through Almannagjá gorge, and the pools where it cuts through the lava are dived by local clubs and more cautious visitors who want the Thingvellir experience at shallower depths. The cold is identical to Silfra; a drysuit is still required. The shallow depth makes these accessible to drysuit-certified Open Water divers without Advanced certification.

## Planning your Iceland dive trip

Silfra and the other Thingvellir fissures are accessible year-round — the water temperature is consistently near 2°C regardless of season, and the access road stays open through winter. Summer (June to August) offers the longest daylight hours and the calmest weather for coastal and fjord dives. Most Silfra operators require a drysuit certification; many offer a combined drysuit orientation and Silfra dive for certified divers transitioning to drysuit use.
