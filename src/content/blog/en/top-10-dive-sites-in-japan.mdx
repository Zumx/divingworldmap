Japan is one of the most diverse diving destinations on earth, yet it occupies a strange position in the minds of most travelling divers — familiar as a cultural destination but underestimated as an underwater one. The country's island chain stretches 3,000 kilometres from the semi-tropical waters of Okinawa to the cold, bull-kelp-choked shores of Hokkaido, and almost every kilometre of that range holds something worth exploring. In the south you find coral reefs, manta rays and sea turtles. In the north, you find Steller sea lions and ethereal jellyfish drifting through visibility measured in the tens of metres. Throw in some of the Pacific theatre's most historically significant wrecks and a genuinely mysterious underwater stone monument, and you have a country that could occupy a serious diver for months. ## 1. Okinawa Main Island, Blue Cave and Surroundings

The Blue Cave at Cape Maeda is probably the most-photographed dive site in Japan, and justifiably so. Sunlight entering through an underwater tunnel creates an electric blue reflection off the white sand floor that bathes the interior in an otherworldly glow. But the attraction goes beyond the light show. The surrounding walls and sea floor host grouper, lionfish, sea turtles, and an abundance of nudibranchs. Depths range from 5 to 25 metres. Water temperature: 22–29°C. Open Water certification. Best season: April to November; visibility peaks in summer.

## 2. Ishigaki Island, Manta Scramble

Manta Scramble off Ishigaki in Okinawa Prefecture is one of the most reliable manta cleaning stations in the world. Between roughly June and November, manta rays arrive at a specific cleaning site in 10–15 metres of water to have parasites removed by wrasse. It is not uncommon to see six to twelve mantas at once. Divers kneel or crouch on the sandy bottom and watch in silence — the protocol is strict about buoyancy and noise. Water temperature: 25–29°C. Open Water certification; good buoyancy control is essential to avoid disturbing the cleaning behaviour. Best season: June to October.

## 3. Yonaguni Monument, Yonaguni Island

The Yonaguni Monument sits at the far southwestern tip of the Japanese archipelago, about 100 kilometres east of Taiwan. Whether the stepped stone structures at 5–25 metres are natural rock formations or human-made remains contested among geologists, but the debate does nothing to diminish the diving. Large hammerhead sharks patrol the site in winter, often in groups of several individuals. Even without the sharks, the angular terraces, columns and connecting tunnels create a dive landscape unlike anything else in the Pacific. Water temperature: 22–28°C. Intermediate certification recommended for the deeper sections and for managing current. Best season: January to March for hammerheads.

## 4. Kerama Islands, Okinawa

The Kerama Islands, a 40-minute ferry ride west of Naha, are known for water clarity so exceptional the local tourism board coined the phrase 'Kerama Blue' to describe it. Visibility routinely exceeds 30 metres. The reefs hold an outstanding diversity of hard corals, and the islands are one of the best sites in the Pacific for encounters with humpback whales from January to March. The dive sites range from shallow coral gardens for beginners to walls dropping to 40 metres for advanced divers. Water temperature: 20–28°C. Best season: April to November for reef diving; January to March for whales.

## 5. Castle Rock (Shiraho), Ishigaki

Off the southern tip of Ishigaki near Shiraho village sits a submerged pinnacle known to local dive guides as Castle Rock or Sentan. The site ascends from around 30 metres to about 8 metres and is regularly swept by strong current, which is the reason for its extraordinary fish life. Schools of barracuda, surgeon fish and jack orbit the top of the pinnacle, while the walls below attract trevally, grey reef sharks and, in the right season, whale sharks. Advanced certification required for current management. Best season: May to October.

## 6. Wannai, Iriomote Island

Iriomote, the wildest of the Okinawan islands, offers the dive site Wannai — a dramatic channel between two small islets where strong tidal currents aggregate an enormous biomass of fish. The channel walls are covered in sea fans and barrel sponges, and manta rays pass through regularly. The entry and exit require careful timing relative to the tidal cycle; local operators know the window. Depths range from 10 to 35 metres. Water temperature: 23–28°C. Advanced certification; this is not a site for divers who cannot hold position in moving water. Best season: May to October.

## 7. Jogashima (Joga Island), Kanagawa

North of Okinawa, the diving changes character entirely. Jogashima off the tip of the Miura Peninsula near Yokosuka offers kelp forests, rocky reefs and a marine life community more typical of temperate Japan than the tropics. Octopus, sea pens, frogfish and extraordinary concentrations of nudibranchs reward divers who go slowly and look closely. The site runs along the outer reef wall that descends to about 30 metres. Water temperature: 15–23°C. Open Water certification; a 5–7 mm wetsuit is recommended year-round. Best season: May to October for warmest water; autumn and winter offer spectacular nudibranch diversity.

## 8. Outer Bay, Iriomote

Iriomote's outer bay — known to local guides as the outer reef or soto-umi area — offers open-water encounters with large pelagic species. Hammerhead sharks, whale sharks and occasional thresher sharks pass through this zone during migration seasons. The terrain is a mix of coral bommies and sandy channels running down to 30–40 metres. Current can be strong and unpredictable. This is a site that demands honest self-assessment of experience level. Advanced certification minimum. Best season: April to October.

## 9. Tsubota Island Wrecks, Yaeyama Islands

During World War II, a number of Japanese naval and merchant vessels were sunk in and around the Yaeyama Islands. The wrecks off Tsubota and nearby islets now lie in 15–30 metres, heavily colonised by coral and sponge and hosting dense populations of fish that have found shelter in the wreckage. Several of the wrecks are accessible as multiple-site days. The historical context is sombre — some sites were troop transports — and operators treat them with appropriate respect. Advanced certification recommended. Best season: April to October.

## 10. Kerama Whale Shark Encounters, Open Sea

Between August and October, whale sharks aggregate in surface-temperature waters around the outer Kerama Islands, following concentrations of plankton. Snorkelling encounters are more common than scuba dives, but some operators run open-water dives to 15–25 metres in the vicinity of aggregations. Encounters cannot be guaranteed, but the site success rate is high during peak season. These open-ocean dives are conducted from liveaboard-style day boats with strong drifting capability. Advanced certification recommended for comfort in open-water conditions. Best season: August to October.

## Planning Your Dive Trip to Japan

The core of Japanese diving is in Okinawa and the Ryukyu Islands, best from April through November. For serious encounters — manta rays, hammerhead sharks, whale sharks — specific windows between June and October produce the most reliable results. The north of Japan (Hokkaido, Honshu temperate sites) is a year-round proposition with appropriate thermal protection; drysuits are used in winter. International flights land at Naha (Okinawa) and from there inter-island flights or ferries access Ishigaki, Iriomote and Yonaguni. Nitrox is available at most professional operations. Dive operators typically offer instruction from Open Water through Divemaster in English. [Open the map](/map) to locate operators and plan your routes across this exceptional archipelago.
