## Why Equalization Matters

Every diver who has descended more than a metre knows the sensation: a growing pressure in the ears, a muffled quality to the water noise, and if ignored, a sharp pain that demands immediate ascent. That feeling is not a quirk — it is physics. Water pressure increases by roughly one atmosphere for every ten metres of depth, and that pressure acts on every air space in the body. The middle ear, a small cavity behind the eardrum connected to the throat via the Eustachian tubes, bears the brunt of it.

The eardrum is flexible enough to deform when pressure on either side is unequal. Descent pushes water pressure against the outer ear while the middle ear still holds surface-pressure air. The tube connecting the middle ear to the back of the throat — the Eustachian tube — can be opened voluntarily or involuntarily to allow higher-pressure air through, equalising the space. When that fails, the eardrum stretches painfully; if the diver keeps descending, it can rupture. Barotrauma to the ears is the most common diving injury, and nearly every case is preventable with correct technique.

## The Valsalva Manoeuvre and Its Limits

Most entry-level courses teach the Valsalva manoeuvre first because it is simple: pinch the nose, close the mouth and blow gently. The increased pressure in the throat and Eustachian tubes forces air into the middle ear. It works well when used early and often during descent, but it has a critical weakness.

Blowing hard against a closed nose generates pressure passively rather than opening the Eustachian tube actively. If the tube is already locked shut by negative pressure from too-deep a descent, no amount of blowing will open it. This is the squeeze situation. Divers who descend too quickly and wait until pain arrives before equalising often discover that Valsalva no longer works. Forcing the manoeuvre at that point can cause reverse block, overpressure injuries, or in rare cases rupture of the round or oval window membrane, a far more serious injury than a perforated eardrum.

The lesson: equalise early, equalise often, and never descend through pain.

## Toynbee, Frenzel and Voluntary Tubal Opening

The Valsalva is not the only option, and for many divers it is not even the best one. The Toynbee manoeuvre combines nose pinch with swallowing. Swallowing opens the Eustachian tube briefly while the pinch creates the pressure differential needed to push air through. Many divers find this gentler and more reliable than Valsalva alone. Swallowing without pinching — simply moving the jaw and throat muscles — can be enough for divers with naturally wide Eustachian tubes.

The Frenzel manoeuvre is preferred by freedivers and experienced scuba divers who want precise control at depth. It requires practice to learn but demands almost no physical effort once mastered. The diver pinches the nose, seals the glottis (the structure in the larynx that closes when you hold your breath), and uses the tongue as a piston to compress the air in the throat — making a 'K' or 'G' sound. Because the lungs are disconnected by the closed glottis, there is no risk of forcing too much pressure into the inner ear. The Frenzel is particularly useful in head-up or horizontal trim and at greater depths where lung volume has reduced.

Voluntary tubal opening — relaxing and tensing specific throat muscles to open the Eustachian tubes without any manoeuvre — is something a small percentage of divers can do naturally. It cannot be reliably taught, but awareness of it may help divers who notice they can clear with jaw movements alone.

## Descending Technique

Equalization is as much about descent rate and body position as it is about technique. Feet-first descent is substantially easier for equalization than head-first. When the diver descends feet first, the Eustachian tubes open more naturally due to gravity acting on the soft tissue of the throat. Head-down descents — common when chasing a shot line or following a wall — push the Eustachian tubes toward closure and make all methods harder.

A controlled descent rate of around nine metres per minute gives time to equalise every metre or two without rushing. Experienced divers tend not to think in units; they simply pause when pressure starts to build, clear, and continue. The habit to develop is proactive equalisation — clearing before any discomfort rather than reacting to it. Some dive instructors recommend clearing at the surface, again at one metre, and then continuously throughout the first ten metres, which is where the pressure change per metre of depth is greatest. Below twenty metres the percentage pressure change per metre drops, and equalisation becomes less demanding.

## Dealing with Difficulty

Divers who routinely struggle to equalise are not unusual. A significant proportion of recreational divers have Eustachian tubes that are naturally narrow, partially blocked by past ear infections, or affected by mild congestion. Several strategies help.

Decongestant sprays used before diving can reduce swelling in the Eustachian tube lining and allow easier opening. Oral decongestants carry the risk of wearing off mid-dive, leaving the diver unable to equalise on ascent as the sinus and middle-ear spaces re-expand — this reverse squeeze on ascent, sometimes called a reverse block, can be extremely painful and in severe cases requires medical evacuation to decompress the sinuses. Topical sprays with a shorter duration are generally the safer choice, and diving with any decongestant should be a temporary measure, not a long-term solution.

Chronic difficulty equalising deserves a visit to an ear, nose and throat specialist. Structural narrowing, polyps, or scarring from previous infections can all be assessed and sometimes treated. Divers who have had middle-ear surgery should obtain medical clearance before returning to the water.

## Sinus Equalisation

The ears are not the only air spaces that need attention. The sinuses — the frontal sinuses above the eyes and the maxillary sinuses in the cheeks — also contain air and are connected to the nasal passages. Sinus squeeze during descent produces a dull ache behind the eyes or across the cheekbones. Unlike the ears, the sinuses equalise passively through their natural drainage pathways; there is no active technique for them beyond keeping the passages clear. A diver with a head cold or blocked sinuses who dives anyway is likely to experience sinus pain and possibly blood-stained mucus in the mask after the dive, as capillaries in the sinus lining rupture under the differential.

The rule is straightforward: if you cannot breathe freely through both nostrils at the surface, do not dive.

## Mask Squeeze

Masks also require equalisation, though the fix is simple. As depth increases, the air space inside the mask compresses and the lower pressure relative to the face can cause the mask to seal tighter, pressing the skirting against the face and creating suction against the eyes. Divers are taught to exhale gently through the nose into the mask during descent. This is usually automatic after a few dives, but novices sometimes forget and arrive at the bottom with reddened, bloodshot eyes and petechiae — the small haemorrhages caused by the suction of mask squeeze.

## Long-Term Ear Health

Ears are the recurring vulnerability in a diving career. Repeated barotrauma, even minor episodes, can scar the Eustachian tube lining and make future equalization progressively harder. Ear infections after diving — typically from contaminated water trapped in the ear canal — are unrelated to equalization but benefit from the same attentiveness. Ear plugs are generally not compatible with scuba diving because they trap an air space between the plug and eardrum that cannot be equalised.

Looking after your ears means descending carefully, never ignoring pain, and seeking prompt medical assessment after any squeeze that produces hearing loss, tinnitus, or vertigo. [Open the map](/map) to find dive sites near you and plan descents that match your current comfort and technique.

## Practice Above Water

Because the Frenzel manoeuvre involves unfamiliar throat and tongue movements, it pays to practice on dry land. Many freedivers spend hours learning the muscle control needed before they enter the water. Scuba divers who find Valsalva unreliable often see immediate improvement after a week of practicing Frenzel while watching television or commuting. The movements feel strange at first — the tongue pressed against the hard palate, the glottis held shut, the back-of-throat 'K' — but they become quick and reliable with repetition.

Equalization is one of the few dive skills that can be meaningfully rehearsed outside the water, and for many divers, investing that time transforms a frustrating, pain-limited descent into an effortless one.
