Colombia is one of the few countries in the world with coastlines on two separate oceans, and its diving exploits both. In the Caribbean, the island groups of San Andrés and Providencia, the wall diving off the mainland coast near Taganga, and a growing ecotourism scene around the Rosario Islands give Caribbean-flavoured tropical diving within a short flight of Bogotá. In the Pacific, the situation is different and more dramatic: Malpelo Island, one of the most remote dive sites in the western hemisphere, and the Gorgona Island national park deliver the kind of schooling hammerhead and open-ocean pelagic encounters that are vanishingly rare in the Caribbean. Colombia's dive infrastructure has improved markedly over the past decade, and the underwater environment itself reflects the reduced fishing pressure of the Pacific coast's difficult access.

## 1. Malpelo, Pacific Ocean

Malpelo is an isolated oceanic island 506 kilometres west of the Colombian mainland, accessible by a 36-hour liveaboard journey from Buenaventura. The UNESCO World Heritage site is justifiably famous for its schooling scalloped hammerhead sharks — the aggregations here are among the most consistent in the world, with hundreds of animals possible during peak periods from June through September. Silky sharks, whale sharks, mola mola, and enormous schools of jacks and snapper accompany the hammerheads. The topography is dramatic: sheer underwater cliffs, passages between rock pinnacles, and the open blue-water column where the pelagic action happens. This is advanced diving — current, depth, and the cognitive demands of open-water pelagic encounters require Advanced Open Water at minimum, and most operators prefer experienced divers.

## 2. Providencia Island, Caribbean

The island of Providencia lies 90 kilometres north of San Andrés and feels several decades behind it in development. This is largely to Providencia's diving advantage: the Seaflower Biosphere Reserve that encompasses both islands is centred here, and the coral reef system around Providencia is in significantly better health than most comparable Caribbean sites. The coral ring around the island includes a near-continuous outer reef wall dropping from around 12 metres to recreational limits, with some sections deeper. Caribbean reef sharks are reliably sighted, and the hard coral coverage — including healthy stands of elkhorn coral that have largely disappeared from the wider Caribbean — is among the best in the region. Open Water for the shallower sections; Advanced for the outer wall.

## 3. San Andrés, Caribbean

San Andrés is the more accessible and more heavily visited of Colombia's Caribbean islands, and its dive sites reflect that duality — some areas show the pressure of high diver traffic, while others, particularly on the island's less-developed eastern side and at the El Acuario snorkel area, retain genuine charm. The wall diving off the north end of the island drops to 40 metres with impressive hard coral coverage. Nurse sharks rest on the sandy bottom near the reef crest; eagle rays are commonly sighted in the channels. The liveaboard boat industry that serves Providencia often stops at San Andrés; for independent visitors, the island has several PADI operators running well-organised day trips.

## 4. Taganga, Caribbean Coast

Taganga is a fishing village that became a backpacker hub and, as a consequence, developed one of the most accessible and affordable dive training scenes in the Caribbean. The diving itself is not exceptional by the standards of the offshore islands — the sites closest to the mainland are affected by river runoff and boat traffic — but the outer sites, particularly those near the Tayrona National Natural Park boundary, improve markedly. Visibility reaches 15 to 20 metres on good days. The reef fish diversity near Tayrona Park is good, and the site offers straightforward, uncomplicated diving at Caribbean prices that are significantly lower than comparable island destinations. PADI Open Water certification from Taganga is popular among budget travellers.

## 5. Gorgona Island National Park, Pacific

Gorgona Island, 35 kilometres off the Colombian Pacific coast, was until 1984 the site of a high-security prison — a history that gave the island its long isolation and its reefs their current condition. The waters around the island hold humpback whales from June through October (Colombia is one of the most important Pacific humpback calving areas in the world), along with whale sharks, dolphins, and all the species of the Tropical Eastern Pacific reef: sea turtles, moray eels, and endemic Pacific reef fish not found in the Caribbean. Dive operators from Guapi on the mainland run day trips; the national park also has accommodation on the island. Current can be significant around the outer points.

## 6. Bombita, San Andrés Archipelago

Bombita is a specific site within the San Andrés dive area — a seamount that rises to relatively shallow water and is known among local operators for its consistent shark action. Blacktip reef sharks are nearly always present, and the site aggregates the kind of mid-water fish populations — jacks, snapper, barracuda — that indicate a healthy current-swept reef. Advanced Open Water recommended due to depth and current variability.

## 7. Torin, Caribbean Sites

Torin is among the more established reef sites in the Colombia Caribbean accessible from Cartagena-based operators. The Rosario Islands group off Cartagena — technically the closest island diving to a major Colombian city — includes varied reef topography at depths from 8 to 25 metres. The tourism pressure around Cartagena is significant and some of the inshore reefs have suffered accordingly, but the outer Rosario sites maintain reasonable coral health and the full complement of Caribbean reef fish. This is accessible, straightforward Caribbean diving appropriate for all certification levels.

## 8. Punta Venado, Pacific Coast

Along the Colombian Pacific coast between Buenaventura and Tumaco, a series of rocky points and headlands produce interesting dive environments at depths from 10 to 25 metres. These sites see minimal dive traffic and are largely unexplored in the international dive literature. The Pacific coast's biological richness — driven by nutrient upwellings and the Humboldt Current's northern influence — translates to dense fish populations and good macro life. The logistics of Pacific coast diving outside Buenaventura are genuinely challenging and require local knowledge; operators with boats willing to work these sites are few.

## 9. Playa Vaca, Caribbean

Playa Vaca is a reef site accessible from San Andrés that provides a more relaxed alternative to the busier wall dives. The shallow reef structure at depths of 8 to 15 metres supports a good collection of macro life including Christmas tree worms, flamingo tongue cowries, and various blennies. The site is appropriate for Open Water divers and for those who prefer a slower, more observational style of diving to the dramatic open-water encounters at the wall sites and offshore islands. Night dives here are productive.

## 10. Cartagena Bay Historic Wrecks, Caribbean

The bay and outer coast near Cartagena holds numerous historically significant wrecks from the colonial era — Spanish galleons, privateers, and various merchant vessels from centuries of maritime commerce. Many are in water too deep for recreational diving, but some shallower examples are accessible to working with specialist operators. The sediment and reduced visibility of inshore Caribbean water make these sites more archaeological than aesthetic, but for history-minded divers the context of Cartagena's colonial past gives the wrecks a dimension that purely recreational sites lack.

## Planning a Diving Trip to Colombia

The Caribbean sites (San Andrés, Providencia, Taganga) are diveable year-round, with the clearest conditions generally from December through April. The Pacific sites require more planning: Malpelo liveaboards are limited in number and fill months in advance for the peak hammerhead season (June–September); Gorgona Island diving is best from June through November when humpback whales are present. Taganga has a developed backpacker dive scene with multiple operators competing on price; San Andrés and Providencia have more conventional resort-style operations.
