Jacques Cousteau rated the Poor Knights Islands among the world's top dive sites, and the reputation has stuck for good reason. This cluster of ancient volcanic islands lies about 23 kilometres off the Tutukaka coast of New Zealand's North Island, surrounded by deep water and bathed by the East Auckland Current, a warm flow that peels off the Coral Sea and carries subtropical water and the occasional tropical stray far south of where they belong. The result is a marine reserve where temperate kelp forests share the same walls as fish you would expect in Queensland, riddled with arches, caves and tunnels carved into the soft rock.

## Volcanic Islands and a Warm Current

The Poor Knights are the eroded remnants of rhyolitic volcanoes that erupted roughly 11 million years ago. Their cliffs drop sheer into the sea and continue underwater as vertical walls, and the soft volcanic rock has been hollowed by the ocean into an extraordinary collection of caves, archways and tunnels. There is no shallow shelf to speak of — the islands rise from deep water — which means the diving is dramatic from the moment you descend.

The defining oceanographic feature is the warm current. While the surrounding New Zealand coast is firmly temperate, the East Auckland Current keeps the water around the Poor Knights a few degrees warmer than the mainland and seeds it with subtropical and tropical species. Lord Howe coralfish, mado, demoiselles and even the occasional tropical wanderer mix with snapper, blue maomao and kingfish, producing an assemblage found nowhere else in New Zealand.

## Caves, Arches and Riko Riko

The islands' geology is half the appeal. Rikoriko Cave, on the western side, is reckoned to be one of the largest sea caves in the world by volume — a vast domed chamber that boats can motor right into, where the acoustics are so good that concerts have been performed inside. Diving and snorkelling at its mouth reveals walls of jewel anemones and encrusting life that thrives in the low light.

Elsewhere the islands are pierced by archways and swim-throughs. Sites like Blue Maomao Arch fill with dense, shimmering schools of their namesake fish, the blue maomao stacking up in the dappled light beneath the arch in numbers that can blot out the surface. Northern Arch, a deep gap between two pinnacles, is famous for the sheer density of fish and for the rays, kingfish and the occasional larger visitor that patrol it. The terrain rewards good buoyancy and a slow, exploratory pace.

## A No-Take Marine Reserve

The Poor Knights became a fully protected marine reserve, with all fishing banned in the waters immediately around the islands. The effect over the decades has been dramatic. Fish that are wary and scarce on the fished mainland coast are abundant and unbothered here: large snapper hang in the open, schools pack the arches, and the food web is intact in a way that is increasingly rare. Diving the reserve is a glimpse of what the New Zealand coast looked like before commercial and recreational fishing thinned it.

Landing on the islands themselves is prohibited to protect the seabird colonies and the rare reptiles, including tuatara, that survive there. All activity happens in or on the water, which keeps the impact concentrated and manageable. The reserve status is the single biggest reason the diving is as good as it is.

## Marine Life Through the Seasons

The mix of temperate and subtropical life changes through the year. Summer, roughly January to April, brings the warmest water — sometimes into the low 20s Celsius — the best visibility, and the peak of the subtropical influx carried down on the current. This is also when divers have the best chance of encountering the seasonal aggregations of stingrays that gather around the islands, sometimes in startling numbers stacked under the arches.

Winter diving is colder and the water can be greener, but the resident fish life remains, and the lack of crowds has its own appeal. Bronze whaler sharks pass through, kingfish hunt the pinnacles, and the dense invertebrate life on the walls — nudibranchs, anemones, sponges — is a constant regardless of season. Visibility at the Poor Knights is often excellent, frequently exceeding 20 to 30 metres in summer conditions.

## Getting There and Diving It

Access is by boat from Tutukaka, a small harbour town on the Northland coast about a 25-minute drive from Whangarei. The crossing takes under an hour, and day boats run two-dive trips with a surface interval, often including a cruise into Rikoriko Cave. The islands suit every level: sheltered sites work for newly certified divers, while the deeper walls, the bigger arches and the caves reward experience.

New Zealand has a strong diving culture spread across both islands, from operators on the Northland coast to clubs and centres far to the south such as Dive Otago and the long-running Dolphin Underwater Club, which speak to how seriously the country takes its underwater world. But for sheer spectacle in a single day trip, the Poor Knights are the country's standout, combining geology, protected fish life and that improbable subtropical warmth.

## Snorkelling, Freediving and the Surface World

The Poor Knights are not only a scuba destination. The clear water and the dense fish life close to the surface make the islands superb for snorkelling and freediving, and many day boats carry snorkellers alongside divers. The mouths of the caves, the shallow tops of the arches and the kelp-fringed walls in the first few metres are alive with maomao, snapper and demoiselles, and on a calm summer day the surface activity rivals what is happening below.

Above the waterline the islands are a seabird stronghold, with vast colonies of Buller's shearwaters and other petrels that wheel around the cliffs at dusk. The combination of a thriving seabird population overhead and the protected fish life beneath is part of what makes a day at the Poor Knights feel like a glimpse of a more intact natural world. Boats often pause to let visitors take in the towering cliffs, the blowholes and the sheer scale of the rock before or after the dives.
