Sudan's Red Sea coast is, for many experienced divers, the finest diving in the entire Red Sea. That assessment comes from divers who have seen Egypt, the Maldives, and the Pacific, and still return to Sudan's offshore reefs for the scale of the marine life encounters that the northern Red Sea's deepwater walls and seamounts produce. The Sudanese coast's relative inaccessibility — there is no developed dive resort infrastructure comparable to Sharm el-Sheikh or Hurghada — has preserved its reefs in a condition that more accessible destinations can no longer replicate. Shark populations here are genuinely large. The wrecks are remarkable. And the connection to diving history is direct: Jacques Cousteau used Sha'ab Rumi as the site of his Conshelf II underwater habitat in 1963, living on the reef for a month to prove long-term underwater habitation was possible. Water temperatures range from 22°C in January–February to 32°C in August. Visibility is typically exceptional — 20–40 metres on the offshore reefs. ## 1. Sha'ab Rumi, Northern Sudan

Sha'ab Rumi is the historical and experiential pinnacle of Sudanese diving. The reef's northern end hosts the remains of Cousteau's Conshelf II habitat — the underwater house where six men lived for 30 days in 1963. The shark cage, tool shed, and main habitat structure are still in place on the reef slope at around 10–30 metres, encrusted with coral and sponge but recognisable, and represent one of the most significant sites in the history of underwater exploration. The reef itself is world-class: the Prechamber on the southwest tip is a natural bowl where grey reef sharks gather in numbers that can exceed 20 individuals in a single pass. Hammerhead sharks school in the blue water off the north face. The wall is vertical and immaculate. Advanced Open Water required; dive with experienced Sudanese liveaboard crew.

## 2. Umbria Wreck, Port Sudan

The Umbria is the finest accessible wreck in the Red Sea and one of the top five wrecks in the world. An Italian passenger-cargo vessel requisitioned as a transport in WWII, it was scuttled by its own crew on 10 June 1940 — the morning Italy entered the war — to prevent its cargo of bombs and fuses from falling into British hands. It sits upright in 37 metres, its holds still containing approximately 360,000 artillery shells, 60 Fiat CR.42 aircraft, and hundreds of cases of Chianti. The wreck is completely intact and penetrable at multiple levels. The shallowest sections are accessible to Open Water divers; the full wreck requires Advanced certification. Visibility inside the cargo holds can reach 20 metres. The Umbria is in Port Sudan Harbour itself — accessible without a liveaboard. Remarkable historical artefacts preserved in place.

## 3. Sanganeb Atoll, Northern Sudan

Sanganeb is the only true oceanic atoll in the Red Sea, a ring of coral rising from abyssal depths that encloses a lagoon, with a lighthouse structure on its northern tip. The outer walls of Sanganeb are among the most dived sites in Sudan — the north plateau, north tip, and southwest corner all produce different experiences. The north plateau at 15–25 metres hosts enormous schools of barracuda and jack that can form cylinders of fish around a hovering diver. The south tip wall drops vertically to beyond 200 metres, and hammerhead aggregations are frequently observed here in the morning, before the sun reaches full intensity. Grey reef sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and occasional tiger sharks are regular on the outer reef. Advanced Open Water. Best season October through April.

## 4. Sha'ab Suedi, Toyota Wreck

Sha'ab Suedi contains two wrecks in the same site: the Blue Belt, an Egyptian freighter sunk in 1977, and the Toyota Wreck, a more recent casualty whose cargo of new Toyota trucks is still visible in the holds, the vehicles relatively intact on the bottom at 28 metres. The Blue Belt is more substantially encrusted and biologically rich, its hull draped in soft corals and gorgonians; the Toyota Wreck's relative newness means the cargo is clearer and the structural details are sharper. Both wrecks are accessible to sport divers. The combination of two wrecks in a single dive is the site's particular attraction. Visibility in the lagoon averages 15–20 metres. Open Water for the shallow sections; Advanced for the deeper holds. Liveaboard access.

## 5. Angarosh, Northern Sudan

Angarosh is a remote reef system north of Sanganeb, accessible only by liveaboard, whose shark populations are among the most concentrated in the Red Sea. Hammerhead sharks aggregate here throughout the year, with peak numbers from October through January. Morning dives on the north face of the reef in the early hours — the period when liveaboard dive masters typically schedule the hammerhead dives — regularly produce encounters with aggregations of 20–50 hammerheads schooling in the cold thermocline layer at 25–30 metres. Grey reef sharks are permanent residents. The reef structure itself is a dramatic atoll-edge wall with pristine coral coverage. This is serious, exposed diving: current can be strong, and the open-water context means good buoyancy and navigation skills are essential. Advanced Open Water minimum.

## 6. Mesharifa, Northern Sudan

Mesharifa is a less-frequented reef north of Sha'ab Rumi that rewards liveaboard itineraries that extend beyond the standard Port Sudan–Sanganeb–Sha'ab Rumi circuit. The reef's south face has a dramatic wall that drops from about 5 metres to well beyond recreational limits, covered in large sea fans and beautiful soft coral trees. Napoleon wrasse are numerous here — a sign of healthy reef with minimal fishing pressure. Schooling fish are abundant on the upper reef, and the blue water off the wall edge regularly produces oceanic whitetip sharks, one of the Red Sea's most charismatic open-water species. Advanced Open Water required. Visibility 25–35 metres in the October–April season. A full day at Mesharifa typically yields three dives on different sections of the reef.

## 7. Sha'ab Ambar, Central Sudan

Sha'ab Ambar is a mid-coast reef that provides calm, sheltered conditions in the lagoon suitable for night diving and relaxed daytime exploration between more demanding offshore sites. The lagoon interior has sandy patches with garden eels in large colonies, and the reef crest supports good hard coral coverage with dense communities of Moorish idol and surgeonfish. The outer reef wall is exposed to open water and provides a more demanding dive with better pelagic sightings. Moray eels here are large and inquisitive. This site suits divers looking for a varied day: a demanding outer wall dive in the morning, a relaxed lagoon dive in the afternoon, and a productive night dive in the sheltered shallows. Open Water for lagoon; Advanced for the outer wall.

## 8. Abington Reef, Northern Sudan

Abington is one of the northernmost major reefs in Sudan's liveaboard circuit, close to the Egyptian border, and its coral walls are in exceptional condition from years of minimal dive traffic. The northern side of the reef has a current-fed wall where grey reef sharks hold station against the flow, and schools of barracuda spiral overhead. The eastern side descends into a garden of enormous table corals, some exceeding 3 metres in diameter. Visibility is consistently above 25 metres. The reef's isolation means it is only dived by liveaboards with specific northern itineraries, and the combination of pristine corals and healthy shark populations justifies the extra distance. Advanced Open Water recommended. Best October through March.

## 9. Merlo Reef, Port Sudan Area

Merlo Reef is one of the shallower, more accessible sites within range of Port Sudan, making it useful as an acclimatisation or entry dive at the start of a liveaboard trip. The reef crest at 5–8 metres has good hard coral coverage with plenty of reef fish activity, including large populations of angelfish, butterflyfish, and surgeonfish. Turtles are common in the shallows. The outer slope descends to about 25 metres over a series of coral terraces. Lionfish inhabit the crevices — as everywhere in the Red Sea — and the local octopus population is actively visible during day dives. Open Water level throughout. Visibility 15–20 metres. A practical warm-up before the more demanding offshore sites.

## 10. Precontinent Reef (Sha'ab Rumi Precontinent), Northern Sudan

Immediately adjacent to the main Sha'ab Rumi diving is the area Cousteau designated Precontinent, where the secondary structures of his Conshelf II operation — the smaller habitat pods, the underwater camera platforms — remain on the reef. These structures are at shallower depths than the main habitat, accessible to Open Water divers, and provide extraordinary close encounters with history. The saucer-shaped garage pod where Cousteau's two-person submersible was housed sits at about 15 metres, now a coral-encrusted dome. The reef around it has recovered fully from the 1963 disturbance and is among the most photogenic sections of Sha'ab Rumi. Shark presence is consistent. Open Water level for the shallow structures; Advanced for the main habitat complex at depth. Year-round access by liveaboard.

## Planning Your Sudan Dive Trip

Access to Sudan's diving is exclusively by liveaboard operating from Port Sudan, the country's main Red Sea port. International flights reach Port Sudan direct from regional hubs or via Cairo; several liveaboard operators co-ordinate flights with departure dates. The main liveaboard season runs October through April, when winds are more predictable and water temperatures are comfortable without the intense summer heat. The Sudanese government requires permits for diving certain areas, which reputable liveaboard operators include in their booking arrangements. Some nationalities face visa restrictions for Sudan; check current requirements before booking. Standard trips run 7–10 days covering Sha'ab Rumi, Sanganeb, Angarosh, and the northern reefs. The infrastructure is basic by Egyptian or Maldivian standards — Sudan liveaboards attract divers who prioritise underwater quality over above-water luxury — but the diving itself is consistently rated among the best in the world.
