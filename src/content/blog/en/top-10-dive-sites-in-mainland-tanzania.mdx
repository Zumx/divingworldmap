Mainland Tanzania faces the warm western Indian Ocean along a coast that runs from the Kenyan border in the north to Mozambique in the south, and its diving is quieter, wilder and less developed than the famous reefs of Zanzibar offshore. The mainland's great attraction is Mafia Island, a marine park where whale sharks feed in the nutrient-rich water pushed out by the vast Rufiji River delta, alongside some of the healthiest reefs in the region. North of there, the reefs off Dar es Salaam and the resort coast at Bagamoyo and beyond offer accessible diving, while the far south around the Mnazi Bay-Ruvuma estuary marine park near the Mozambique border holds pristine, rarely-dived reefs. Water temperatures are tropical, from around 25°C in the cooler months to 29°C in the warm season, with visibility typically 15 to 30 metres. The diving is shaped by the same monsoon rhythm as Zanzibar, with the calmer northeast monsoon from December to March offering the best conditions and the whale shark season.

## 1. Mafia Island Marine Park

Mafia Island, south of Zanzibar and off the Rufiji delta, is the jewel of mainland Tanzania's diving — a marine park protecting an extensive system of reefs, channels and the calm waters of Chole Bay. The reefs here are healthy and diverse, with hard and soft corals, large groupers, napoleon wrasse, reef sharks, turtles and dense fish life. The park's protected status and relative remoteness have kept the diving exceptional, and the lagoon and channel sites offer everything from gentle reef dives to current-swept channels. Mafia sees far fewer divers than Zanzibar, rewarding those who make the journey with quieter, pristine reefs.

## 2. Whale Sharks off Mafia Island

The waters off Mafia Island host one of the most reliable whale shark aggregations in the Indian Ocean, drawn by the plankton-rich water flowing from the Rufiji delta. Unlike many destinations where whale sharks are seasonal migrants, Mafia hosts a population that feeds in the shallows for much of the year, with peak activity typically from around October to March. The encounters are usually surface-based snorkelling rather than scuba, allowing close observation of these gentle giants feeding near the surface. It is one of the great wildlife experiences of East Africa and the main reason many divers travel to Mafia.

## 3. Kinasi Pass, Mafia Island

Kinasi Pass is the channel connecting Chole Bay to the open ocean off Mafia, and it is the signature dive of the marine park. Tidal currents funnel through the pass, bringing nutrients and concentrating marine life — large groupers, napoleon wrasse, reef sharks, turtles, rays and dense schools of fish. The coral on the channel walls is healthy and varied. The current makes this a drift dive that suits divers with some experience, and the concentration of large marine life on a good tide is exceptional. Kinasi Pass showcases why Mafia's diving is regarded as among the best in the region.

## 4. Chole Bay Reefs, Mafia Island

Chole Bay, the sheltered lagoon at the heart of Mafia Island Marine Park, offers a series of gentle reef dives protected from the open ocean. The calm conditions and shallow, coral-rich sites make this ideal for relaxed diving, training and macro photography, with abundant reef fish, moray eels, octopus and the occasional turtle. The bay's protected aspect means diving is possible even when conditions outside the pass are rough. It complements the more demanding channel dives, giving Mafia a full range of diving from beginner-friendly lagoon reefs to current-swept passes.

## 5. Dar es Salaam Reefs and Islands

Off the coast of Dar es Salaam, Tanzania's largest city, lie a string of islands and reefs accessible from the mainland — including the Dar es Salaam Marine Reserve system protecting islands such as Bongoyo and Mbudya. The reefs support good fish life, coral and the occasional larger visitor, and the proximity to the city makes them accessible day dives. While the diving here does not match the pristine reefs of Mafia, the island reserves offer a convenient and pleasant introduction to mainland Tanzanian diving for those based in or passing through Dar es Salaam.

## 6. Sinda and Mbudya Islands, Dar es Salaam

The small islands off Dar es Salaam, including Mbudya and Sinda, sit within marine reserves and offer reef diving and snorkelling in clear water close to the city. The reefs support reef fish, coral and the typical Indian Ocean marine life of the region, and the sheltered conditions suit a range of levels. These island reserves are popular as day trips from Dar es Salaam, combining easy diving with the chance to relax on quiet island beaches. They provide accessible diving on a coast where the most spectacular sites require longer journeys south.

## 7. Mtwara and the Southern Reefs

Mtwara, in the far south of Tanzania near the Mozambique border, gives access to some of the country's most pristine and least-dived reefs. The southern coast here sees very few divers, and the reefs are in excellent condition, supporting rich coral and abundant fish life. The diving requires more effort to reach and is more weather-dependent, but it rewards adventurous divers with a genuine sense of exploration on reefs that remain largely untouched by dive tourism. Mtwara is the gateway to the Mnazi Bay-Ruvuma estuary marine park.

## 8. Mnazi Bay-Ruvuma Estuary Marine Park

The Mnazi Bay-Ruvuma Estuary Marine Park, in the far south near the Mozambique border, protects an extensive system of reefs, mangroves and the estuary of the Ruvuma River. The reefs here are among the healthiest in Tanzania, with diverse hard and soft corals, large fish, turtles and the occasional larger pelagic visitor, and the protected status keeps fishing pressure low. The remoteness means very few divers, pristine conditions and a true wilderness diving experience. This is mainland Tanzania at its wildest, suiting experienced divers willing to travel far for unspoiled reefs.

## 9. Bagamoyo Coast Reefs

The coast around Bagamoyo, north of Dar es Salaam, offers reef diving along a historically significant stretch of the Tanzanian mainland. The reefs here support coral and fish life typical of the region, and the relatively undeveloped nature of the coast means quiet diving away from the crowds. Bagamoyo's historical interest above water — as a former major port on the Indian Ocean trade routes and slave trade — adds cultural depth to a diving trip. The diving complements the island reserves off Dar es Salaam for those exploring the central mainland coast.

## 10. Ras Kutani and the Southern Mainland Reefs

South of Dar es Salaam, the coast around Ras Kutani and the stretch towards the Rufiji delta offers reef diving in warm, clear water with abundant marine life and far fewer divers than the offshore islands. The reefs support good fish populations, coral and the occasional larger visitor, and the relative seclusion of this coast makes for relaxed, uncrowded diving. The proximity to the Rufiji delta and Mafia Island to the south means this stretch of coast benefits from the same nutrient-rich waters that support the region's exceptional marine life.

## Planning Your Trip

Mainland Tanzania's prime dive season runs from roughly December to March during the calmer northeast monsoon, which also coincides with the peak whale shark season off Mafia. The southern monsoon from June to September is windier and can limit access to the more exposed southern reefs, while April and May bring the long rains when many operators reduce activity. Water is warm year-round, so a 3mm wetsuit suffices. Mafia Island is the standout destination and requires a flight or boat transfer; Dar es Salaam serves the accessible island reserves; and the far south around Mtwara rewards adventurous divers with pristine, remote reefs. Marine park fees apply at Mafia and Mnazi Bay. Open Water covers many sites; the channel dives at Mafia call for some experience with current.
