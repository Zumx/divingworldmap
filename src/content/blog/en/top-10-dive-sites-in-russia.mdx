Russia is the largest country on Earth, and its dive sites are scattered across that vastness in a way that defies any single trip. There is no warm reef circuit here, no easy week of bath-water diving. Instead Russia offers extremes: the frozen White Sea where divers cut holes in metre-thick ice, the impossible clarity of Lake Baikal, the silted wrecks of the Black Sea, and the bizarre flooded landscapes of inland quarries. The diving culture is hardy and technical, built around drysuits, cold-water training, and clubs with long histories. For divers willing to embrace the cold, the rewards are unlike anything in the tropics.

## The White Sea Under the Ice

The White Sea, an arm of the Barents Sea reaching down towards Karelia, is Russia's signature cold-water destination. In late winter, dive bases near the Karelian shore cut access holes through ice that can exceed a metre in thickness, and divers descend on tethers into water hovering around minus 1.5 degrees Celsius. The visibility beneath the ice is extraordinary, often exceeding 30 metres, with shafts of green light filtering down through the frozen ceiling. The marine life is rich and strange: soft corals, anemones, wolffish, lumpsuckers, and the occasional beluga whale. This is serious ice diving, requiring a full drysuit, redundant regulators rated for cold water, and ice-diving certification.

## Lake Baikal

Lake Baikal in Siberia is the deepest and oldest freshwater lake on the planet, holding roughly a fifth of the world's unfrozen fresh water. Its water is famously clear, with visibility that can reach 40 metres in the right season, and it is home to endemic species found nowhere else, including the Baikal seal, the only exclusively freshwater seal in the world. Divers explore steep underwater cliffs, sponge gardens unique to the lake, and dramatic drop-offs along the western shore near Olkhon Island. The water is cold year-round, rarely climbing far above 10 degrees Celsius even in summer, and winter ice diving here is a bucket-list experience for the truly committed.

## Black Sea Wrecks off Crimea and the Caucasus

The Black Sea coast offers Russia's most accessible warm-weather diving, relatively speaking. The water is brackish and the deep layers are anoxic, which has the curious effect of preserving wrecks remarkably well below the oxygenated surface zone. Dive centres along the coast run trips to the steamer wreck of the Azov and numerous other sunken vessels, several of which date from the wars of the twentieth century. Local operators such as those running the site known as the hull of the steamer Azov take advantage of summer water temperatures that climb into the low twenties Celsius. Visibility is modest compared to the White Sea, but the historical density of wrecks makes this a draw for divers who love submerged history.

## The Sunken Wrecks and Tank Alley

Among the more curious Russian dive attractions are the deliberately created underwater museums. Sites like the one known as the Alley of Leaders, where busts of historical figures stand on the seabed, and dive parks where divers can explore a sunken bus, a railway carriage, and other unexpected objects, give Russian quarry and coastal diving a distinct character. These artificial sites, often found near established clubs and bases, are popular training grounds precisely because they give divers something memorable to navigate towards in otherwise featureless water.

## Inland Quarries and Flooded Mines

For the millions of Russians who live far from any sea, flooded quarries are the everyday answer. Sites like the quarry at Hurghada, an inland flooded pit named with a touch of irony after the Egyptian resort, and numerous other former extraction pits across the country offer cold, clear, sheltered water ideal for training. Bases such as Aquaglobus and the dive base of the Alternativa club have built thriving communities around these sites, complete with platforms, submerged training objects, and graduated depth profiles. The water is cold and visibility varies, but the convenience and safety of a sheltered quarry make these the backbone of recreational diving in much of the country.

## The Northwest Lakes and Karelian Walls

The lakes of the northwest, near the Finnish border, hold some of the most dramatic freshwater diving in European Russia. Underwater walls such as the Mustasaari Wall and the Putsaari Wall plunge into dark, tannin-stained water, their faces draped with freshwater sponges. The visibility is lower than Baikal, coloured by the surrounding forests and peat, but the sheer geology of these submerged cliffs is striking. Dive clubs from St Petersburg run regular weekend trips north into Karelia to explore these sites, often combining the diving with the wider appeal of the lake-and-forest landscape.

## Far Eastern Diving in the Sea of Japan

Russia's Pacific coast around Vladivostok and the Sea of Japan offers cold-water diving with a temperate Pacific cast of characters. Giant Pacific octopus, scallops, sea urchins, and kelp forests fill the dives around the islands of Peter the Great Gulf. The water is cold and the season short, peaking in late summer and autumn when temperatures and visibility are at their best. It is a region little known to foreign divers but well loved by the local community, who prize its abundance of large invertebrates and the wild, undeveloped feel of the coastline.

## Planning a Russian Dive Trip

Diving in Russia demands proper cold-water preparation regardless of the site. A quality drysuit, cold-rated regulators, and appropriate thermal undergarments are essential, and ice diving requires specific certification and a tethered, supervised setup. Trips are best arranged through established clubs and bases, which handle logistics in a country where dive infrastructure is concentrated rather than ubiquitous. Seasons matter enormously: the White Sea ice diving peaks in spring, Baikal has both a summer and an ice season, and the Black Sea is a summer affair. Plan around the water, not the calendar.
