In February 1944, the United States launched Operation Hailstone against the Imperial Japanese Navy's forward base at Truk, a sprawling natural anchorage in the central Pacific now known by its Chuukese name, Chuuk Lagoon. Over roughly two days, carrier aircraft sent more than fifty ships and over two hundred aircraft to the bottom. The lagoon, ringed by a barrier reef and dotted with high volcanic islands, closed over the wreckage like a lid. Eight decades later, that wreckage remains where it sank, and the lagoon holds the densest concentration of shipwrecks anywhere on the planet — a flooded museum of the Pacific War that divers reach by flying to one of the most remote corners of Micronesia.

## A Lagoon That Became a Graveyard

Truk was the most important Japanese base in the central Pacific, often called the Gibraltar of the Pacific for its natural defences. The barrier reef encircling the lagoon created an anchorage large enough to shelter an entire fleet, and the Japanese had fortified the surrounding islands with airfields, gun emplacements, and supply depots. When American intelligence confirmed the scale of the base, the assault was overwhelming and swift. Many of the sunken vessels were not warships but armed merchant freighters — Maru ships — loaded with the materiel of war. It is precisely these cargo holds, stuffed with the everyday machinery of a military supply chain, that make Chuuk such an extraordinary place to dive.

## What Lies in the Holds

The cargo wrecks of Chuuk are time capsules. Descend into the holds of vessels like the Fujikawa Maru and you find aircraft fuselages, propellers, and a famous row of Zero fighter parts. The San Francisco Maru, deep at around 50 to 64 metres and known to technical divers as the Million Dollar Wreck, carries tanks on its deck, trucks, mines, and shells. The Nippo Maru holds a tank, artillery pieces, and a wheelhouse where the helm still stands. Throughout the fleet, divers pass sake bottles, gas masks, medical supplies, lanterns, ammunition, and the personal effects of crews. Human remains are present at several sites and are treated, rightly, as the war graves they are — not to be touched, disturbed, or removed.

## The Diving Itself

Chuuk offers something for nearly every level of trained diver, though the most celebrated wrecks reward depth and experience. Shallow wrecks and upper structures sit within recreational limits, their decks and masts encrusted with soft coral, sponges, and anemones, patrolled by reef fish, lionfish, and the occasional reef shark. The deeper wrecks, and any meaningful penetration of the holds, are the province of technical divers with appropriate training in decompression and overhead environments. Visibility in the lagoon is generally good, water temperatures hover comfortably around the high 20s Celsius year-round, and most operators run dives from day boats based at the few resorts on Weno, the main island.

## A Reef Reclaiming the Steel

What strikes many divers most is not the war but the life. Eight decades of warm, nutrient-rich water have turned the steel hulls into thriving artificial reefs. Hard and soft corals sheet the railings, masts rise as coral-crusted towers, and shoals of glassfish swirl through cargo holds in living clouds. Hawksbill turtles, eagle rays, and large grouper are regular sightings. The contrast — instruments of destruction now cradling some of the richest reef life in Micronesia — gives Chuuk a contemplative, almost reverent quality that sets it apart from any other wreck diving destination.

## Getting There and Diving Responsibly

Chuuk lies in the Federated States of Micronesia, reached by air via Guam, with connections through major Pacific hubs. It is genuinely remote, and a trip here is a commitment of time and planning. Most divers stay at one of a small number of dive-oriented resorts and dive the wrecks over a week or more, often combining recreational and technical days. The lagoon is a protected war grave under Chuukese and international understanding, and the ethical standard is absolute: take nothing, move nothing, and treat the human remains and personal effects with the respect owed to the men who died here. Removing artefacts is both illegal and a violation of the sites' sacred character.

## Technical Diving and the Deepest Wrecks

For technical divers, Chuuk is a destination of pilgrimage. The San Francisco Maru sits beyond recreational limits and demands trimix, careful planning, and a strong team. The Aikoku Maru, the Hanakawa Maru, and the deeper sections of the larger freighters reward divers who have invested in the training to explore overhead environments safely. Penetration of the holds carries the standard hazards — silt-out, entanglement, the disorientation of confined steel — compounded by the depth and the age of the wreckage, which is steadily corroding. Decompression obligations are significant, and the remoteness of Chuuk means self-sufficiency and conservative planning are not optional. Many of the world's most experienced wreck divers regard a Chuuk trimix trip as a career highlight.

## Why Chuuk Endures

Chuuk Lagoon is more than a dive site; it is a place where history is suspended in salt water, neither salvaged nor forgotten. The wrecks are slowly losing their battle with corrosion, and the fuel oil they still hold poses a long-term environmental concern that authorities and researchers continue to monitor. For now, though, the ghost fleet remains, draped in coral, patrolled by fish, and visited by divers who come not for adrenaline but for the strange, solemn experience of swimming through a moment in history that the lagoon has held intact for the better part of a century.
