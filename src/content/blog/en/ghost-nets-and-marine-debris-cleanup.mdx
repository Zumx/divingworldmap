There is a particular kind of silence on a wreck or reef where a ghost net has settled. Fish avoid it. The net hangs in the current like a torn curtain, and tangled within its mesh are the calcified remains of what it has already caught: crabs, fish skeletons picked clean, the occasional turtle shell. A net does not stop fishing when it is lost. It drifts and snags and continues to trap animals, which die, attract scavengers, trap those scavengers in turn, and so the cycle repeats. Marine biologists call this ghost fishing, and the scale of it is staggering.

## What Ghost Gear Actually Is

Ghost gear is any fishing equipment that has been abandoned, lost, or discarded at sea — nets, longlines, traps, pots, and the synthetic rope and float lines that connect them. The United Nations Environment Programme and the Food and Agriculture Organization estimate that somewhere between 500,000 and 1 million tonnes of fishing gear enters the ocean every year. Because most modern gear is made from nylon, polyethylene, and other durable plastics, it does not biodegrade. A nylon gillnet can persist in seawater for 400 to 600 years, slowly fragmenting into microplastics but never truly disappearing.

The gear is lost for ordinary reasons. Storms tear nets loose from their moorings. Trawls snag on rocky bottom and the crew cuts the line rather than lose the vessel. Gear conflicts on crowded fishing grounds sever buoy lines. In some fisheries, gear is simply dumped at the end of a trip because disposal ashore costs money. Whatever the cause, the result is the same: kilometres of monofilament and mesh left to drift through the water column or drape across reefs and wrecks.

## The Toll on Marine Life

The damage is indiscriminate. Ghost nets catch the species the fishery targeted, but they also entangle turtles, dolphins, seals, sharks, and seabirds that dive for fish. A loggerhead turtle that swims into a drifting net cannot surface to breathe and drowns within an hour. Larger animals like whales can tow entangling gear for months, the line cutting through skin and blubber until infection or starvation kills them. Studies in the Puget Sound and the Baltic have documented individual derelict nets that killed thousands of crabs over a single season before recovery teams reached them.

On coral reefs the mechanism is different but no less destructive. A net snagged on a reef saws back and forth with every swell, abrading the living coral surface and breaking off branching colonies that took decades to grow. The net also shades the coral beneath it, blocking the sunlight the symbiotic algae need. Where a net settles, the reef beneath it usually dies, and the dead skeleton is colonised by the fleshy algae that prevent new coral from recruiting.

## Why Divers Are Central to the Solution

Most ghost gear lies below the reach of surface operations, draped over structure that boats cannot grapple without doing further damage. Removing it requires divers who can assess the situation underwater, decide what can be cut free safely, and lift the gear without dragging it across the reef. This is skilled, hazardous work. A net is effectively a trap that does not distinguish between fish and diver, and entanglement underwater is one of the more dangerous situations a diver can face. Teams work in pairs or larger groups, each diver carrying line cutters and shears, and they establish clear communication and emergency procedures before entering the water.

Organisations like the Global Ghost Gear Initiative, Healthy Seas, and Ghost Diving have built international networks of trained technical divers who specialise in this work. Ghost Diving, founded in the Netherlands, has removed hundreds of tonnes of net from wrecks across Europe and beyond. The Olive Ridley Project focuses on the Indian Ocean, combining net removal with research into the turtle entanglements that gave the organisation its name.

## The Mechanics of a Cleanup Dive

A recovery operation begins with reconnaissance. Divers survey the gear to understand how it is attached, where it is under tension, and what marine life has colonised it. A net that has been in place for years often hosts a community of its own, and removing it carelessly can do more harm than leaving it. The team then cuts the net into manageable sections, attaches lifting bags, and inflates them in stages to bring each section to the surface where a support vessel hauls it aboard. A large net can take several dives over multiple days to clear completely.

Lift bags are essential because a waterlogged net is astonishingly heavy. A section that two divers can barely move on the bottom becomes manageable when a calibrated lift bag provides the buoyancy, and dangerous when it provides too much. Runaway ascents caused by over-inflated bags are a known hazard, which is why this work sits firmly in the technical diving domain rather than recreational.

## What Happens to the Recovered Gear

Recovered nets do not simply go to landfill. A growing circular economy has emerged around ghost gear. The Healthy Seas initiative partners with the yarn manufacturer Aquafil, whose ECONYL process regenerates nylon waste — including recovered fishing nets — into new nylon yarn used for carpets, swimwear, and clothing. Recovered nets are cleaned, sorted by material, and fed back into manufacturing rather than buried. This closes the loop and gives the cleanup effort an economic dimension that helps fund continued operations.

## How Recreational Divers Can Contribute

Not every diver is qualified to cut tensioned net from a wreck at 40 metres, but every diver can contribute. Reporting the location of ghost gear to local marine authorities or to apps run by the Global Ghost Gear Initiative gives recovery teams the coordinates they need. Carrying a small line cutter and removing loose monofilament fishing line — the kind that snags on shallow reefs and entangles fish — is within the ability of any competent recreational diver, provided they avoid net under tension. Participating in organised cleanup dives run by local clubs builds both the skill and the data set. Above all, divers who understand the problem become advocates for the gear-marking and reporting policies that prevent gear from being lost in the first place.
