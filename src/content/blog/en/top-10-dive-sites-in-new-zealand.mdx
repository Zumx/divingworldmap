New Zealand's diving reputation rests on a handful of exceptional sites, but the country's underwater geography runs far beyond the famous names. The country stretches across 1,600 kilometres of latitude, from subtropical waters in the north where dense schools of fish swirl around volcanic islands, to the cold, dark, alien fjords of Fiordland in the south, where a permanent layer of tannin-stained freshwater sits atop the sea and creates darkness at depth that fools deep-sea species into living in shallow water. Add a scattering of offshore seamounts, a political act that sent a Greenpeace ship to the bottom of Auckland Harbour, and one of the best accessible marine reserves in the southern hemisphere, and you have a country that rewards divers who explore seriously. ## 1. Poor Knights Islands, Northland

Jacques Cousteau reportedly named the Poor Knights among the top dive sites he had seen, and the reserve status granted in 1981 has only improved on what he found. The islands sit 24 kilometres offshore in current-swept subtropical water, and the diversity of fish life is extraordinary — massive schools of blue maomao and demoiselle fill underwater arches and caverns with living walls of fish. Sponge gardens on the deeper walls below 20 metres are among the finest in the Southern Hemisphere. The large caverns — the most famous being Rikoriko Cave, claimed to be one of the largest sea caves in the world — are entered from the water and accessed by dive boat. Water temperature: 18–22°C. Open Water certification for cavern entries; good buoyancy essential. Best season: January to May for warmest water and best fish aggregations.

## 2. Rainbow Warrior Wreck, Northland

The Rainbow Warrior, the Greenpeace flagship sunk by French secret service agents in Auckland Harbour in 1985, was towed to the Cavalli Islands off Northland in 1987 and deliberately scuttled as an artificial reef. She now lies at 26–28 metres on her port side, largely intact and thoroughly colonised by sponges, anemones and the full complement of northeastern New Zealand reef fish. The political history adds emotional weight to an already compelling dive. The swim through the wheelhouse, where light filters through portholes, is one of the most atmospheric wreck dives in the Pacific. Advanced certification recommended. Best season: November to May.

## 3. Fiordland, South Island

Diving Fiordland is an exercise in entering a genuinely alien environment. The ancient fjords — Milford Sound (Te Anau-Fiordland), Doubtful Sound, Dusky Sound — are filled with seawater, but the surface is overlain by a permanent freshwater lens, sometimes 5–10 metres deep, created by extreme annual rainfall exceeding 7,000 millimetres in parts of the interior. This freshwater layer blocks nearly all ambient light, creating darkness that begins within a few metres of the surface. Black coral trees grow at 5 metres instead of their usual 30+ metre depth. Brachiopods — living fossils found normally only in deep water — live on the fjord walls in the shallows. Sea temperatures in the fjords hold at 11–14°C year-round. Drysuit certification strongly recommended. Best season: year-round; February to April offers best visibility.

## 4. Goat Island Marine Reserve, Auckland Region

Established in 1975, Goat Island (Cape Rodney-Okakari Point Marine Reserve) was New Zealand's first marine reserve and remains one of the most accessible. The reef directly off the beach holds fish life that has recovered dramatically since protection began — snapper of a size rarely seen in the general marine environment, large blue cod, stingrays lying in sand channels, and crayfish (rock lobster) in rock crevices. The depth is modest — 5–15 metres — and the site is suitable for snorkellers, trainee divers and experienced divers alike. Water temperature: 16–21°C. Open Water certification. Best season: December to March.

## 5. Mokohinau Islands, Hauraki Gulf

The Mokohinau Islands sit 50 kilometres northeast of Auckland in the outer Hauraki Gulf, offshore enough to receive clean oceanic water and the attendant pelagic life. Hammerhead sharks are seen here during summer, along with mako sharks passing the outer reefs. The islands' underwater terrain is varied: kelp forests on the shallow reef, sand channels between the structures, and walls below 20 metres with sponge communities. Fish sizes are noticeably larger than inshore sites due to lower fishing pressure. Advanced certification for the deeper sites. Best season: January to April.

## 6. White Island (Whakaari), Bay of Plenty

White Island is New Zealand's most active marine volcano, and diving around its submerged flanks produces one of the most geologically striking dive experiences in the southern Pacific. Hydrothermal vents bubble from the sea floor, creating warm patches in otherwise cool water and fumarole fields that stretch across the rock. The volcanic rock is colonised by large sea fans and bryozoan colonies, and the fish life includes schooling kingfish (yellowtail amberjack) and large snapper. The site requires boat access from Whakatane. Water temperature: 18–22°C. Open Water certification for standard sites; the volcanic terrain demands careful buoyancy. Best season: November to April.

## 7. Aldermen Islands, Coromandel Peninsula

The Aldermen Islands, off the Coromandel Peninsula, offer a mix of shallow reef, deep walls and excellent pelagic action in a location accessible from Tairua. The most spectacular aspect is the massive schools of kingfish that shadow divers through the sites, particularly at the bait-ball structures that form on the eastern walls during summer. Large stingrays rest in sand channels between reef structures. Visibility is good in summer, reaching 15–20 metres. Open Water certification for shallow reef sites; Advanced for the walls below 20 metres. Best season: December to April.

## 8. Sugar Loaf Islands Marine Protected Area, Taranaki

The Sugar Loaf Islands off New Plymouth protect a small but biodiverse rocky reef system that is one of the few places in New Zealand where New Zealand fur seals and divers regularly interact. The seals are inquisitive and playful in the water, diving alongside divers at close range in the kelp. The reef provides habitat for large spotted black grouper, blue cod, and the extraordinary seahorse populations that inhabit the weed-covered rock. Depths: 5–20 metres. Water temperature: 15–20°C. Open Water certification. Best season: October to March.

## 9. Mahia Peninsula, Hawke's Bay

Mahia is an undervisited diving location on the eastern coast of the North Island, known among local divers for its exceptional visibility — it benefits from clear southerly currents — and for the dense populations of crayfish on its reef systems. Depths run from shallow boulder reef to walls at 25–30 metres on the outer points. Large stingrays and bronze whaler sharks patrol the outer reef. Shore diving is practical from several access points. Water temperature: 15–20°C. Open Water certification for the shallow sites. Best season: January to May.

## 10. Kaikoura Canyon Walls, South Island

The submarine canyon at Kaikoura comes very close inshore — the edge of deep oceanic water is reachable within a short boat ride — and the canyon walls drop to depths that bring sperm whales to the surface, but it also creates upwellings that push cold nutrient-rich water onto the reef. The dive sites on the rocky reef shallows around the canyon rim are consistently productive: crayfish, blue cod, paua (abalone), and large grouper inhabit the kelp and rock. Water temperature: 13–18°C. A 7 mm wetsuit or drysuit is appropriate. Open Water certification. Best season: October to April.

## Planning Your Dive Trip to New Zealand

The main dive season in New Zealand runs from November to April, when water temperatures are highest and visibility peaks. Year-round diving is possible, especially at Fiordland, but cold water protection becomes essential outside summer months. The Poor Knights Islands are accessed by day charter from Tutukaka, near Whangarei, and require advance booking during peak season. Fiordland diving is run through operators based in Te Anau and Manapouri; Doubtful Sound liveaboard trips offer multiple dives per day in the fjord environment. Nitrox is available at most professional operations in the North Island; less reliably in the South Island. Equipment rental is of good quality at established operators.
