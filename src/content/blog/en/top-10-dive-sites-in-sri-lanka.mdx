Sri Lanka occupies a teardrop-shaped position in the Indian Ocean south of India, and its diving geography is governed almost entirely by the monsoon. The island has two coastlines with overlapping but distinct seasons: the southwest coast around Hikkaduwa and Unawatuna is calm and accessible from November through April, while the northeast around Trincomalee and Pigeon Island opens up from April through September when the southwest is buffeted by the southwest monsoon. This means a diving visitor who times a trip carefully can have good diving on at least one coast for virtually any week of the year. The island's dive history carries significant WWII weight — some of the largest aircraft carrier wrecks in the world lie in Sri Lankan waters, sunk during Japan's Indian Ocean raid in April 1942. Water temperature is consistently warm, 27 to 30°C year-round, and reef diving is accessible to Open Water certified divers across most sites. [Open the map](/map) to plan your route between coasts.

## 1. HMS Hermes, Trincomalee

HMS Hermes was the world's first purpose-built aircraft carrier, launched in 1920 and sunk in April 1942 by Japanese naval aircraft during the Indian Ocean raid. She lies on her starboard side in 50 to 57 metres of water, roughly 10 kilometres south of Batticaloa on the east coast. The Hermes is one of the most historically significant and visually dramatic wreck dives in the world. At 182 metres long, it is too large to see in its entirety on any single dive. The upper structure begins at around 30 metres — a depth accessible to recreational divers with Advanced Open Water — and includes the flight deck, island superstructure and gun emplacements visible against a blue-water backdrop. The deeper sections of the hull require technical or at minimum Advanced Open Water with strong gas management; penetration requires wreck certification. Lionfish, batfish and large grouper inhabit the superstructure; pelagic species including grey reef sharks circle in the open water above. Visibility is typically 15 to 25 metres.

## 2. Great Basses Reef, Southern Sri Lanka

The Great Basses is a reef lighthouse rock 10 kilometres offshore from the fishing village of Kirinda in southern Sri Lanka. The reef around the lighthouse base holds one of the most intact groups of Indian period wrecks in Asia — seventeenth and eighteenth century trading vessels lying at 25 to 35 metres, their cargo of copper coins and silver scattered across the reef in areas not yet cleared by excavation. The diving is technically straightforward but logistically demanding: the site is accessible only in the brief window between March and April when the sea is calm enough to make the boat crossing, and even then, conditions can change quickly. Visibility is generally good — 20 to 25 metres. The fish life around the basses is prolific; hammerhead sharks are reported seasonally. The remoteness means few dive operators cover this site, and those who do provide multi-day expeditions rather than day trips.

## 3. Pigeon Island National Park, Trincomalee

Pigeon Island lies about a kilometre offshore from Nilaveli Beach, 15 kilometres north of Trincomalee, and its marine national park status protects one of Sri Lanka's better-preserved coral reef systems. The reef extends around the island's two rock outcrops to depths of 15 to 25 metres, with hard coral coverage that is recovering from the 1998 bleaching event. Blacktip reef sharks are the most commonly cited attraction — they cruise the outer reef edge reliably enough to be expected rather than hoped for. Turtle encounters are frequent: both green and hawksbill turtles feed on the reef. The site is most notable in recent years for periodic whale shark visits, particularly between July and September when they enter the bay around the island. Open Water certification is sufficient for the main reef sites; the depth and current is manageable. Boats from Nilaveli Beach reach the island in ten minutes.

## 4. Hikkaduwa Coral Sanctuary, Southwest Coast

Hikkaduwa was Sri Lanka's original dive destination, drawing divers and snorkellers since the 1980s, and the coral sanctuary just offshore from the main beach town retains a quality that survives the diver pressure and the storm damage of the 2004 tsunami. The reef runs parallel to shore at 3 to 10 metres in the shallower section and extends to around 20 metres in the deeper zones. Hawksbill and green turtles are resident and have become extremely tolerant of divers — close approaches without touching are routine. The coral diversity is good, with table corals, brain corals and branching Acropora recovering well in the sections that received post-tsunami restoration effort. Parrotfish, triggerfish and surgeonfish provide constant activity over the reef. The site is ideal for Open Water students and newly certified divers; visibility averages 10 to 15 metres in the November to April season.

## 5. Trincomalee Harbour Wrecks

Trincomalee's deep natural harbour, one of the largest in the world, was a major British naval base through WWII, and the sea floor holds several accessible wrecks from that period. The most notable recreational wreck is the bow section of a destroyer in about 25 metres of water near the harbour entrance, colonised with soft corals and encrusted sponges. A second wreck — believed to be a supply vessel — lies at 30 to 35 metres and retains its cargo holds largely intact. Visibility in the harbour is typically 10 to 15 metres, less than the outer reef sites but adequate for wreck photography. The harbour wrecks are best accessed during the northeast season (April through September) when the main east coast diving operates. Advanced Open Water is recommended for the deeper sites; the harbour wreck at 25 metres suits experienced Open Water divers with good gas management.

## 6. Bar Reef Marine Sanctuary, Kalpitiya

Bar Reef, off the Kalpitiya Peninsula on the northwest coast, is one of Sri Lanka's largest protected reef systems, covering roughly 67 square kilometres of reef and sandy channels. The area is accessible from approximately December through April, and the diving is characterised by strong currents, good visibility of 15 to 25 metres, and a large volume of unexplored reef relative to diver visits. Nurse sharks rest under the coral ledges; schooling fish species — snapper, fusilier, surgeonfish — move through the reef in dense formations. The Kalpitiya area is also one of the best places in Sri Lanka to see whale sharks between October and April. The reef system is remote enough that most visits involve overnight stays in the basic guesthouses in Kalpitiya; dive operators run day trips from the beach. Advanced Open Water recommended given the currents.

## 7. Little Basses Reef, Southern Sri Lanka

Ten kilometres east of the Great Basses, the Little Basses is a shallower submerged reef system at 10 to 18 metres, surrounded by sand channels and accessible in the same brief annual window as its larger companion. The coral cover on the Little Basses is denser than at the Great Basses, and the fish life — parrotfish, triggerfish, eagle rays resting on the sand — is more abundant in recreational diving depth. The history of the site includes its exploration by Arthur C. Clarke, who dived in Sri Lanka from the 1950s onwards and described the Little Basses extensively. The wrecks here are older and less intact than at the Great Basses, but the combination of reef quality and historical context makes the site distinctive. Like the Great Basses, access is limited to a few weeks per year when sea conditions allow.

## 8. Unawatuna Reef, South Coast

Unawatuna is a crescent-shaped bay 15 kilometres south of Galle, with a fringing reef at 8 to 18 metres accessible by short boat or even by snorkelling in the shallower sections. The reef was damaged in the 2004 tsunami but has recovered substantially over the intervening two decades, and turtle encounters here are among the most reliable on the southern coast — multiple individuals feed on the seagrass and coral alongside the reef edge every day. The diving is gentle and well-suited to new divers; visibility averages 10 to 15 metres in the season, and the water temperature of 28 to 30°C makes a 3mm shorty sufficient for comfort. The site operates from November through April. Several dive centres in the town cater to the substantial backpacker and resort tourist population.

## 9. Galle Harbour Wrecks, South Coast

The approaches to Galle harbour on the southwest tip of Sri Lanka hold several older wrecks in recreational depth range. The most accessible is a cargo steamer sitting at 20 to 24 metres roughly two kilometres from the harbour entrance, with its hold open and colonised by fish life. A second, smaller wreck believed to be a nineteenth-century sailing vessel lies nearby at 15 metres, visible as a scattered distribution of ballast stones and bronze fittings over the sandy bottom. Galle is the main dive hub for the south coast during the northeast season; operators there cover both the harbour wrecks and the further offshore sites. Visibility in the harbour approaches averages 10 to 15 metres. Open Water certification suits the shallower wreck; Advanced Open Water for the deeper steamer.

## 10. Weligama Bay, South Coast

Weligama is a wide, sheltered bay near Matara on the deep south coast, with reef diving at 8 to 20 metres in the outer sections of the bay. The site is best known among divers for its aggregation of blue-spotted ribbontail rays, which rest on the sandy seabed between the coral heads throughout the season. Turtle encounters are reliable; an octopus den on the outer reef has become a regular stop on guided dives. The bay's calm inner waters make it a preferred site for Open Water training dives in the November to April season, and several dive schools operate from the main beach road. Visibility is typically 10 to 15 metres — not the best on the south coast but adequate for the reef life and macro opportunities the site offers.

## Planning Your Sri Lanka Dive Trip

The southwest coast (Hikkaduwa, Unawatuna, Weligama) dives from November through April. The northeast coast (Trincomalee, Pigeon Island, Nilaveli) is open from April through September, with May and June typically the clearest months. The two seasons overlap briefly at the shoulder months, making it possible to plan trips in late April or October that access both coasts. Colombo and Mattala airports serve the island internationally; Trincomalee is a five-hour drive or a short flight from Colombo. Equipment rental and PADI/SSI instruction are available at all main dive centres. Nitrox is available at the better-equipped operators in Trincomalee and Hikkaduwa. For the Hermes wreck, book directly with operators in Batticaloa or Trincomalee who run the trip specifically. [Open the map](/map) to locate dive operators and sites.
