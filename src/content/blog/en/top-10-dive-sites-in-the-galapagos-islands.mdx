There is no gentle way into Galapagos diving. The archipelago sits roughly 1,000 kilometres west of mainland Ecuador, where three major currents collide over a volcanic hotspot, and the result is some of the most dramatic big-animal diving on the planet. Water temperatures swing from a balmy 26 degrees Celsius down to a bracing 16 in the same week, depending on which current is winning. The currents that feed this abundance also rip across the dive sites, so most serious Galapagos diving is done from a liveaboard, with depth and experience requirements that exclude the casual holiday diver. What you get in return is the rare chance to share open water with scalloped hammerheads in the hundreds, whale sharks the size of buses, and the only marine iguanas on Earth.

## Why the Galapagos Is Different

Three ocean currents converge here: the cool Humboldt sweeping up from the south, the warm Panama Flow from the north, and the deep, nutrient-rich Cromwell Undercurrent welling up against the western islands. That upwelling fuels an explosion of plankton, which feeds everything up the chain. The marine reserve covers roughly 138,000 square kilometres and is policed against industrial fishing, so the biomass you encounter is something most divers have never seen. The trade-off is real, though: surge, thermoclines, and washing-machine currents are the norm, and dive operators such as the well-known Dive and Surf and Mares Dive Center brief hard on negative entries and reef hooks before anyone gets wet.

## Darwin's Arch and the Hammerhead Wall

Darwin Island, at the remote northern tip of the archipelago, is the headline act. The iconic rock arch collapsed in 2021 and is now called the Pillars of Evolution, but the diving below it is untouched. You drop onto a plateau at around 18 metres, hook into the rock, and wait. Scalloped hammerheads arrive in schools that can number in the hundreds, cruising the current line in slow, silvery ribbons. Between June and November the whale sharks appear, almost all of them large, pregnant females, gliding past close enough to read the scars on their flanks. This is a deep, advanced site with strong current and no shelter, reachable only by liveaboard on a multi-day northern itinerary.

## Wolf Island

A few hours south of Darwin, Wolf Island delivers a similar cast with more variety in the topography. Sites such as Shark Bay and Landslide stack hammerheads, Galapagos sharks, and silky sharks against steep walls and boulder slopes. Wolf tends to be slightly more forgiving than Darwin in terms of current, but only slightly. Dolphins frequently join the dives here, and the cleaning stations draw turtles in numbers. Nitrox is strongly recommended for the repetitive deep profiles that both northern islands demand, and most liveaboards offer it as standard.

## Cabo Marshall and the Manta Highway

On the eastern side of Isabela, Cabo Marshall is the place for giant oceanic mantas. The wall drops into blue water and the mantas patrol the edge, sometimes in groups, often within touching distance though you should never touch. The current can be fierce, sweeping you along the wall like a conveyor belt, which is part of the appeal. Schooling barracuda and the occasional mola mola round out the experience. The water here is often cooler, so a 7mm wetsuit with a hood is sensible even in the warmer months.

## Cousins Rock

Near Santiago, Cousins Rock is a sloping volcanic terrace that ranks among the best macro and mid-water sites in the central islands. Seahorses cling to the black coral, and hawksbill turtles graze the ledges. In season this is a reliable spot for hunting sea lions and even the occasional pelagic visitor sweeping in from the deeper water. It is a more relaxed dive than the northern walls, making it a favourite for the second or third dive of the day.

## Gordon Rocks

Within day-trip range of Santa Cruz, Gordon Rocks is the eroded rim of an old volcanic crater, nicknamed the Washing Machine for good reason. The surge through the central channel can be ferocious, and operators reserve it for advanced divers. The reward is hammerheads close to a populated island, plus eagle rays, turtles, and the constant possibility of a Galapagos shark cruising the blue. It is the site that lets land-based divers from Puerto Ayora taste the big-animal action without committing to a liveaboard.

## Fernandina and the Diving Marine Iguanas

The western islands hold the archipelago's strangest spectacle. Around Fernandina and Cape Douglas, the marine iguanas, the only sea-going lizards on the planet, dive to graze on algae-covered rocks, holding their breath for minutes at a time as they cling to the bottom. The cold Cromwell upwelling makes this some of the coldest diving in the islands, often dipping below 16 degrees Celsius, but it also brings flightless cormorants, Galapagos penguins darting like torpedoes, and red-lipped batfish on the sandy patches. This is a far western itinerary, less about big sharks and more about the bizarre endemic life.

## Planning Your Galapagos Trip

Liveaboards run roughly week-long northern circuits that include Darwin and Wolf, and you will need a logged minimum of around 50 to 100 dives plus an advanced certification to board most of them. The warm season runs December to May with calmer water and better visibility for hammerheads; the cool season from June to November is colder and greener but brings the whale sharks. Budget for the marine reserve entrance fee, the Galapagos transit card, and a flight from Quito or Guayaquil to Baltra or San Cristobal. A good 7mm suit, a hood, gloves, and a reef hook are essential rather than optional.
