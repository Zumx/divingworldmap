Tanzania sits at the heart of the Western Indian Ocean's richest marine geography. Its coastline and offshore islands straddle some of the most biodiverse coral systems in the world, where the warm Mozambique Channel currents feed spectacular reef growth and attract large pelagic visitors year-round. The country's dive profile is remarkably varied: you can spend a week on Zanzibar exploring shallow gardens teeming with nudibranchs, then take a liveaboard to Pemba's vertical walls, and finish at Mafia Island's pristine atoll before the season turns. Tanzania rewards divers who plan carefully around the monsoons and who are prepared for conditions that range from glassy and clear to genuinely challenging. ## 1. Mnemba Atoll, Zanzibar

Mnemba is the crown jewel of Zanzibar's dive scene. The atoll sits roughly four kilometres off the northeast tip of Unguja Island and its outer wall drops from the surface to well beyond recreational limits. Visibility regularly exceeds 25 metres in the dry season. The resident pod of bottlenose dolphins is one of the most reliable cetacean encounters in the Indian Ocean, and sea turtles are almost guaranteed on every dive. The northeast corner of the atoll, where the current picks up, consistently produces reef sharks, eagle rays, and schools of bumphead parrotfish. Expect water temperatures between 26°C and 29°C from June to October. Open Water certification is sufficient for the shallower sites; the outer wall suits Advanced divers.

## 2. Leven Bank, Pemba Channel

Leven Bank is a seamount rising from around 40 metres to roughly 14 metres below the surface in the Pemba Channel, roughly 20 kilometres off Pemba Island. The combination of open-ocean exposure and upwelling nutrient-rich water makes it one of Tanzania's most prolific big-fish sites. Hammerhead sharks patrol the summit in the cooler months, accompanied by wahoo, dogtooth tuna, and the occasional whale shark. The current can be ferocious, and conditions change quickly; this site demands Advanced certification at minimum and ideally drift-diving experience. June through September offers the best visibility, often 30 metres or more.

## 3. Njao Gap, Pemba Island

Pemba is less visited than Zanzibar, and Njao Gap rewards divers willing to make the trip. The site is a channel cut through a shallow reef system where the flooding tide accelerates dramatically, creating a drift of serious power. On the calmer side of the channel, the wall is draped in enormous sea fans and barrel sponges, and large napoleon wrasse hang in the midwater. Visibility can touch 40 metres in ideal conditions. Pemba's reefs have experienced less fishing pressure than those around Zanzibar, and it shows — the fish biomass is noticeably higher. Advanced divers are comfortable here; beginners should explore the calmer reef sections.

## 4. Ras Mkumbi, Mafia Island

Ras Mkumbi is the northern promontory of Mafia Island, where the reef edge drops into deep blue water and the current pushes nutrient-rich water from the Kilindoni Channel across the reef flat. The site is famous for its whale shark season: between October and March, juvenile whale sharks gather to feed on the fish spawn produced by the full moon. Diving with whale sharks on Mafia is subject to PADI-affiliated guidelines and the encounters tend to be calm and prolonged. Outside whale shark season, the site produces schooling fish in enormous numbers and the occasional manta ray. Certification: Open Water.

## 5. Kinasi Wall, Mafia Island

Kinasi Wall runs along the western edge of the Mafia Island Marine Park, plunging from around five metres to depths beyond recreational range. The wall is smothered in soft corals, and the hard coral coverage at the crest is among the best preserved in Tanzania, partly because the marine park restricts access and boat anchoring is prohibited. Yellowfin grouper, titan triggerfish, and dense clouds of anthias populate every section of the wall. Moray eels are common under coral heads, and leopard sharks rest on sandy ledges at depth. Best season is June through September; June and July offer the calmest seas for wall diving.

## 6. Coral Garden, Zanzibar South Coast

The south coast of Unguja between Kizimkazi and Menai Bay holds several sites collectively called Coral Garden. The topography is gentler than Mnemba — broad hard-coral terraces at eight to eighteen metres — and the site consistently rewards macro photography. Look for ornate ghost pipefish hiding in feather stars, frogfish parked on rubble patches, and flatworms in improbable patterns. The site dives best at slack tide on the flood. Water temperature rarely drops below 25°C here, even in July and August. Open Water divers are perfectly at home; the lack of current on calm days makes it an excellent second dive after a morning wall dive.

## 7. Emerald Bay, Zanzibar North

Emerald Bay wraps around the northeastern corner of Unguja and offers a variety of conditions depending on which part of the bay you enter. The inner section is protected and shallow, ideal for snorkelling and training dives, with tables of staghorn coral at four metres housing clownfish, pufferfish, and juvenile napoleon wrasse. The outer lip of the bay drops to around 22 metres onto a sandy slope studded with coral bommies. Octopus are exceptionally common here and remain approachable with patient technique. Visibility ranges from 10 to 20 metres depending on season. June through September is the clearest period.

## 8. Fundu Lagoon Drop-Off, Pemba Island

Fundu Lagoon on Pemba's western coast has a reef system that transitions quickly from a shallow lagoon at one to five metres into a near-vertical wall that descends past 30 metres. The wall section is covered in black coral trees, rare in East Africa at recreational depths, and large gorgonian fans begin at around 18 metres. Pygmy seahorses have been recorded on some fan species here. The lagoon itself is a nursery environment for juvenile reef fish, making the complete site — lagoon plus wall — a good half-day's diving. Water clarity is best from June to September. Advanced divers are recommended for the wall section.

## 9. Shauri Moyo Wreck, Zanzibar

Shauri Moyo is a small wooden dhow that sank in a sheltered bay on the southwest coast of Unguja at around 15 metres. The wreck has been down long enough to be heavily encrusted and colonised; lionfish have taken up residence in the wheelhouse and a resident green moray eel occupies the hold. It is not a large or dramatic wreck, but it is exceptionally accessible and the marine life density around the structure is remarkable by any standard. Night dives reveal a completely different cast of characters, including sleeping parrotfish, hunting cone snails, and active Spanish dancer nudibranchs. Open Water divers; excellent for night diving certification dives.

## 10. Big Blue Bay, Mafia Island

Big Blue Bay is the entry point for Mafia Island Marine Park's deeper offshore reef system. The site involves a short drift over a hard-coral ridge at around 12 metres before the reef edge opens into open water. Barracuda school in large formations above the crest, and the deeper terrace at 25 metres often holds grey reef sharks circling in the current. The site is best dived on the incoming tide; the ebb current can produce strong upwelling that challenges buoyancy control but also concentrates filter-feeding invertebrates and the pelagics that target them. Advanced certification recommended; novices can dive the shallower ridge section in calm conditions.

## Planning Your Tanzania Dive Trip

Tanzania's dive season is governed by two monsoon cycles. The northeast monsoon (kaskazi) runs from November to March and brings calmer seas to Pemba and Zanzibar's northern sites; the southeast monsoon (kusi) from May to October makes the northeast coast choppy but brings exceptional visibility to the south coast and offshore sites. The classic season for clarity and mild conditions across all destinations runs from June through September. Mafia Island's whale shark encounters peak between October and February. Most operators on Zanzibar and Mafia are PADI-affiliated and run well-equipped double-deck dive dhows. Pemba requires a connection through Zanzibar by light aircraft or ferry, and fewer operators serve it — book well in advance. [Open the map](/map) to compare dive centres across Tanzania's islands.
