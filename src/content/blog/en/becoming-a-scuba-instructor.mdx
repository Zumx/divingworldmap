## The Weight of the Rating

When a scuba instructor signs a certification card, they are attesting that the person named on it has the knowledge and skills to dive safely without supervision in conditions similar to those in which they trained. That attestation carries real consequence. Instructors who certify students who are not ready — because the student pushed back, because the resort needed the revenue, because it was the last day and the weather was bad — occasionally end up connected to accidents. The responsibility is not theoretical.

This is why the instructor development course (IDC) is genuinely demanding, and why the instructor examination (IE), conducted by an independent PADI examiner, is standardised internationally. The same performance standards apply in Thailand, Mexico, and Australia. A candidate who cannot demonstrate confined water skills to specification, who cannot deliver an accurate knowledge development session, or who fails the written exams does not pass. About a third of IDC candidates in any given cohort require remediation before completing the IE.

## Prerequisites Before the IDC

The path to instructor requires meaningful diver experience. PADI's IDC prerequisite list includes divemaster certification, 100 logged dives, and a recent (within the past 12 months) Emergency First Responder certification. The more experienced a candidate's diving background, the less likely they are to hit problems in the water during the IDC. An IDC candidate with 150 dives and two years of active divemaster work enters the course with contextual knowledge that cannot be replicated by accelerated training.

Most instructor trainers recommend that candidates spend at least a year working as divemasters before attempting the IDC. That year exposes them to the full range of student problems — people who cannot equalise, people who panic, people who lie about their experience, people who turn up with borrowed equipment they have never used — and builds the reflexes needed to respond calmly. Teaching diving is largely crisis prevention. The instructor who notices a student's increasing anxiety at 8 metres and brings them to the surface smoothly, before anything goes wrong, is doing the job correctly.

## What the IDC Covers

The IDC typically runs over five to nine days, depending on the course director and the candidate group. It covers two primary areas: knowledge development and water skills.

Knowledge development sessions require candidates to present specific topics — pressure and volume, the effects of pressure on the body, dive tables, equipment — accurately and at a level appropriate for entry-level students. The presentations are evaluated on accuracy, pacing, use of visuals, and ability to handle student questions. Candidates who learned their dive theory by rote during their own training discover during the IDC that they need to understand it structurally to teach it.

Confined water and open water teaching evaluations follow a detailed scoring rubric. The evaluator watches everything: whether the candidate gives a clear student performance requirement before each skill, whether they position themselves effectively to monitor the group, whether their demonstrations are precise, and how they respond to student errors. A common weakness in IDC candidates is the instinct to complete a skill before checking that all students have performed it — something that looks fine in recreational diving but is a teaching failure.

## The Instructor Examination

The IE is a two-day assessment conducted by a PADI examiner who has no financial interest in the IDC operation. Candidates are evaluated on written examinations covering dive theory, teaching standards, and physics; on a classroom knowledge development presentation; and on confined water and open water teaching performance. The separation between IDC and IE ensures that instructor trainers cannot pass students simply because they want to.

Passing the IE delivers the PADI Open Water Scuba Instructor (OWSI) rating, which is the foundation teaching qualification. From there, instructors can apply for the PADI IDC Staff Instructor rating after six months and 25 certifications, and eventually for Course Director status, which permits training other instructors.

SSI's route to instructor is structured similarly, with a Teaching Skills Evaluation taking the place of the independent examiner model. NAUI, BSAC, and GUE each have their own instructor development tracks, often with longer training periods and more emphasis on technical diving competence.

## Teaching Open Water Courses

The Open Water Diver course is the product most new instructors spend the majority of their time delivering. It involves four knowledge development sessions, five confined water dives (typically in a pool), and four open water dives. Managing a group of four to eight students through this curriculum requires patience, organisation, and the ability to teach the same material repeatedly without losing precision or engagement.

The confined water sessions are where most problems surface. Mask clearing, regulator recovery, and buoyancy control — skills that experienced divers perform without conscious thought — are genuinely difficult for many beginners, particularly at depth. An instructor who cannot identify why a student is struggling and offer an alternative approach is limited in their effectiveness.

## Building a Teaching Career

The economics of dive instruction vary significantly by location and operation. At entry-level positions in popular diving destinations — Koh Tao, Bali, Hurghada, Dahab — many instructors work for accommodation and diving, with minimal cash salary. The income improves with experience, reputation, and speciality qualifications. Instructors who teach technical diving, freediving, rebreather courses, or specialty programmes like underwater photography attract a different clientele and charge accordingly.

Instructor positions on liveaboards in destinations like the Maldives, the Raja Ampat, or the Galápagos offer better remuneration and access to exceptional diving, though the work is physically intensive. Some instructors build careers teaching pool programmes for resorts without ocean access, managing dive camps, or working in dive retail with occasional course delivery.

The instructors who build lasting careers are generally those who stay curious about diving itself. Teaching the Open Water course for the five hundredth time is not inherently dull if the instructor is also pursuing technical certifications, cave training, or marine biology photography. The water keeps rewarding attention.

## Standards, Ethics, and the Long View

An instructor's relationship with the certifying agency is ongoing. Log books get audited, incidents get investigated, and reputations travel quickly in the diving community, which is smaller than it appears. The instructors who generate repeat customers, referrals, and long careers are those who teach conservatively, communicate honestly with students, and refuse to certify people who are not ready. The short-term pain of failing a student is trivial compared to the long-term consequence of certifying someone who goes on to be involved in an accident.
