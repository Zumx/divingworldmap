Few states pack as much diving variety into their borders as Florida. To the south, the only living coral barrier reef in the continental United States runs the length of the Keys; off the Atlantic shore, dozens of deliberately scuttled ships have become towering artificial reefs; and inland, the limestone aquifer feeds a constellation of crystal-clear springs and flooded cave systems unlike anything in the marine world. A diver could spend a morning drifting a coral wall in the Gulf Stream and an afternoon hovering over the gin-clear basin of a freshwater spring. The water is warm and forgiving in the south, a constant 22°C in the springs year-round, and the sheer range of sites makes Florida a complete diving destination in itself.

## Ginnie Spring, High Springs

On the Santa Fe River near High Springs, Ginnie Spring is one of the most beloved freshwater dives in North America. The main spring boil produces a vast volume of perfectly clear water that holds a steady 22°C regardless of season, glowing an almost luminous blue in the sunlight. Open-water divers can explore the basin and the famous Ballroom cavern, a large air-filled chamber illuminated by shafts of daylight, while certified cave divers continue into the extensive cave system beyond the grate. It is a prized training ground and a place where divers first understand the strange stillness of subterranean water.

## Devil's Den, Williston

Devil's Den is a prehistoric spring inside a dry collapsed cavern near Williston — a subterranean pool reached by a stairway descending through rock. Hot, mineral-laden steam once rose from the opening on cold mornings, giving rise to its devilish name. Inside, divers descend into water of remarkable clarity around 22°C, swimming among ancient rock formations and fossil beds where the remains of Ice Age animals have been recovered. The enclosed, cathedral-like chamber and the columns of light from the surface opening make it one of the most atmospheric dives in the state.

## Blue Grotto Spring, Williston

Also near Williston, Blue Grotto is the largest clear-water cavern dive open to certified divers in Florida. The main basin drops to around 30 metres, and an open cavern at depth holds an air bell where divers can surface and converse beneath the rock. A resident soft-shell turtle named Virgil has greeted visitors here for decades. The clarity, the gentle depth profile and the open cavern make it a favourite for cavern training and a memorable introduction to overhead environments.

## Rainbow Spring and the Rainbow River, Dunnellon

The Rainbow River, fed by Rainbow Spring near Dunnellon, is a postcard of clear, slow-moving water over white sand and waving eelgrass. Divers and snorkellers drift gently downstream past turtles, gar, bass and the occasional manatee, with the springs themselves bubbling up through the sandy bed. The constant 22°C flow and superb visibility make it an effortless, scenic dive, popular for both training and pure enjoyment. It is one of the clearest waterways in the United States.

## Ichetucknee and Alexander Springs

The Ichetucknee Spring system, a National Natural Landmark, is famed for tubing but also offers serene snorkelling and cavern diving in some of the purest water imaginable. Further south in the Ocala National Forest, Alexander Springs offers an easy, shallow open-water dive in a broad spring pool surrounded by subtropical forest — an ideal beginner site with abundant fish and turtles. Together with nearby Juniper Springs and the deep, technical Eagle's Nest cave system, central Florida's springs form a freshwater diving region without equal.

## John Pennekamp Coral Reef State Park, Key Largo

John Pennekamp was the first undersea park in the United States and remains the gateway to the Keys reef. Within its waters lie a string of classic reef sites — Molasses Reef, French Reef, the Elbow — where shallow coral gardens teem with parrotfish, angelfish, barracuda and rays. The signature attraction is the Christ of the Abyss, a bronze statue of Christ standing in around eight metres of water, arms raised, encrusted with coral and instantly recognisable. The shallow depths and warm, clear Gulf Stream water make this the perfect introduction to Florida reef diving.

## The USCGC Duane and Bibb, Key Largo

Off Key Largo, the twin Coast Guard cutters Duane and Bibb were sunk in 1987 as artificial reefs. The Duane stands upright in around 30 metres and is one of the most charismatic wreck dives in the Keys, festooned with coral and patrolled by barracuda, goliath grouper and reef sharks in the current. The Bibb lies on its side nearby and is a more advanced dive. The combination of intact superstructure, abundant pelagic life and reliable Gulf Stream clarity makes the Duane a must-dive for wreck enthusiasts.

## The USS Spiegel Grove, Key Largo

The Spiegel Grove is a 155-metre former navy landing ship dock sunk off Key Largo in 2002, one of the largest ships ever intentionally scuttled for divers. Sitting in around 40 metres, it is an advanced wreck demanding good gas planning and respect for current, but its scale is breathtaking — vast cargo decks, cranes and passageways now cloaked in marine growth. Goliath grouper the size of a person hover in the shadows, and the open water around the wreck often holds reef sharks and large pelagics.

## The Vandenberg, Key West

The Hoyt S. Vandenberg, a 159-metre former missile-tracking ship, was sunk off Key West in 2009 and is now one of the world's premier artificial reefs. Its enormous radar dishes and superstructure rise dramatically from a sandy seabed at around 40 metres, with the upper decks reachable by advanced divers around 30 metres. The wreck attracts dense fish life, goliath grouper and the occasional shark, and its sheer photogenic scale has made it a bucket-list dive. It also stood in as a film set before its sinking.

## The Atlantic Artificial Reefs off Fort Lauderdale and the Anglin's Pier Reef

Along the southeast coast off Fort Lauderdale and Pompano Beach, decades of artificial-reef deployment have created an underwater landscape of wrecks, tyres, and limestone, all swept by the Gulf Stream. The shore-accessible Anglin's Pier Reef near Lauderdale-by-the-Sea lets divers explore reef and structure straight from the beach, encountering nurse sharks, turtles and tropical fish. The drift conditions here, where divers ride the current and let the boat follow their bubbles, define southeast Florida wreck and reef diving.
