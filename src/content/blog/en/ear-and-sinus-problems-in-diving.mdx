## Pressure and the Body's Air Spaces

Descending beneath the surface puts every gas-filled space in the body under increasing pressure, and the body must equalise those spaces to avoid pain and injury. For most divers, the ears are the first space to complain. The tympanic membrane — the eardrum — sits between the outer ear canal and the middle ear space. The middle ear connects to the throat via the Eustachian tube, and equalisation happens when the diver forces air through that tube to match middle ear pressure with the surrounding water pressure.

The physics is unforgiving: by 10 metres of depth, ambient pressure has doubled to 2 bar, meaning the diver needs to equalise the pressure difference of one full atmosphere across the eardrum. Most novice divers feel this sharply between 1 and 4 metres depth, where the percentage change in pressure is greatest per metre of descent. The speed of descent and the sensitivity of individual Eustachian tube anatomy determine how much difficulty the diver encounters.

## Equalisation Methods

The Valsalva manoeuvre — pinching the nose and blowing gently — is the equalisation technique taught in virtually every entry-level course and practised by the majority of recreational divers. It works by pressurising the nasopharynx and forcing air through the Eustachian tube opening. The technique is effective, but it has limitations: it requires muscular effort, it can be difficult to perform in a forceful current or when the diver is disoriented, and used too forcefully it creates a significant pressure spike that can rupture the oval or round window — the membranes between the middle and inner ear.

The Frenzel manoeuvre is used by most freedivers and increasingly taught in scuba courses because it is more efficient and less forceful. Rather than using the chest and diaphragm to generate pressure, the Frenzel uses the tongue and throat muscles — the glottis is closed, the tongue pushes backward against the soft palate, and small bursts of air are pushed into the Eustachian tube openings. Once learned, Frenzel is faster and gentler than Valsalva and allows equalisation in more body orientations.

The Toynbee manoeuvre — swallowing while pinching the nose — opens the Eustachian tube through the action of the palatal muscles and is useful for fine-tuning equalisation or for mild cases. Some divers use Toynbee as a primary technique in the first few metres of descent. A full jaw-hinge manoeuvre, in which the jaw is moved as if yawning, can open the tube mechanically, and the combination of jaw movement and light Valsalva often clears a tube that resists plain blowing.

## Ear Barotrauma: Causes and Grades

If equalisation fails and the diver continues to descend, the eardrum is drawn inward by the relative negative pressure in the middle ear. The structures of the middle ear — the ossicles and the drum itself — stretch and eventually rupture. Symptoms progress from mild discomfort through sharp pain to sudden relief followed by vertigo if cold water enters the middle ear through a perforation.

The vertigo caused by caloric stimulation from a ruptured eardrum is serious: the rapid spinning sensation it creates has caused disoriented divers to lose awareness of up and down and make emergency ascents in the wrong direction. The correct response to sudden vertigo at depth is to stop descending, hold position, and wait for the sensation to resolve before ascending slowly. The vertigo from caloric stimulation typically passes within seconds to a minute, unlike the sustained vertigo of inner ear barotrauma.

Grading of ear barotrauma runs from Grade 0 — no change, no symptoms — through Grade 1 (mild redness of the eardrum) to Grade 5, representing a complete perforation with blood in the middle ear and external canal. Most mild barotraumas — Grade 1 and 2 — resolve without treatment within days, though diving should be suspended until the drum has healed. Perforations (Grade 5) require cessation of diving and medical review; they usually heal spontaneously within a few weeks but carry the risk of introducing infection and causing permanent hearing change.

## Reverse Blocks: The Ascent Problem

A reverse block, or reversed ear, occurs on ascent rather than descent. As the diver rises and ambient pressure decreases, the gas in the middle ear must vent back through the Eustachian tube. Normally this happens passively. If the tube is partially obstructed — by mucus from a cold, nasal congestion, or swelling from an earlier difficult descent — the gas cannot vent freely and the pressure differential now pushes outward against the eardrum from inside.

The result is intense ear pain that typically halts around 5 to 8 metres depth. The instinct to push through to the surface is wrong: the only correct action is to descend a metre or two to relieve the pressure differential, then ascend extremely slowly in increments of half a metre, waiting for the tube to open spontaneously. In most cases it does, with an audible pop. If it does not resolve, the diver should be assisted to a depth where the pain resolves and brought up by the guide in controlled stages.

Reverse blocks are most common when divers use decongestant medication before diving to manage a cold or congestion, dive anyway, and then find that the decongestant wears off during the dive, causing the nasal passages to swell again during the ascent. The dive medicine position on this is clear: do not dive when congested. The risk of a reverse block at depth is a genuine emergency scenario, not a mild inconvenience.

## Sinus Barotrauma

The frontal sinuses — the air cavities in the forehead above the eyes and in the cheekbones — equalise through narrow openings into the nasal passage. They equalise passively in most divers and require no active technique, but when the sinus openings are swollen or blocked by infection, allergic inflammation, or polyps, equalisation fails and the sinus tissues bleed into the cavity to fill the pressure differential.

Frontal sinus barotrauma produces a deep, severe headache above the eyes during descent that intensifies rapidly. Cheekbone sinus (maxillary) problems produce pain and pressure in the cheekbones and upper teeth. Both are resolved in the same way as middle ear barotrauma — ascend until the pain resolves, do not attempt to push through. Blood in the mask after surfacing, running from the nose, is a sign that barotrauma has occurred; it looks dramatic but is usually a minor bleed from the sinus lining rather than a serious injury.

## External Ear Canal: Swimmer's Ear

Otitis externa — swimmer's ear — is an infection of the outer ear canal caused by prolonged moisture exposure. It is not a pressure injury but is among the most common diving-related medical problems because the outer ear canal is continuously submerged during every dive. The combination of moisture, warmth, and the microtrauma of earplug use or water-trap removal creates conditions in which bacterial or fungal infection takes hold rapidly.

Symptoms are pain in the outer ear, particularly on manipulation of the ear lobe or tragus, itching, and occasionally discharge. Treatment is topical antibiotic or antifungal drops and temporary cessation of water exposure. Prevention involves drying the ears thoroughly after every dive — tipping the head and gently pulling the ear lobe to drain water, then using a hair dryer on a low setting at arm's length — and avoiding cotton swabs, which pack wax further into the canal and remove the protective acid layer of skin.

Divers who are prone to recurrent otitis externa benefit from a preventive acidifying rinse — dilute acetic acid or proprietary swimmer's ear drops — applied after each dive to restore the natural pH of the canal skin. In warm-water locations like the Red Sea or the Maldives, where divers do five dives a day in 28-degree water, swimmer's ear is a serious occupational hazard for divemasters and instructors who spend every day in the water.

## When Not to Dive

The cardinal rule in dive medicine: if you cannot equalise on the surface before getting into the water, do not get in. Pinching the nose and gently equalising on land before entry is a reliable pre-dive test. If either ear requires significant effort or refuses to clear at atmospheric pressure, it will be worse at depth.

Active ear or sinus infection, a perforated eardrum, or an ear surgery within the last six to twelve weeks are all absolute contraindications. If in doubt, a consultation with an ENT physician or a dive medicine specialist — available at major dive medicine centres including the British Diving Medical Committee or DAN-accredited facilities — provides clarity.
