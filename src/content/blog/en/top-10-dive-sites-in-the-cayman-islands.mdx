The Cayman Islands — Grand Cayman, Cayman Brac, and Little Cayman — sit atop the Cayman Ridge between Cuba and Central America, surrounded on three sides by water that drops thousands of metres within a kilometre of shore. The three islands have together produced more influential names in recreational diving history than almost any other destination: wall diving as a sport effectively began here when Bob Soto introduced tourists to the Grand Cayman drop-off in the 1950s. Today the Caymans offer something unusual in the Caribbean: rigorous marine protection combined with a mature, professional dive industry and some of the clearest water in the Atlantic basin, regularly exceeding 30 metres of visibility. Stingray City alone draws enough visitors to sustain an entire tourism economy; the diving is considerably more than that. [Open the map](/map) to explore Cayman dive sites.

## 1. Bloody Bay Wall, Little Cayman

Bloody Bay Wall is the defining dive of the Cayman Islands for many who have made the trip to Little Cayman. The wall begins at around seven metres — unusually shallow — and drops straight down for an extraordinary distance, with vertical faces exceeding 1,800 metres before the ocean floor. The coral coverage at the lip is among the healthiest in the Caribbean; plate corals, star corals, and enormous brain corals mark the edge, and Nassau grouper are present in numbers rarely seen outside fully protected reserves. Tarpon hover in midwater off the wall. Little Cayman has a population of roughly 200 people and the island's limited visitor numbers ensure the wall sees nothing like the pressure of Grand Cayman sites. Advanced Open Water for the wall; Open Water for reef-crest exploration.

## 2. USS Kittiwake Wreck, Grand Cayman

The USS Kittiwake is a decommissioned United States Navy submarine rescue vessel deliberately sunk in January 2011 at around 20 metres off Seven Mile Beach on Grand Cayman's west coast. The ship is 77 metres long and sits upright on a sandy bottom with its superstructure rising to around five metres below the surface. The Kittiwake was properly prepared before sinking — doors removed, potential hazards eliminated — making it one of the most accessible wreck penetrations in the Caribbean. Every compartment is swimable in a single breath of gas. The wreck has developed a rich coral and sponge community and is now home to large schools of French grunts, yellowtail snapper, and resident moray eels. Open Water divers; excellent night dive.

## 3. Stingray City, Grand Cayman

Stingray City is a sandy shallows in the North Sound of Grand Cayman at around three metres where southern stingrays — historically attracted to fishing boats cleaning their catch — have gathered for decades. Today between 20 and 40 stingrays of considerable size occupy the site, and they have become entirely habituated to humans. The interaction is conducted as a snorkel or shallow dive; experienced divers often choose the adjacent Sandbar site at one metre of depth for photography. The rays' behaviour — swimming between legs, accepting squid from closed fists — is a product of decades of interaction, not training. It is the most-visited marine attraction in the Caribbean. Open Water; snorkellers equally welcome.

## 4. Babylon, Grand Cayman

Babylon is a section of the Grand Cayman north wall east of Rum Point, reached by a short boat ride from the north side operators. The wall top sits at around 12 metres and the drop-off is decorated with enormous barrel sponges — some exceeding two metres in diameter — and deep-water sea fans at 25 to 35 metres. The barrel sponge density at Babylon is remarkable; a single dive passes dozens of these ancient invertebrates, some estimated to be several centuries old. Hawksbill turtles feed on the sponges and are generally unbothered by divers. Visibility is consistently high, often 25–30 metres. Advanced Open Water for deeper exploration of the wall; Open Water divers content at the crest.

## 5. Eden Rock and Devil's Grotto, Grand Cayman

Eden Rock and Devil's Grotto are adjacent shore-dive sites on Grand Cayman's south side, within walking distance of George Town. Eden Rock is a coral pinnacle rising from around 15 metres to a flat top at six metres, surrounded by tarpon in a near-permanent silver school. Devil's Grotto is a series of tunnels and caverns in the reef cutting from around five to 15 metres; inside the tunnels, thousands of silversides pack the chambers and tarpon follow, exploding through the baitfish walls in dramatic acceleration. Both sites are swimable as a single dive and are among the best-value dives in the Caymans — park, gear up, walk in. Open Water; no guide required, though the grotto passages suit divers with good buoyancy.

## 6. The Tunnels, Grand Cayman

West Bay at the northern end of Seven Mile Beach has several sites known collectively as The Tunnels, where ancient coral formations have created wide swim-through passages at six to 15 metres. The passages are wide enough for two divers side by side and are illuminated at both ends, so claustrophobia is not a factor. Inside, spotted eagle rays frequently shelter during the day, resting on sandy patches within the tunnels, and nurse sharks tuck under coral overhangs. The surface conditions here are usually the calmest on Grand Cayman due to the shelter provided by the reef. Open Water divers; excellent first tunnel-diving experience.

## 7. Cayman Brac North Wall

Cayman Brac, the eastern island, has two walls — north and south — but the north wall is consistently calmer and more colourful. The Brac's north wall drops from a reef crest at 5–10 metres to depths beyond recreational range, and the soft coral coverage below 20 metres is exceptional; orange elephant ear sponges and large black coral trees appear regularly at 25–30 metres. The island has only a handful of dive operators, which keeps the sites quiet; on many dives you will share the wall only with your buddy. Cayman Brac is reached by short flight from Grand Cayman. Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper wall sections.

## 8. MV Captain Keith Tibbetts Wreck, Cayman Brac

The Captain Keith Tibbetts is a deliberately sunk Russian frigate — the Brigadier General Vladimir Romanov — given to Cayman Brac by Cuba and sunk in September 1996. The wreck lies on its side at 27–30 metres with the top of the hull at around 15 metres. At 100 metres in length, it is the largest diveable wreck in the Caribbean. Gun mounts, torpedo tubes, and torpedo loading equipment remain on the hull and have become encrusted with coral. Large green moray eels patrol the gun deck. The wreck sits at the edge of the north wall; after exploring the superstructure, divers can back-roll off the hull and hang above the drop-off in open water. Advanced Open Water for the deeper sections.

## 9. Little Cayman North Wall: Mixing Bowl

The Mixing Bowl is the deepwater transition zone at the northern end of Bloody Bay Marine Park where multiple current streams converge, creating an upwelling that concentrates nutrients and the animals that feed on them. The site is dived by boat from Little Cayman's small cluster of dive resorts and requires timing around the tidal cycle. When conditions align, schools of horse-eye jacks and permit swirl in the upwelling, and eagle rays glide past the wall top. Nassau grouper at the Mixing Bowl are some of the largest recorded in the Caymans. The current makes this an Advanced-level site; an experienced guide is recommended on first visits.

## 10. Stingray Alley, Cayman Brac

While Grand Cayman's Stingray City is famous worldwide, Cayman Brac has its own version at a quieter site off the island's northeast. Southern stingrays have congregated here over many years, attracted by fishing activity, and a small number of resident individuals — perhaps a dozen — have become habituated in a similar manner to their Grand Cayman counterparts. The shallows sit at around four metres on a sandy flat adjacent to a short reef. Because Cayman Brac attracts far fewer visitors, the experience is more intimate; it is not uncommon to have the site to yourself with a single guide. Open Water; suitable for snorkellers.

## Planning Your Cayman Islands Dive Trip

The Cayman Islands dive year-round. Water temperatures stay between 24°C in January and February and 29°C in August and September. Hurricane risk exists from June through November; Grand Cayman was significantly impacted by Hurricane Ivan in 2004, though the reef system has recovered well. Grand Cayman has the most complete infrastructure, with operators running multiple boats per day to all sides of the island. Little Cayman's small resort community means booking well in advance is essential; most visitors stay for a week at one of the dive resorts and make multiple dives per day on Bloody Bay Wall. Cayman Brac sits between the two in terms of development. All three islands have strict marine park regulations including no-take zones.
