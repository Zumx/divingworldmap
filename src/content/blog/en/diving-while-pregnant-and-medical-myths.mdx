Few subjects in diving generate as much confident misinformation as medical fitness. Ask around a dive club and you will hear that asthmatics can never dive, that anyone on blood pressure medication is barred, that a single ear infection ends a diving career. Much of this folklore dates from an era when the rules were deliberately conservative because the science was thin. Over the past three decades the picture has changed: large studies, accumulated case data, and the work of bodies like the Divers Alert Network and the United Kingdom's Sport Diving Medical Committee have replaced blanket bans with individual risk assessment. The single firm exception that remains is pregnancy.

## Pregnancy: The One Genuine Hard Stop

On pregnancy the medical consensus is unusually clear and unusually cautious. Every major diving medicine authority — DAN, the Undersea and Hyperbaric Medical Society, and the certification agencies PADI, SSI and BSAC — advises against scuba diving at any stage of pregnancy. The reasoning is not that diving has been proven dangerous to the fetus; it is that it cannot ethically be proven safe, and the theoretical risks are serious enough to warrant a precautionary line.

The central concern is the fetal circulation. An adult diver who absorbs nitrogen under pressure relies on the lungs to filter out gas bubbles during decompression. The fetus, however, has a circulation that largely bypasses the lungs through the foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus, meaning any venous gas bubbles formed during ascent could pass directly into the arterial circulation and reach the developing brain or heart. Animal studies in the 1970s and 1980s found increased rates of birth defects and limb abnormalities in offspring of pregnant animals exposed to hyperbaric conditions. The data are imperfect and the human evidence is limited to retrospective surveys, but the surveys that exist suggest elevated rates of miscarriage and abnormality among women who dived while pregnant. Given that the activity is entirely optional, the advice is unambiguous: do not dive while pregnant or while trying to conceive, and resume only after delivery. Snorkelling at the surface, by contrast, carries no decompression risk and is generally considered fine.

## Asthma: From Absolute Ban to Case-by-Case

For decades, asthma was an automatic disqualification, and the fear was logical. The worry is air trapping: if a constricted airway holds gas that expands on ascent, it could rupture lung tissue and cause arterial gas embolism, the most feared diving injury. There was also concern that cold, dry compressed air and exertion could trigger bronchospasm at depth.

Modern guidance is far more nuanced. The current consensus, reflected in the positions of the UK Diving Medical Committee and DAN, is that well-controlled asthma is not in itself a barrier. The key distinctions are the type and control of the asthma. A person whose asthma is triggered by cold air, exercise or emotion — exactly the conditions of diving — remains at higher risk and is usually advised against it. But someone with mild, stable asthma triggered only by, say, seasonal pollen, who has normal lung function tests and no symptoms for an extended period, may be cleared after a proper assessment, sometimes including spirometry before and after exercise. The rule of thumb many diving physicians apply is that an asthmatic who needs their reliever inhaler before exertion should not dive that day, but stable, controlled asthma off daily symptoms is increasingly compatible with recreational diving.

## Diabetes: Manageable With Discipline

Diabetes was once treated as another automatic exclusion, on the reasonable fear that a hypoglycaemic episode underwater could cause confusion or loss of consciousness with fatal consequences. Today, insulin-dependent diabetics dive regularly under protocols developed by DAN and the agencies, most prominently after a landmark workshop that produced practical guidelines.

The framework rests on stability and self-management. Candidates must have well-controlled diabetes with no recent severe hypoglycaemic episodes, no significant complications affecting the eyes, kidneys or nerves, and the discipline to follow a strict regimen: checking blood glucose before, during the surface interval, and after dives; aiming for a blood sugar comfortably above the resting baseline before descent; carrying oral glucose; and diving with a buddy who knows the condition and the signs of trouble. Depth and time limits are often kept conservative. With these measures, the diabetes diving record has been reassuringly good, a clear example of how structured risk management has replaced a blanket no.

## Hearts, Ears and the PFO Question

Cardiovascular fitness genuinely matters, because diving imposes real loads: cold water, exertion, and the redistribution of blood when immersed. Well-controlled high blood pressure on most medications is generally compatible with diving, though certain drugs that blunt the heart-rate response to exertion warrant a physician's review. Significant coronary disease, recent cardiac events or poorly controlled arrhythmias are different matters and require careful, often individual, assessment, because sudden cardiac events are a leading cause of diving fatalities, particularly in older divers.

The patent foramen ovale, or PFO, deserves special mention because awareness of it has grown sharply. Roughly a quarter of the population has this small flap-like opening between the heart's upper chambers, a remnant of fetal circulation that never fully sealed. In most people it is harmless, but under the pressure changes of diving it can let venous gas bubbles cross into the arterial side, raising the risk of unexplained decompression sickness, particularly of the inner ear or skin. Divers who suffer repeated unexplained decompression illness are now often investigated for a PFO with a bubble-contrast echocardiogram. The ear itself is the most common source of minor diving complaints — barotrauma from failure to equalise — but these are issues of technique and timing rather than permanent disqualification.

## Why the Myths Persist, and How to Get a Real Answer

The folklore endures partly because it is simpler to remember a flat no than a nuanced maybe, and partly because the conservative rules of the past were repeated by instructors who learned them as gospel. The honest position is that diving is a strenuous activity in an unforgiving environment, and that almost every chronic condition raises questions worth answering before descent. But for most conditions, the modern answer is an assessment, not an automatic refusal.

The practical route is a diving medical examination by a physician trained in hyperbaric and diving medicine — not a general practitioner unfamiliar with the field. Bodies such as DAN maintain referral networks of such doctors, and many dive destinations have clinics experienced in fitness-to-dive assessment. A would-be diver with a managed condition should gather their medical records, see a diving doctor, and get a real answer grounded in current evidence rather than relying on what someone said at the dive shop. The one place where the precautionary line holds firm, no assessment required, remains pregnancy.
