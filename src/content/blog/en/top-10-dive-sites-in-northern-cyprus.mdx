The boat noses out of Kyrenia harbour just after eight, past the round Venetian tower and the old Lusignan castle, into a sea that in high summer reads twenty-seven degrees on the dive computer before you have even rolled in. Northern Cyprus offers a particular kind of Mediterranean diving: warm, clear, lightly trafficked, and built on limestone that the sea has carved into walls, arches and swim-throughs. The marine life is classic eastern Med — dusky groupers tucked under ledges, moray eels with their jaws working, the occasional cruising amberjack — and the season runs comfortably from April to November. Here are ten dives, and the context, that make the north worth the trip.

## Kyrenia (Girne): the diving heart of the north

Almost every dive in Northern Cyprus begins at Kyrenia, known in Turkish as Girne. The town is the base for the region's operators, including the long-established North Cyprus Scuba Centre, and the coast immediately east and west of the harbour is where most reef and wall diving happens. Boats are small and the runs are short — many sites sit ten to twenty minutes out — so it is normal to fit two dives into a morning and be back for lunch on the quayside. The rocky shoreline drops quickly to diveable depth, giving you walls and boulder fields within easy reach of a single-engine RIB.

## The Zenobia: famous, but on the southern side

No honest account of Cyprus diving can skip the Zenobia, even though it does not lie in the north. The MS Zenobia was a Swedish-built roll-on roll-off ferry that capsized and sank in June 1980 off Larnaca, on the southern, Republic-of-Cyprus side of the island. She rests on her port side in around forty-two metres of water, cargo lorries still chained to her decks, and is routinely ranked among the best wreck dives in the world. Divers based in Kyrenia will tell you about her with obvious pride, then add the honest caveat: reaching the Zenobia means crossing to Larnaca in the south, not diving off the northern coast. Knowing that distinction up front saves disappointment.

## Wrecks and artificial reefs off Kyrenia

The north has its own wreck diving, more modest but rewarding. Several vessels and structures have been placed or have come to rest along the Kyrenia coast, and they perform the job all wrecks do in a warm sea: they concentrate life. Within a few seasons a bare hull becomes a reef in miniature, its rails furred with growth, its holds sheltering schools of bream and the resident grouper that claims the best shadow. These are accessible dives, generally well within recreational limits, and they make an easy counterpoint to the natural reef sites.

## Walls and drop-offs

The signature of northern Cypriot diving is the wall. Limestone reefs off Kyrenia tilt and break into vertical faces that fall away into blue, and finning along them you keep one shoulder to the rock and watch the cracks. Groupers hang in the lee, lobster antennae feel from the holes, and morays — both the green and the brown spotted kind — are a near-certainty. The walls catch the light beautifully in the clear summer water, and because the eastern Mediterranean is comparatively low in nutrients, visibility is often generous, frequently beyond twenty-five metres.

## Swim-throughs, caverns and arches

Where the rock is right, the sea hollows it out, and the Kyrenia coast has a fine collection of swim-throughs, short caverns and arches. These are the dives that newer divers remember: you slip out of the sunlight into a cool blue tunnel, the walls close to either fin tip, and a shaft of light marks the far exit. Octopus tuck into the recesses, often given away only by a discarded shell midden at the entrance, and cardinalfish hover in the dark in tight, hovering shoals. Good buoyancy is the price of admission, but none of it is technical.

## The Karpaz (Karpas) Peninsula: the remote frontier

For divers willing to drive, the long finger of the Karpaz Peninsula reaching northeast toward Syria offers the wildest diving on the island. The reefs here are remote and lightly dived, the water often even clearer, and the marine life noticeably less shy for the lack of pressure. Logistics are the catch — it is a long way from Kyrenia, and infrastructure is sparse — so most divers reach the Karpaz on an organised day trip or a short expedition rather than on a whim. Those who make the effort are rewarded with healthy posidonia meadows, big groupers and a genuine sense of diving somewhere off the map.

## Marine life and the posidonia meadows

The biological backbone of these waters is posidonia, the endemic Mediterranean seagrass that carpets the sandy patches between reefs. Its meadows are nurseries: they oxygenate the water, anchor the sediment and shelter juvenile fish, which in turn feed the predators the reefs are known for. Over and around them you find dusky groupers, amberjack moving in fast silver packs, moray eels, octopus, bream, wrasse and the inevitable damselfish clouds. It is not the technicolour density of a tropical reef, but it is a complete and self-sustaining temperate ecosystem, and reading it is half the pleasure.

## The Kyrenia ship: 2,300 years underwater

Cyprus has been a maritime crossroads for millennia, and the north holds the proof. The Kyrenia ship is a Greek merchant vessel from the fourth century BC, wrecked off the Kyrenia coast and recovered by archaeologists in the late 1960s. Its remarkably preserved hull, along with its cargo of amphorae and almonds, is conserved and displayed inside Kyrenia Castle, overlooking the harbour. You cannot dive it — it is long since raised — but standing before a hull that sailed before the Roman Republic existed gives the whole coastline a deeper resonance when you return to the water.

## Conditions, seasons and planning your dives

Plan for an April-to-November season, with the warmest and calmest water from June through September, when surface temperatures climb toward twenty-seven or twenty-eight degrees and a shorty or a thin wetsuit is plenty. Spring and autumn are cooler and quieter, and the visibility holds well year-round thanks to the clear, low-nutrient sea. Most sites are gentle on current, which makes the north friendly to newly qualified divers and to families, while the walls and Karpaz reefs give experienced divers somewhere to stretch. Booking through an established centre such as North Cyprus Scuba Centre smooths the logistics, including the question of whether to add a southern crossing for the Zenobia.
