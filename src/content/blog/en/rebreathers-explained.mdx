## The Problem with Open-Circuit Scuba

Every time you exhale through a standard regulator, the gas goes directly into the water as a stream of bubbles. That exhaled gas still contains roughly 16 to 17 percent oxygen — you absorbed only a fraction of what you breathed in — but it is discarded regardless. The inefficiency is dramatic: on a typical air dive, you use less than a quarter of the oxygen in your cylinder. The rest, mostly nitrogen, is exhaled and lost.

For recreational diving on a half-day trip, this is an acceptable waste. For technical divers descending to 80 metres for 90 minutes, or for military and scientific divers who need to avoid bubbles entirely, the wastefulness becomes untenable. Rebreathers exist to solve that problem.

## How a Closed-Circuit Rebreather Works

A closed-circuit rebreather — commonly abbreviated CCR — does not vent exhaled gas. Instead, it routes the breath through a scrubber canister filled with a chemical absorbent, typically soda lime, which removes carbon dioxide. The scrubbed gas is then recirculated into the breathing loop, and oxygen is injected to replace what the diver's body consumed.

The result is a system where the same small volume of gas circulates continuously, losing only the oxygen actually metabolised by the diver. A CCR diver uses a fraction of the gas a comparable open-circuit diver would consume. A two-litre oxygen cylinder and a three-litre diluent cylinder can support many hours of diving — something no set of open-circuit tanks could match.

The oxygen injection is controlled either manually by the diver setting a constant mass flow valve or — in most modern CCRs — automatically by an electronic closed-circuit oxygen controller. These electronics read partial pressure oxygen sensors in the breathing loop and inject oxygen as required to maintain a set point, typically around 1.3 bar of partial pressure oxygen. Maintaining the right oxygen level in the loop is the central challenge of CCR diving, and understanding it is the first thing any CCR student must internalise.

## Semi-Closed Rebreathers

A simpler variant, the semi-closed rebreather (SCR), circulates a fixed fraction enriched-air nitrox mixture through the loop, periodically venting a portion of exhaled gas. SCRs are mechanically simple and less failure-prone than fully electronic CCRs, but they produce some bubbles and are considerably less efficient than closed-circuit designs. They are used in some military and recreational applications but have largely been eclipsed by CCRs in the broader technical diving community.

## The Advantages

The gas efficiency advantage is obvious, but CCR diving offers other meaningful benefits. The absence of exhaled bubbles makes CCRs dramatically quieter than open-circuit systems: marine life, particularly large pelagic fish, does not react to a CCR diver the way it reacts to the explosive hiss of a regulator stage. Divers report far closer approaches to sharks, mantas, and whale sharks while on CCR.

The breathing gas is also warm and humid throughout the dive. Open-circuit regulators deliver cold, dry gas from a pressurised cylinder — cooling and dehydrating the diver's airway over a long dive. CCR gas is exhaled, passes through the warm scrubber, and returns at near body temperature. On long cold-water dives, this is physiologically meaningful.

The ability to optimise the oxygen partial pressure throughout the dive also affects decompression. A CCR maintains a high oxygen partial pressure — around 1.3 bar — during the entire dive, which slows nitrogen loading and accelerates off-gassing relative to any fixed fraction open-circuit mix. Long CCR dives at technical depths can require significantly less decompression time than the equivalent open-circuit dive.

## The Risks and Why Training Is Demanding

Every advantage comes with a corresponding demand. The scrubber canister that removes carbon dioxide must be packed correctly and must not be exhausted. A used or improperly packed scrubber allows CO2 to pass back into the breathing loop — a condition known as a CO2 breakthrough. The first symptoms — headache, disorientation, rapid breathing — can escalate to unconsciousness within seconds. Scrubber failures are among the most serious CCR incidents.

The oxygen partial pressure must stay within a viable range. Too low — hypoxia — and the diver loses consciousness without warning, usually before any sensation of distress. Too high — hyperoxia — and oxygen toxicity can cause convulsions. The electronics and sensors that manage this require pre-dive checks, calibration, and an understanding of failure modes that far exceeds anything in recreational open-circuit diving.

CCR training is extensive. Entry-level courses from agencies including PADI (Poseidon, rEvo, JJ-CCR models), TDI, IANTD, and DSAT typically require 20 to 30 dives on the unit before certification, multiple days of classroom instruction, and sustained mentoring from an active CCR instructor. The investment in time and money is substantial, and the training community is generally insistent that it cannot be abbreviated.

The equipment itself is expensive — a quality CCR unit costs between several thousand and over ten thousand euros — and requires regular servicing, including scrubber canister material replacement after every dive. This is not gear that can sit in a bag between annual trips.

## Who Dives CCR

Technical divers who regularly undertake long, deep dives are the primary CCR community. For someone diving to 70 metres on trimix for 90 minutes followed by staged decompression, the CCR's gas efficiency and superior decompression profile make it the rational choice. Underwater photographers and videographers value the silence and close wildlife encounters. Scientific and military divers may require bubble-free operation.

Recreational CCR diving exists — some manufacturers specifically market entry-level units to recreational divers, and there are valid reasons to explore it — but the training burden and equipment complexity mean most recreational divers are better served by open-circuit until they have a clear need for what CCR uniquely provides.

## Buoyancy on a CCR

One underappreciated aspect of CCR diving is the way buoyancy management changes. Open-circuit divers inflate and deflate their BCD to maintain neutral buoyancy as they consume gas and as depth changes. On a CCR, the main cylinders carry very little gas, so buoyancy changes with depth are driven almost entirely by wetsuit or drysuit compression, not cylinder weight. The diver compensates primarily by adjusting lung volume — breathing a larger or smaller tidal volume to fine-tune position. This is a subtle and continuous control that feels intuitive to some divers and alien to others.

The breathing loop itself has volume and buoyancy. A counterlung — the soft bag that absorbs exhaled gas before it passes through the scrubber — sits either on the chest or back depending on the unit design. Its volume changes as you breathe in and out, and this affects buoyancy in a way open-circuit divers are not accustomed to managing. Mastering the interaction between lung volume, counterlung volume, and suit buoyancy is one of the more time-consuming aspects of early CCR training.

## Getting Started

If CCR genuinely interests you, the first step is an introductory pool or shallow-water session with a certified CCR instructor. Most training centres that offer CCR courses will arrange a try-dive on a unit before you commit to the full course. This gives you a realistic sense of the cognitive demands — monitoring the heads-up display, managing buoyancy through lung volume rather than BC inflation, tracking the handset readouts — before spending significant money.

The CCR market now spans units aimed at purely recreational diving, such as the Poseidon MK6, through mid-range expedition units like the rEvo and Hollis Prism 2, to advanced technical platforms such as the JJ-CCR and Megalodon. The appropriate entry point depends heavily on what type of diving you intend to do and what training is available for a given unit in your region.
