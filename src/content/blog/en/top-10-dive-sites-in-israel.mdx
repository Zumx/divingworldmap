Israel offers one of the most geographically improbable diving experiences in the world: a sliver of Red Sea coastline at Eilat in the south, and an altogether different underwater Mediterranean world stretching north along more than 190 kilometres of coast. The contrast is remarkable. Eilat's Gulf of Aqaba plunges steeply from the shore, exposing divers to hard coral gardens, steep walls, and impressive wrecks in water that stays warm year-round. The Mediterranean side trades reef complexity for history — ancient harbour ruins, amphora fields, and the occasional shipwreck in clear but cooler water. Both coasts reward divers who invest the time to explore them properly.

## 1. Coral Beach Nature Reserve, Eilat

The protected waters of Coral Beach Reserve, just south of Eilat city, represent the most accessible and ecologically significant reef in Israel. The reserve shelters a fringing reef that begins almost at the surface and slopes to around 40 metres. Hard corals dominate the shallower sections — table acropora, massive brain corals, and fields of Stylophora — while lionfish, moray eels, and schools of glassfish pack the crevices. Water temperature ranges from 21°C in winter to 27°C in summer. Open Water certification is fine for the upper reef; the deeper wall rewards more experienced divers. Dive the reserve early morning before the snorkelling crowds arrive.

## 2. The Satil Wreck, Eilat

The Satil is Israel's premier wreck dive. This 57-metre Israeli Navy missile boat was deliberately sunk in 1994 as an artificial reef at around 22 to 27 metres in the Gulf of Aqaba. The vessel lies upright and is largely intact, making penetration of the wheelhouse and main deck corridors accessible to divers with basic wreck training. Scorpionfish sit motionless on the railings, and the upper superstructure is carpeted in soft corals and sponges. Visibility in this part of Eilat regularly exceeds 20 metres. Currents are mild. An Advanced Open Water certification is recommended to reach the deeper sections comfortably.

## 3. The Japanese Gardens, Eilat

Named for its intricate, almost manicured appearance, the Japanese Gardens reef stretches along the north beach of Eilat at depths between four and 14 metres, making it ideal for both beginners and underwater photographers chasing macro subjects. The site hosts an unusually dense variety of nudibranchs, flatworms, and small crustaceans among the finger corals and sea fans. Turtle sightings are routine. Because it is shallow and sheltered, visibility holds well even when a light northerly wind ruffles the surface. The site is also popular for night dives, when octopus, squid, and hunting lionfish become conspicuous.

## 4. Moses Rock, Eilat

Moses Rock sits just north of the Egyptian border at Taba, reachable by day trip from Eilat. The site is centred on a large coral head rising from about 20 metres to near the surface. Around its base, white-tip reef sharks rest on sandy patches, and Napoleon wrasse patrol the perimeter with characteristic indifference to divers. The surrounding reef slopes gently to 30 metres and beyond, where large grouper hold in the blue water. Best dived October through May when visibility can exceed 25 metres and water temperatures sit at a comfortable 22–24°C. Suitable from Open Water level; Advanced credentials allow fuller exploration of the deeper shoulder.

## 5. The Caesarea Underwater Archaeological Park, Mediterranean Coast

Caesarea is unique among Israeli dive sites — a partially submerged Herodian harbour built around 22 BCE and expanded by the Romans, Crusaders, and Ottomans over succeeding centuries. Divers swim over fallen columns, mosaic floors, bronze statues, and the remains of harbour walls at depths between two and nine metres. The park is divided into marked trails accessible to Open Water divers. Water temperature ranges from 17°C in winter to 28°C in August. Visibility can reach 15 metres in calm conditions but drops significantly after storms. The site is a UNESCO-listed archaeological zone; nothing may be touched or removed.

## 6. Achziv Marine Reserve, Northern Mediterranean

Achziv, near the Lebanese border, is Israel's second protected marine reserve and the Mediterranean shore's best diving. Sea grass meadows give way to rocky reefs at five to 18 metres, sheltering grouper, bream, and occasional octopus. The reserve's protection from fishing pressure means fish populations here are noticeably denser than elsewhere on the Mediterranean coast. Water clarity is best from May through October when thermoclines stabilise and summer warmth reaches 26–28°C. Surge can make the shallows uncomfortable in winter swells. Entry from the shore is straightforward, and local operators in Nahariya run guided dives and equipment rental.

## 7. The Underwater Museum of Caesarea (Caesar Augustus Statue Site)

Distinct from the main archaeological park, this section of the Caesarea seabed preserves a life-size replica of the Caesar Augustus statue alongside amphora reconstructions and anchor replicas placed to simulate the feel of an ancient trading route. Depths range from three to eight metres, making it a valid introduction dive for newly certified divers and an evocative orientation to the history of the site. Summer months bring the best visibility. The site is managed alongside the main park and requires the same entry permit. Strong surge after westerly storm events can make the site unsafe; operators monitor conditions closely.

## 8. The Dolphin Reef, Eilat

Dolphin Reef operates as a semi-enclosed lagoon where a resident pod of bottlenose dolphins lives freely, choosing whether to interact with divers or not. Dives here are managed and structured, reaching maximum depths of around 12 metres over sandy seafloor. The experience is genuinely unusual — dolphins at full speed will pass centimetres from your mask, then curl away into the blue. Beyond the dolphins themselves, the sandy bottom hosts garden eels, flounder, and occasional rays. All certification levels are welcome. Booking in advance is essential, as the number of divers allowed in the water at any one time is strictly limited.

## 9. Coral Island (Pharaoh's Island), Gulf of Aqaba

Technically part of Egypt but only minutes by boat from Eilat, Coral Island (Jazīrat Fir'awn) is regularly included on cross-border day trips organised by Eilat operators. The reef encircles the island at depths of five to 30 metres, offering impressive hard coral coverage and a good population of reef fish including surgeonfish, angelfish, and barracuda. The 12th-century Crusader fortress on the island's summit provides historical context above water. Best conditions fall between October and May. Egyptian dive permits are required; most Eilat operators handle paperwork as part of the day-trip package.

## 10. The Amphora Trail, Ashkelon

Off the coast of Ashkelon, a scattering of Roman-era amphoras lies at eight to 14 metres on a mixed sand and rock bottom — the remnants of a merchant vessel lost sometime around the 1st to 3rd century CE. The amphoras are not concentrated in a tight debris field but are dispersed across several hundred square metres, suggesting the ship broke apart on impact or drifted as it sank. Grouper have colonised the larger amphoras, using them as shelter. Visibility here is variable — 10 to 15 metres on calm summer days, considerably less after winter storms. Best dived June through September.

## Planning Your Dive Trip to Israel

Israel's two coastlines have fundamentally different seasons. Eilat's Red Sea is diveable year-round; the period from October to May offers the best visibility and avoids peak summer heat on land, though summer water temperatures of 26–27°C are genuinely comfortable. The Mediterranean coast is best from April to October when water temperatures and visibility peak together. Operators in Eilat are numerous and well-equipped, catering to everything from first-try discover dives to technical courses. The Mediterranean coast has fewer dedicated dive centres, with Caesarea, Ashkelon, and the Nahariya area each supporting small operations.
