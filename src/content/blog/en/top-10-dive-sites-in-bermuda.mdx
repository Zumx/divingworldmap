Bermuda sits alone in the western Atlantic, about 1,070 kilometres east of Cape Hatteras — far north of the usual Caribbean diving zone, yet warmed by the Gulf Stream enough to sustain the northernmost coral reef system in the world. That geographical isolation has produced a marine environment unlike anywhere else in the Atlantic: endemic species found nowhere else, an extraordinary density of shipwrecks accumulated over centuries of treacherous navigation in poor charts, and water that stays clear and blue for most of the year. Water temperatures range from around 18°C in February and March to 29°C in August. The shallow reef platform surrounding the island — the Bermuda Platform — is roughly 24 kilometres wide and strewn with the wrecks that made these waters infamous. Visibility is frequently 25 to 30 metres, sometimes more.

## 1. Cristóbal Colón Wreck, Northwest Breakers

The Cristóbal Colón is the largest shipwreck in Bermudian waters — a Spanish luxury liner of nearly 11,000 tons that ran onto the Northwest Breakers reef in 1936. The hull broke apart on impact and the remains are spread across a large area of reef in depths from 8 to 24 metres, making this an accessible dive that can be treated as reef diving or wreck diving depending on your interest. The structural steel has long since been colonised by coral and sponge, and large sections of hull plate form dramatic photographic subjects. Bermuda chub, blue parrotfish, and various grouper species are resident. Open Water certification sufficient for all sections.

## 2. Mary Celestia Wreck, South Shore

Not to be confused with the ghost ship Mary Celeste, this is the Confederate blockade runner Mary Celestia, a side-wheel paddle steamer that sank in 1864 while running the Union blockade with a cargo of supplies for the Confederacy. The wreck sits in 17 metres of water off the south shore, upright and largely intact in outline despite its age. The paddle wheels are clearly recognisable. In 2011 a section of the hull was exposed by hurricane action, revealing intact cargo including champagne bottles, perfume vials, and personal effects — some of which remain in situ under Bermuda's strict heritage protection regulations. Lionfish, introduced to Atlantic waters, are present here; sightings of nurse sharks under the stern are common.

## 3. North Rock, North Shore

North Rock is not a wreck but a coral-encrusted sea mount rising from around 20 metres to within a few metres of the surface, marking the northern edge of the Bermuda Platform. It is one of the best pure reef dives in Bermuda, with coral coverage including healthy stands of brain coral, pillar coral, and various plate corals. Bermuda sergeant major, yellowtail snapper, and Nassau grouper are the typical reef fish; green turtles are regularly sighted on and around the reef structure. The site is exposed to northern swells in winter but dives well from late spring through autumn. Advanced Open Water certification recommended when currents run.

## 4. Constellation and Nola Wrecks, Northwest Reef

The Constellation is a four-masted schooner that sank in 1943 while carrying a cargo of drugs, medical supplies, and building materials — an eclectic mix that inspired the Peter Benchley novel *The Deep* and the subsequent film. The wreck sits in 10 to 18 metres of water, largely buried in the sand with structural elements protruding, adjacent to the earlier wreck of the Nola. The two wrecks have essentially merged into a single dive site. Drug ampules from the medical cargo have been found on the site for decades; Bermuda law prohibits removal, but the story draws curiosity divers as much as the marine life does. Good site for photography, with clear shallow water and dense fish populations.

## 5. Hermes Wreck, South Shore

The Hermes was a small American commercial vessel sunk deliberately in 1984 to create an artificial reef, and in the four decades since it has developed into one of Bermuda's most photogenic wreck dives. The ship sits upright in 24 metres with the wheelhouse accessible at around 18 metres, colonised by sponges in orange and purple and permanently attended by a large school of French grunt. A green turtle has been resident around the bow section for years. The structural integrity is good — the hull is intact enough to be identifiable as a ship while also being sufficiently open for natural light to penetrate the holds. Open Water divers can handle the shallowest sections; Advanced divers can work the full wreck.

## 6. Montana Wreck, North Shore

The Montana is a Confederate blockade runner — a side-wheel steamer like the Mary Celestia — that sank in 1863 on the North Breakers reef. She rests in depths from 10 to 18 metres, with the boilers and engine assembly the most distinctive remaining structures. The wooden hull has long since gone; what remains is the iron machinery and structural ironwork, draped in sponge and soft coral. Visibility here is generally excellent because the north shore location benefits from open-ocean water with little sediment. Horse-eye jacks school around the boilers; spotted moray eels inhabit the iron crevices.

## 7. L'Herminie Wreck, Southwest Reef

L'Herminie is a French warship — a 60-gun frigate — that wrecked on the southwest reef in 1838 in a storm while returning from the Caribbean. The wreck is historically significant as one of the largest warships ever lost on the Bermuda reef, and archaeologists have conducted surveys of the scattered cannon and anchor chain that still mark the site. The depth range is shallow — 3 to 10 metres — making this appropriate for all certification levels and accessible as a snorkel site. Bronze cannons, iron cannon balls, and anchor chain sections are visible in situ among the coral.

## 8. Taunton Wreck, Bermuda Platform

The Taunton is a British cargo steamship that sank in 1920 after striking the reef in fog. The wreck is in good structural condition for its age, sitting in 24 to 30 metres of water on the outer reef slope. The holds are accessible with penetration torches, and the engine room is a focal point for Bermuda wreck divers. Significant marine growth covers the hull — orange elephant-ear sponges dominate the exterior, and black coral grows on the deeper sections of the stern. Advanced Open Water certification recommended; some sections suit Advanced or higher based on depth and penetration interest.

## 9. South West Breakers Wall, South Shore

Beyond the wreck diving, Bermuda has genuine wall diving on the outer edge of the platform where the reef drops from around 18 metres to depths beyond recreational limits. The South West Breakers area offers some of the most dramatic of these wall sections, with deep-water gorgonians, black coral trees, and the occasional open-ocean visitor — silky sharks, oceanic triggerfish — that don't appear on the shallow reef. Dive boats from operators in Hamilton can reach the outer wall, but conditions need to be calm and the dive is not appropriate for beginners. Best in summer when the Gulf Stream is closest and water clarity peaks.

## 10. Cathedral, Mid-Platform

A coral formation of impressive scale mid-platform on the south side of the island, Cathedral takes its name from the large archway formation through which divers can swim from one side to the other. Depths to around 18 metres. The arch itself is draped in sponges and provides a natural frame for wide-angle photography. The surrounding reef supports a healthy mix of Caribbean reef species including parrotfish, trumpetfish, and the resident turtle population that has made Bermuda's reef system notable for its green turtle density.

## Planning a Diving Trip to Bermuda

The dive season runs from April through November, with July through October providing the warmest water and best visibility. Bermuda has no budget diving — accommodation costs and the relative remoteness keep it at the higher end of dive destination pricing, but the wreck density and water quality justify the investment for serious wreck divers. Main operators are based in Hamilton, St George's, and the west end near the Royal Naval Dockyard. Most run scheduled two-tank trips to the major wreck sites. Nitrox is available. Bermuda's strict conservation regulations mean wreck artefacts must not be removed; photography is encouraged, collection is not.
