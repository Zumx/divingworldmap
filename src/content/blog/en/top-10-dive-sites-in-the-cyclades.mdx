The Cyclades scatter across the central Aegean in a rough circle around the sacred island of Delos, and the diving here is defined by volcanic geology, exceptional water clarity and the deep blue of an open, island-studded sea. Santorini is the dramatic centrepiece — a flooded volcanic caldera whose black walls plunge straight into the depths — while Mykonos, Paros, Naxos and the smaller islands offer reefs, walls, caverns and a handful of wrecks. Water temperatures run from around 16°C in winter to 25°C in late summer, and visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres in the calm months. The Aegean is not a soft-coral sea; expect rocky reefs encrusted with yellow and orange life, schools of bream and damselfish, octopus, moray, the occasional grouper and amberjack, and the meltemi — the strong summer north wind that dictates which sites are diveable on any given day. Operators run from the main harbours of each island, and the central position makes island-hopping by ferry a natural way to sample several different diving characters in one trip.

## 1. Santorini Caldera, Nea Kameni

Diving the Santorini caldera is one of the most distinctive experiences in the Mediterranean. The caldera was formed by the catastrophic Minoan eruption around 1600 BC, and divers descend the steep volcanic walls into water of extraordinary clarity and depth. Near the active volcanic islet of Nea Kameni, divers can feel warm water seeping from the seabed and see the rust-coloured staining of mineral-rich vents. The walls drop dramatically, the rock is black and otherworldly, and the marine life is sparse but the geology is the star. Advanced Open Water is recommended given the depth and the lack of a shallow reef to retreat to.

## 2. Caldera Wall and Indian House, Santorini

Along the inner caldera, the Indian House wreck — a small vessel resting on the volcanic slope — provides a focal point for a wall dive that descends from the surface into deep blue. The wall is encrusted with sponges and the occasional gorgonian, and schools of bream patrol the rock. The water clarity here is exceptional, and the sensation of hovering over a void where the bottom is far below is unique to Santorini. The site suits divers comfortable with deeper profiles and good buoyancy, and is typically run from the dive centres in the caldera-side villages.

## 3. Mykonos Reefs and Caverns

Mykonos offers a different, gentler character than Santorini — rocky reefs, swim-throughs and small caverns at recreational depths around the island's coastline. The clear Aegean water and the rocky topography support octopus, moray, scorpionfish and large schools of damselfish and bream. Several sites feature dramatic underwater rock formations and tunnels where light filters through, and the diving suits Open Water through Advanced divers. The island's lively reputation above water belies a relaxed, scenic dive scene below, and operators run daily boat trips to a rotation of sites depending on the meltemi.

## 4. Anhydros / Dragonisi Cave, Mykonos

Off the eastern coast of Mykonos, the islet of Dragonisi is fringed with sea caves and caverns that monk seals are known to frequent. The diving here combines the dramatic overhead topography of the caverns with the clear water of the open Aegean, and divers may glimpse the rare Mediterranean monk seal — one of the world's most endangered marine mammals — though sightings are never guaranteed. The caverns require good buoyancy and a torch, and the exposed position means the meltemi often closes the site. When conditions allow, it is among the most memorable dives in the Cyclades.

## 5. Paros Reefs and Wrecks

Paros sits centrally in the Cyclades and offers a mix of reef diving and a couple of accessible wrecks at recreational depths. The reefs are typical Aegean rock — overhangs, ledges and rubble fields populated by octopus, moray and reef fish — while the wrecks add structure and a focus for the dive. The island's central ferry connections make it a practical base for exploring the surrounding islands, and the dive centres around Parikia and Naoussa run trips to sites that suit a range of certification levels. Conditions are clear and warm through the diving season.

## 6. Naxos Reefs

Naxos, the largest of the Cyclades, offers reef and wall diving along its coastline with the clear water and rocky topography characteristic of the central Aegean. The sites range from gentle reefs suitable for training to deeper walls and drop-offs, and the marine life follows the Aegean pattern of octopus, moray, grouper and abundant smaller reef fish. The island sees less dive tourism than Santorini or Mykonos, which can mean quieter sites and more relaxed diving. Operators based on the island run boat trips to a selection of reefs depending on the wind.

## 7. Milos and the Volcanic Coast

Milos is a volcanic island in the western Cyclades whose dramatic above-water geology — the famous lunar landscapes and coloured cliffs — continues underwater in the form of rock formations, caverns and clear water. Diving here reveals the island's volcanic origins, with interesting topography and the occasional sign of geothermal activity. The marine life is typical Aegean reef fauna, and the relative remoteness keeps dive traffic low. Milos rewards divers seeking dramatic scenery and a quieter alternative to the busier Cycladic islands, with operators running trips around the island's coastline.

## 8. Ios Reefs

Ios offers reef diving in clear Aegean water around its rocky coastline, with sites suitable for a range of experience levels. The topography features rocky reefs, ledges and small caverns, and the marine life includes octopus, moray, bream and damselfish. The island's central position and clear water make it a pleasant stop on a Cycladic diving itinerary, and the relatively low dive traffic means relaxed conditions on most sites. Operators run boat trips depending on the meltemi, which strongly influences which parts of the coast are sheltered on any given day.

## 9. Folegandros and the Small Cyclades

The smaller, less-developed islands of the Cyclades — Folegandros, Sikinos and the islets around Naxos — offer wilder, less-dived reefs for those willing to seek them out. The diving is characterised by dramatic rocky topography, clear water and undisturbed marine life, with the trade-off of limited operator coverage and more weather dependence. These sites suit experienced divers travelling with knowledgeable local guides, and they offer a sense of exploration increasingly rare in the more developed parts of the Aegean. The dramatic cliffs above water continue as walls and drop-offs below.

## 10. Amorgos Wall and Wreck

Amorgos, on the eastern edge of the Cyclades, is famous as the filming location for the diving film The Big Blue, and its dramatic cliffs plunge into deep water that has drawn freedivers and scuba divers alike. The walls drop steeply, the water is exceptionally clear, and the open-sea position attracts larger pelagic visitors. There is also wreck diving accessible from the island. The exposed eastern position means the meltemi must be respected, and the deeper walls suit Advanced divers. Amorgos combines cinematic scenery with genuine Aegean wall diving in a relatively remote setting.

## Planning Your Trip

The Cyclades dive season runs from April to November, with the warmest and calmest water from June to September — though this also coincides with the peak meltemi season, when the strong north wind can close exposed sites for days at a time. Flexibility is essential; experienced operators rotate between sheltered sites depending on the wind. Santorini and Mykonos are the main bases with the most operators, while islands like Paros, Naxos and Milos offer quieter alternatives. Ferries connect the islands efficiently, making multi-island itineraries straightforward. A 5mm wetsuit suits most of the season. Advanced Open Water is recommended for the deeper caldera and wall dives; the caverns require good buoyancy and a torch.
