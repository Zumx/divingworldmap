## The Fundamental Difference

Diving dry changes the physics of buoyancy management in one critical way: the suit itself becomes a variable buoyancy device. In a wetsuit, buoyancy is largely fixed at any given depth — the foam neoprene has a specific displaced volume and that volume changes with depth as pressure compresses the material, but the diver has no real-time control over it. In a drysuit, the diver controls the volume of gas inside the suit continuously, adding air from a low-pressure inflator connected to the first stage and venting it through a dump valve, typically positioned on the upper arm or shoulder.

This additional dimension of control sounds like it should simplify buoyancy, and eventually it does — but for most divers transitioning from wetsuit diving, the first sessions in a drysuit are characterised by confusion, over-correction, and the unpleasant experience of floating helplessly towards the surface legs-first with an over-inflated suit. Understanding why this happens, and how to prevent it, is the core of drysuit training.

## Buoyancy: The BCD Relationship

The first rule of drysuit diving that instructors emphasise is to use the suit as your primary buoyancy device and the BCD as a supplement, not the other way around. This is counterintuitive for divers who have trained on a BCD in a wetsuit, where the jacket or wing is the only buoyancy tool. The reason for the rule is practical: the BCD controls buoyancy symmetrically, whereas the drysuit allows gas to migrate to the highest point of the suit — typically the feet and legs when the diver is head-down, and the upper torso and shoulders when horizontal.

If a diver enters the water and fills the BCD without adding suit gas, they will be cold immediately as the suit collapses against the skin and the insulating air layer is absent. They will also feel the suit squeezing on their feet and legs as depth increases and outside pressure compresses the suit material. The correct sequence is to add enough gas to the suit at the surface to eliminate the squeeze, then manage buoyancy during descent primarily through suit inflation and deflation, reserving the BCD for fine-tuning.

Weighting for drysuit diving requires reassessment. Most divers need more weight in a drysuit than in an equivalent wetsuit because the suit material itself is more buoyant. Shell drysuits — made from nylon, trilaminate, or similar non-foam materials — are less inherently buoyant than compressed foam neoprene drysuits, but both require adjustment to your existing weighting. Conducting a proper buoyancy check at the surface before every dive, and particularly when diving in unfamiliar conditions or with new undergarments, is essential practice.

## Trim and Foot Valves

Horizontal trim — the head-level-with-the-feet body position that good divers maintain throughout a dive — is harder to achieve in a drysuit because gas in the suit naturally accumulates in the highest point of the body. On a horizontal diver, that is the lower legs and feet. As the legs rise, the gas migrates further toward the feet, the legs rise further in a positive feedback loop, and the diver tips into a head-down position. In a silty environment like a wreck or a freshwater quarry, this transition happens fast and the fin-kick required to correct it stirs up silt rapidly.

The solutions are positional and mechanical. The positional solution is to keep the feet slightly lower than the torso — a subtle tilt that runs counter to the perfect horizontal preferred in warm-water diving. The mechanical solution is to practise venting the suit before the gas migrates. A dump valve on the left shoulder or upper left arm, when extended upward, is the highest point of the suit during normal horizontal trim. A small, frequent vent rather than a single large dump prevents the over-accumulation that causes the feet-up problem.

Some divers, particularly those who do extensive overhead environment diving in drysuits, add ankle dump valves to allow venting regardless of body position. These are standard fitting on suits designed for cave and technical diving.

## Undergarments and Thermal Management

The drysuit provides no thermal protection of its own beyond the thin shell material. All insulation comes from the undergarment — the layer worn inside the suit. The choice of undergarment determines comfort throughout the dive and requires thought matched to the actual water temperature.

In UK coastal water at 8 to 12 degrees Celsius, a substantial undersuit — a Thinsulate or GORE-Tex-compatible system, or a thick argon-inflated suit combination — is appropriate. Many cold-water divers use argon rather than air to inflate their suits, because argon has lower thermal conductivity than air and provides meaningfully better insulation at the same suit volume. Argon is supplied from a small dedicated cylinder clipped to the BCD, connected to the suit inflator independently from the main breathing gas supply.

In warmer water — the Scottish lochs in summer at 14 to 16 degrees, or the Mediterranean at 18 to 20 degrees — a lighter fleece undergarment allows drysuit diving in conditions where a thick wetsuit would also be acceptable. The drysuit advantage in moderate temperatures is primarily dryness and the resulting absence of post-dive cold as evaporation cools a wet body, rather than active thermal superiority.

Boot and glove management matters in cold water. Dry gloves, which seal to the suit's wrist ring system, provide dramatically better hand protection than any wetsuit glove equivalent at temperatures below 10 degrees. They require fitting specific to the suit and take a dive or two to adjust to, but divers who have used them in genuinely cold conditions rarely return to wet gloves.

## Emergency Procedures: The Runaway Ascent

The runaway ascent — in which a drysuit over-inflates and a diver rises rapidly to the surface with no way to vent gas fast enough — is the defining emergency scenario of drysuit diving and the reason the skill set is taken seriously.

The scenario develops when a suit inflator sticks in the open position, when a diver is inverted and unable to reach their shoulder dump, or when a diver over-inflates during a period of inattention. As the diver rises, the gas in the suit expands, increasing buoyancy further. Without intervention the ascent accelerates. The correct response has two components: first, tuck into a ball to maximise drag and slow the ascent; second, extend the dump valve arm directly overhead regardless of body orientation and vent aggressively. If the inflator is stuck open, disconnect it — most inflator connections allow a pull-to-disconnect motion.

A stuck inflator can often be felt before it becomes critical: an unusual stiffness in the suit or a sensation of unexpected buoyancy increase signals the problem. Divers who catch it early can disconnect and ascend in a controlled manner. Divers who notice it late, when they are already moving upward, have fewer options.

Practising the emergency procedure in a pool or shallow, calm water before relying on it in cold water is standard PADI Dry Suit Specialty curriculum and common sense. The movements — tucking, extending the arm, finding and disconnecting the inflator — need to be reflexive rather than remembered under stress.

## Drysuit Training and Progression

The PADI Dry Suit Specialty is a logical starting point, though BSAC, NAUI, and SSI all offer equivalent drysuit training. The prerequisite is typically Open Water certification, and the course covers the theory of suit types, weighting, buoyancy control, trim, and emergency procedures over a minimum of two open-water dives.

Many divers who complete the course find that genuine competence takes considerably longer — five to ten dives to feel comfortable with suit management, and closer to thirty before the micro-adjustments of suit and BCD become as automatic as they were in a wetsuit. This is normal and does not represent a failure of the course. Cold-water diving is simply demanding, and the combination of thermal discomfort, reduced manual dexterity in gloves, and the additional cognitive load of suit management creates a learning curve that rewards patience.
