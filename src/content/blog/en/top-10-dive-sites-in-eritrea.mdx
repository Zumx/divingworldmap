Eritrea holds one of the most pristine stretches of Red Sea coastline accessible to divers, and that pristineness is a direct consequence of its inaccessibility. Years of political isolation and challenging logistics have kept recreational diving pressure on the Dahlak Archipelago and adjacent reefs extremely low, and the marine ecosystems show it. Reefs here carry the dense fish populations and large apex predators — grey reef sharks, lemon sharks, Napoleon wrasse of truly impressive size — that characterise the Red Sea before mass tourism. The archipelago comprises more than 200 islands and islets, most uninhabited, spread across a vast shallow sea interspersed with deeper channels and passes. Diving is conducted almost exclusively from liveaboards or chartered dhows operating out of Massawa, and the logistical complexity of operating in Eritrean waters means that visitor numbers remain a fraction of what comparable Red Sea destinations attract. Water temperatures range from 24°C in January to 32°C in summer, and visibility is typically 20–30 metres. A spirit of genuine exploration characterises every dive here.

## 1. Dahlak Archipelago — Outer Reefs

The outermost reefs of the Dahlak face the open Red Sea and carry the full weight of pelagic nutrients that sweep along this coast. Wall dives here descend from 5–8 metres at the reef crest to beyond 40 metres, with healthy hard coral cover on the upper sections and black coral and large gorgonians below 25 metres. Grey reef sharks are a near constant presence, and schools of barracuda in the hundreds are common on the current-swept points. Napoleon wrasse of 150cm or more patrol the reef edges. Advanced Open Water minimum for the deeper walls; Open Water divers can enjoy the upper reef. Access only by liveaboard.

## 2. Difnein Island

Difnein is a mid-archipelago island whose surrounding reef encompasses a range of dive environments within a small area — a shallow lagoon with calm snorkelling and beginner diving, a steepening reef slope on the exposed eastern face, and a small wreck in manageable depths. The lagoon hosts dugong, making this one of the few sites in the Red Sea where these animals are regularly encountered underwater. The reef slope to the east produces consistent reef shark sightings and excellent hard coral topography. Best approached on a liveaboard itinerary that includes a night moored in the lagoon, as dawn and dusk produce the most productive dugong-watching. Open Water level for lagoon dives; Advanced for outer reef.

## 3. Madote

A compact reef islet with dramatic wall diving on its seaward side. The wall begins at 6 metres and drops steeply past 40, with some of the densest soft coral growth in the archipelago. The particular feature of Madote is a series of large coral bommies that rise from the sand at 20–25 metres, each one encrusted with crinoids and cup corals and surrounded by clouds of glassfish that in turn attract circling trevally. Turtles rest on the sand between the bommies. The site is calm on most days due to its orientation. Advanced Open Water for the deeper sections; the upper reef is accessible to Open Water divers.

## 4. Dissei Island

Dissei lies near the southern edge of the main archipelago and sees even less diving pressure than the islands to the north. The surrounding reef is notable for its extraordinary density of intact table corals — Acropora tables that have clearly been undisturbed for decades, growing to extraordinary diameters. Fish populations beneath and around the tables include large schools of snapper and a consistent resident population of nurse sharks resting on sandy patches between coral formations. Whale sharks have been reported seasonally in the adjacent channel. Liveaboard access; Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper sections.

## 5. Green Island

A tiny vegetated island in the central archipelago whose eastern reef wall is considered by those who have dived it to be among the most colourful in the Red Sea. The upper reef between 8 and 20 metres carries orange and pink soft corals, enormous yellow tube sponges, and encrusting coralline algae of vivid purple. Reef fish density is very high, including thick aggregations of anthias, and the overhangs shelter dozens of resting soldierfish and bigeye. Below 25 metres the wall transitions to black coral and whip corals extending horizontally into the current. Macro photography here is outstanding. Advanced Open Water recommended.

## 6. Massawa Harbour Wrecks

The old port of Massawa contains several wrecks dating from the Second World War and the Eritrean War of Independence. The most accessible, in 15–22 metres of water just off the historic Ottoman-era island town, are encrusted with coral growth after decades of submersion. Fish life is excellent — the structures function as artificial reefs, and the shelter they provide has attracted grouper, moray, and schools of fusiliers. Visibility is lower than in the open archipelago due to harbour sediment, but the historical interest of diving wrecks in a port city with centuries of layered history compensates. Open Water level adequate; accessible from Massawa town.

## 7. Sheik Said Island Passage

A narrow channel between two islands in the northern archipelago where tidal flow concentrates planktonic nutrients and draws large pelagics. Manta rays are seasonal visitors between November and March, using the passage as a cleaning station. The channel walls are draped with soft corals and the current-swept surfaces are encrusted with filter feeders — tunicates, hydroids, and crinoids. Drift diving through the passage is the standard approach; you are deposited in calm water on the far side after a fast, exhilarating run through the channel. Advanced Open Water minimum; current timing is essential and operators know the tidal windows.

## 8. Seil Adum Reef

A remote offshore reef east of the main archipelago that rises from 40 metres to within 2 metres of the surface. The summit is hammered by surge but the sides are spectacular: large fan corals and sea whips cover the upper walls, and the deeper sections reveal black coral trees and resting nurse sharks under overhangs. The remote location — only reachable by liveaboard with a committed captain — means encounters with large fish are common, including reef sharks circling the summit and occasional hammerheads at depth. Advanced Open Water required; this site is for experienced divers who are comfortable managing buoyancy in a surge environment.

## 9. Hawakil Bay Mangrove Edges

The extensive mangrove systems of Hawakil Bay on the southern Eritrean coast provide a different experience from reef diving but are ecologically significant and interesting to explore in shallow water. The root systems shelter juvenile fish of dozens of species, and moving slowly through the channels between mangrove islands at 2–5 metres produces encounters with young grouper, cuttlefish, and diverse nudibranch species. The site is best approached by kayak or tender from a liveaboard. Open Water level; snorkelling is equally rewarding. An important context for understanding why the adjacent reefs remain so productive.

## 10. Nora Island Wall

The southernmost dive site in the Eritrean archipelago described in this selection, Nora Island sits near the passage toward the Yemeni coast. The seaward wall is steep and exposed to periodic heavy current, which has produced an unusually dense growth of large gorgonian sea fans on the mid-wall between 20 and 35 metres. Grey reef sharks are present on every dive, and schools of batfish hover in the water column near the reef edge. The site is infrequently dived given its distance from Massawa, which means marine life is notably bold and unhabituated. Liveaboard access; Advanced Open Water required.

## Practical Information

The optimal diving season in Eritrea runs from October through April, when sea temperatures are comfortable, visibility is at its best, and the Shamal wind system is less disruptive. Summer months bring reduced visibility and higher surface temperatures that can make liveaboard conditions uncomfortable. All diving in the Dahlak Archipelago requires coordination with licensed Eritrean operators, and permits are necessary for entering the archipelago. Liveaboard capacity is limited by the small number of approved vessels — booking well in advance is essential.
