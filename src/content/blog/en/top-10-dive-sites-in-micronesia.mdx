The Federated States of Micronesia encompasses more than 600 islands scattered across roughly 2.7 million square kilometres of western Pacific Ocean, and the diving within its boundaries covers the full spectrum of what tropical ocean diving can offer: the world's greatest concentration of accessible WWII wrecks in Chuuk Lagoon, manta ray encounters at Yap that have become the defining benchmark for the species, pristine outer-atoll reefs in Pohnpei, and shark diving of the kind that used to happen accidentally and is now sought deliberately. The water temperature hovers around 28–29°C year-round, visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres on the outer reefs, and the wreck diving in Chuuk exists on a scale that takes multiple trips to comprehend.

## 1. Fujikawa Maru, Chuuk Lagoon

The Fujikawa Maru is widely regarded as Chuuk's most photogenic wreck and a strong candidate for the best wreck dive anywhere in the Pacific. This 133-metre Japanese aircraft ferry rests at 15 to 34 metres with three Zero fighter planes in its forward hold — recognisable by their circular engine cowlings — and a cargo of spare parts, oxygen torpedoes, and aviation equipment. The top deck and upper superstructure start at 15 metres, making significant portions of the wreck accessible to Advanced divers. Soft coral and sea fan growth is exceptional throughout. The wreck is penetrable at multiple points, though proper wreck training and a torch are essential. Visibility inside the holds can be significantly reduced by silt disturbance.

## 2. Shinkoku Maru, Chuuk Lagoon

The Shinkoku Maru, a 152-metre oil tanker, is perhaps the most coral-covered wreck in Chuuk — the entire superstructure from bridge to stern gun is draped in soft corals, making it simultaneously a diving destination and an extraordinary piece of living art. The shallowest deck structures begin at around 12 metres; the sand-covered stern anchor rests at 40 metres. The engine room is accessible and worth exploring for its intact gauges and instrumentation. Schooling glassfish occupy the hold spaces. This wreck rewards multiple dives: one pass cannot do justice to its length or detail. Advanced Open Water minimum for comprehensive exploration.

## 3. Sankisan Maru, Chuuk Lagoon

The Sankisan Maru is Chuuk's truck wreck, and it is extraordinary. This Japanese army transport vessel's holds contain intact military trucks, motorcycles, artillery pieces, and field equipment exactly as loaded before Operation Hailstone, the US air attack that sank the Japanese fleet in February 1944. The wreck sits at 12 to 42 metres, with the hold openings at accessible recreational depths. The combination of military artefacts in their original positions — gas masks still hanging on hooks, ammunition boxes stacked against bulkheads — and the encrusting coral growth that has claimed everything creates an atmosphere unlike any other wreck dive. Advanced Open Water with wreck specialisation for the holds.

## 4. Yap Manta Ray Cleaning Stations

Yap Island sits 800 kilometres southwest of Chuuk, and it occupies a unique position in diving history: its manta ray encounters at cleaning stations in Mi' and Goofnuw channels have been studied continuously since the 1970s, producing the longest dataset on individual manta identification and behaviour in the world. Reef manta rays arrive at fixed cleaning stations along the channels' edges, hovering while small cleaner wrasse remove parasites. Dives involve positioning on the bottom or a coral head at five to 20 metres and waiting — a patient approach rewards divers with close, prolonged encounters with animals that can span three to four metres across. The channels run with current; experience with drift is helpful.

## 5. Yap Shark City

A deep current-swept reef off Yap's outer coast is locally known as Shark City for the reliable concentration of grey reef sharks, white-tip reef sharks, and occasionally hammerheads in the blue water above the reef slope at 18 to 30 metres. The site is characteristically Yap — nutrient-rich current bringing both the manta rays and the shark density — and it provides one of the Pacific's more accessible and reliable shark dives. Pelagic fish including tuna and barracuda work the current with the sharks. Advanced Open Water certification appropriate for the depth and current exposure. Dive on a flood tide for best results.

## 6. Pohnpei Outer Reef Walls

Pohnpei, the FSM's largest island, is ringed by a barrier reef with outer walls dropping from the surface to beyond 40 metres. The walls carry impressive coral coverage and are visited by sharks, eagle rays, and large pelagics. Compared to Chuuk and Yap, Pohnpei attracts far fewer dive visitors, and the reef health reflects this reduced pressure. Sea turtles are common on the barrier reef; dolphins frequently accompany boats on the crossing to the outer reef. The island's infrastructure for diving is more limited — a handful of local operators versus Chuuk's liveaboard fleet — but the diving is genuinely excellent for those willing to organise access independently.

## 7. Truk Lagoon: Heian Maru

The Heian Maru was a Japanese passenger liner converted to a submarine tender; at 163 metres it is the longest wreck in Chuuk Lagoon. It rests at 15 to 35 metres and is notable for the submarine periscopes and torpedo equipment still visible in the aft section. The bridge deck at around 15 metres is accessible to Advanced divers and offers exceptional photography given the visibility and the soft coral growth on the railings and equipment. The hold of the Heian Maru contains the largest single artefact field in Chuuk: row upon row of oxygen torpedoes. Penetration of this area requires wreck specialisation and proper dive configuration.

## 8. Kepirohi Waterfall Reef, Pohnpei

Just outside the barrier reef near the famous Kepirohi waterfall on Pohnpei's north coast, a mixed reef system at five to 20 metres provides the best accessible reef diving near the island's main town, Kolonia. The site benefits from freshwater input that creates a mild halocline at certain tidal states, and this nutrient flow supports exceptional soft coral and sponge growth. Gobies on sea fans, crocodilefish on rubble, and a good nudibranch population make this a productive macro site. Suitable from Open Water level. Surface conditions can be choppy with southeast trade winds; calmer in the morning.

## 9. Blue Lagoon, Chuuk Atoll

Inside Chuuk Lagoon, the Blue Lagoon sites sit in shallower protected water away from the main wreck field, offering certified divers an easier orientation dive and snorkellers access to some of the lagoon's coral gardens. The coral here does not match the outer Pacific reefs but provides a pleasant contrast to the wreck-focused diving. The atoll's enclosed lagoon maintains warm, calm water year-round, making conditions here more consistent than the exposed outer atolls. Open Water level and a good option for refresher dives before committing to the deeper wrecks.

## 10. Chuuk Lagoon: Nippo Maru

The Nippo Maru is the designated 'artefact wreck' of Chuuk — smaller than the main fleet wrecks at around 72 metres, it sits at 40 to 48 metres in the deepest recreational range, but its upper features at around 30 metres include an antiaircraft gun, military trucks, and field equipment in a level of preservation that makes it the wreck dive most likely to produce a single striking image. A tank sits in the hold with its barrel aimed upward through the open hatch. The depth makes this an Advanced dive with careful gas management; Nitrox is strongly recommended. Visibility in Chuuk's sheltered lagoon water typically runs 20 to 30 metres.

## Planning Your Micronesia Dive Trip

Chuuk's weather is most reliable from January through April. Yap is best for mantas from December through April when the northeast trade winds are calm and manta concentrations at the cleaning stations peak. Pohnpei is diveable year-round in the protected lagoon; the outer reefs are more weather-dependent. Most serious Chuuk visitors arrive on liveaboards based out of Weno; Truk Stop Hotel and Blue Lagoon Resort also offer day-boat diving. Yap divers base in Colonia. Nitrox is available in both Chuuk and Yap. Nearest recompression facilities for FSM are in Guam.
