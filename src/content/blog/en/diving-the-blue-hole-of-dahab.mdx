A short drive north of the Bedouin town of Dahab, on Egypt's Sinai coast, a flight of stone steps leads down to a cluster of beachfront cafés where camels rest in the shade. From the water's edge, a circle of dark blue interrupts the turquoise reef flat. This is the Blue Hole, a near-perfect submarine sinkhole that drops to around 100 metres, and it carries a reputation few dive sites can match: it is at once one of the most beautiful and one of the most dangerous places to put on a tank in the Red Sea. The cafés are pleasant; the cliff above them is lined with memorial plaques.

## The Anatomy of the Sinkhole

The Blue Hole is a vertical limestone shaft, roughly 130 metres across at the surface, formed when an ancient cave collapsed. It sits just inside the fringing reef, separated from the open Red Sea by a saddle of reef called El Bells and a shallow lip. The interior walls are encrusted with hard and soft corals, sea fans and sponges, and patrolled by anthias, butterflyfish, and the occasional passing trevally. Visibility regularly exceeds 25 metres, and the water is warm year round, from the low twenties in winter to around 28 degrees Celsius in summer.

For a diver who stays within recreational limits, this is a gentle and gorgeous site. You can enter from the saddle in a few metres of water, drift along the inner wall, and ascend without ever approaching danger. The trouble begins only when divers are tempted by the feature that has made the Blue Hole infamous.

## The Arch

At around 55 metres of depth, a natural tunnel pierces the outer wall of the shaft, connecting the bowl of the Blue Hole to the open sea. This is the Arch, a swim-through roughly 26 metres long that opens onto a breathtaking view of the reef wall dropping into the abyss. It is, by any measure, a spectacular dive. It is also well beyond recreational depth, at a level where nitrogen narcosis is significant, gas planning is unforgiving, and the margin for error shrinks toward nothing.

The Arch has killed an alarming number of divers, by some counts well over a hundred. The recurring pattern is not equipment failure or freak accident. It is recreational divers, often without technical training, attempting the Arch on a single tank of air, becoming narked, losing track of depth or direction, missing the tunnel and continuing down the sheer wall, and running out of gas or losing consciousness. The plaques on the cliff above the cafés commemorate many of them.

## Why It Demands Respect

The Blue Hole is not intrinsically more dangerous than other deep dives in the Red Sea. The hazard is psychological as much as physical: the site is easy to reach from shore, the entry is trivial, and the Arch is a tangible, named objective that tempts under-qualified divers to overreach. A 55-metre dive requires proper gas, ideally trimix to manage narcosis, redundant systems, decompression planning, and the training to execute it. None of that is optional, and the consequences of treating it as optional are written on the memorial wall.

Responsible operators in Dahab will not take recreational divers through the Arch, and the better dive centres turn away anyone whose certification or attitude suggests they will attempt it unprepared. If you want to dive the Arch, do it properly: complete technical training, dive it with an appropriate gas plan and a team, or do not dive it at all. There is no shame in enjoying the upper reef and leaving the Arch to those equipped for it.

## The Recreational Dive

Treated sensibly, the Blue Hole is one of the loveliest shore dives in Egypt. The classic recreational route starts at El Bells, a narrow chimney in the reef a short walk north along the shore. You descend the chimney, exit onto the open reef wall at around 28 metres, and drift south along a wall draped in gorgonians and glassfish until you cross the saddle into the Blue Hole itself at about 6 metres, finishing the dive in the calm bowl. The profile keeps you within recreational depth throughout and showcases the best of the site without ever going near the Arch.

Early morning is the time to go. The dive sites around Dahab, the Blue Hole included, fill with traffic as the day warms, and the light on the reef wall is finest in the first hours. The town itself, a relaxed former fishing village turned diving and freediving hub, makes an easy and affordable base, with operators running shore dives up and down the coast at sites like the Canyon and the Islands.

## Dahab as a Freediving Capital

The Blue Hole is not only a scuba site. Its calm, deep, accessible water has made Dahab one of the world's great freediving destinations. Apnea athletes train and compete here, descending the shaft on a single breath along a weighted line, and the town hosts regular freediving courses and competitions. The same depth that makes the Arch lethal for unprepared scuba divers makes the Blue Hole an ideal training environment for the discipline, sheltered from current and chop, with a reliable bottom far below.

## Planning Your Dive

Dahab is reached by road from Sharm el-Sheikh airport, roughly an hour and a half south. Most diving is shore-based, which keeps costs low and schedules flexible. An Advanced Open Water certification opens up the recreational Blue Hole and El Bells route comfortably; nitrox is widely available and sensible given the depths involved. Bring a torch if you intend to dive El Bells, and choose an operator that takes the safety briefing seriously. The Blue Hole rewards the prepared and punishes the reckless, and the difference between the two outcomes is entirely a matter of judgement.
