Papua New Guinea sits at the apex of the Coral Triangle, the region of ocean encompassing more marine species than anywhere else on Earth. The country's coastline, islands, and offshore reefs support an astonishing diversity of hard and soft corals, and its WWII wreck inventory ranks among the richest in the Pacific. Diving here means choosing between world-class wall diving, muck diving that has spawned entire photographic careers, historic wrecks draped in 80 years of coral growth, and open-ocean encounters with schooling hammerheads and whale sharks. Water temperatures range from 26°C to 30°C depending on season and depth. Visibility is typically excellent in the dry season, May through October, often exceeding 30 metres on exposed walls. Papua New Guinea is not a budget destination — getting there requires long-haul flights to Port Moresby and then regional connections — but divers who make the journey consistently rate it among the top five diving nations in the world. ## 1. Kimbe Bay, West New Britain

Kimbe Bay is a volcanic deep-water bay on the north coast of West New Britain island, and its underwater topography is remarkable: more than 50 coral seamounts rise from depths of 300 metres to within a few metres of the surface, each one encrusted with coral and teeming with fish life. The diversity is staggering — over 400 coral species and 900 fish species have been recorded in the bay. Sites like Joelle's Reef and Bradford Shoals produce aggregations of barracuda, trevally, and surgeonfish in the thousands. Hammerhead sharks appear regularly on the deeper seamounts. Water temperature is 28–30°C; visibility 20–30 metres. A liveaboard or shore-based resort at Walindi Plantation is the standard way to dive here. Advanced Open Water recommended for seamount diving.

## 2. Tufi Fjords, Northern Province

Tufi is one of PNG's most unusual dive regions: a series of flooded coral fjords on the north Papuan coast that combine dramatic topography with extraordinary marine life. The fjord walls drop sheer into water too deep to sound with a dive computer, and the encrusting life on those walls — lace corals, large sea fans, enormous barrel sponges — is in pristine condition because few divers reach this remote area. Muck diving in the fjord floors produces spectacular finds: mimic octopus, flamboyant cuttlefish, blue-ringed octopus, and bizarre pipefish species. Visibility varies with runoff; outer sites in the dry season (May–October) reach 25 metres. The only realistic access is via a small resort at Tufi. Mixed certification requirements; Open Water for the shallower sites, Advanced for the walls.

## 3. Rabaul WWII Wrecks, East New Britain

Rabaul was Japan's main Pacific base after its capture in 1942, and the town's harbour and surrounding Simpson Harbour hold the remains of a fleet of Japanese ships sunk by Allied bombing in 1944. The wrecks sit in 20–50 metres of water and have accumulated 80 years of coral growth. The Hakiai Maru is a substantial freighter accessible to sport divers, its holds still containing trucks and war material. The submarine Ro-33 lies at 30–40 metres — a challenging dive but one of the most historically significant in the Pacific. Visibility in the harbour averages 10–15 metres; conditions outside are cleaner. Advanced Open Water and a wreck specialty course are strongly recommended. Rabaul dive operators are small but experienced.

## 4. Witu Islands, West New Britain

The Witu (or French) Islands lie offshore in the Bismarck Sea and receive very few visiting divers, which means their reefs are in extraordinary condition. The volcanic pinnacles here — some of them active, with warm vents visible on the reef — rise from abyssal depths to within a few metres of the surface and are blanketed in soft coral. Pygmy seahorses have been found here on the sea fans, and the walls support colonies of rare mushroom corals. Mantas are regular visitors. This is genuinely remote diving; access requires a liveaboard or a private charter from Kimbe. Visibility is exceptional, frequently above 30 metres. Advanced Open Water required; divemasters familiar with the currents are essential.

## 5. Milne Bay, Eastern Province

Milne Bay is historically significant as the site of the first Allied land defeat of Japanese forces in WWII, and underwater it holds the corresponding wreck inventory. The SS Muscoota, an American patrol vessel, lies in about 22 metres; a Japanese Zero fighter is accessible in the shallows. But Milne Bay's larger draw is its muck diving. The black-sand volcanic slopes off Dinah's Beach and Observation Point host phenomenal cryptic life: mimic octopus, rhinopias scorpionfish, painted frogfish, ribbon eels, and the occasional hairy frogfish that takes patience and sharp eyes to find. Water temperature is 27–29°C. Milne Bay Resort has operated here for decades and maintains excellent local knowledge.

## 6. Lissenung Island, New Ireland

Lissenung is a tiny island off New Ireland that serves as the base for a small, highly regarded dive operation. The surrounding reefs are a mix of healthy walls, coral gardens, and several WWII wrecks in accessible depths. The Japanese freighter Sanko Maru sits in 18 metres with its holds intact and open, and the resident population of bumphead parrotfish visits the nearby reef crest at dawn in a grazing herd of 60 or more fish. Mandarin fish perform their evening mating display at dusk on the rubble patches just in front of the resort jetty — one of the reliable mandarin fish sites in PNG. Advanced Open Water for the wreck; Open Water sufficient for reef sites. Visibility typically 20–25 metres.

## 7. Fathers Reefs, East New Britain

Fathers Reefs is a collection of coral pinnacles rising from deep water off the Duke of York Islands between Rabaul and New Ireland. The vertical walls here are exceptionally colourful, covered in bright orange and yellow soft corals, and the fish life is correspondingly dense — schools of anthias in the thousands hover at the wall face while trevally patrol overhead. The site is exposed to ocean swell, making surface conditions occasionally rough, but underwater is typically calm with good visibility of 20–25 metres. This is some of PNG's most photogenic diving. Accessible from Kokopo (near Rabaul) by boat. Advanced Open Water recommended given the depth and potential current. Best season May through October.

## 8. Black Jack, New Britain

Black Jack is an offshore seamount southeast of Kimbe Bay that rises to about 15 metres below the surface. The top of the mount is washed by currents that bring nutrient-rich water, sustaining an abundance of fish life that exceeds even PNG's normally high baseline. Schools of barracuda spiral upward in tornadic formations, big-eye trevally pack into spherical bait balls, and hammerhead sharks are regular visitors, particularly during the October–March warm season. Mantas appear year-round. This is advanced drift diving; the currents are strong and can reverse without warning. Only experienced divers familiar with open-water conditions should attempt it. Plan with an inflatable SMB and follow your guide's lead on entry and exit timing.

## 9. Cape Vogel, Milne Bay Province

Cape Vogel juts into the Solomon Sea northeast of Milne Bay and has a series of dramatic wall dives that plunge from the surface to unmeasured depths. The walls are classic PNG: compact with soft corals in red, orange, and purple, sea fans reaching two metres across, and crevices filled with soldierfish and glassy sweepers. Barracuda school in the blue water off the wall at 25 metres. The current here can be significant, making this a drift dive along the wall face at controllable speed. Visibility reaches 30 metres in the dry season. Remote access — typically by liveaboard or chartered vessel from Alotau — keeps diver pressure near zero. Advanced Open Water required.

## 10. Bootless Bay, National Capital District

Bootless Bay, just south of Port Moresby, is the most accessible diving in PNG and often the first experience visiting divers have before heading to more remote areas. The bay has a small collection of wrecks and reef sites within easy boat range. The wreck of the MV Pacific Gas sits in about 12 metres, upright and intact, with substantial fish life including large schools of batfish that hover at the bow. The nearby Sinai reef system has healthy hard coral at 5–15 metres suitable for all certification levels. Visibility in the bay is typically 10–15 metres — less impressive than PNG's offshore sites, but the accessibility is unmatched, and the wreck diving is genuinely rewarding. Year-round diving; a good warm-up before onward travel to Kimbe or Milne Bay.

## Planning Your Papua New Guinea Dive Trip

Port Moresby is the main international gateway, with Air Niugini and other regional carriers providing connections to Lae, Hoskins (for Kimbe Bay), Rabaul, and Alotau (for Milne Bay). A liveaboard is the most efficient way to cover multiple regions; several boats operate in PNG year-round, with itineraries ranging from Kimbe Bay to Milne Bay and the Bismarck Sea. Shore-based resorts at Walindi, Tufi, and Milne Bay offer more comfortable, slower-paced alternatives. The best diving conditions run from May through October, when southeast trade winds bring cleaner water and reduced rainfall. November through April is wetter, with some sites experiencing reduced visibility, though water temperatures are higher and some liveaboard operators find this period excellent for muck-diving sites. All visiting divers require a PNG tourist visa; most nationalities can obtain these on arrival.
