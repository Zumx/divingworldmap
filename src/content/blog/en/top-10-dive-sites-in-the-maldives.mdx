The Maldives is an oceanic country unlike any other on earth — 1,200 islands arranged in 26 atolls across nearly 900 kilometres of the equatorial Indian Ocean, none rising more than a few metres above sea level. The dive geography is equally unusual: channels called kandus cut between the atolls, funnelling enormous volumes of water twice daily on each tide cycle, and it is this relentless movement of plankton-rich water that drives the Maldives' extraordinary marine productivity. Manta rays gather to feed in the channels; hammerhead sharks school on seamounts and corner reefs; whale sharks cruise the outer atolls. The water temperatures fluctuate narrowly — between 26°C and 30°C year-round — and the visibility, when not disturbed by plankton blooms during the southwest monsoon, routinely exceeds 20 metres. The Maldives is a destination that rewards divers at every certification level but its defining experiences are current-driven, pelagic encounters. ## 1. Maaya Thila, North Ari Atoll

Maaya Thila is widely regarded as one of the finest dive sites in the Maldives and among the most celebrated in the Indian Ocean. The thila — a Maldivian term for a submerged reef pinnacle — rises from around 30 metres to its top at six metres below the surface. The site is medium-sized; an experienced diver can circumnavigate it in a single tank. White-tip reef sharks are the main attraction and a reliable one: a resting population of six to ten individuals occupies the site at virtually all times, often lying on sandy ledges at 20–25 metres. At night, white-tips become aggressively active hunters and night dives at Maaya Thila are genuinely dramatic. Napoleon wrasse, grey reef sharks, and large hawksbill turtles complete the roster. Advanced Open Water; current-diving experience recommended.

## 2. Fish Head (Mushimasmingili Thila), North Ari Atoll

Fish Head — also called Maagaa Thila, or more formally Mushimasmingili Thila — is a protected diving area in North Ari Atoll known particularly for its grey reef shark population. A dozen or more grey reefies regularly patrol the top of the thila at 15–25 metres, circling against the current in tightly coordinated formations that suggest territorial behaviour. The site was protected by a special regulation in the 1990s specifically to prevent shark fishing; the population has been stable and growing for decades. The current here is substantial on the push; the best diving is done by descending onto the downcurrent corner and drifting around the reef while watching the sharks in the blue water. Advanced Open Water.

## 3. Banana Reef, North Malé Atoll

Banana Reef is one of the oldest named dive sites in the Maldives — it was among the first spots identified and regularly dived by the pioneering Italian divers who developed Maldivian tourism in the early 1970s. The reef curves in a shallow crescent shape, hence the name, and runs from around five metres at the top to 30 metres on the outer slope. The soft coral coverage on the overhangs and wall sections between 15 and 25 metres is exceptionally dense, and the site's long protection history shows in the size of resident grouper and the boldness of napoleon wrasse. Snapper schools fill the water column above the crest. The site is dived frequently due to its accessibility from Malé and the nearby resort islands; go early in the morning for the quietest experience. Open Water.

## 4. Manta Point (Guraidhoo Corner), South Malé Atoll

Manta Point is a cleaning station on the outer edge of South Malé Atoll where oceanic manta rays — with wingspans up to four metres — gather to be cleaned by wrasse and to feed on the dense plankton that the tidal current concentrates. The site functions differently depending on the current direction: on the outgoing tide, the mantas line up at the cleaning stations at around 15 metres and hover motionless for extended periods, wings spread, while small labrids pick parasites from their gill slits and cephalic fins. On the incoming tide, the mantas circle and feed in the water column, sometimes approaching the surface. Underwater photographers find this site among the most technically demanding due to the combination of current, close subjects, and constantly changing light angles. Open Water; strong finning technique essential.

## 5. Fotteyo Kandu, Felidhoo Atoll

Fotteyo Kandu is a channel in Felidhoo (Vaavu) Atoll, roughly three hours south of Malé by speedboat. The channel cut between two large reef structures accelerates the tide and produces one of the most spectacular drift dives in the country. The walls of the channel are covered in large soft corals and sea fans, and grey reef sharks patrol the corners in numbers that regularly exceed 20 individuals. Hammerhead sharks are sighted on the outside of the channel at depth, particularly in the early morning on calm days. The current can reach several knots and the dive is conducted as a pure drift — enter up-current, move with the flow, exit into the lagoon. Advanced Open Water and current diving experience are required here; the site is not suitable for less experienced divers.

## 6. Hammerhead Point, Rasdhoo Atoll

Rasdhoo is a small atoll to the north of Ari Atoll, easily reached by speedboat from the northern Ari resorts, and its outside corner at Madivaru has developed a reputation as the most reliable hammerhead aggregation site in the Maldives. Scalloped hammerheads school on the deep outer slope and wall between 25 and 40 metres, typically in the early morning before sunrise; operators begin the boat run in darkness to time the dive for first light. Diver buoyancy must be precise as the school is easily disturbed, and remaining stationary below the school rather than swimming toward it produces the best encounters. Water temperature at depth in the cooler months can drop to 24°C. Advanced Open Water; early-morning dive logistics require experience with pre-dawn water entry.

## 7. Lankan Finolhu (Manta Point), North Malé Atoll

Lankan Finolhu is a sandbank just north of Lankanfinolhu Island in North Malé Atoll, and the reef on its southern side is one of the most productive cleaning stations for reef mantas in the country. Unlike the larger oceanic mantas of South Malé, these are resident reef mantas — smaller, with individual markings on their belly that researchers use to identify and track individuals. The site has contributed significantly to manta ray research and population monitoring. Visibility varies with the plankton cycle; on days when the water is green with plankton, the mantas feed in spectacular aggregations but the photographic conditions are challenging. Clear-water days offer extraordinary video opportunities. Open Water.

## 8. Kudarah Thila, South Ari Atoll

Kudarah Thila is a protected site in South Ari Atoll with a distinct two-level structure: a sandy plateau at 20 metres surrounds a deeper section that drops to around 30 metres. The plateau hosts an unusual density of soft coral growth — large Dendronephthya trees in yellow, pink, and red — and the resident fish population includes large numbers of sweetlips, batfish, and the exceptionally photogenic oriental sweetlips, whose polka-dot juvenile stage is often visible on the shallow sections of the reef. Nurse sharks rest on the sandy plateau. Current on this thila can be strong; the optimal dive strategy is to use the deeper section of the reef as a windbreak and work along the coral-covered walls rather than the open plateau. Advanced Open Water.

## 9. Vadhoo Caves, North Malé Atoll

Vadhoo is a small island near the southern entrance to North Malé Atoll with a reef section that features several genuine cave formations cut into the reef limestone at depths between 10 and 22 metres. The caves are not large — the passages are wide and well-lit from both ends — but they concentrate marine life in extraordinary density: large grouper and Napoleon wrasse block the passage interiors, and leopard sharks rest on sand patches within the largest cavern. On an incoming tide, the caves produce a gentle current that draws baitfish inside, and the effect on an afternoon dive is of diving through living walls of fish. Open Water; buoyancy control is important to avoid disturbing the sandy cave floors.

## 10. Mirihi Outside, South Ari Atoll

The outer reef wall running south from Mirihi Island in South Ari Atoll is one of the Maldives' most reliable sites for whale shark encounters. South Ari Atoll has a protected whale shark zone — established in 2009 — covering the outer reefs where these fish aggregate year-round. Unlike most whale shark encounters elsewhere, which are chance events, Mirihi's whale sharks are genuinely resident: juvenile individuals, typically six to eight metres in length, spend extended periods feeding on the zooplankton produced by the atoll's outer reef. Encounters are conducted as in-water swims rather than scuba dives, following MWSRP (Maldives Whale Shark Research Programme) protocols: no touching, maintain distance, avoid flash photography. The reef itself is outstanding — dense table coral at five to 12 metres and a drop-off rich in large pelagics. Open Water.

## Planning Your Maldives Dive Trip

The Maldives has two seasons: the northeast monsoon (December to April) brings calm seas and clear visibility to the northern and central atolls; the southwest monsoon (May to November) reverses the current and is optimal for South Ari's outer atolls and the southern channels. Neither season is unworkable, but the northeast monsoon is the classic 'best conditions' period. Most visitors stay either at island resorts — with house reefs and day trips — or aboard liveaboards that cover multiple atolls in a week. Liveaboards are the only practical way to reach Felidhoo Atoll, the southern atolls, and the outer channels that host the most dramatic current diving. Major international airports in Malé require onward seaplane or speedboat transfers to the resort islands. [Open the map](/map) to compare sites and plan your route through the atolls.
