Travelling with scuba equipment is one of those skills that separates the efficient dive traveller from the person standing at the check-in desk with two overweight bags and a cylinder they cannot take on the plane. The logistics are entirely manageable once you understand the rules that govern travelling with dive gear — the genuine ones, not the myths that circulate in dive shops. Done well, packing your own kit is far more comfortable and economical than renting at your destination; done badly, it turns the first day of your dive holiday into an expensive salvage operation.

## What You Can and Cannot Fly With

The most important starting point is the distinction between what is prohibited and what requires declaration or documentation. Scuba cylinders — whether aluminium or steel — cannot be carried on commercial flights unless they are completely empty, valve removed or open, and certified clean by a technician. An empty cylinder with its valve in place and marked 'empty' is not sufficient for most airlines; the valve must be physically open or removed to satisfy the requirement for demonstrable emptiness. Most dive travellers accept this and rent cylinders at their destination rather than carry their own.

Regulators, BCDs, wetsuits, fins, and dive computers are standard baggage and attract no special attention. Dive knives are carry-on prohibited but checked baggage-legal in most jurisdictions, as are spear tips and pole spear components. Spearguns are treated as firearms by some carriers and require firearms declarations; check the specific carrier's policy and the destination country's import rules before travelling with a speargun at all.

Lithium batteries in dive computers and underwater lights fall under carry-on rules rather than checked baggage rules for spare batteries. Installed batteries — the one actually in the device — can go in checked baggage. Spare lithium cells above 100Wh are restricted or prohibited in checked bags on most carriers, which matters for powerful video lights with large battery packs.

## Airline Weight Allowances and the Reality

Most major carriers allow two checked bags for transatlantic and transpacific itineraries, with individual bag limits of 23kg (50lb) or 32kg (70lb) depending on fare class. This sounds generous until you start weighing a packed dive bag. A full set of recreational dive gear — BCD, regulator set, computer, wetsuit, fins, mask, and ancillary items — typically weighs 12–18kg depending on the wetsuit thickness and fin style. Add a bag of clothes, sunscreen, and normal travel items and you are at or near the limit.

The strategies experienced dive travellers use are straightforward. Wear your heaviest items — boots, thick wetsuit if needed, and heaviest fins — through the airport if the airline weighs carry-on bags too. Pack your BCD bladder fully inflated around your regulator to provide cushioning and fill space efficiently rather than wasting volume. Roll wetsuits tightly; they compress significantly and a 5mm full suit rolled into a cylinder takes far less space than it looks like it should.

For liveaboard trips, where soft bags are strongly preferred or required by the boat's gear storage layout, a dedicated dive duffel with a 120–130-litre capacity and separate compartments for wetsuits and equipment is the standard choice. Hard cases protect equipment better but are heavier and impractical on many liveaboards.

## Protecting Your Equipment in Transit

Regulators are the most expensive and most delicate component in most divers' kit, and the first-stage dust cap is the most important thing to keep on until the regulator enters the water. In transit, second stages should be stored so they cannot be crushed — a folded wetsuit beneath and around the first stage is usually sufficient padding. Regulator bags with internal pockets that separate the first stage from the second stages prevent the second stages from acting as levers against the first stage when the bag is compressed by adjacent luggage.

BCDs are durable but air cells can be punctured by sharp objects in luggage. Inflating the BCD partially before packing provides both cushioning and a diagnostic check — if the bladder has deflated when you arrive, you have found a leak before getting in the water rather than during it. The inflator hose and dump valves are the most vulnerable components; packing the hose tucked inside a fold of the BCD prevents it from being caught and torn.

Dive computers and cameras should travel in carry-on rather than checked luggage whenever possible, both because of the battery restrictions and because the value-to-size ratio of these items makes them poor candidates for checked bag risk.

## Renting Versus Carrying

The calculus on renting versus carrying shifts depending on the destination, trip length, and your attachment to gear that fits. For a one-week trip to a well-equipped dive resort in the Maldives or Caribbean, renting fins, mask, and wetsuit is cost-effective and logistically simple. For a two-week liveaboard in a remote location, or any trip where rental quality is uncertain, carrying your own kit is almost always worth the baggage fees.

The items most worth carrying regardless of trip length are your own mask, dive computer, and regulator. Masks are highly personal and rental masks rarely fit well; even a small face seal leak turns a pleasant dive into a constant clearing exercise. A rental regulator that breathes poorly is a safety issue, not merely a discomfort, and rental computers require learning an unfamiliar interface underwater. Everything else — fins, BCDs, wetsuits — can be rented without significant compromise at most professional operations.

## Managing Rental Gear at the Destination

When you do rent gear, inspect it before entering the water rather than at the surface. Check BCD inflator hoses for cracking at the fitting, check regulator second-stage mouthpieces for tears, and test the BCD bladder holds air by inflating fully and checking for leaks before donning the kit. Rental fins should be sized carefully; a fin that fits poorly increases fatigue and reduces propulsion efficiency.

Return rinsed gear on the same day you dive it. Most rental operations charge replacement fees for equipment returned with salt damage from improper rinsing, and the dive centre's kit will be in better condition for the next group if it has been maintained. Treating rental gear with the same care as your own is both ethical and practical — the quality of rental equipment at a small operation often reflects the care taken by past users.

## Customs and Documentation

Arriving in some countries with significant dive equipment can attract customs attention from officers unfamiliar with the kit. Having receipts or photographs of your equipment stored digitally is useful if questions arise about whether gear was purchased abroad and should attract import duty. Many countries explicitly exempt tourist-use sporting equipment from customs assessment, but having documentation for expensive items — underwater camera housings, video lights, rebreathers — prevents lengthy delays.

Rebreather travellers face the most complex journey: the scrubber material (soda lime) is classified as a hazardous material by most carriers and cannot be transported on commercial flights at all. Scrubber must be sourced at the destination, which requires advance coordination with the liveaboard or dive centre. Gas mixes for rebreather divers similarly require advance arrangement. ## After the Trip

Rinse everything thoroughly in fresh water immediately on return — ideally within hours rather than days. Salt crystals embedded in wetsuits, BCDs, and regulators accelerate material degradation in storage. A BCD bladder should be rinsed internally by introducing fresh water through the oral inflator and slucking it around before draining. Regulators should be rinsed with their first-stage dust cap in place to prevent water intrusion into the first stage, then dried before storage. Wetsuits should be hung in shade rather than direct sun to prevent neoprene degradation from UV exposure. Equipment stored properly after a trip returns the next season in diving-ready condition; equipment dumped in a bag still damp returns with mould, salt crust, and degraded components.
