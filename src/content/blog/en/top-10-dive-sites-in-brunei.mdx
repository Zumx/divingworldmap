Brunei is one of Southeast Asia's most overlooked dive destinations, which is both its disadvantage and its most compelling feature. The small sultanate on the northern coast of Borneo has a coastline of modest length but a continental shelf that drops from shallow mangrove-fringed shores to deep-water sites in relatively short distances, and — crucially — an oil industry that deposited a series of remarkable wrecks on the seafloor during the Second World War. The wrecks off Brunei's coast are among the finest in Southeast Asia, rivalling those of Truk Lagoon and the Philippines in structural integrity and marine growth, yet seeing only a fraction of the diver traffic. Water temperatures sit comfortably between 28°C and 30°C year-round. Visibility averages 10 to 20 metres on the wreck sites, with better conditions in the March to October dry season.

## 1. Champion Wreck, South China Sea

The Champion is a small cargo vessel from the Second World War era that rests in approximately 25 to 33 metres of water in good structural condition. The wreck is mature enough to have developed impressive coral and sponge growth — the superstructure is draped in soft corals, sea fans grow along the railing lines, and the hull plating carries encrusting sponges in orange and yellow. Resident fauna includes large grouper and sweetlips around the bow, and lionfish — both the common Pterois volitans and the smaller devil firefish — inhabit the superstructure and hold entrances. Advanced Open Water certification recommended; strong divers can cover the full wreck in a single dive. The site is a benchmark Brunei experience.

## 2. Australian Wreck, South China Sea

The Australian Wreck — known locally by that name, though its precise identity has been subject to some historical debate — is a larger vessel than the Champion, sitting in depths from around 20 to 38 metres and offering more extensive exploration. The engine room and holds are accessible with appropriate torches and buoyancy skills. The wreck's age and the warm Borneo waters have combined to produce extraordinary biological richness: nudibranchs of multiple species inhabit the encrusted hull sections, schools of batfish hover over the stern, and a resident population of bumphead parrotfish sometimes appears on the upper sections of the hull. This is a wreck for those who want to feel that they are truly exploring rather than following a pre-defined tour.

## 3. Bolkiah Wreck, Offshore Brunei

Named for the sultanate dynasty, the Bolkiah is one of several steel vessels on the Brunei continental shelf whose origins are partially disputed or have been simplified by local naming conventions. Whatever its precise history, it is a substantial wreck in water between 25 and 40 metres, in good condition, with established marine growth. Particularly notable are the feeding aggregations of fish that form above the wreck during tidal transitions — the current sweeping over the hull structure concentrates plankton and small fish, which in turn attract larger predators. Advanced Open Water is the practical minimum; those comfortable with current and depth will get the most from this site.

## 4. Dolphin 88, Offshore Brunei

The Dolphin 88 is a more modern wreck by Brunei standards — a vessel sunk within living memory rather than the Second World War era — but it has become an excellent artificial reef dive. The steel hull is intact and the smaller scale makes it possible to cover the whole wreck in a single dive with time to spare for exploration. Lionfish are prominent, as they are on most Brunei wrecks; the warmer, still sections of the interior are favoured by large sleeping nurse sharks. Depth range is accessible to Advanced Open Water divers and confident Open Water divers with appropriate supervision. Night dives on the Dolphin 88 are particularly productive for macro photography.

## 5. Abana Reef, Brunei Shelf

Moving away from the wrecks, Abana Reef is the most significant natural reef dive in Brunei's accessible waters. The reef structure is built on a rocky bottom on the continental shelf, at depths from around 12 metres down to the shelf edge at 25 metres plus. The coral coverage is mixed — this is not a pristine Indo-Pacific reef in the style of Tubbataha or Raja Ampat — but the fish life is excellent, particularly the larger species that the relatively undived Brunei shelf supports. Bumphead parrotfish, large Napoleon wrasse, and occasional hammerhead sharks have been reported. Current can run on the exposed sections. Open Water certification for the shallower areas; Advanced recommended for the deeper reef slope.

## 6. Empire Wreck (Empire Confidence), South China Sea

Another substantial Second World War era vessel, the Empire Confidence sits in water between 28 and 40 metres and is considered one of Brunei's premier wreck dives by operators who know the site well. The stern section is the most photogenic, with a large propeller and rudder assembly rising to around 25 metres and providing a dramatic structural focal point. The holds contain vehicle components and general cargo that have corroded and become encrusted over the decades. Soft coral density here is among the highest on any Brunei wreck — the nutrient-rich waters of the Borneo continental shelf support biological growth that would not be possible in clearer, more oligotrophic tropical water.

## 7. Semabok Reef, Near Muara

Closer to the main port of Muara than the offshore wreck sites, Semabok Reef is a shallow patch reef on the continental shelf that is used by Brunei operators as a training site and a first dive for visitors unfamiliar with the area. Depth to around 15 metres. The reef has seen some bleaching impact but supports healthy populations of reef fish, sea turtles (green turtles are reliably sighted), and the macro life that the warm Borneo water favours: frogfish, various goby and shrimp partnerships, and nudibranchs. Open Water certification appropriate throughout.

## 8. Seria Wrecks, Western Offshore

The oil-rich western coast around Seria has its own set of offshore structures and wrecks linked to the oil industry. Some of these are active or recently decommissioned platforms; others are older steel structures that have been sunk over decades and are now well colonised. The combination of artificial structure and current creates impressive aggregations of fish, particularly jacks, barracuda schools, and various trevally species. Access to some sites requires coordination with Brunei's oil industry; operators based in Seria and Kuala Belait can arrange appropriate permissions.

## 9. Jerudong Beach Reef, West of Bandar Seri Begawan

Shore diving from Jerudong Beach gives access to a patchy reef system in 10 to 20 metres of water. This is not a spectacular reef by international standards but offers an accessible entry point for early-morning or late-afternoon dives without the logistical overhead of a boat trip. Octopus, various flatfish, and the smaller reef species common to Borneo's inshore waters are the typical fauna. Seahorses have been found in the seagrass sections. The site is best in the dry season when visibility improves; during the Northeast Monsoon from October to February, sediment plumes can reduce visibility substantially.

## 10. Labuan Wrecks (Cross-Border), Off Brunei

Technically in Malaysian federal territory but geographically adjacent to Brunei and frequently combined with Brunei dive trips by operators working from Muara, the Labuan wrecks deserve mention. The Australian Wreck and Blue Water Wreck off Labuan Island are both substantial Second World War vessels in excellent condition at 30 to 33 metres. Brunei-based operators with the appropriate permits can take groups here. The Labuan sites add variety to a Brunei diving itinerary and, for dedicated wreck divers, make the overall northwestern Borneo region one of the most rewarding wreck-diving destinations in Asia.

## Planning a Diving Trip to Brunei

The best conditions run from March through October, when the Northeast Monsoon has passed and the South China Sea calms. November through February can see reduced visibility and occasional heavy rain. The dive infrastructure is modest by regional standards — Brunei has no major dive resort economy and operators are small — but the operators who do run trips to the offshore sites are genuinely knowledgeable and the low diver numbers mean sites are rarely crowded. Equipment hire is available but limited; bringing key personal items (regulator, computer, BCD) is advisable. Most sites require a 20- to 45-minute boat ride from Muara.
