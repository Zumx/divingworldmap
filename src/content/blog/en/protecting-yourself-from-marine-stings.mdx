The overwhelming majority of marine animals want nothing to do with divers. Stings and envenomations are almost never aggression; they are defence, or simple accident — a hand placed on the wrong rock, a fin brushing a tentacle, a careless reach into a crevice. Yet the venoms involved range from a mild itch to genuinely life-threatening, and the correct first-aid response differs dramatically between species. The single most dangerous thing a diver can do is apply the wrong treatment confidently. Understanding which creatures sting, how to avoid them, and what actually helps is core safety knowledge for anyone who dives in warm or temperate seas.

## The Golden Rule: Look, Don't Touch

Nearly every marine sting a diver suffers is preventable, and the prevention is almost embarrassingly simple: maintain good buoyancy, keep your hands to yourself, and never put a body part anywhere you cannot see. The reef is not a handrail. A diver hovering neutrally a metre above the bottom, finning carefully and keeping gauges and hoses clipped close to the body, is at vanishingly low risk from the vast majority of venomous animals, which sting only on contact.

Exposure protection is the second line of defence. Even in warm tropical water, a thin full-length wetsuit or a dive skin guards against jellyfish tentacles, hydroid stings and incidental brushes against fire coral. Many tropical divers who would otherwise dive in shorts and a rash vest choose a full 3mm suit specifically for sting protection. Gloves are a more nuanced choice — useful against stings but discouraged on many fragile reefs because they encourage touching. Where gloves are permitted, they prevent a great many minor stings to the hands.

## Jellyfish and the Box Jellyfish Threat

Jellyfish are the stings divers encounter most often. Most species deliver a painful but harmless welt, but a few are genuinely dangerous. The Australian box jellyfish, found in the coastal waters of northern Australia and across the Indo-Pacific, carries venom potent enough to cause cardiac arrest within minutes. The tiny Irukandji, also of northern Australian and tropical waters, produces a delayed and severe syndrome of pain, hypertension and a sense of impending doom.

For tropical box jellyfish stings, the established first aid is to flood the area liberally with vinegar, which deactivates the undischarged stinging cells before they can fire more venom. This is why vinegar bottles are kept on many tropical Australian beaches and dive boats. Crucially, vinegar is the right response for these box jellyfish but is not universally correct — for some other jellyfish species it can worsen the sting. For most ordinary jellyfish stings, rinse with seawater, not fresh water, carefully remove any visible tentacle with tweezers, and immerse in hot water to ease pain. Never rub the area, as this discharges more stinging cells.

## Fire Coral and Hydroids

Despite the name, fire coral is not a true coral but a colonial hydroid, common across Caribbean and Indo-Pacific reefs as mustard-yellow to brown encrusting or branching growths. A brush against it produces an immediate burning sting and often a raised, itchy welt that can persist for days. Stinging hydroids and feather hydroids deliver a similar, usually milder, sting.

These are contact stings, entirely avoidable with good buoyancy and awareness. If stung, rinse with seawater, apply vinegar to deactivate residual stinging cells, and avoid scratching, which spreads irritation and risks infection. The sting is rarely dangerous but the welts can be genuinely uncomfortable, and a secondary skin infection is the most common complication, so keeping the area clean matters.

## Stonefish, Lionfish and Venomous Spines

The most serious venomous-spine injuries come from the stonefish, a master of camouflage that lies motionless on reefs and sandy bottoms across the Indo-Pacific, looking exactly like an encrusted rock. Stepping on one, or placing a hand on it, drives venom-laden spines into the flesh and produces excruciating pain, swelling, and in severe cases systemic effects. Lionfish, scorpionfish, weeverfish and several rays deliver related spine venoms of varying severity.

The crucial point for all of these venoms is that they are heat-labile — the proteins break down with heat. The correct first aid is hot-water immersion: place the affected limb in water as hot as can be tolerated without scalding, around 45°C, for thirty to ninety minutes, which dramatically reduces the pain and degrades the venom. Do not apply ice, which makes the pain worse. A stonefish sting also warrants urgent medical attention, as antivenom exists and severe envenomations need monitoring. Weeverfish, hidden in the sand of European and Mediterranean beaches, cause the same scenario for waders and shore divers, and respond to the same hot-water treatment.

## Cone Shells and Blue-Ringed Octopus

Two animals deserve special mention because they are beautiful, tempting to pick up, and capable of killing. Cone shells, the elegantly patterned gastropods of tropical reefs, fire a venomous harpoon-like tooth that can deliver a paralysing neurotoxin; the larger fish-hunting species, such as the geographer cone of the Indo-Pacific, have caused human fatalities. The blue-ringed octopus, a small, golf-ball-sized animal of Indo-Pacific waters that flashes iridescent blue rings when agitated, carries tetrodotoxin potent enough to cause respiratory paralysis.

For both, there is no antivenom and no field treatment beyond life support. The prevention is absolute and simple: never pick up cone shells, never handle small octopuses, and resist the universal temptation to collect pretty shells from the reef. These envenomations are medical emergencies requiring immediate evacuation, rescue breathing if breathing stops, and hospital care. They are also, in practice, almost entirely avoidable by the diver who keeps their hands off the wildlife.

## Building the Right First-Aid Knowledge

The pattern across all marine stings is that the correct response is species-specific, and getting it wrong can worsen the injury. The two great heuristics worth memorising are: vinegar for tropical box-jellyfish-type stings, and hot water for venomous-spine injuries like stonefish, lionfish and weeverfish. For anything causing systemic symptoms — difficulty breathing, chest pain, collapse, a spreading numbness — the response is the same regardless of species: treat it as a medical emergency, summon help and evacuate. Carrying a small first-aid kit with vinegar, tweezers and a means of heating water, and diving with the knowledge of what lives in the local water, turns most marine stings from a frightening event into a manageable inconvenience.
