Western Australia spans some 12,500 kilometres of coastline, from tropical coral in the north to cool, kelp-fringed temperate reefs in the south, giving its diving an extraordinary range. The Ningaloo Reef, a rare fringing reef that meets the shore, draws whale sharks and manta rays; the remote Rowley Shoals offer some of the most pristine coral diving in the country; and around Perth, the limestone reefs and historic wrecks of Rottnest Island deliver world-class temperate diving. Water temperature swings from a warm 28°C off the Pilbara to a cool 18°C around Perth in winter, so exposure protection varies hugely with latitude. The best season depends entirely on where you point your fins.

## 1. Ningaloo Reef Whale Sharks, Exmouth

Ningaloo, fringing the North West Cape near Exmouth, is one of the few places on Earth where whale sharks aggregate predictably and can be swum with under licence. From around March to July, after the mass coral spawning, these gentle giants — the largest fish in the sea, reaching 12 metres — cruise the surface to feed on plankton. The encounters are by snorkel rather than scuba, strictly regulated to protect the animals. The reef itself, accessible directly from the beach at sites like Turquoise Bay, offers superb coral, turtles and reef fish in shallow, clear water ideal for all divers.

## 2. Navy Pier, Exmouth

Navy Pier near Exmouth is consistently ranked among the best shore dives in the world. Beneath a working naval jetty, the pilings at 4 to 16 metres are encrusted with sponge and coral and crowded with an astonishing density of life: grey nurse sharks, wobbegongs, vast schools of trevally and batfish, moray eels, lionfish, nudibranchs and the resident giant Queensland grouper. Because it sits on military land, diving is by guided arrangement only and tides dictate timing. The combination of macro life, big fish and dramatic structure in a single shore dive makes it utterly unique.

## 3. Rowley Shoals

The Rowley Shoals are three coral atolls some 260 kilometres off the Kimberley coast near Broome, reachable only by liveaboard during a short season from September to December. These pristine reefs, almost untouched by humans, offer staggering visibility often beyond 40 metres, vibrant coral, and exhilarating drift dives through the channels where tidal currents rip across the lagoon edges. Potato cod, Maori wrasse, reef sharks, manta rays and vast schools of fish populate the walls and passes. The Rowleys are widely considered the finest coral diving in Western Australia, a true remote-wilderness destination.

## 4. HMAS Swan, Dunsborough

The HMAS Swan, a 113-metre former naval destroyer escort, was scuttled in Geographe Bay off Dunsborough in 1997 to create one of Australia's premier wreck dives. Sitting upright in 30 metres, the ship is largely intact and accessible, with multiple penetration routes through the bridge, engine room and corridors for trained wreck divers. It has become an artificial reef draped in soft coral and home to schooling fish, nudibranchs and the occasional larger visitor. Depths range from 6 metres at the superstructure to 30 at the seabed, suiting Advanced divers and offering progressive challenge.

## 5. Rottnest Island Wrecks and Reefs

Rottnest Island, a short ferry from Perth, sits amid a graveyard of historic shipwrecks and limestone reef in cool, clear water. Sites around the island feature swim-throughs, caves and ledges at 5 to 30 metres, with the warm Leeuwin Current carrying tropical species south to mingle with temperate fish. Wrecks scattered around the island add historical interest, while the reefs host crayfish, wobbegongs, leafy sea dragons and a rich invertebrate life. Visibility is excellent in summer and autumn, and the variety of sites makes Rottnest the cornerstone of diving near Perth.

## 6. Busselton Jetty

Busselton Jetty, the longest timber-piled jetty in the Southern Hemisphere at almost two kilometres, is a celebrated temperate dive in Geographe Bay. The pilings, in 3 to 8 metres of water, have become an artificial reef cloaked in soft coral, sponge and ascidians, supporting an extraordinary fish and invertebrate community for such shallow water. An underwater observatory at the jetty's end lets non-divers glimpse the marine life. The shallow, sheltered conditions make it ideal for newer divers, photographers and night dives, and the sheer length of the structure offers seemingly endless exploration.

## 7. Coral Bay, Ningaloo

Coral Bay, at the southern end of Ningaloo Reef, offers some of the most accessible reef diving in the state, with healthy coral gardens reachable directly from the beach or by short boat ride. Reef sharks, turtles, rays and a rich cast of reef fish populate the shallow, clear water, and manta rays are present year-round at nearby cleaning stations. The sheltered bay and gentle conditions make Coral Bay perfect for beginners and families, while still offering the same whale shark and manta encounters that make Ningaloo famous, away from the busier Exmouth side.

## 8. Marmion Marine Park, Perth

Marmion Marine Park, just off Perth's northern beaches, was Western Australia's first marine park and offers convenient, varied diving on limestone reef close to the city. Sites feature caves, ledges and swim-throughs at 5 to 20 metres, with seagrass meadows, kelp and a temperate cast of wobbegongs, crayfish, octopus and reef fish. The warm Leeuwin Current occasionally brings tropical strays. Conditions are calmest in summer, when visibility improves and the swell drops. As an accessible, protected area within reach of the city, Marmion is a favourite for local divers and training.

## 9. Geographe Bay and the South West

Geographe Bay, sheltering the towns of Busselton and Dunsborough, is the heart of diving in the cooler South West, combining the HMAS Swan and Busselton Jetty with reef and seagrass habitats rich in temperate life. The bay's protected waters host leafy sea dragons, the iconic and elusive creatures that make the region a draw for macro photographers, along with rays, wobbegongs and seasonal fish aggregations. The Leeuwin Current's influence brings surprising diversity. Calm summer conditions and a range of sites from shallow jetty to deep wreck make the South West a complete diving region in its own right.

## 10. Albany Wrecks, South Coast

Albany, on Western Australia's wild south coast, is home to the HMAS Perth, a former guided-missile destroyer scuttled in King George Sound in 2001 as an artificial reef and dive attraction. The 130-metre wreck sits in around 35 metres of cool, clear water, drawing schooling fish, rays and a growing community of marine life on its sponge-encrusted hull. The cooler southern water and greater depth suit experienced divers, and the dramatic granite coastline above adds to the sense of adventure. Albany rounds out WA's diving with a southern, temperate wreck experience far from the tropical north.
