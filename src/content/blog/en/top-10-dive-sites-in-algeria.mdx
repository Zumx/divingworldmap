Drop into the water off Sidi Fredj on a calm June morning and the first thing you notice is the silence. The Algerian coast runs for roughly 1,200 kilometres along the southern Mediterranean, yet it sees a fraction of the dive traffic that crowds the northern shore. Visibility regularly reaches 20 to 30 metres in summer, the water warms to around 24°C by August, and the reefs still hold the dusky groupers and red coral that decades of pressure have thinned elsewhere. This is one of the last genuinely quiet corners of the Mediterranean, and the diving rewards anyone willing to look past its reputation.

## Sidi Fredj and the Algiers Coast

The marina at Sidi Fredj (the old French Sidi Ferruch) is the natural base for diving near the capital. The headland shelters a string of rocky reefs that begin in the shallows and step down to 25 or 30 metres, where boulder fields and short walls are draped in yellow gorgonians and patches of red coral. Octopus tuck into every crevice, moray eels watch from the rock, and schools of bream and saddled seabream drift over the sand. The proximity to Algiers means you can dive a fresh site in the morning and be back in the city for lunch. Spring and autumn bring the clearest water; summer is busiest but still uncrowded by Mediterranean standards.

## The Roman Ruins of Tipaza

Tipaza, about 70 kilometres west of Algiers, is the single most evocative dive on this coast. The Roman and Phoenician port left a submerged archaeological field just offshore, and divers glide over the remains of harbour works, fallen columns and scattered amphorae lying among the posidonia seagrass in 5 to 15 metres of water. The UNESCO-listed ruins on land continue beneath the surface, so a dive here is as much history as marine life. Keep your fins high off the bottom: the seagrass meadows are protected nurseries, and the silt clouds easily. The shallow depth makes this an ideal site for less experienced divers and for long, contemplative second dives.

## Bou Ismail Bay

The broad sweep of Bou Ismail Bay, between Algiers and Tipaza, gives shelter when the open coast is exposed to north winds. Several dive operators in the bay run trips to reefs that combine sandy plains with rocky outcrops at 12 to 20 metres. It is a forgiving training ground, with gentle currents and good light, and a reliable place to find cuttlefish, peacock wrasse and the occasional stingray on the sand. The bay is also where many local clubs run their open-water courses, so the infrastructure is solid and the sites are well known.

## Diving with Deca Diving Club

Algeria's recreational scene runs on a network of clubs rather than glossy resort centres, and joining one is the easiest way to reach the better reefs. The Deca diving club is among the established operations on the central coast, organising guided boat dives, training and equipment hire for visitors who arrive without their own gear. Booking through a club also smooths the practical side: Algeria requires a bit more paperwork than most Mediterranean destinations, and local guides know which sites are open, which need permits, and where the conditions will be best on any given day. Treat them as your first point of contact rather than an afterthought.

## Bejaia and Gouraya National Park

East of Algiers, the coastline turns dramatic. Bejaia sits beneath the cliffs of Gouraya National Park, where the mountains plunge straight into the sea and form steep underwater walls. Cap Carbon and the surrounding capes offer drop-offs that fall well past recreational depth, with caves and overhangs sheltering groupers, lobster and red coral on the deeper ledges. Visibility here is often superb, and the scenery above water is as much a draw as the diving below it. This is more committing terrain than the Algiers reefs, suited to divers comfortable with depth and the occasional current.

## The Habibas Islands off Oran

Far to the west, off Oran, the Habibas Islands (Îles Habibas) form a small marine reserve that many regard as Algeria's finest diving. The protected status has allowed marine life to recover, and the granite reefs around the islands hold large dusky groupers, dense gorgonian fields and some of the healthiest red coral on the coast. The islands sit in clear, exposed water, so trips depend on weather and are usually run as full-day boat excursions from Oran. Because it is a reserve, access can be regulated; go with a licensed operator who can arrange permission. For many divers, the Habibas alone justify the journey.

## El Kala and Taza National Park

At the far eastern end of the country, near the Tunisian border, the El Kala region and the nearby Taza National Park protect a stretch of coast where Mediterranean reefs meet coastal lagoons and wetlands. The rocky points here see very few divers, and the reefs feel genuinely wild, with posidonia meadows, rocky reef fish and clear summer water. Annaba, the nearest large city, provides the logistics. This corner of Algeria is for the explorer rather than the box-ticker: expect to do some organising, and expect to have the sites largely to yourself.

## Marine Life, Seasons and Practicalities

Algeria's underwater cast is classic western Mediterranean: dusky and brown groupers, octopus, moray and conger eels, scorpionfish, seabream, the protected red coral and the vast posidonia meadows that anchor the whole ecosystem. Loggerhead turtles pass through, and dolphins are a common boat-trip bonus. The season runs roughly from May to October; July and August are warmest and calmest, while spring and autumn give the clearest water and fewest people. Water temperatures range from around 16°C in spring to 24°C in late summer, so a 5mm wetsuit covers most diving. Bring documentation, dive through a recognised club, and respect the protected zones and the seagrass. The reward is Mediterranean diving as it once was, almost to yourself.
