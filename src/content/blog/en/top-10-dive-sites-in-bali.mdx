Bali packs a remarkable variety of diving into a single small island, because it sits on the edge of the Coral Triangle where deep, cold currents from the Indian Ocean meet warm reef water. The northeast coast around Tulamben and Amed is famous for shallow muck and an iconic wreck reachable from the beach. The Nusa islands to the southeast funnel powerful, chilly currents that draw oceanic sunfish and reef mantas. Reef temperatures range from a warm 28°C in the north to a startling 18°C or lower on the Penida thermoclines, so exposure protection and current experience matter as much as where you choose to dive. Visibility swings from 5 metres in muck to over 30 metres on the channels.

## 1. USAT Liberty Wreck, Tulamben

The Liberty is one of the most accessible and beloved wreck dives on Earth. The US Army cargo ship was torpedoed in 1942, beached at Tulamben, and finally slid into the sea in the 1963 eruption of Mount Agung. It now lies parallel to shore in 5 to 30 metres, its 120-metre hull broken into sections and smothered in gorgonians, soft corals and sponges. Divers wade in directly from the black-sand beach. Bumphead parrotfish school over the wreck at dawn, garden eels carpet the surrounding sand, and the macro life on the hull is exceptional. Open Water divers can enjoy the shallower sections; the deeper stern suits Advanced.

## 2. Tulamben Drop-Off

A short walk south of the Liberty, the Tulamben Drop-Off is a near-vertical wall descending past 60 metres into the blue. The shallows are a riot of hard and soft coral, while the wall holds gorgonian fans, black coral and a steady traffic of reef fish. This is a classic spot for night diving, when Spanish dancers, decorator crabs and hunting cephalopods emerge. The site is reachable from shore, with minimal current on most days, making it a relaxed dive for all levels. Look for the resident school of jacks that often gathers along the upper wall.

## 3. Manta Point, Nusa Penida

Manta Point off Nusa Penida's rugged south coast is one of the most reliable reef manta sites in Indonesia. A cleaning station and feeding area at 5 to 15 metres draws mantas with wingspans of three metres or more, sometimes a dozen or more circling at once. The water here is cooler and the swell can be significant, with surge moving divers back and forth, so the site favours those comfortable in moving water. Mantas are present year-round, though numbers peak in the wet season from December to March. The dramatic cliffs above add to the sense of wild remoteness.

## 4. Crystal Bay, Nusa Penida

Crystal Bay is the most famous place in Bali to encounter the ocean sunfish — the mola mola — which rises from deep water to be cleaned by reef fish, most reliably from July to October. The bay's clear water and coral-fringed slope are beautiful in their own right, but the cold, fast current that brings the mola can be challenging and unpredictable. Water temperature can plunge below 20°C on the thermocline. The site is for experienced divers who can manage downcurrent. A small pinnacle in the bay's centre and a cavern on its edge add interest beyond the headline animal.

## 5. Seraya Secrets, near Tulamben

Seraya Secrets is Bali's premier muck-diving site, a gentle black-sand slope where the appeal is entirely in the small and the strange. At 5 to 20 metres, divers hunt for harlequin shrimp, frogfish, mimic octopus, ghost pipefish, mantis shrimp and a long list of nudibranchs. The site is shore-accessible and almost always calm, perfect for long, slow dives with a sharp eye and a good guide. Photographers return again and again. The reef holds little large structure — the reward here is the parade of rare critters living in the volcanic sand.

## 6. Amed and Jemeluk Bay

Amed, strung along the northeast coast, is a relaxed dive village with a series of shore-accessible reefs and a small Japanese-era wreck. Jemeluk Bay offers a coral-covered slope and wall reaching around 40 metres, with healthy hard coral in the shallows and pelagic visitors at the edge. A coral-restoration project and an underwater postbox add quirky interest. Conditions are generally calm and the visibility good, making Amed an ideal base for newer divers and for those wanting easy, repeated shore dives along a coast dotted with traditional jukung fishing boats.

## 7. Blue Lagoon, Padangbai

Blue Lagoon, near the ferry port of Padangbai in the southeast, is a sheltered bay with a sandy bottom and scattered coral bommies at 3 to 18 metres. It is a favourite for training and check-out dives, but it also rewards experienced divers with a rich cast of macro and reef life: blue-ringed octopus, leaf scorpionfish, crocodilefish and frequent turtles. Reef sharks sometimes patrol the bay's outer edge. The calm, accessible nature of the site and its reliable marine life make it one of the most consistent dives in the southeast corner.

## 8. Gili Tepekong, Candidasa

Off Candidasa, the rocky islet of Gili Tepekong delivers some of Bali's most exhilarating advanced diving. Strong, unpredictable currents and a cold thermocline make the site demanding, but the rewards include white-tip and grey reef sharks, mola mola in season and big schools of pelagics sweeping the channel. A cavern known as The Toilet, where surge pushes divers up and down, is the site's notorious signature. This is strictly a dive for experienced visitors who can read and manage current; it is not a place for relaxed reef cruising.

## 9. Menjangan Island

In the far northwest, within West Bali National Park, Menjangan Island offers Bali's clearest water and calmest conditions. Its walls and coral gardens at 5 to 40 metres are draped in gorgonians and soft coral, with visibility regularly beyond 30 metres. A small anchor wreck adds historical interest. The protected, current-free reefs are ideal for relaxed diving, underwater photography and longer bottom times, and the contrast with the cold-current sites of the southeast could hardly be greater. Turtles, reef fish and the occasional reef shark complete the picture.

## 10. Nusa Lembongan and Mangrove Point

Off Nusa Lembongan, Mangrove Point is a sloping reef with healthy coral and brisk drift diving along the island's northeast edge. Divers fly along the wall in clear water carried by the same currents that feed Penida's mantas and sunfish, passing turtles, reef sharks and dense schools of fusiliers. The site can be done as a relaxed drift on a gentle tide or a fast, exhilarating ride on a strong one, so timing and a good operator matter. Lembongan also serves as a quieter base than the busy south for exploring the wider Nusa archipelago.
