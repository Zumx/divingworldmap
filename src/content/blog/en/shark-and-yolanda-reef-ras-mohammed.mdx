At the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula, where the Gulf of Suez and the Gulf of Aqaba meet the open Red Sea, the reefs of Ras Mohammed National Park drop into some of the bluest, clearest water in the world. The classic dive here links two submerged pinnacles — Shark Reef and Yolanda Reef — in a single drift that combines a sheer plankton-fed wall, schooling fish in their thousands, and one of the strangest wreck sites anywhere: a scatter of toilets and bathtubs on the seabed, the cargo of a freighter called the Yolanda. It is, by common consent, one of the finest dives in the Egyptian Red Sea.

## Ras Mohammed and Why It Is So Rich

Ras Mohammed was one of Egypt's first marine protected areas, established to safeguard the reefs at the very tip of Sinai. The headland sits where deep water comes close to shore and strong currents sweep nutrient-rich water up the reef faces, fuelling an explosion of life. The result is some of the densest fish populations in the northern Red Sea, set against walls of hard and soft coral in extraordinary visibility that routinely exceeds 30 metres.

The park's position also makes it a crossroads for open-water species. Pelagic fish move through on the current, and the combination of sheer walls, abundant baitfish and clear water creates the conditions for the big aggregations the site is famous for. Most divers reach it on day boats from Sharm el-Sheikh, a short run around the headland, or as part of a northern Red Sea liveaboard itinerary.

## Shark Reef: The Wall and the Schools

Shark Reef is the first of the two pinnacles, a near-vertical wall that plunges into deep blue water. The dive usually begins here, dropping in and drifting along the wall with the reef on one shoulder and the open sea on the other. The wall itself is encrusted with coral, but the spectacle is in the water column: vast schools of snapper and barracuda hang in formation, batfish drift in curious groups, and in the summer months the famous aggregations of bohar snapper and big-eye trevally mass in their thousands ahead of spawning.

The name promises sharks, and in season grey reef sharks patrol the deeper water off the wall, with the occasional thresher, hammerhead or whitetip passing through. They are most reliably seen in the warmer months when the schooling fish concentrate. The current along the wall does the work of the dive, carrying divers effortlessly toward the second pinnacle.

## Yolanda Reef and Its Surreal Cargo

The drift continues to Yolanda Reef, named for the MV Yolanda, a Cypriot freighter that ran onto the reef in 1980 and eventually slid into the depths. The main hull is now far too deep for recreational divers — it lies beyond technical limits down the slope — but the ship's cargo spilled across the reef shelf as she broke up, and that cargo is the attraction. Scattered across the seabed in recreational depths is the freight she was carrying: toilets, bathtubs, sinks and bathroom fittings, lying improbably among the coral.

The sight of a neat row of toilets on a coral seabed in the middle of the Red Sea is one of diving's great absurdities, and it has made the site unmistakable. Among the bathroom fittings the captain's BMW car and other cargo were also recorded over the years. Marine life has colonised the wreckage, and the contrast between the surreal man-made litter and the surrounding reef is the lasting memory most divers take away.

## Diving the Drift

The standard dive is a drift: divers descend at Shark Reef, ride the current along the wall, cross the sandy saddle between the two pinnacles, and finish among the Yolanda cargo before ascending. The current can be strong and is the defining feature of the dive — it determines the direction, the route and the difficulty. On a stronger day the dive is suited to experienced divers comfortable with drift and depth; on a calm day it is more forgiving.

Because the wall drops away into deep water, depth discipline matters. The schooling fish and the sharks tend to be deeper, and the temptation to chase them down the blue is real. Good buoyancy, attention to the dive computer and a guide who knows how to read the current are what turn this into a safe, world-class dive rather than a risky one.

## Beyond the Reef: Wrecks and the Wider Park

Ras Mohammed sits within reach of some of the Red Sea's most celebrated dives. The SS Thistlegorm, a World War Two British freighter sunk with a cargo of trucks, motorcycles and munitions, lies in the Strait of Gubal not far away and is among the world's most famous wreck dives. Closer reefs like Shag Rock and the dive sites around the strait fill out a northern Red Sea itinerary, and liveaboards routinely combine Ras Mohammed, the Thistlegorm and the surrounding reefs in a single trip.

Further south, the Egyptian Red Sea unfolds into a string of celebrated regions and house reefs — from the southern parks down toward the Sudanese border at sites associated with areas like Wadi Lahami, Zabargad and the Lahami Bay reefs, where operations such as Barakuda Lahami Bay and the Red Sea Diving Safari camps base their diving, along with house reefs at Hamada, Yatouche and beyond. Ras Mohammed remains the headline of the north.

## Conditions and Season

Diving at Ras Mohammed is good year-round, but the experience shifts with the seasons. Summer, roughly June to September, brings the warmest water — into the high 20s Celsius — and the great spawning aggregations of snapper and trevally that draw divers from around the world, along with the most reliable shark activity. Visibility is excellent throughout the year, but the big-fish spectacle peaks in the warm months.

Winter brings cooler water, in the low 20s Celsius, and a calmer reef, with fewer of the massed schools but still superb wall diving and the ever-present Yolanda cargo. Whenever you go, the combination of the wall, the fish and the surreal wreckage makes Shark and Yolanda Reef a dive that lingers in the memory.
