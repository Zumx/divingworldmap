Finland is a country defined by water — tens of thousands of lakes, a long Baltic coastline, and a diver culture that embraces cold, dark, brackish conditions with characteristic Nordic pragmatism. The dive sites here divide neatly into three categories: the flooded industrial sites like the Ojamo limestone mine, which offer clear inland diving in a spectacular submerged landscape; the Baltic Sea wrecks, which rival Estonia's in their extraordinary preservation due to the low-salinity, low-oxygen water; and the lake and coastal sites that attract local clubs and training operations year-round. The famous Vrouw Maria wreck — an 18th-century Dutch merchant vessel that sank in 1771 with a cargo of Dutch Old Masters paintings destined for Catherine the Great — is the most historically significant wreck in Finnish waters, though its protected status and depth in excess of 40 metres makes it a technical diving objective. Water temperatures in the Baltic range from just above freezing in winter to 18–20°C at the surface in summer; lakes can reach 22°C in July. Drysuits are standard equipment for all but summer surface-layer diving.

## 1. Ojamo Mine (Ojamon Kaivoslampi)

The flooded limestone quarry at Ojamo near Lohja is the most celebrated inland dive site in Finland. Mining ceased and drainage stopped, revealing an extraordinary submerged landscape of mining tunnels, ventilation shafts, and quarry chambers. The water clarity is exceptional — visibility regularly reaches 20 metres or more in the clear limestone-filtered water — and the pale rock walls create a bright, otherworldly atmosphere quite unlike the green-tinted Baltic. Maximum depth in the accessible sections reaches around 30 metres. The site operates as a managed dive park with surface facilities, and courses including drysuit training are conducted here regularly. Advanced Open Water recommended for deeper tunnels; drysuit strongly advised.

## 2. Vrouw Maria Wreck Site

The 18th-century Dutch brigantine Vrouw Maria sank in the Turku Archipelago on 9 October 1771 during a storm, taking her cargo of artworks and luxury goods destined for the Russian imperial court to the bottom. The wreck lies in approximately 41 metres and was discovered in 1999; it is protected under Finnish maritime archaeology legislation, meaning diving near the hull requires authorisation. For qualified technical divers who obtain permits, the sight of an intact 250-year-old wooden sailing vessel standing upright on the seafloor with its masts still in place is extraordinary. The Baltic's cold, low-oxygen conditions have preserved the ship in a state that would be impossible in warmer waters.

## 3. Hanko Wrecks, Gulf of Finland

The port town of Hanko at the southwestern tip of Finland was a critical shipping waypoint for centuries, and the surrounding waters contain a rich accumulation of wrecks from different eras. The most frequently dived lie in 15–35 metres off the Hanko peninsula, including preserved cargo vessels from the 19th century and several vessels from the Winter War and Continuation War of the early 1940s. The clarity of the outer Finnish Gulf waters around Hanko is often better than at inland Baltic sites. Advanced Open Water for the deeper sites; Open Water divers can access the shallower debris and rocky reef sections. Hanko-based dive clubs run regular excursions.

## 4. Iso-Melkuttimen Sukellustukikohta, Archipelago Sea

One of several dedicated dive bases in the Turku Archipelago, Iso-Melkuttimen provides access to the shallow reefs and rocky drop-offs of the outer archipelago. The archipelago is characterised by thousands of granite islands separated by channels that funnel tidal flow, creating current-swept reefs with dense populations of blue mussels, sea stars, and the Baltic's characteristic encrusting organisms. The diving is atmospheric rather than spectacularly colourful, but the granite topography is dramatic. Drysuit necessary; Open Water level adequate for most sites. The base offers accommodation and equipment support, making multi-day archipelago diving practical.

## 5. Turku Archipelago Wrecks

Beyond the Vrouw Maria, the Turku Archipelago contains dozens of documented wrecks ranging from medieval cogs to 20th-century steamships. Several lie in diveable recreational depths — 15 to 35 metres — including preserved 19th-century wooden hulls whose integrity after a century or more underwater demonstrates the preserving properties of the Baltic. The archipelago's maze of islands creates navigational complexity that rewards divers who invest time in local knowledge through a Turku-based club or operator. Advanced Open Water for the majority of sites. The wreck density here is arguably greater per square kilometre than anywhere else in Nordic waters.

## 6. Pellinki Island, Gulf of Finland

A large island in the eastern Gulf of Finland near Porvoo, Pellinki offers accessible coastal diving on granite reef and boulder structures with moderate depths of 10–20 metres. The site is popular with the Helsinki diving community for weekend trips and is used for drysuit training. Marine life is characteristic Baltic: perch and pike in the shallower areas, eel sightings in the deeper rocky sections, and occasional grey seal interactions in the outer waters. Open Water with drysuit adequate for most sections. The island has a handful of summer cottages and a small community; arrive by ferry from Porvoo.

## 7. Lake Päijänne — Vääksy Canal Zone

Finland's second-largest lake provides freshwater diving with conditions that vary dramatically between the clear, deep central basin and the productive but lower-visibility shallows near river mouths. The canal area at Vääksy where Lake Päijänne connects to the Vesijärvi system sees strong currents in spring and autumn that concentrate fish and create interesting drift dives. Pike exceeding a metre are resident in the weed-beds, and large perch school in the deeper areas. Summer visibility in the clear central lake can reach 5–8 metres. Thick wetsuit adequate in summer; drysuit necessary from September onwards. Accessible year-round with the right thermal protection.

## 8. Suomenlinna Sea Fortress Ruins

The UNESCO-listed Suomenlinna fortress outside Helsinki extends underwater — the submerged remnants of the 18th-century fortifications including cannon emplacements, stone walls, and anchor chains are scattered in 5–15 metres of water around the islands. The site is unusual in that it combines archaeological interest with accessible depths and straightforward boat access from Helsinki's market square. Visibility varies from 3 to 8 metres depending on season and weather. The shallowness and historical interest make this a good urban dive for those with limited days in the capital. Open Water with drysuit; night dives here are atmospheric.

## 9. Åland Islands Wrecks

The Åland archipelago, a Swedish-speaking autonomous region of Finland between the Finnish and Swedish mainlands, holds some of the most significant wrecks in the entire Baltic. The archipelago sits on the main historical shipping lane between Stockholm and St Petersburg, and the combination of rocks, shoals, and foul weather has sunk hundreds of vessels over centuries. The most famous is the champagne ship — the Jönköping, a German cargo vessel sunk in 1916 whose cargo of unopened champagne bottles in remarkable condition was recovered by salvors in the 1990s. Other wrecks in diveable depths offer impressive preserved structures. Advanced Open Water minimum; technical qualifications open access to the most historically significant deeper hulls.

## 10. Kotka Outer Islands, Gulf of Finland

The outermost islands off the port city of Kotka in southeastern Finland face the open Gulf of Finland and experience stronger currents and better visibility than sheltered inner areas. The rocky reefs here carry dense populations of bladder wrack and sea lettuce, and the associated fish populations include substantial perch, bream, and the occasional burbot in deeper rocky crevices. The main attraction for dedicated wreck divers is the cluster of Second World War vessels — Finnish, Soviet, and German — in the outer Gulf, some in Advanced Open Water depth ranges. Kotka has an active diving community and several clubs offer guided trips to the outer islands.

## Practical Information

Finnish Baltic diving is best from June through August, when surface temperatures permit longer dives and the long summer daylight extends the practical dive window. Winter ice diving is practiced extensively at inland lakes including Ojamo — courses are available and the experience of descending through a hole in the ice into a frozen landscape is one of diving's more memorable variations. Drysuit proficiency is not optional in Finnish waters; plan to complete drysuit training before arrival or in the first days of your trip through one of the Helsinki or Turku clubs.
