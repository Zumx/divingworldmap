Costa Rica occupies a narrow isthmus where two oceans meet, and the diving on each coast tells a completely different story. The Pacific side is where the serious pelagic action happens: cold, nutrient-dense upwellings sustain aggregations of scalloped hammerhead sharks, whale sharks, manta rays, marlin and dolphin schools in densities that make it one of the top destinations in the world for open-water pelagic encounters. The Caribbean coast is calmer and warmer, with a more conventional coral-reef character, but sees less organised dive infrastructure. Pacific diving ranges from the accessible sites around the Nicoya Peninsula to the remote offshore pinnacles of Cocos Island, 550 kilometres out into the open Pacific. Water temperature varies: the Caribbean holds steady at 27–29°C, while Pacific upwelling sites can drop to 18°C. Advanced Open Water certification is recommended for most serious Pacific diving.

## 1. Cocos Island, Pacific Ocean

Cocos Island is perhaps the finest pelagic dive destination in the world, a UNESCO World Heritage Site lying 550 kilometres southwest of the Nicoya Peninsula. The island's submarine topography — seamounts, cleaning stations, and current-swept pinnacles — concentrates schooling hammerheads in columns of hundreds, whale sharks year-round, marlin, tuna, bottlenose dolphins and manta rays. Signature sites include Dirty Rock, Manuelita, and the Dos Amigos Grandes seamounts. Depths range from 15 to 40 metres at most sites; currents can be powerful and unpredictable. Cocos is accessible only by liveaboard from the mainland port of Puntarenas, with crossings taking 36 hours. This is expert-level diving — Advanced Open Water minimum, with significant drift and current experience strongly expected. The season runs year-round, with June through December particularly productive.

## 2. Bat Islands (Islas Murciélagos), Guanacaste

The Bat Islands are a cluster of rocky islets off the northern Guanacaste coast in Santa Rosa National Park, accessible from Playas del Coco or Tamarindo by boat. The signature dive is an encounter with Pacific bull sharks — large, inquisitive individuals that inhabit the waters around the islands, particularly in the June-to-December season when the warm-water layer retreats and upwelling brings cooler, productive water. Depths run to around 25 metres over rock and sand; visibility varies with season, often 10 to 20 metres. There are no permanent mooring lines, and surface conditions can be challenging. Advanced Open Water is recommended. Manta rays also pass through in the wet season, and the rock walls are covered in orange and white encrusting organisms characteristic of high-nutrient Pacific waters.

## 3. Catalina Islands (Islas Catalinas), Guanacaste

The Catalina Islands are the most accessible major dive site on the northern Pacific coast, two hours by boat from the Papagayo Gulf dive centres. The islands' current-swept points host schools of spotted eagle rays — particularly large groups from January through April when the aggregations can number dozens of individuals — along with white tip reef sharks resting on sand between the rocks, schools of snappers and occasional Mobula rays during the right season. Depths at the main sites run 10 to 25 metres. Visibility averages 10 to 20 metres, with the clearest conditions in the dry season from December through April. Open Water certification covers most of the Catalina sites, though current at the points warrants some drift experience.

## 4. Caño Island Biological Reserve

Caño Island sits roughly 20 kilometres off the Osa Peninsula and is a protected biological reserve — fishing is prohibited in the surrounding waters, and this protection has allowed the fish biomass to recover to impressive levels. Hammerhead sharks are present year-round here, along with white tips, bull sharks, whale sharks during their transit season, schools of jacks, and large moray eels. The coral formations are modest by Caribbean standards but the fish diversity is excellent. Several dive sites run along the island's western face at 10 to 25 metres. Visibility averages 10 to 15 metres, occasionally better in the dry season. Boat transfers run from Drake Bay or Sierpe. Open Water certification is sufficient; Advanced preparation is useful for the deeper cleaning stations.

## 5. Gulf of Papagayo, Guanacaste

The Gulf of Papagayo in northern Guanacaste is the hub of Costa Rica's Pacific dive industry, with Playas del Coco, Playa Hermosa and Playa Ocotal hosting multiple operators running daily boats to the nearby islands and seamounts. The sites here are appropriate for all certification levels — shallow rocky reefs at 8 to 15 metres with garden eels, moray eels and dense schools of fish provide solid diving for Open Water graduates, while the deeper pinnacles and current zones around the gulf mouth are reserved for Advanced divers. Octopus, sea turtles and occasional dolphins are regular sightings. The dry season from December to April brings the best visibility and calmest surface conditions. Water temperature ranges from 22°C in the upwelling months to 29°C in October.

## 6. Uvita and Marino Ballena National Park

Marino Ballena National Park, named for the humpback whale tail shape of its offshore sandbar, protects a stretch of southern Pacific coast that sees humpback whales from August through October and again from December through March — both Northern and Southern Hemisphere populations pass through. The nearby diving is on rocky reefs at 10 to 20 metres with good fish life, white tip sharks, and rays. The park is less developed for diving than Guanacaste, with fewer operators, but the whale encounters make the season compelling. Whale sharks have also been documented here. Water temperature runs 26–29°C in the whale months. Open Water certification is sufficient for the reef sites.

## 7. Osa Peninsula — Drake Bay

Drake Bay, on the Osa Peninsula — one of the most biodiverse regions on Earth — provides access to Caño Island as well as local rocky reefs and river-mouth sites with unusual fish diversity. The Osa dive sites see very limited visitor pressure compared to the Guanacaste hub, and several operators here run small-group trips that combine diving with the Corcovado National Park wildlife experience. Local reef dives at 10 to 20 metres produce hammerheads, eagle rays, sea turtles, and large schools of fish. Visibility averages 8 to 15 metres; the wet season from May through November brings stronger currents and reduced surface visibility but often better pelagic activity at depth. Advanced Open Water recommended.

## 8. Playa del Coco — El Bajo

El Bajo is a seamount complex near Playa del Coco that rises to 18 metres from surrounding depths and concentrates pelagic life above the pinnacle. Schools of chevron barracuda, Moorish idols, and mixed snappers hold station above the rock; white tip sharks rest on the sandy base; and with luck the occasional hammerhead makes an appearance near the thermocline. Visibility is highly variable — 8 metres on a bad day, 25 metres at its best. This is the kind of site where patience and good buoyancy in blue water pay off: hovering motionless above the pinnacle and watching the parade of pelagics pass is more productive than chasing individual fish. Advanced Open Water is recommended; drift can be significant on the exposed side of the mount.

## 9. Manuel Antonio — Local Reefs

Manuel Antonio National Park on the central Pacific coast is primarily known as a wildlife park, but the marine reserve that extends from the beach encompasses coral-encrusted rocky reefs at 8 to 18 metres. White tip reef sharks, sea turtles, moray eels and dense snapper schools are the main attractions. The visibility here is typically 6 to 12 metres — less than the northern Gulf sites — but the accessibility from the park beach and the calm conditions within the bay make it a good introduction to Pacific Costa Rica diving. Operators in Quepos, 7 kilometres from the park, run daily boat trips. Open Water certification is sufficient. Best conditions run January through April.

## 10. Caribbean Coast — Puerto Viejo and Manzanillo

The Caribbean coast of Costa Rica, anchored by Puerto Viejo de Talamanca near the Panamanian border, offers a completely different experience: warm water at 26–29°C, calmer conditions, and small coral reefs at depths of 6 to 15 metres. The Gandoca-Manzanillo Wildlife Refuge protects the best reefs on this coast, with reasonable hard coral coverage and typical Caribbean reef fish including French angels, parrotfish and midnight parrotfish. Visibility averages 10 to 15 metres. Diving infrastructure is modest — a handful of small operators based in Puerto Viejo — and the reefs here have suffered some coral bleaching in recent years. Nevertheless, the Caribbean side provides a relaxed and warmer alternative to the Pacific, particularly during the Pacific's strong upwelling season when water temperatures drop.

## Planning Your Trip

The Guanacaste dry season from December to April is the classic time to dive Costa Rica's Pacific north: calm seas, better visibility, and the eagle ray aggregations at the Catalinas. Cocos Island is accessible year-round by liveaboard with departures from Puntarenas; the crossing is long and sea conditions variable. Advanced Open Water certification is strongly recommended for Cocos and the Bat Islands. Pacific coast operators cluster around Playas del Coco, with smaller hubs at Tamarindo, Drake Bay and Quepos. The Caribbean coast is best dived September through October and February through March when sea conditions are most settled.
