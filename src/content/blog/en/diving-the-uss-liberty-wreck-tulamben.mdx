In the village of Tulamben on Bali's quiet northeast coast, the dive begins not with a boat ride but with a walk across a beach of black volcanic pebbles, fins in hand, into the gentle shallows. A few dozen metres out, the broken hull of a 120-metre cargo ship lies on a sloping bank of volcanic sand, encrusted with coral and swarming with fish. This is the USAT Liberty, one of the most dived wrecks in the world, and arguably the most accessible great wreck dive anywhere. No long crossing, no decompression obligation, no advanced certification required: you simply walk in.

## A Ship with Two Endings

The Liberty was a United States Army cargo ship, a steam freighter pressed into service carrying supplies across the Pacific in the Second World War. In January 1942 she was torpedoed by a Japanese submarine in the Lombok Strait while carrying railway parts and rubber. Too badly damaged to reach a major port, she was taken under tow toward Singaraja but flooded too quickly, so her crew beached her on the shore at Tulamben, where her cargo was salvaged and she was left to rust on the sand, largely intact and above the waterline.

There she sat for two decades. Then, in 1963, Bali's Mount Agung erupted catastrophically, killing more than a thousand people and reshaping the landscape. The tremors and lava flows shifted the beached hulk off the sand and into the sea, where she broke apart and slid down the slope to settle parallel to shore between roughly 5 and 30 metres. The volcano that devastated the region gave divers one of the great wrecks of the world, and the black volcanic sand of Tulamben is the legacy of the same eruptions.

## The Dive Profile

The Liberty's accessibility is its defining feature. The stern lies in around 5 metres of water, shallow enough to free-dive or snorkel, while the bow reaches down to about 28 to 30 metres, deep enough to interest an experienced diver. The wreck runs parallel to the beach only a short swim from the entry, so divers of every level find a profile that suits them. Open Water divers explore the shallow stern and midships; more experienced divers drop to the bow and the deeper debris. Currents are usually mild, and the site is dived day and night.

Decades underwater have transformed the broken steel into an artificial reef of extraordinary density. Hard and soft corals, gorgonians, barrel sponges and hydroids cover the hull so thoroughly that the original structure is often hard to read. Visibility is generally good, though it can drop when surge stirs the fine volcanic sand. The walk-in entry over the loose pebbles, in full kit and against small waves, is the only genuinely awkward part of the dive, and a little care there saves a twisted ankle.

## Life on the Wreck

What sets the Liberty apart is the sheer biomass it supports. A resident school of bigeye trevally hangs over the wreck in a slow-turning silver wall that divers can swim into, parting and reforming around them. Great barracuda hang in the blue. A famous school of bumphead parrotfish, large and prehistoric-looking, sleeps near the wreck and can be found cruising the structure at dawn, when an early dive often produces the best big-animal action.

The wreck is equally celebrated for small life. Tulamben is a macro photographer's paradise: pygmy seahorses cling to gorgonians on the hull, ghost pipefish drift in the debris, ribbon eels poke from the sand, and a parade of nudibranchs decorates the coral. Night dives reveal Spanish dancers, decorator crabs, hunting lionfish and the bioluminescent flicker of the surrounding sand. Few wrecks reward both the wide-angle and the macro lens so generously.

## Tulamben's Other Sites

The Liberty is the star, but Tulamben is a complete dive destination. Just along the shore, the Coral Garden is a shallow, easy site scattered with small artificial reef structures and statues, ideal for training, check dives and relaxed photography. A short distance further, the Drop Off, also called the Tulamben Wall, plunges down a steep volcanic slope draped in sea fans and black coral, with gorgonians and the chance of pelagic visitors in the blue. Many operators run all three as a day of shore diving from a single base.

Tulamben also makes an easy hub for Bali's wider northeast coast. The muck-diving and reef sites around Seraya and Amed lie a short drive away, and the whole region is known for sleepy black-sand bays where the diving is excellent and the crowds thin. Compared with the busier south of the island, this coast is quiet, with dive lodges set among rice terraces and the great cone of Mount Agung dominating the skyline inland.

## Planning a Tulamben Trip

Tulamben sits about three hours by road from Bali's Ngurah Rai airport in the south, and most divers stay in one of the village's dive lodges to make the most of dawn and night dives on the Liberty. The wreck can be busy in the late morning when day-trippers arrive by bus from the south, so basing yourself locally and diving early is the way to have it nearly to yourself. Water is warm year round, typically 27 to 29 degrees Celsius, and the dry season from roughly April to October generally brings the calmest conditions and best visibility. An Open Water certification is enough for the shallow wreck; nitrox extends bottom time for those working the deeper bow on repeated dives.
