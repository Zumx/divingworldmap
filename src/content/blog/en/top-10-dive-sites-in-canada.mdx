Canada's diving splits between two entirely different worlds. On the Pacific coast, British Columbia offers cold, plankton-rich water that sustains one of the greatest densities of marine life anywhere in the northern hemisphere — wolf eels, giant Pacific octopus, six-gill sharks and fields of anemones colonise walls and artificial reefs from Vancouver Island to the Sunshine Coast. On the opposite side of the continent, Ontario's fresh inland lakes preserve a fleet of 19th-century schooners in near-pristine condition, their timbers still intact in water cold enough to inhibit decay. Both traditions require commitment to cold-water exposure protection — a drysuit is standard for year-round diving in either region — but the reward is world-class diving with minimal crowds and extraordinary biodiversity.

## 1. Tobermory Fathom Five, Ontario

Fathom Five National Marine Park on Lake Huron's Georgian Bay contains more than twenty documented wrecks in depths of 6 to 30 metres, and the park's freshwater cold temperatures have preserved them in a condition unmatched in salt water. The Sweepstakes, a well-preserved wooden schooner that sank in 1885, lies in Cove Island harbour in just 6 metres and can be viewed from a glass-bottom boat as well as dived. The City of Grand Rapids steam ferry sits in 9 metres nearby. Visibility runs 10 to 20 metres in summer. The park's Blue Heron Dive Shop and other operators in Tobermory town run daily guided wreck dives from July through September. Open Water certification covers the shallower wrecks; the deeper sites at 25 to 30 metres require Advanced.

## 2. Brockville Wrecks, Ontario

Brockville on the St. Lawrence River in eastern Ontario is one of Canada's best-known freshwater wreck-diving destinations. The river's fast current complicates logistics — divers must plan entries and exits around tidal timing and use surface floats — but the visibility, reaching 20 metres when conditions align, reveals steel and wooden steamers that sank in the 19th and early 20th centuries. The Keystorm, an early 20th-century steel tug, rests in 30 to 45 metres at the bottom of a wall that descends from the shore. The Lillie Parsons schooner, also accessible here, lies in 24 metres. Advanced Open Water and some drift-diving experience are advisable. Summer is the primary season; water temperature ranges from 18°C in summer to near-freezing in winter.

## 3. Kelvin Grove Dive Site, British Columbia

Kelvin Grove is a shore dive site on Howe Sound, accessible by kayak or the rocky shore near Lions Bay. It descends over boulders and rock reef to around 18 metres, with the marine life that defines BC diving in concentrated form: painted greenlings, copper rockfish, giant Pacific octopus in crevices, and dense carpets of plumose anemones on the walls. Visibility averages 8 to 12 metres, with better conditions in winter when plankton levels drop. A 7mm wetsuit or drysuit is necessary; water temperature ranges from 7°C in winter to around 14°C in summer. This site suits Advanced Open Water divers and above due to the boat traffic in Howe Sound and the requirement for good buoyancy on the anemone-covered walls.

## 4. Porteau Cove Diving Area, British Columbia

Porteau Cove Provincial Park on Howe Sound is one of the most accessible and best-maintained dive sites in British Columbia. The park includes an artificial reef created from two scuttled vessels — the Saskatchewan and the Chaudiere, both former Royal Canadian Navy frigates — sitting in 18 to 27 metres within a few minutes' swim of the beach. Lingcod, quillback rockfish, nudibranchs and giant Pacific octopus colonise the wrecks. The park has dedicated diver-change facilities and floats, and the bottom provides a sandy secondary diving area with flatfish and tube worms. Visibility is moderate but consistent at around 8 metres. A drysuit or heavy wetsuit is standard. The site handles two dives in a day without returning to the surface in strong conditions.

## 5. Whytecliff Park Dive Site, West Vancouver, British Columbia

Whytecliff Park, at the tip of the West Vancouver peninsula, is a dedicated marine reserve established in 1993 — the first in BC. The no-take rules have allowed fish populations to grow dense and bold. Divers enter at the rocky point and follow a rock wall covered in crimson anemones and orange sponge to around 20 metres, with a secondary plateau at 12 metres that concentrates lingcod, sea perch and the occasional harbour seal. Wolf eels occupy crevices at the base of the wall and have become accustomed to divers, approaching to within half a metre. Visibility averages 8 to 15 metres. A drysuit is strongly recommended. The site is legally reserved — no collecting — and diving etiquette is taken seriously by the regular local community.

## 6. Ansell Point Dive Site, Sunshine Coast, British Columbia

Ansell Point is a technical dive site on the Sechelt Inlet for experienced divers comfortable with depth and current. The point creates a downwelling where cold, nutrient-rich water sweeps across the reef, sustaining extraordinary aggregations of crimson cloud sponge — a species of giant glass sponge that forms reef structures hundreds of years old. The clouds descend from around 15 metres to well below recreational limits, but the upper edge is accessible to divers with Advanced certification and good cold-water experience. Six-gill sharks are occasionally encountered at depth. Water temperature is consistently cold at 8–12°C year-round. Boat access is required; local operators on the Sunshine Coast run day trips.

## 7. White Lady (Repulse Rock), British Columbia

Repulse Rock, known locally as the White Lady site for the pale anemone columns that cover the reef structure, lies in Saanich Inlet on Vancouver Island. The site descends from around 12 metres to 30 metres over a wall heavily colonised by plumose anemones — white and orange columns reaching 30 centimetres — interspersed with rockfish and the occasional wolf eel. Visibility in the inlet tends toward the green end of the spectrum, typically 6 to 10 metres, and the light at depth is subdued even in summer. A torch reveals the true colour of the walls. Drysuit diving is standard here. Advanced Open Water is recommended, and familiarity with low-visibility navigation in enclosed-inlet conditions is useful.

## 8. Frank E. Vigor Wreck, Ontario

The Frank E. Vigor is a steel steamer that sank in Georgian Bay in 1943 after a collision and now lies in 30 to 40 metres in the cold, clear water near Tobermory. The ship is largely intact, sitting upright on the bottom, with deck fittings, machinery and the hull form still recognisable. Visibility at depth on a calm summer day can reach 18 metres. This is a deeper wreck requiring Advanced Open Water certification and comfort with descent to recreational limits. The site is dived by boat from Tobermory and is often packaged with a shallower dive on the Sweepstakes. The cold water — around 8°C at depth even in summer — makes thermal protection critical; a drysuit is advisable.

## 9. Willis Wreck, Ontario

The Willis is a wooden schooner lying in 15 to 21 metres in Georgian Bay near Tobermory, with a structural integrity uncommon even in Great Lakes freshwater wrecks. The hull planking is clearly defined, the anchor winch remains on the bow, and the hold is open and explorable with care. Visibility typically runs 12 to 15 metres. The site is accessible to Advanced Open Water divers and is regularly dived by boat from Tobermory dive operators. The Georgian Bay wrecks, of which the Willis is one of the better-preserved examples, offer a window into the working vessels of the 19th-century Great Lakes trade — the scale of the vessels and the detail preserved in the cold freshwater is remarkable.

## 10. M. I. Wilcox Wreck, Ontario

The M. I. Wilcox is a wooden steam vessel in Georgian Bay that sank in the early 20th century, lying in 18 to 27 metres with the engine and boiler largely intact amidships. The wreck is deeper and slightly more demanding than the shallower park sites and is best treated as an Advanced Open Water dive in a drysuit with good buoyancy control to avoid disturbing the silt that has accumulated on the wooden decking. Visibility runs 10 to 18 metres. It is a quieter site than the Sweepstakes or the City of Grand Rapids and is favoured by experienced divers seeking a less-trafficked exploration. Water temperature at the bottom is typically 7–10°C even in July.

## Planning Your Trip

Ontario wreck diving runs from July through September, when lake surface conditions are calm and water temperature is most manageable. British Columbia diving is year-round; winter offers better visibility as plankton blooms subside, and water temperature changes little between seasons when diving in a drysuit. Tobermory is served by Highway 6 from Owen Sound; Porteau Cove and Whytecliff Park are within 45 minutes of Vancouver. Drysuit certification is a practical necessity for BC diving and is strongly recommended for Ontario diving below 20 metres. A local dive operator will know current conditions, tidal timing and which sites have recently produced the best sightings.
