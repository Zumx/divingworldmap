The Azores sit alone in the middle of the North Atlantic, roughly fifteen hundred kilometres from mainland Portugal, nine volcanic islands scattered across deep blue water that drops to abyssal depths within sight of land. Diving here is not about lagoons and shallow coral gardens. It is about seamounts, basalt arches, big pelagic animals, and long boat rides into open ocean. The reward for the crossing is some of the most thrilling blue-water diving in the Atlantic, and a season — roughly July to October — when the water warms to twenty-two or twenty-four degrees and the migratory giants arrive.

## Princess Alice Bank

If the Azores have a signature dive, it is Princess Alice Bank, a submerged seamount about forty-five nautical miles southwest of Pico and Faial. The pinnacle rises from enormous depth to within roughly thirty-five metres of the surface, and reaching it means a boat ride of two to three hours across open ocean. The draw, in summer, is the gathering of Mobula tarapacana — the sicklefin devil ray, often called the Chilean devil ray — which congregate over the bank in schools that can number in the dozens or even hundreds. Divers descend the shotline into blue water and hang at depth while the rays wheel past in slow, deliberate formation, their broad wings catching the filtered light. This is an advanced dive: there is no bottom in sight, the current can be strong, and good buoyancy and air discipline are essential.

## Blue and Mako Shark Dives off Faial and Pico

The same deep water that draws the rays draws sharks. Off Faial and Pico, operators run baited drift dives for blue sharks and, less predictably, shortfin mako sharks through the summer months. The boat motors offshore until the depth sounder reads hundreds of metres, drifts with a scent trail, and divers enter the open blue to wait. Blue sharks are elegant, curious, and astonishingly beautiful in clear oceanic water; makos are faster and more electric in their movements. These dives are tightly managed by experienced crews, and they offer an encounter with apex predators in their true element rather than over a reef. A typical day begins early, with a long steam out to deep water and a careful briefing on positioning, behaviour and the strict rules around not touching the animals. The reward is intimate time with creatures most divers only ever see in documentaries, hanging weightless in clear blue with nothing below but a thousand metres of ocean.

## Formigas Islets and the Dollabarat Reef

Far out between São Miguel and Santa Maria lie the Formigas Islets, a scatter of rocks marking the top of a vast underwater system, and nearby the submerged Dollabarat Reef. Together they form one of the great pelagic reserves of the Atlantic. The reef rises toward the surface in a maze of basalt ridges and swim-throughs, and the surrounding water teems with life: schooling jacks and barracuda, dusky groupers, mantas and mobulas in season, and patrolling sharks. The remoteness keeps the site pristine, and reaching it requires settled weather and a long offshore run, usually as a full-day expedition.

## Ambrósio, Santa Maria

Santa Maria, the southernmost and oldest island, is the Azores' summer hotspot for ray and manta aggregations. The site known as Ambrósio functions as a cleaning station, where mobula rays and the occasional reef manta circle in to be tended by smaller fish. In the warm months, divers can find themselves surrounded by graceful, slow-moving rays in clear water over a volcanic bottom. Santa Maria also offers warmer, calmer conditions than the central islands, which makes it a favourite base for divers chasing the summer pelagic season.

## Volcanic Arches, Caves and Basalt Reefs

Not every Azorean dive demands a two-hour crossing. The islands themselves are young volcanoes, and their underwater flanks are sculpted into arches, caverns, tunnels and dramatic basalt walls. Dives close to shore off Pico, Faial, São Miguel and Terceira lead through swim-throughs draped in encrusting life, past columns of hexagonal basalt and into caves where light filters down in shafts. These sites are perfect for second dives, for less experienced divers, and for days when the offshore swell keeps the bigger boats in harbour. The geology is part of the spectacle: cooling lava has left hexagonal columns and lava tubes that feel almost architectural, and the black rock makes the colours of fish and encrusting sponges leap out. Sites like the cliffs and ledges around the islands also shelter octopus, scorpionfish and shoals of bream, so even a shallow coastal dive rarely disappoints.

## Marine Life Through the Seasons

The Azorean reefs hold a distinctly Atlantic cast of characters. Dusky groupers, some of them genuinely large, hold station in the rocky reef; barracuda hang in loose silver schools; ornate wrasse flicker over the basalt in their gaudy colours; and moray eels coil in the cracks. Loggerhead turtles drift through on their oceanic wanderings. Above the divers, the Azores are one of the world's premier regions for whales and dolphins, and it is not unusual for a dive boat to pause for a passing pod on the way out to the seamounts. The pelagic season peaks alongside the warmest water, when the devil rays and sharks arrive on the offshore banks.

## When to Go and What to Expect

The diving season runs roughly from July to October, when sea temperatures climb to around twenty-two to twenty-four degrees and visibility offshore is often superb. This is when the migratory animals appear and when the long crossings to Princess Alice and Formigas become reliably possible. Outside that window, the Atlantic weather turns and many offshore trips are cancelled. Expect cool-water exposure protection, surface intervals on a rolling boat, and dives planned around current and depth rather than around a reef map. These are conditions for divers comfortable in open water.

## Choosing an Operator

Because the best Azorean dives are demanding offshore expeditions, the operator matters enormously. In the central group around Pico and Faial, established outfits such as Amigos do Mar and Nautilus-SUB run the long trips to Princess Alice Bank and the shark dives, with the boats, gas and experienced crews that blue-water diving requires. A good operator will brief you honestly on current, depth and the realities of a featureless blue descent, and will scrub a trip when the weather does not cooperate. Trust that judgement; the ocean here does not negotiate, and the best dives reward patience and respect.
