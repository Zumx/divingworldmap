Richelieu Rock breaks the surface of the Andaman Sea only at the lowest tides, a single tooth of limestone rising from open water roughly 18 kilometres east of the Surin Islands and far from any coast. There is nothing to see above the waterline but a scatter of barnacle-crusted rock and, often, a longtail boat or two waiting out the current. Below, the picture changes completely. The pinnacle plunges in a horseshoe-shaped wall to around 35 metres, every square centimetre of it draped in soft coral, and for many Thai divers it is simply the best single dive site in the country.

## Where Richelieu Sits and How You Get There

The rock lies in the far north of the Thai Andaman, closer to the Burmese border than to Phuket, which means it is almost always dived from a liveaboard rather than a day boat. The classic itinerary runs north from Khao Lak or Tap Lamu pier through the Similan Islands, on to Koh Bon and Koh Tachai, and finishes at Richelieu as the highlight before the long run home. A handful of fast day-trip boats reach it in season, but they pay for the privilege with a three-hour-plus crossing in each direction.

Most of the established Khao Lak and Phuket operators run the route. Big Blue, Andaman Scuba, Khao Lak Explorer Diving Center, Khao Lak Fun Divers and Tu Khao Lak Travel & Service all sell Similan-and-Richelieu trips, while Similan Dive Center built much of its reputation on this exact circuit. Trips depart from Thap Lamu near Khao Lak, and the marine-park season runs roughly from mid-October to mid-May, when the park is open and the sea is calm. Outside those months the park closes and the rock is off-limits.

## The Soft-Coral Wall

The single thing every diver remembers about Richelieu is the colour. The pinnacle is coated in dense thickets of soft coral — predominantly the purple and magenta Dendronephthya that flush full and vivid when the tide is running and they are feeding. At slack water they deflate to flaccid grey lumps, so the timing of a dive matters enormously. Hit the rock on an incoming current with the corals inflated and the whole structure glows violet, fuchsia and orange in a way that no amount of photography quite captures.

Among the coral the macro life is relentless. Tiger-tail seahorses cling to gorgonians, harlequin shrimp work the crevices, ornate ghost pipefish drift in pairs, and tomato anemonefish guard their hosts at every depth. The rock is a genuine candidate for the best macro dive in the Andaman as well as the best big-animal one — a rare combination.

## Big Animals in the Blue

Richelieu's reputation rests partly on what it shares with the open ocean around it. The pinnacle acts as a magnet for schooling fish — great clouds of yellow snapper, big-eye trevally and chevron barracuda wheel around the structure — and those baitfish in turn draw predators. Schools of barracuda and trevally hunt actively, and the surrounding blue regularly produces manta rays gliding past on the current.

It is also one of the most reliable whale shark sites in Thailand, although reliable is a relative word with the planet's largest fish. Sightings cluster in the late season, roughly February through April, when plankton blooms thicken the water. There is no guarantee on any given dive, but Richelieu produces more whale shark encounters than any other Thai site, and the hope of one keeps divers coming back trip after trip.

## Reading the Current

This is not a beginner's dive. Richelieu sits in open water with no shelter, and the current can switch direction and strength through a single dive. Guides plan the descent to drop on the up-current side of the pinnacle and work the protected faces, using the rock itself as a windbreak. When the flow is strong, divers tuck into the horseshoe and let the wall block the worst of it.

Advanced Open Water certification and good buoyancy are effectively prerequisites, both to handle the current and to avoid crushing the very soft coral that makes the site special. A reef hook is rarely needed, but a surface marker buoy is essential — a diver who loses the pinnacle in the current can surface a long way from the boat in open sea.

## When to Go and What Else Is Nearby

The window is dictated by the marine park. The Similan and Surin parks open in mid-October and close around mid-May; outside that the monsoon makes the crossing miserable and the park authorities shut the area entirely. February to April brings the calmest seas, the best visibility and the peak of the plankton-driven big-animal action, including the whale shark window.

A Richelieu liveaboard rarely visits the rock alone. The same trips take in the granite swim-throughs of the Similans, the manta cleaning stations at Koh Bon, and the pinnacle at Koh Tachai. Operators further south on islands like Raya near Phuket and even the Gulf-side outfits such as Chumphon Diving Center give a sense of how spread out Thai diving is, but for this particular pinnacle the launch point is always the Andaman coast around Khao Lak.

## Conservation Pressure

Richelieu's fame is also its biggest threat. On a busy day in high season the small pinnacle can host dozens of divers at once, and fin-kicks, dropped gauges and clumsy buoyancy take a steady toll on the fragile soft coral. The Thai marine-park authorities periodically close sites to allow recovery, and several Similan reefs have been shut to diving for exactly this reason in recent years.

Responsible operators brief hard on no-touch diving, cap group sizes and avoid the worst of the crowding by timing dives around the day-boat traffic. The reward for getting it right is a pinnacle that, dived at slack-to-incoming tide with the corals in full bloom, remains one of the most spectacular single dives anywhere in Southeast Asia.
