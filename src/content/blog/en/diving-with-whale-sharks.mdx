## The Largest Fish in the Ocean

Rhincodon typus — the whale shark — holds the title of the world's largest fish by a margin that is difficult to absorb on paper. Verified measurements from specimen weighing and photogrammetry studies suggest maximum lengths of 12 to 13 metres, and unverified accounts of larger individuals exist. Most of the animals encountered by divers and snorkellers in tourist contexts are juveniles between 4 and 8 metres, which is a scale that only becomes comprehensible when a tail fin the width of a car passes a metre in front of your face.

Despite the name and the bulk, whale sharks are filter feeders. Their wide, flat heads contain mouths that can be over a metre across, fringed with over 300 rows of tiny teeth that play no role in feeding. The animal filters enormous volumes of water to extract plankton, small fish eggs, and krill. The seasonal aggregations that make whale shark diving predictable are driven by food — upwellings, spawn events, and plankton blooms that concentrate prey in accessible water columns.

The species is classified as endangered by the IUCN. Targets of the gill plate trade, vessel strikes, and plastic ingestion all contribute to population pressure across the Indo-Pacific and Atlantic populations.

## Djibouti and the Gulf of Tadjoura

The Gulf of Tadjoura in Djibouti is one of the most accessible and consistently reliable whale shark sites in the world, and also one of the least visited, which means encounters here retain a quality that has been diluted elsewhere by tourism pressure. From October through January, juvenile whale sharks aggregate in the gulf in association with spawning events of zooplankton carried by the converging currents of the Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden.

The diving and snorkelling is in relatively shallow water — typically 5 to 15 metres — with visibility that ranges from excellent to moderate depending on plankton concentration. Water temperatures run 27 to 30 degrees Celsius and a 3mm suit is comfortable. The operators in Djibouti City run half-day trips to the aggregation sites, and the numbers of tourists in the water on any given day are a small fraction of the crowds at better-known sites. Encounters often involve animals spending extended time in the surface layer where the plankton is densest.

## Ningaloo Reef, Western Australia

The Ningaloo Marine Park on the northwest coast of Western Australia is the most reliable whale shark destination in the world at scale. From around mid-March through late July, following the mass coral spawning event on the reef, whale sharks aggregate in numbers. In peak months, multiple animals may be in the water simultaneously, and the combination of warm, clear water — 23 to 27 degrees Celsius, visibility 15 to 25 metres — and the long-established regulations around the encounter makes Ningaloo the benchmark.

Operations are run out of Exmouth and Coral Bay. Aircraft spotters locate animals from the air and radio coordinates to boats, which drop snorkellers into the water ahead of the animal's path. Scuba diving with whale sharks is permitted at Ningaloo under specific conditions — divers must maintain positions at the surface or at the level of the animal's tail, never swimming ahead of or above the shark. Maximum group sizes, minimum distances, and time limits are enforced by guides in the water.

The broader reef at Ningaloo is worth exploring on its own terms. The coral reef reaches almost to the beach at several points, accessible without a boat, and the manta rays, dugongs, and reef fish community make it one of the outstanding marine environments in the Indian Ocean. ## Isla Holbox and the Yucatan Platform, Mexico

In the Yucatan Peninsula, whale sharks aggregate above the shallow banks north of Isla Holbox and Isla Contoy from late June through September. The aggregation is linked to the spawning of little tunny and other fish, and the sharks arrive in numbers — estimates have suggested several hundred animals in the area during peak weeks, though the number actually encountered on any day by any single boat is typically two to five.

The Holbox aggregation is snorkelling rather than scuba diving. The animals are feeding at or just below the surface, and scuba divers would disturb the feeding pattern by ascending bubbles and positioning complications. Entering gently from a boat, swimming parallel to the animal and not in front of its path, and maintaining at least two metres of lateral distance is the standard protocol. Fins are used; touching is prohibited under Mexican law, and violations carry significant fines for operators who permit them.

Water temperatures here are 28 to 30 degrees, visibility is often 10 to 20 metres in calm conditions, and the flat, warm water of the banks is accessible even to confident snorkellers without diving experience. The combination of accessibility and the extraordinary scale of the aggregation makes this one of the world's great wildlife spectacles. The proximity to Cancun's airport makes logistics straightforward.

## Hanifaru Bay, Maldives: Plankton Bonanza

The Hanifaru Bay aggregation in the Maldives is principally a manta ray gathering, but whale sharks are a regular component of the same plankton bloom from June through November. On exceptional days, both species feed simultaneously in the same compressed area of water, and the intersection of a whale shark and a manta ray vortex is one of those underwater events that makes you stop swimming.

The whale sharks here are most commonly seen cruising through the bay in the feeding lane — not stationary like the spiralling mantas but moving slowly with mouths open, filtering continuously. The regulations that apply to manta interactions apply equally to whale sharks, and the guides in the water manage groups carefully.

The challenge at Hanifaru is visibility. During peak plankton, the water can be green to the point of opacity at 4 to 5 metres, which means encounters are often close and brief rather than extended blue-water observations. The density of life in those few metres of green water compensates entirely for the limited sightlines.

## Etiquette, Regulations and What Actually Harms

The global framework for whale shark interaction has converged on broadly similar rules: no touching, no riding, no flash photography (the ampullae of Lorenzini — the electroreceptors on the head — are sensitive and flash causes avoidance behaviour), minimum distances, group size limits, and no blocking the animal's path. The specific numbers differ by jurisdiction, but the principles are constant.

The behaviour most damaging to aggregations over time is not individual rule-breaking but the cumulative effect of too many boats and too many people. Sites that have grown from modest visitor numbers to mass tourism in a decade — some operators at popular sites now have dozens of boats in the water on peak days — show changes in animal behaviour including shorter surface time, earlier departures, and reduced aggregation sizes. The economics of whale shark tourism provide funding for conservation but the volume of activity, if poorly managed, undermines the resource that funds it.

The best operators run small groups, enforce protocols actively rather than nominally, and contribute encounter data to research databases. The Wildbook for Whale Sharks platform collects photographs of individual sharks identified by their unique spot patterns — a photograph of the left flank behind the gills is the most useful format — and has built an identification database of thousands of individuals from around the world. Submitting your photographs is a direct and meaningful contribution to understanding a species whose migration routes and population dynamics remain poorly understood.
