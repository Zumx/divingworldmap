The first time a diver swims over a bleached reef, the impression is unsettlingly beautiful. The corals glow a luminous, ghostly white, as if someone had bleached the colour out of the seafloor. It looks clean, even ornamental. But that whiteness is a coral under extreme stress, stripped of the microscopic partners that keep it alive, and within weeks the same reef may turn the dull brown-grey of algae-choked dead skeleton. Understanding what is happening in that window is one of the most important things a modern diver can carry into the water.

## What Bleaching Actually Is

A reef-building coral is not a single organism but a partnership. The coral animal, a tiny polyp, hosts millions of single-celled algae called zooxanthellae in its tissues. These algae photosynthesise and hand over as much as 90 percent of the energy the coral needs to grow and lay down its calcium-carbonate skeleton. They also give the coral its colour — the browns, golds and greens of a healthy reef come largely from the algae, not the animal.

When the coral is stressed, this relationship breaks down. The algae begin producing toxic compounds, and the coral expels them. Without its algae, the coral's transparent tissue reveals the white skeleton beneath, which is what we see as bleaching. The coral is still alive at this point. If conditions improve quickly enough, it can take its algae back and recover. If the stress persists, the coral starves, and it dies.

## The Triggers

Heat is the dominant cause of mass bleaching. Corals live close to their thermal maximum, and a rise of just 1 to 2 °C above the usual summer peak, sustained for a few weeks, is enough to break the symbiosis. Scientists track this with a measure called degree heating weeks — the accumulated thermal stress over time. The global bleaching events of 1998, 2010, 2016 and the years since were all driven by anomalously warm water, frequently amplified by El Niño conditions.

Heat is not the only trigger. Intense sunlight combined with calm, clear water increases the photosynthetic stress on the algae. Freshwater run-off, pollution and sedimentation after storms can bleach localised patches. Even unusually cold water can cause it. But the events that flatten entire reef systems over thousands of kilometres — the kind that struck large stretches of the Great Barrier Reef in 2016 and 2017 — are heat events, and they are becoming more frequent as ocean temperatures climb.

## What Recovery Looks Like

A bleached coral that survives does not bounce back instantly. First it must reacquire zooxanthellae, sometimes from a slightly different, more heat-tolerant strain. Over the following months its colour returns and it resumes growth. Fast-growing branching corals such as Acropora can recover visibly within a season if the heat passes; massive, slow-growing corals like Porites are more resistant to bleaching in the first place but take far longer to repair damage.

Full reef recovery is measured in years to decades. After the 1998 event, parts of the Seychelles and the Maldives took ten to fifteen years to rebuild meaningful coral cover, and some never returned to their former state. Recovery depends on surviving adult colonies, a supply of larvae from healthy reefs upstream, clean water, intact herbivore populations that keep algae in check, and crucially, enough time before the next heat spike. When events come too close together, reefs lose the breathing room they need.

## When Reefs Fail to Recover

Not every reef comes back. If bleaching kills the adult corals and no larvae arrive to recolonise, the dead skeleton is quickly overgrown by fleshy algae. Once algae dominate, they can suppress new coral settlement, and the reef shifts into an alternative stable state — an algal reef rather than a coral one. This phase shift is hard to reverse, especially where overfishing has removed the parrotfish and surgeonfish that would otherwise graze the algae down.

Repeated bleaching is the real killer. A reef that might survive one event can be finished by a second before it has recovered. The northern Great Barrier Reef lost roughly half its shallow-water coral in the back-to-back events of 2016 and 2017. The trajectory is not uniform — some reefs, particularly those flushed by cool upwelling or strong currents, fare far better — but the overall picture across the tropics is of reefs being pushed harder and given less time to recover.

## What Divers See and Can Document

Divers are uniquely placed to witness and record bleaching. A bleached reef in the early phase is stark white, fluorescing in shades of pink, blue and yellow as some corals produce protective pigments in a last-ditch response. Weeks later the picture changes: dead corals turn brown as algae take hold, tissue sloughs away, and the structure begins to crumble. Knowing the difference between a freshly bleached, still-living coral and a long-dead, algae-covered skeleton tells you whether you are watching a crisis or its aftermath.

Citizen-science programmes such as CoralWatch and Reef Check train divers to record bleaching severity using simple colour-reference cards, feeding observations into global databases. A logged dive with photographs and a date can become a genuine data point. Operators in the Maldives, the Red Sea and the Coral Triangle increasingly brief recreational divers on how to report what they see.

## What Helps, and What Divers Can Do

Nothing a diver does underwater will counter ocean warming, but local pressures absolutely affect whether a reef survives a heat event. Reefs with healthy fish populations, good water quality and no physical damage recover better. Choosing operators that enforce no-touch policies, anchor on moorings rather than dropping on coral, and support marine protected areas tilts the odds. Reef-safe sunscreen, careful buoyancy and never standing on the reef are small acts that aggregate across millions of divers.

The larger lever is carbon. Bleaching frequency tracks global temperature, and the long-term survival of coral reefs depends on limiting warming. Some hope lies in active restoration — coral nurseries, assisted gene flow toward heat-tolerant strains, and reef gardening projects in Indonesia, the Florida Keys and the Caribbean — but these buy time rather than replace the need to cool the planet.
