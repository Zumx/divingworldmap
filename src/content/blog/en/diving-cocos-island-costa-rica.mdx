Some 550 kilometres southwest of the Costa Rican mainland, alone in the open Pacific, a single jungle-cloaked island rises from the deep. Cocos Island, Isla del Coco, is uninhabited but for a small contingent of park rangers, draped in perpetual cloud and laced with waterfalls that plunge straight off its cliffs into the sea. Jacques Cousteau called it the most beautiful island in the world, but it is what surrounds it that draws divers from across the planet. Cocos sits atop a productive upwelling at the meeting of major currents, and its surrounding seamounts attract some of the densest concentrations of large pelagic life in the ocean — above all, the scalloped hammerhead sharks that school here in numbers seen in few other places on Earth.

## A Journey Before a Dive

There is no airport on Cocos, no resort, no day-trip. Reaching it means a crossing of roughly 36 hours by liveaboard from Puntarenas on the Costa Rican Pacific coast, across open ocean that can be anything from glassy to genuinely rough. Trips run eight to eleven days, with most of that time spent at the island diving its renowned sites. This commitment is part of the experience: Cocos is not a casual destination but a pilgrimage, and the divers who make it tend to be experienced, serious, and prepared for demanding conditions. Operators handle the logistics from Puntarenas, and the long passage is repaid the moment the seamounts come alive.

## The Hammerhead Spectacle

The image that defines Cocos is a wall of scalloped hammerheads, dozens to hundreds of them, cruising in slow formation against the blue. They gather at cleaning stations on the seamounts, where small king angelfish and barberfish pick parasites from their skin, and divers who settle quietly against the rocks can watch the sharks circle within metres. Dirty Rock and Alcyone, a submerged seamount named after Cousteau's vessel, are the legendary hammerhead sites. The experience depends on patience and good positioning rather than chasing; the sharks are skittish, and the reward goes to divers who can hold a steady position in current and let the spectacle come to them.

## More Than Hammerheads

Cocos is a big-animal destination across the board. Galapagos sharks, silvertip sharks, and whitetip reef sharks are abundant, the latter famous for their nocturnal hunting frenzies that night-diving guests can witness. Marbled rays, mobula rays, and the occasional manta pass through. Schools of jacks and snapper mass in silver clouds, and the island is one of the more reliable places in the world to encounter whale sharks. Pods of bottlenose dolphins escort the boats and join dives. Even sailfish and marlin appear in the blue. The sheer biomass of Cocos, fed by the upwelling, is its defining quality.

## The Demanding Conditions

Cocos diving is not gentle. The seamounts are exposed, currents can be strong and unpredictable, surge is common, and the water is cooler than the tropical latitude suggests — often in the low-to-mid 20s Celsius, with cold thermoclines at depth. Visibility is variable, sometimes excellent, sometimes reduced by the very plankton that fuels the food chain. A reef hook is standard equipment for holding position at the cleaning stations, and a surface marker buoy is essential for pickups in open water. Operators require substantial logged experience, and many recommend or require nitrox. This is advanced diving in a remote setting, and honesty about one's capabilities is essential.

## A National Park and a Protected Corridor

Cocos Island National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the oldest marine protected areas in the eastern Pacific. In recent years its protections have been dramatically expanded, and it now anchors one end of a migratory corridor linking Cocos to the Galapagos and other eastern Pacific islands — a route used by hammerheads, sea turtles, and whale sharks moving between the region's seamounts. The protection matters enormously: scalloped hammerheads are endangered globally, and Cocos represents one of their last great refuges. Diving here is strictly regulated, with park fees, ranger oversight, and firm rules on diver behaviour around the animals.

## When to Go

Cocos can be dived year-round, but the seasons shift the experience. The rainy season, roughly June to November, brings rougher crossings and lower visibility but is the peak window for large hammerhead schools and the best chance of whale sharks. The drier months, December to May, generally offer calmer seas and clearer water, with strong shark action throughout. There is no truly bad time to visit a site this rich, but divers chasing the great hammerhead aggregations often favour the wetter half of the year despite the rougher passage.

## Preparing for Cocos

The divers who get the most from Cocos arrive with the right preparation. Build experience in current at destinations like the Galapagos, the Maldives, or Indonesia before committing to the crossing. Refine your buoyancy and air consumption so you can hold position at a cleaning station without disturbing the sharks. Be comfortable deploying a surface marker buoy and diving as part of a tight team. Operators such as Rocket Frog Divers are among the names divers encounter when researching trips and gear for Costa Rica's Pacific. Approached with the right experience, Cocos delivers encounters that rank among the most extraordinary in all of diving.
