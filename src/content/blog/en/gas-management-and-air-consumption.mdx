## Why Gas Management Is the Foundation of Safe Diving

Running low on air underwater is not a malfunction — it is a planning failure. Every diver knows the sensation of glancing at a gauge that has dropped faster than expected, the quiet unease that follows, and the abbreviated dive that results. With a solid gas management system, that scenario simply does not happen. Gas management encompasses everything from calculating how quickly you personally consume air, through planning how far into a dive you can travel before turning back, to dealing with the genuine emergencies that arise when plans unravel. It is a skill set that repays investment at every level of diving, from open-water students to technical divers running multiple cylinders.

The foundation is a single number: your Surface Air Consumption rate, universally abbreviated to SAC rate. Everything else follows from it.

## Calculating Your SAC Rate

Your SAC rate is the volume of gas you consume per minute at the surface, expressed in bar per minute for a given cylinder size, or more usefully in litres per minute regardless of cylinder. To calculate it you need three pieces of information: the pressure drop across a dive (in bar), the cylinder's water capacity (in litres, stamped on the valve block), and the average depth of the dive in metres.

The formula is straightforward. Multiply the pressure drop by the cylinder volume to get the total litres consumed. Divide by the dive time in minutes to get a consumption rate in litres per minute at depth. Then divide by the absolute pressure at average depth — which is 1 bar for every 10 metres plus 1 bar for the surface — to arrive at your surface equivalent consumption.

A recreational diver who is fit, calm and experienced typically settles around 12 to 18 litres per minute. A nervous student or someone working hard against a current can hit 30 litres per minute or more. Calculating your SAC rate after several dives and averaging the results gives you a reliable personal baseline. Recalculate periodically, because the number changes as your buoyancy improves and your underwater composure deepens.

## Planning from SAC Rate to Turn Pressure

Once you know your SAC rate, you can plan any dive with real numbers. For a simple out-and-back dive on a 12-litre cylinder starting at 200 bar, begin by working out how many bar per minute you will consume at the planned depth. At 20 metres the absolute pressure is 3 bar, so a SAC of 15 litres per minute translates to 45 litres per minute at depth, which is 45 ÷ 12 = 3.75 bar per minute from a 12-litre cylinder.

The rule of thirds, borrowed from cave diving and applied widely in technical and overhead-environment diving, divides your starting gas into three portions: one third out, one third back, one third in reserve. For recreational open-water diving many agencies teach a simpler rule — turn at 100 bar, surface with 50 bar — but this only works if the planned dive time fits within that budget. Calculating your actual turn pressure using your SAC rate is always more precise.

A practical habit is to build a gas plan on a slate before any dive where the route matters: note starting pressure, planned depth, expected duration, turn pressure, and minimum return pressure. Reviewing that plan with your buddy before entering the water takes less than a minute and eliminates the single most common cause of abbreviated recreational dives.

## The Impact of Depth, Exertion and Stress

Depth multiplies consumption directly and linearly. The same breathing rate that burns 15 litres per minute at the surface consumes 45 litres per minute at 20 metres, 60 at 30 metres, and 75 at 40 metres. This is why a diver who comfortably completes a 50-minute dive at 15 metres on a single 12-litre cylinder may find the same cylinder barely sufficient for 25 minutes at 30 metres.

Exertion amplifies the effect. Swimming hard against a mild current at 25 metres can double your consumption relative to neutral hovering at the same depth. Pre-dive planning should account for the likely conditions, not a best-case scenario. If there is known current at a site — as at Richelieu Rock in the Andaman Sea, or at any of the passes in French Polynesia's atolls — build a conservative estimate into the plan.

Stress raises consumption even when physical exertion is low. A diver managing a mask flood, struggling with unfamiliar equipment, or anxious about a deeper section of the dive will breathe harder without moving faster. This is why new divers consume disproportionately more air than experienced ones at identical depths. The reduction in consumption that comes with experience is largely a reflection of reduced anxiety and improved breathing technique.

## Monitoring Your Gas During the Dive

Planning is worthless without in-water monitoring. Check your pressure gauge or dive computer's gas readout at regular intervals — at least every five minutes on a deeper dive, more frequently if conditions are demanding. Note your depth and time each time you check, not just the number on the gauge.

Some divers use a simple mental checkpoint system: note the pressure at the start of each major phase of the dive (descent, bottom, ascent, safety stop), compare it to the planned consumption, and adjust the plan if the actuals diverge. If you have burned significantly more gas than expected during the descent or at depth, call the dive earlier rather than hoping consumption will improve.

Dive computers with air-integrated transmitters display remaining gas volume and, on more sophisticated models, a calculated time remaining at current depth based on your present consumption rate. These tools are genuinely useful, but they respond to current consumption and cannot anticipate the increased consumption of an ascent against surge or a long surface swim back to the boat. Treat the computer's projection as a guide, not a guarantee.

## Buddy Gas Planning and Minimum Turnaround Pressure

When diving with a buddy, gas management must account for the possibility that one of you might need to donate gas to the other. The practical implication is that the team's gas plan should be based on the diver with the higher consumption rate, and the minimum return gas must cover both divers back to safety at that rate.

In technical diving this is formalised by planning to have enough gas for both divers to complete the ascent — including all decompression stops — if one diver loses all their gas at the furthest point of the dive. Recreational divers need not go that far, but the principle of planning for the higher-consuming buddy, and of treating the buddy's remaining gas as a shared resource, is sound at every level.

Communicating your gas status frequently is as important as monitoring it. A team where both divers call out their pressure at regular intervals catches potential problems early, when options remain. A diver who is embarrassed to admit low gas and delays signalling has converted a manageable situation into an emergency.

## Building Better Air Consumption Over Time

SAC rate improvement comes from two sources: technique and fitness. Improved buoyancy control eliminates the constant fin work that unconsciously raises consumption. A diver hovering neutrally without sculling or frog-kicking for position breathes more slowly and more deeply than one who is perpetually fighting their trim.

Slow, diaphragmatic breathing — breathing from the belly rather than the chest — delivers more oxygen per breath and reduces the respiratory rate. Practicing this on the surface, during yoga, swimming or any aerobic activity trains the mechanics. Underwater, conscious attention to exhalation — making sure each breath is fully exhaled before the next inhalation — prevents CO2 buildup, which is itself a driver of the urge to breathe.

Cardiovascular fitness matters as well. A fitter diver recovers more quickly from exertion, returns to a low consumption rate faster after a demanding section of dive, and has a greater physiological reserve when conditions become demanding. None of these improvements happen automatically — they require deliberate practice and honest tracking of your numbers over time.

Reviewing your SAC rate after every dive and noting the conditions is the simplest form of progress tracking. Over a season, the trend line almost always improves if you are diving regularly and paying attention. That number, quietly declining dive by dive, is one of the clearest markers of growing competence in the water.
