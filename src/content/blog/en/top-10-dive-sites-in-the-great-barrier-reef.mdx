Stretching more than 2,300 kilometres down the Queensland coast, the Great Barrier Reef is the largest living structure on Earth, an interlocking system of around 3,000 individual reefs and 900 islands so vast it can be seen from orbit. For divers it offers an almost unmanageable wealth of choice, from the friendly giant cod of the far north to the Coral Sea walls plunging into the abyss, and the haunting wreck of the SS Yongala further south. Water temperature ranges from 24°C in winter to 30°C in summer, and visibility on the outer reef regularly passes 30 metres. The dry season, June to October, brings the calmest seas and clearest water; liveaboards reach the wildest northern sites.

## 1. Cod Hole, Ribbon Reef No. 10

Cod Hole, at the northern end of the Ribbon Reefs off Lizard Island, is one of the most famous dives on the planet, built around an encounter with enormous, fearless potato cod. These groupers grow well over a metre and weigh up to 60 kilograms, and they approach divers with the curiosity of a friendly dog, hovering at eye level at around 18 metres. The site sits on a reef pass swept by mild current, with healthy coral, reef sharks and schooling fish all around. It is accessible to Open Water divers and is the signature dive of any northern Ribbon Reefs liveaboard trip.

## 2. The Ribbon Reefs

The Ribbon Reefs are a chain of ten narrow reefs running along the edge of the continental shelf north of Cairns, reachable only by liveaboard and home to some of the reef's most pristine diving. Sites like Steve's Bommie — a pinnacle rising from 30 metres swarming with anthias, lionfish, stonefish and pipefish — and Pixie Pinnacle showcase the reef at its most concentrated and colourful. Current can vary, but most sites suit a broad range of divers. The Ribbon Reefs are also the launch point for minke whale encounters in June and July, when the curious whales approach snorkellers and divers.

## 3. SS Yongala, off Townsville

The Yongala is widely regarded as the finest wreck dive in the Southern Hemisphere. The passenger steamer sank in a cyclone in 1911 with 122 people aboard and lay undiscovered until 1958. It rests in 14 to 30 metres off Cape Bowling Green, intact and now an artificial reef teeming with life: giant Queensland grouper, bull sharks, sea snakes, marble rays, turtles and dense schools of trevally. Currents can be strong, so Advanced certification and logged open-water experience are required. There is no penetration, out of respect for the wreck as a grave. Dive it October to May before cyclone season peaks.

## 4. Osprey Reef, Coral Sea

Osprey Reef is a submerged atoll in the Coral Sea, around 350 kilometres northeast of Cairns and reachable only by liveaboard, where walls drop sheer past 1,000 metres into clear blue water. The North Horn site is famous for shark action, with grey reef and whitetip sharks gathering in numbers, occasionally joined by hammerheads and silvertips. Visibility frequently exceeds 40 metres. The remote location and the sheer drop-offs give Osprey a wild, oceanic character utterly different from the inshore reefs. It is a bucket-list destination for divers seeking big animals in pristine, current-washed water.

## 5. Flynn Reef, off Cairns

Flynn Reef is one of the most popular day-trip sites from Cairns, an outer reef offering excellent coral and accessible conditions within a few hours of port. Sites like Gordon's Mooring and Tracy's Bommie feature coral gardens, swim-throughs and bommies at 5 to 25 metres, populated by turtles, reef sharks, giant clams, anemones and a rich cast of reef fish. Visibility is usually good and current mild, making Flynn ideal for newer divers and certification courses. It offers a genuine outer-reef experience without the commitment of a liveaboard, and is a reliable, rewarding introduction to the region.

## 6. Norman Reef, off Cairns

Norman Reef, another outer-reef day-trip favourite from Cairns and Port Douglas, is known for sites such as Troppos and the Playground, where coral bommies, walls and sandy channels at 5 to 20 metres host green turtles, white-tip reef sharks, Maori wrasse and schooling fish. The reef has several permanent pontoon moorings, making it a busy but well-managed destination. Conditions are generally calm and visibility good, particularly in the dry season. Norman is a strong choice for divers wanting a varied, accessible outer-reef day with a high chance of turtle and shark encounters.

## 7. Holmes Reef, Coral Sea

Holmes Reef, in the Coral Sea between Osprey Reef and the mainland, is a liveaboard destination prized for its walls, swim-throughs and shark dives. Sites like Amazing and Nonki feature dramatic drop-offs, caverns and bommies rich in soft coral and fans, with grey reef sharks, white-tips and large pelagics patrolling the blue. Visibility is excellent, often beyond 30 metres. As a Coral Sea reef it offers the same oceanic, big-animal character as Osprey but with its own distinct topography. Holmes is frequently combined with Osprey on extended Coral Sea liveaboard itineraries.

## 8. Lady Elliot Island

At the southern tip of the Great Barrier Reef, Lady Elliot Island is a coral cay famous as the manta ray capital of the reef, where reef mantas are present year-round and gather in large numbers from May to August. The island is surrounded by easy shore and boat dives over coral and lagoon at 5 to 20 metres, with turtles, leopard sharks and excellent visibility thanks to its position away from river runoff. As a strictly managed eco-resort island, it offers some of the cleanest water and healthiest reef in the southern section, ideal for both diving and snorkelling.

## 9. Heron Island

Heron Island, a coral cay in the Capricornia Cays at the reef's southern end, offers more than 20 dive sites within a short boat ride and excellent diving directly off the reef edge. The Bommie and Heron Bommie are renowned, hosting turtles, reef sharks, rays and seasonal aggregations of fish, with the Coral Sea's famous turtle nesting taking place on the island's beaches in summer. Depths run 5 to 20 metres with good visibility and generally calm conditions. Heron combines accessible, world-class reef diving with a genuine research-station and conservation atmosphere.

## 10. SS Oakland and the Swain Reefs

Far offshore in the southern Great Barrier Reef, the Swain Reefs are a remote complex of more than 300 reefs reachable only by extended liveaboard, offering pristine coral, abundant sharks and a true wilderness feel. The historic wreck of the SS Oakland and other scattered relics add interest among the maze of bommies and lagoons. Sites here see grey reef sharks, schooling pelagics, turtles and untouched hard coral in water that few divers ever reach. The Swains reward those seeking solitude and adventure far from the day-trip crowds, with visibility and reef health among the best in the south.

## Explore on the map
