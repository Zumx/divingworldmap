The Bahamas occupies a unique position in Atlantic diving — an archipelago of over 700 islands scattered across the northwestern Atlantic, sitting on the edge of one of the deepest trenches in the region. The topography is dramatic: shallow banks and clear flats give way within metres to walls that descend thousands of feet. The country has built a worldwide reputation on its shark diving, its vast cave and blue hole systems, and on the extraordinary clarity of its water, which regularly exceeds 30 metres of horizontal visibility. The Gulf Stream moderates temperatures so that even northern islands like Grand Bahama rarely see water below 24°C in winter. The Bahamas is equally suited to a beginner completing their first open-water dives in calm and warm water and to an advanced diver seeking deep blue-water shark encounters. ## 1. Tiger Beach, Grand Bahama

Tiger Beach is a shallow sandy site at around 12 metres northwest of Grand Bahama Island that has become arguably the world's most famous location for diving with tiger sharks without a cage. Large female tiger sharks — some exceeding four metres — visit year-round, with peak numbers between October and April. The encounters are conducted by experienced operators who manage the interaction without chumming, relying on the sharks' resident behaviour. Lemon sharks, Caribbean reef sharks, and great hammerheads are frequent bonuses. This is a mature, professionally managed site; inexperienced divers should complete at least 50 logged dives and a shark-diving briefing before attempting it. Advanced Open Water and relevant experience recommended.

## 2. Dean's Blue Hole, Long Island

Dean's Blue Hole on Long Island is the world's deepest known marine blue hole, plunging to 202 metres from a circular opening around 25 metres wide at the surface. The walls of the hole drop vertically and the colour transition from turquoise to deep indigo happens within the first 20 metres. Recreational divers can explore the entrance rim and descend to around 40 metres along the wall, where the cave opens into a chamber roughly 60 metres wide. Freediving competition records have been broken here repeatedly — Dean's is the site of the annual Vertical Blue competition — but scuba divers exploring the entrance zone see healthy reef life on the rim and remarkably clear water within the hole. Advanced certification; dive with a guide familiar with the site.

## 3. Andros Blue Holes and Wall

Andros Island has the third-largest barrier reef in the world running along its eastern coast, and the combination of offshore wall diving and inland blue holes makes it one of the most underrated dive destinations in the Caribbean. The Andros barrier reef drops from a healthy reef crest at around 5 metres to an almost sheer wall that falls to beyond 180 metres. Caribbean reef sharks are common on the wall, and black grouper in large sizes populate the crest. Inland, accessible by short boat rides, are ocean-connected blue holes — flooded cave systems — where the thermocline is visible as a shimmering halocline layer at around 18 metres. Cave divers access the full systems; recreational divers explore the first chambers. Advanced certification for the wall; cavern certification for blue hole entries.

## 4. Bimini Road and Wall, Bimini

Bimini sits close enough to Miami that divers regularly fly in for a long weekend. The famous Bimini Road — a formation of large flat limestone blocks at around six metres that some divers romantically associate with Atlantis — is more geologically interesting than archaeologically significant, but it is a distinctive dive. More compelling is the Bimini wall on the western side of the island, where the reef drops from 10 metres to well beyond recreational limits in a series of ledges colonised by large grouper, angelfish, and spotted eagle rays. Bull sharks are resident in the harbour area and great hammerheads visit the shallows between December and March, patrolling sandy patches in 5–8 metres of water. Open Water for the Road; Advanced for the wall.

## 5. Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park, Exumas

The Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park was the first marine protected area in the Caribbean, designated in 1958, and decades of protection have produced fish populations of exceptional density. Nassau grouper — heavily overfished throughout the Caribbean — are abundant here, and the park's shallow coral gardens teem with species that are comparatively rare outside protected areas. The park has no dive infrastructure of its own; you arrive by private boat or liveaboard. Visibility is typically outstanding, 25–35 metres, and the sites range from shallow caves at two metres to wall sections exceeding 30 metres. The resident nurse shark population at Compass Cay, accessible by dinghy from anchorages, offers one of the most relaxed large-shark encounters in the Bahamas. Open Water throughout.

## 6. Thunderball Grotto, Exumas

Thunderball Grotto on Staniel Cay — named for the James Bond film partly shot here — is a submarine cave system accessible at water level on the surface and driveable from below. Divers enter through submerged arches at around three metres and surface inside a cathedral-like air chamber lit by shafts of sunlight entering through holes in the roof. The interior walls are covered in hard and soft coral, and enormous schools of sergeant majors, snapper, and silversides fill the air-space and water column. It is a genuinely spectacular five-metre dive. Tidal current through the arches picks up significantly; dive on slack water, particularly during tidal extremes. Open Water; tidal awareness essential.

## 7. Spiral Cavern, New Providence

Off the coast of New Providence near Nassau lies a series of coral caverns and swim-throughs at depths of 12 to 22 metres. Spiral Cavern is the most dramatic — a corkscrew tunnel formed in ancient reef limestone that divers enter at 18 metres and exit at around 12 metres after roughly 15 metres of passage. The walls are alive with orange cup corals and the passage hosts resident glassy sweepers that pack the interior at night. This site is dived from Nassau-based operators and is accessible as a day trip from the resort strip. Open Water divers are comfortable here; the passage is wide enough to turn around easily and there are no restrictions or overhead silting hazards.

## 8. AUTEC Wall, Andros

The AUTEC (Atlantic Undersea Test and Evaluation Centre) wall is located off the north coast of Andros and plunges from 8 metres to beyond 1,800 metres in the Tongue of the Ocean. The upper section between 8 and 40 metres is a classic Caribbean wall, with large sponges, deep-water sea fans, and clouds of Creole wrasse hanging in the blue. The remoteness of the site means most visiting divers arrive by liveaboard; day trips from small operators in Fresh Creek are possible. Dolphins are regular companions on the surface interval, and the occasional whale shark passes through in spring. Advanced certification for the wall; Open Water for the reef crest.

## 9. National Park Reef, New Providence

Nassau has a surprisingly strong local dive scene, and the National Park reef system running from Nassau Harbour west to Lyford Cay provides varied diving within 20 minutes by boat of the resorts. The reef crest at five metres is excellent for training dives and is home to large parrotfish, hawksbill turtles, and the ubiquitous Nassau grouper. Nurse sharks rest in groups under coral heads at around 10 metres. The reef edge drops to around 20 metres before transitioning to a sandy slope. A decommissioned tugboat rests in 14 metres at the eastern end of the park — an excellent wreck dive for new divers. Open Water throughout.

## 10. Shark Wall, New Providence

Shark Wall, also known as Shark Arena, is a site operated by Stuart Cove's dive operation about 20 minutes from Nassau by boat. The site is a sandy arena at 15 metres where Caribbean reef sharks congregate — numbers typically range from 20 to 50 individuals during a guided shark feed dive. The wall behind the arena drops to around 35 metres and is independently worth diving for its coral coverage and large grouper. Stuart Cove's operates one of the most professionally structured shark-encounter operations in the Atlantic world; their safety record over decades is strong. Advanced Open Water; no prior shark-diving experience required, but attentive briefing attendance is essential.

## Planning Your Bahamas Dive Trip

The Bahamas dives year-round. Water temperatures range from 24°C in winter (January–March) to 29°C in late summer. Hurricane season runs June through November; while most storms track north of the archipelago, liveaboard operators reschedule in active periods. Nassau and Grand Bahama have the most infrastructure; Andros, Long Island, and the Exumas reward advance planning and a slower pace. Live-aboard trips typically depart Nassau or Freeport and run 7–10 day itineraries through the outer cays. Shark diving on tiger sharks at Tiger Beach is best booked directly with Grand Bahama operators who know the site.
