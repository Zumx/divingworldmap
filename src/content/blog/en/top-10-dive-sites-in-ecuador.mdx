Ecuador contains perhaps the most extraordinary marine environment accessible to recreational divers anywhere on the planet. The Galápagos Islands, 1,000 kilometres west of the mainland in the Pacific Ocean, sit at the convergence of four ocean currents — the Cromwell, the Humboldt, the Panama and the South Equatorial — creating upwellings of cold, nutrient-rich water that sustain a food chain capable of supporting whale sharks, scalloped hammerhead sharks in the hundreds, Galápagos sharks, marine iguanas, flightless cormorants, Galápagos penguins and fur seals, all in the same dive. This is not tropical reef diving: the water is cold — often 16–22°C depending on site and season — and the marine life reflects a subpolar productivity rather than a Caribbean warmth. A 7mm wetsuit is the realistic minimum; many divers use two-piece suits or semi-drys. The payoff is encounters of a scale and intensity unavailable anywhere else at recreational depths.

## 1. Wolf Island, Galápagos

Wolf Island, at the northern extreme of the Galápagos archipelago, is the benchmark site for shark aggregations in the Pacific. The island's submarine topography creates upwellings that concentrate scalloped hammerheads in schools of hundreds — the animals stack vertically in the water column during the cooler season, from June through November, when the Humboldt Current's influence is strongest. Whale sharks are near-guaranteed encounters here for much of the year, particularly large pregnant females from June through December. The diving is in currents that can be powerful and unpredictable; drift experience and Advanced Open Water are essential. Depths run 15 to 30 metres at the productive sites. Wolf is accessible only by liveaboard, taking roughly 18 hours from the port of Puerto Ayora on Santa Cruz.

## 2. Darwin Island, Galápagos

Darwin Arch, a freestanding rock formation at Darwin Island even further north than Wolf, is the signature surface symbol of Galápagos diving. Underwater, the arch creates a current break where whale sharks aggregate in the thermocline layer at 15 to 30 metres. The site was partially damaged by natural collapse in 2021, but the underwater topography and the marine life that relies on the current dynamics remain intact. Hammerhead schools, Galápagos sharks, schools of yellowfin tuna and silky sharks combine to produce one of the most intense pelagic dive environments on Earth. Like Wolf, Darwin is only reached by liveaboard and requires Advanced certification with strong current experience. Water temperature ranges from 18°C in the cool season to 24°C in the warm season.

## 3. Gordon Rocks, Santa Cruz, Galápagos

Gordon Rocks is an eroded volcanic caldera sitting in the ocean north of Santa Cruz, one of the few sites in the Galápagos accessible by day boat from Puerto Ayora. The caldera rim creates a natural amphitheatre at 20 to 26 metres where scalloped hammerheads, Galápagos sharks and eagle rays are resident. The current through the caldera can be powerful and variable — Gordon Rocks has a strong reputation among Galápagos dive operators as one of the most technically demanding day-boat sites, and it is not appropriate for Open Water divers or those without drift experience. When conditions align, the three-dimensional shark experience inside the caldera is unlike anything available from a day boat elsewhere. Advanced certification required; operators will brief carefully before entry.

## 4. Cousin's Rock, Santiago, Galápagos

Cousin's Rock is a pinnacle site off Santiago Island that offers a contrast to the pure pelagic intensity of Wolf and Darwin — here the reef ecology is more complex, with sea horses clinging to black coral branches, schools of razorfish swimming in coordinated formations, Galápagos garden eels extending from sand patches, and hammerheads cruising the outer edge. The site descends to around 30 metres over a wall with black coral and large barrel sponges; sea lions often join divers in the shallower sections. Current is present but generally more manageable than Gordon Rocks. This is a liveaboard site accessible on most itineraries. Advanced certification is standard. Water temperature 16–22°C.

## 5. Kicker Rock (León Dormido), San Cristóbal, Galápagos

Kicker Rock — León Dormido in Spanish — is the most distinctive surface formation in the Galápagos: a vertical tuff cone split by a narrow channel that can be dived as a swim-through at 10 to 20 metres. Hammerhead sharks patrol the outer walls; sea turtles rest on ledges; whitetip sharks lie on the sandy bottom of the channel. The walls are covered in black coral, sea fans and the peculiar cold-water sessile organisms of the Galápagos. This site is accessible by day boat from San Cristóbal, making it the most practically accessible shark dive in the archipelago. Open Water divers can access the shallower sections of the channel with a guide; the outer wall requires Advanced. Current through the channel can strengthen with the tide.

## 6. Bartolomé Island, Galápagos

Bartolomé is famous above water for its volcanic landscape — the same kind of primordial terrain continues underwater. Lava flows form tubes, arches and overhangs at 5 to 20 metres in the island's protected bay, sheltering Galápagos penguins that also dive to catch fish — and can be encountered swimming directly alongside human divers. Sea lions are endemic here. Whitetip sharks rest in the sheltered section, and the cold clear water gives visibility of 15 to 20 metres. This is one of the more accessible Galápagos sites in terms of current and conditions, suitable for Open Water divers on guided dives. It is often included on liveaboard itineraries as a contrast to the pelagic intensity of the northern islands.

## 7. Punta Vicente Roca, Isabela Island, Galápagos

Isabela Island's Punta Vicente Roca is a drift dive along a cliff face that descends from the surface to around 35 metres. The Cromwell Current upwelling strikes here directly, bringing the coldest water in the Galápagos — 14–16°C is not unusual — and feeding a dense biomass in the water column. Mola mola (ocean sunfish) come to cleaning stations here, attended by Galápagos sea turtles and King angelfish that pick parasites from their skin. Marine iguanas forage on algae at 10 metres, swimming with slow rhythmic tail sweeps. Flightless cormorants dive alongside their more mobile cousins from the colonies above. Visibility averages 12 to 20 metres. Advanced certification recommended; drysuit or double 7mm exposure protection needed.

## 8. Floreana — Champion Islet

Champion Islet off the coast of Floreana Island is one of the calmer, more accessible sites in the Galápagos, with a coral reef ecosystem unusual in the cold Galápagos context — here the warmer Panama Current influence occasionally dominates, bringing reef-building coral that is absent from the colder northern islands. Hawksbill turtles, spotted eagle rays and schools of Creole fish are the main residents. The site descends to around 20 metres over a reef slope with reasonable coral coverage and significantly better water temperature than the northern sites — often 22–24°C. Open Water divers can dive Champion on guided liveaboard dives. It is often included on western and southern itineraries as a warmer respite from the cold currents.

## 9. Cabo Marshall, Isabela Island, Galápagos

Cabo Marshall on the northeastern tip of Isabela is a dedicated manta ray site where Pacific manta rays aggregate year-round at a cleaning station. The dive involves descending to around 20 metres on a rock reef and waiting at the cleaning station — a sponge-covered ledge where Klein butterflyfish, King angelfish and wrasse pick parasites from the manta rays' skin. The mantas pass in tight circles overhead, sometimes in groups of three or four simultaneously. Sea lions and the occasional marine iguana swim past. The site is exposed to current from the east, and conditions vary significantly between dives. Advanced certification preferred. This is a patient, observational dive rather than a high-current drift experience.

## 10. Seymour Norte Channel, Galápagos

The channel between North Seymour and Daphne Minor islands runs with consistent current that creates a drift dive feeding hammerheads, Galápagos sharks and whitetip sharks in the 15 to 25-metre range. The topography here is more typical of central Galápagos than the remote northern islands — lower overall shark density than Wolf or Darwin, but more accessible and suitable for Advanced Open Water divers without the full pelagic open-water experience required for the northern sites. Bull rays and spotted eagle rays are common; schools of salema surge past in tightly packed formations. This site is accessible by day boat from Baltra airport or Santa Cruz and is often a first introduction to Galápagos pelagic diving.

## Planning Your Trip

Galápagos diving operates under strict National Park regulations — numbers are controlled, designated dive operators hold permits, and non-certified divers cannot enter the water at park sites. All diving is guided. Liveaboard permits are limited; boats are booked months or years in advance for the best seasonal windows. June through December is the peak season for hammerheads and whale sharks, with colder and cleaner water; January through May brings warmer water but slightly lower pelagic density. A 7mm full suit is essential; a hood and gloves are standard for comfort. Advanced Open Water certification is the practical minimum for most liveaboard itineraries. Day-boat diving from Santa Cruz or San Cristóbal provides access to Gordon Rocks and Kicker Rock for Open Water divers.
