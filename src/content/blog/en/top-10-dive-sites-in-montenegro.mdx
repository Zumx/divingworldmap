Montenegro's Adriatic coastline is among the shortest in the region — barely 300 kilometres — but it encompasses two very different underwater environments. The open coast between Ulcinj and Bar offers warm, clear Mediterranean diving on rocky reefs scattered with Roman and Venetian archaeological traces. Then there is the Bay of Kotor: a deep, fjord-like inlet carved by tectonic activity, with water conditions unlike anywhere else in the Adriatic. Cold, nutrient-rich water wells up from depth in Kotor Bay, supporting cold-water species at Adriatic latitudes including forests of red and yellow gorgonian sea fans, extensive fields of yellow coral (Leptopsammia pruvoti), and patches of white dead man's fingers coral more typical of Norwegian waters. For divers seeking unusual Mediterranean experiences, Montenegro's wreck diving and Kotor Bay ecology offer something genuinely distinct.

## 1. Patrol Boat P-32, Bay of Kotor

The P-32 is the headline wreck dive of Montenegro. This Yugoslav Navy patrol boat was deliberately sunk in 2007 as an artificial reef and now lies at 24 to 30 metres at the bottom of Kotor Bay near Tivat. The vessel, 30 metres in length, sits upright on the sandy bottom with its superstructure beginning at around 22 metres. In the years since sinking it has been colonised by the cold-water organisms typical of Kotor Bay — gorgonians have begun establishing on the rails, and the hull sides are encrusted with the yellow cup corals that characterise the bay's deeper faces. Inside the wheelhouse, the original instrumentation is largely intact. Advanced Open Water level.

## 2. The Žanjice Tunnel, Cape Žanjice

Near the entrance to the Bay of Kotor at Žanjice Point, a natural underwater tunnel runs through the rocky headland at seven to 14 metres. The tunnel itself is around 20 metres long — short enough to be a straightforward swim-through — with walls densely colonised by cup corals, sea squirts, and the yellow encrusting organisms that thrive in the shaded cold-water environment. Conger eels are resident in the crevices at the tunnel's base. The site is accessible from the beach with a short surface swim. One of Montenegro's most photogenic dives for wide-angle work: the light at both ends of the tunnel with the coral-covered walls in between makes for a classic underwater composition.

## 3. Cape Platamuni Wall

Platamuni, on the open coast south of Bar, offers one of Montenegro's more exposed reef dives — a wall descending from five metres to beyond 40 metres, with the upper sections accessible to Open Water divers and the deeper drop-off requiring Advanced certification. The outer Adriatic exposure brings cleaner water and better visibility than the bay sites: 20 metres is common in summer, occasionally exceeding 25 metres. Loggerhead turtles are seen here with some regularity, and the deeper sections of the wall carry the sea fan coverage typical of the Montenegrin outer coast. Best dived from April through October. Surge can make the site uncomfortable in periods of southwest swell.

## 4. Kotor Bay: Yellow Coral Walls

The Bay of Kotor's deep walls are unlike any other dive site in the Adriatic: cold, dark, and profusely colonised by the yellow cup coral Leptopsammia pruvoti and the red gorgonian Paramuricea clavata. These species appear in the Mediterranean at depths where water temperature drops and light diminishes, but in Kotor Bay the cold bottom water brings them to depths of 15 to 25 metres — accessible to Open Water divers with good buoyancy. The experience of hovering before a wall of dense yellow cups interspersed with crimson fan corals is visually extraordinary. Multiple sites around the bay's inner rim offer this environment; operators in Tivat and Herceg Novi know the best walls.

## 5. Lustica Peninsula Reef

The Lustica Peninsula separates the Bay of Kotor from the open Adriatic, and its outer face presents a series of rocky reefs and small walls at five to 25 metres. The transition between the bay's cold ecology and the warmer open Adriatic creates a mixed site with characteristics of both: Mediterranean reef fish including dentex and grouper alongside cold-water sea fans and encrusting organisms. The site is accessible from the shore at several points on the peninsula. A good option for divers who have dived the bay sites and want to experience the contrast of the outer coast. Open Water level for the shallower sections.

## 6. Oliva Wreck, Bar

In the waters off Bar, a cluster of historical trading vessels rests in 20 to 35 metres of water from various periods — the most diveable is a mid-20th-century coastal freighter that settled on its port side. The wreck is not as intact as the P-32 but hosts a productive marine community including grouper, dentex, and the occasional lobster in the cargo hold. Visibility off Bar is typically good: the open Adriatic coast near the port sees 15 to 20 metres in settled summer conditions. Bar is a working port town with one or two dive operators; the diving infrastructure is less developed than Tivat but the sites are less trafficked.

## 7. Bojana River Delta Reef, Near Ulcinj

Near Montenegro's southern border with Albania, the Bojana River creates a delta where freshwater meets the Adriatic. The resulting nutrient-rich coastal water supports a distinctive reef community at three to 12 metres — sea grass beds, sandy channels, and small rocky reef structures. The site is predominantly a macro dive: seahorses are among the species recorded here, along with pipefish, flatfish, and a variety of crustaceans in the sea grass. Visibility is lower than open coast sites — typically five to 10 metres due to freshwater mixing — but the unusual ecology compensates. This is Open Water territory and a legitimate alternative for divers who prefer biology to topography.

## 8. Miriste Reef, Between Tivat and Bar

Along the coast between Tivat and Bar, the Miriste area offers a series of low-profile rocky reef dives at five to 20 metres that are popular with local divers and provide a straightforward alternative to the bay and wreck sites. Octopus are common on the rocky substrate, and the crevices in the reef system host a good population of Mediterranean moray eels and spiny lobster. Grouper of reasonable size hold in the deeper holes. A pleasant reef dive without exceptional features but with solid marine life density in comfortable conditions. Open Water level; best from May through October.

## 9. Ancient Anchor Field, Off Budva

Off the resort town of Budva, a scattering of ancient stone anchors on the sandy seafloor at 15 to 22 metres marks the likely position of an ancient harbour or anchorage point. Individual anchors are impressive in scale, with stock anchors of Hellenistic or Roman period dimensions arranged in a rough pattern consistent with a fleet anchorage rather than a single shipwreck. The archaeological context is not formally excavated or protected in the manner of some Adriatic sites; diving the area is permitted but touching or removing material is prohibited. A compelling dive for the historically inclined, combined with a reef section at the Budva headland.

## 10. Tivat Night Reef

The shallow reef off Tivat marina, dived at night, is one of Montenegro's most accessible and surprisingly productive diving experiences. At depths of three to 12 metres, the site reveals a nocturnal community that includes hunting octopus, bioluminescent plankton through the summer months, and the spectacle of the Kotor Bay cup corals with their feeding tentacles extended — during daylight hours the polyps retract, but at night the yellow cups transform into polyp forests. Conger eels hunt actively at night, and squid are frequently encountered in open water above the reef. This is an ideal night dive for Open Water divers with competent buoyancy.

## Planning Your Montenegro Dive Trip

Montenegro's dive season runs from May through October, with July and August bringing peak conditions: water temperatures of 24–26°C, visibility of 15 to 20 metres on the open coast, and minimal weather disruption. May, June, and September offer excellent diving with smaller crowds than high summer. The Bay of Kotor is diveable year-round given its protected location, though winter water temperatures can drop to 12–14°C, requiring a 7mm wetsuit or drysuit. Most dive operators are based in Tivat and Herceg Novi for bay diving, with additional operations in Budva, Bar, and Ulcinj for the open coast. Hyperbaric facilities in Montenegro are limited; Dubrovnik in Croatia is the nearest reliable recompression option.
