Open a bottle of sparkling water and watch the bubbles erupt. While the bottle was sealed, the gas was dissolved invisibly in the liquid under pressure. The moment you released the cap, the pressure dropped, the liquid could no longer hold all that gas in solution, and it came out as bubbles. This is, almost exactly, what happens inside a diver's body during decompression sickness — the condition divers call the bends. Understanding it is not morbid curiosity; it is the physical reasoning behind every ascent rate, every safety stop, and every dive table ever printed. The bends is the central hazard that the entire structure of dive planning exists to prevent.

## Henry's Law and Nitrogen Loading

The air we breathe is roughly 78 percent nitrogen, a gas the body does not metabolise. At the surface, your tissues hold a small, stable amount of dissolved nitrogen and you never notice it. The trouble begins underwater. Henry's Law states that the amount of a gas that dissolves in a liquid is proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid. As a diver descends, the ambient pressure rises — by one atmosphere for every ten metres of seawater — and the partial pressure of the nitrogen they breathe rises with it.

The consequence is that the body progressively absorbs, or on-gases, additional nitrogen the deeper and longer the dive. This nitrogen dissolves into the blood and then diffuses into the tissues, with fatty tissues and the central nervous system holding more than others. As long as the nitrogen stays dissolved, it does no harm. The danger lies entirely in how it comes back out.

## How Bubbles Form

When a diver ascends, ambient pressure falls and the tissues become supersaturated — they hold more dissolved nitrogen than the lower pressure can sustain. If the ascent is slow, this excess nitrogen diffuses gradually back into the bloodstream, travels to the lungs, and is exhaled harmlessly. This is off-gassing, and it is the entire purpose of a controlled ascent and a safety stop. The lungs are the body's nitrogen exhaust, but they can only clear gas at a finite rate.

If the ascent is too rapid, the pressure drops faster than the nitrogen can be carried away in solution. Just like the sparkling water, the gas comes out of solution as bubbles directly within the tissues and bloodstream. These bubbles are the physical cause of decompression sickness. They lodge in joints, compress nerves, block small blood vessels, and trigger inflammatory reactions where the body treats the bubble surface as a foreign object. Where the bubbles form determines what symptoms appear.

## The Symptoms: From Niggles to Emergencies

Decompression sickness is traditionally divided into two types. Type I is the milder form, dominated by musculoskeletal pain — a deep, dull ache, classically in the shoulders, elbows or knees, that gives the condition its name as divers historically bent the affected joint to ease the pain. Skin symptoms also fall under Type I: itching, a mottled rash called cutis marmorata, and localised swelling. These are unpleasant and must be taken seriously, but they are rarely immediately life-threatening.

Type II is the serious form, involving the nervous system, the inner ear, or the lungs. Neurological symptoms include numbness, tingling, weakness, difficulty walking, bladder dysfunction, and in severe cases paralysis, when bubbles affect the spinal cord. Inner-ear involvement causes vertigo, nausea and hearing loss. Pulmonary decompression sickness, known as the chokes, produces chest pain, a dry cough and breathlessness as bubbles overwhelm the lung's filtering capacity. Type II is a medical emergency demanding immediate treatment.

Crucially, symptoms can be delayed. While some appear within minutes of surfacing, many develop over the following hours, and a significant fraction emerge between one and six hours after the dive. This delay is why divers are warned not to dismiss vague post-dive fatigue, joint aches or odd sensations, and why flying after diving is dangerous: the further pressure drop in an aircraft cabin can provoke or worsen bubbling.

## Why Some Dives Are Riskier

Not all dives load nitrogen equally. Depth and time are the two primary factors — deeper and longer dives on-gas more nitrogen, which is precisely what dive tables and computers track. But several other factors raise individual risk. Repetitive dives over several days allow residual nitrogen to accumulate. Cold and exertion alter blood flow and gas exchange unpredictably. Dehydration thickens the blood and is thought to promote bubble formation, which is why hydration before and after diving is repeatedly emphasised.

A particularly important factor is a patent foramen ovale, a small flap-like opening between the heart's upper chambers present in roughly a quarter of the population. Normally, venous bubbles that form even on safe dives are filtered out by the lungs. In divers with a significant PFO, these bubbles can cross directly into the arterial circulation and reach the brain or spinal cord, raising the risk of serious neurological decompression sickness. This is why divers who suffer unexplained Type II hits are sometimes screened for a PFO.

## Recompression: The Treatment

The definitive treatment for decompression sickness is recompression in a hyperbaric chamber. The logic follows directly from the physics that caused the problem. By raising the ambient pressure around the patient — typically to a pressure equivalent to eighteen metres of seawater — the chamber forces the offending nitrogen bubbles to shrink and redissolve into solution, restoring blood flow and relieving pressure on nerves. The patient breathes pure oxygen, which both accelerates nitrogen washout by steepening the diffusion gradient and delivers oxygen to tissues that bubbles had starved.

The standard protocols, such as the US Navy Treatment Table 6, then bring the patient back toward surface pressure extremely slowly over several hours, allowing the now-dissolved gas to be exhaled rather than re-bubble. Treatment is most effective when started early, which is why rapid evacuation to a chamber matters. While waiting, the single most useful first aid is to have the diver breathe the highest possible concentration of oxygen and to keep them hydrated and lying flat.

## Prevention Is the Real Cure

For all the sophistication of recompression therapy, the bends is overwhelmingly a preventable condition. Slow ascents, no faster than the widely taught rate of around ten metres per minute, give the lungs time to clear nitrogen. A safety stop, conventionally three minutes at five metres, provides a margin of additional off-gassing. Conservative dive planning, generous surface intervals between repetitive dives, staying well within no-decompression limits, good hydration and avoiding alcohol all reduce risk. And the firm rule against flying for a recommended interval after diving exists precisely because the science of bubble formation does not stop at the water's surface.
