Guadeloupe is shaped like a butterfly — two distinct island masses joined at a narrow mangrove isthmus — and its diving reflects the geographical complexity of that unusual form. The western wing, Basse-Terre, carries the heavily forested slopes of La Soufrière volcano and some of the best dive sites in the French Caribbean, most concentrated around the Cousteau Reserve at Pigeon Island. The eastern wing, Grande-Terre, is flatter and drier, its coastline bordered by a continuous fringing reef with conditions that vary from calm lagoon to exposed Atlantic. The outlying islands — Les Saintes, La Désirade, Marie-Galante — each add their own reef systems and character. The Cousteau Reserve, established in 1998, has given the reef around Pigeon Island twenty-five years of protected growth, and the results are visible: the coral cover, fish density, and sheer scale of marine life encounters in the reserve are among the best in the Caribbean. Water temperatures range from 25°C in February to 30°C in September. Visibility averages 20–30 metres, with the best clarity on the outer reef faces in calm conditions.

## 1. Cousteau Reserve — Pigeon Island (Îlet Pigeon)

The twin islets of Pigeon Island off Bouillante on Basse-Terre's west coast anchor the Cousteau Reserve and host what is arguably the most diverse single dive destination in Guadeloupe. The main dive site descends from the surface to 60 metres, with the most productive diving between 10 and 35 metres on the walls and terraced slopes. Large grouper are so habituated to divers that they approach within arm's reach; green and hawksbill turtles are resident on the shallow reef. Schools of Atlantic spadefish circle the reef in hundreds, and the walls carry dense sea fan and black coral growth from 20 metres downward. Open Water for the upper sections; Advanced for the walls. The reserve is often busy but its protected status means consistent quality.

## 2. Jardin de Corail (Coral Garden), Pigeon Island

The shallower section of the Pigeon Island reef system, called the Coral Garden, provides some of the best hard coral diving in Guadeloupe. Massive brain corals, elkhorn and staghorn formations, and extensive sheet coral create a reef topography of considerable complexity between 5 and 18 metres. The fish life here includes feeding French and grey angelfish, abundant yellowtail snapper, and the resident turtle population that defines Pigeon Island diving. The shallow depth and manageable entry conditions make this an excellent site for Open Water divers, newly certified visitors, and those transitioning from snorkelling. Night dives in the Coral Garden reveal sleeping parrotfish in mucous cocoons and active octopus.

## 3. Sec Pâté

Away from the main Pigeon Island area, Sec Pâté is a submerged pinnacle off Basse-Terre's west coast that breaks the surface of the water and drops on its seaward face to around 40 metres. The site is exposed to open Caribbean conditions, which means current varies and the blue-water side produces pelagic encounters: barracuda, jack schools, and occasional silky sharks circling at depth. The walls of the pinnacle are covered in black coral and large orange sponges; the shallower top section has healthy hard coral and abundant reef fish. Advanced Open Water recommended; conditions require assessment. One of the more exhilarating dives in Guadeloupe when current conditions are right.

## 4. La Perle Wall, Bouillante

North of the Cousteau Reserve, La Perle is a wall dive beginning at 8 metres and dropping to 40+, with some of the most impressive sea fan growth in the region. The wall faces northwest and catches the current from the Caribbean, which has produced outstanding filter-feeder communities: enormous barrel sponges, encrusting orange and yellow tunicates, and layers of encrusting corals over the substrate. Barracuda hover above the wall edge constantly. Reef sharks visit the deeper sections intermittently. Advanced Open Water for the lower wall; the upper slope is accessible to Open Water divers in calm conditions.

## 5. Les Saintes — Pain de Sucre

The island group of Les Saintes, 15 kilometres south of Basse-Terre, has a dive site reputation that exceeds even the Cousteau Reserve among regular Guadeloupe visitors. Pain de Sucre — the Sugarloaf — is a cove with a fringing reef that drops from 2 metres to 50+, with exceptional visibility and some of the most intact coral in the outer Caribbean. Nurse sharks rest on the sandy sections; large stingrays cruise the bottom; schools of tarpon move through the mid-water. The site is accessible by ferry from Basse-Terre or by chartering a boat from Trois-Rivières. Advanced Open Water for the deep wall. Day trips to Les Saintes combining diving and above-water exploration of the charming village are a Guadeloupe staple.

## 6. Piton de l'Est, Marie-Galante

The eastern outlier island of Marie-Galante has a lesser-known reef system on its eastern coast where the Atlantic swell and upwellings create a nutrient-rich environment sustaining large pelagic fish populations. Occasional whale shark sightings in the passage between Marie-Galante and the main island are recorded between February and April. The reef on the calmer western side offers accessible diving with good coral cover and turtle encounters. The island sees very few divers, which means fish populations are large and bold. A liveaboard or committed day-charter is the most practical way to dive here from the main island.

## 7. Pointe Noire Wall, Northwest Basse-Terre

The wall off Pointe Noire in the northwest is less frequented than the Bouillante area and shows it — larger fish, less diver impact on the coral, more unpredictable encounters. The wall begins at 12 metres and drops to 35; the upper section carries dense sea plumes and soft corals while the lower wall has black coral and sea whips. Eagle rays are seen here more frequently than at many other Guadeloupe sites, and the occasional passing dolphin school completes an exceptional dive. Advanced Open Water; best conditions from January through May.

## 8. Basse-Terre Harbour Wrecks

The port area around Basse-Terre town contains several semi-interesting wrecks in moderate depths, used primarily by local operators for training dives and as an accessible introduction to wreck diving. The structures are encrusted with colonial tunicates and hydroids, and the fish life around them — grouper sheltering in holds, schools of fusiliers circling the superstructure — is consistent even if visibility in the harbour area is reduced. Not a destination dive, but useful as a complement to reef diving if time and conditions allow.

## 9. La Désirade — Eastern Wall

The easternmost island in the Guadeloupe archipelago, La Désirade is rarely visited by divers despite having one of the most dramatic underwater topographies in the region. The eastern face of the island, exposed to the full force of the Atlantic, drops from the surface in a near-vertical wall with exceptional fish density in the current-swept sections. Nurse sharks are frequently found at depth. The island is accessible by ferry from Pointe-à-Pitre; diving requires either a local guide or a boat-based operator willing to make the crossing. Advanced Open Water minimum; conditions on the exposed eastern face require experience and careful planning.

## 10. Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin, Northern Basse-Terre

The mangrove-lined lagoon of Grand Cul-de-Sac Marin in the channel between Basse-Terre and Grande-Terre is a protected biosphere reserve that serves multiple diving purposes: it is a training area for beginners, a nursery reef for juvenile fish of many species, and a snorkelling destination of considerable biological interest. The water is shallow — typically 3–10 metres — and visibility is lower than the outer reef, but the biodiversity of the mangrove-adjacent reef is high. Seahorses and frogfish are reliably found in the sponge communities; the transition from mangrove root to reef occurs within a single dive. Open Water level; an important ecological context for understanding the entire Guadeloupe reef system.

## Practical Information

Guadeloupe diving is accessible year-round, with the calmest Caribbean Sea conditions from January through May. The summer and autumn season (June–November) brings rain and occasional tropical storms, but the Cousteau Reserve on the leeward west coast remains diveable on most days even during poor weather. Multiple dive operators are concentrated around Bouillante, making the Pigeon Island area the logical base for a diving holiday. Equipment rental is widely available; PADI and SSI certifications are catered for.
