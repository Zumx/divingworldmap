## The Animal That Stops Divers Cold

Few things derail a dive plan as reliably as a nudibranch. You are moving along a coral slope at 18 metres, trying to cover ground before the current picks up, when a thumbnail-sized creature on a piece of rubble freezes the entire group. Someone signals the stop. Cameras appear. Twenty minutes pass. The nudibranch has not moved. This is a scene repeated daily in the waters around Lembeh Strait, in Anilao Bay, across the Coral Triangle and on temperate reefs as cold and distant as the Scottish sea lochs. The animals have a grip on divers that is genuinely difficult to explain to non-divers and entirely obvious to anyone who has ever seen one.

Nudibranchs are shell-less marine gastropods — sea slugs — belonging to the order Nudibranchia. There are more than 3,000 described species, with new ones still being formally named each year. They range from under a centimetre to over 30 centimetres in length. They occur from the intertidal zone to depths beyond 700 metres. Their colours and structures are not random: they are functional, communicating toxicity, providing camouflage, or advertising chemical defences harvested from their prey.

## The Two Great Suborders

The most useful structural distinction for a diver trying to identify nudibranchs in the water is the division between dorid nudibranchs and aeolid nudibranchs. These two groups look and behave differently, and recognising which one you are looking at narrows identification enormously.

Dorid nudibranchs have a smooth or bumpy mantle — the fleshy outer surface — and carry their gills in a rosette arrangement on their backs, towards the posterior end. These gill plumes, called the branchial plume, can often be retracted into a pocket when the animal is disturbed. Dorids tend to be broader and flatter in profile. Classic examples include the Spanish dancer (Hexabranchus sanguineus), which can reach 30 centimetres and whose vivid red-and-white coloration makes it one of the most recognisable nudibranchs on Indo-Pacific reefs, and the many Chromodoris species — compact, oval animals with precise patterns of purple, white and yellow stripes that appear so consistently across individuals that identification to species level is often possible in the field.

Aeolid nudibranchs are covered in structures called cerata — finger-like projections that cover the upper surface of the body and serve as organs of respiration, digestion and defence. Many aeolids feed on cnidarians and are capable of sequestering the stinging cells (nematocysts) from their prey and redeploying them in the tips of the cerata as a chemical deterrent. The Flabellina genus, with its wine-red or purple cerata arranged in neat clusters, is a favourite among temperate-water macro photographers. Glaucus atlanticus, the pelagic blue sea dragon, is technically an aeolid, though it lives at the ocean surface and is unlikely to be encountered on a reef dive.

## Key Features to Observe Underwater

Identifying nudibranchs in the field requires a systematic look at several features. The first is overall body shape and length — a rough estimate in centimetres. The second is the arrangement and colour of the gills (in dorids) or cerata (in aeolids). Third, note the rhinophores: the paired sensory organs projecting from the head, which are present in all nudibranchs and are often distinctively coloured or textured. In many Chromodoris species the rhinophores are ringed in a contrasting colour that matches the gill plumes, providing a useful secondary identification marker.

Pay attention to the substrate the animal is resting on, because many nudibranchs are dietary specialists found exclusively on specific prey organisms. A white-and-orange nudibranch on a hydroid colony is almost certainly feeding there. A Phyllodesmium species will rarely be found far from its octocoral prey. Substrate context can distinguish between two species with similar colouration that have entirely different food preferences.

Photographs are invaluable and should be taken from directly above and from the side if possible. The underside of the foot is diagnostic in some genera but rarely photographable in the field. Documenting the date, depth, water temperature and geographical location adds information that can assist subsequent identification from reference materials or submission to citizen-science databases.

## Regional Hotspots and What to Expect

The Coral Triangle — encompassing the waters of Indonesia, the Philippines, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands and Timor-Leste — holds the greatest nudibranch diversity on the planet. Lembeh Strait in North Sulawesi is probably the world's single most famous macro diving destination, and nudibranchs are a primary reason. The black sand muck slopes from 5 to 25 metres host extraordinary densities of species, including multiple Chromodoris and Hypselodoris species side by side, the elaborate Nembrotha kubaryana with its green-tipped gills and dark spotted body, and genuinely bizarre forms like Melibe species, which abandon the conventional nudibranch body plan in favour of a hood-like oral veil for trapping crustaceans.

Anilao in the Philippines offers similar diversity in a rocky reef setting, with the advantage that the sites are shallower and accessible for longer dives. The Tulamben area of Bali, while famous for the USAT Liberty wreck, has extensive rubble slopes that are among the best nudibranch hunting grounds in Indonesia.

In the cooler waters of the northeastern Atlantic and Mediterranean, aeolids dominate. Fjords in Norway and the coasts of the British Isles host Coryphella and Flabellina species in remarkable concentrations during the late winter and spring months when their hydroid prey is abundant. Visibility can be excellent in these conditions, and water temperatures of 6 to 10 degrees Celsius are manageable with appropriate exposure protection.

The California coast, from Monterey south, carries a distinctive fauna that includes the banana yellow Peltodoris nobilis and multiple Hermissenda species. Japanese waters, particularly around the Izu Peninsula and in Sagami Bay, are documented by local photographers to a degree that has produced some of the most detailed regional identification resources available anywhere.

## Resources and the Taxonomy Problem

Nudibranch taxonomy is in a state of productive upheaval. Molecular phylogenetic studies conducted over the past two decades have reorganised the classification of the group substantially, splitting genera, synonymising species, and revealing that several widely-used names are actually composites of multiple cryptic species distinguishable only by DNA. The practical consequence for an identifying diver is that some names found in older field guides — published before roughly 2010 — may no longer be valid, or may refer to a different species than the one depicted in the photograph.

The most reliable current online resource is Sea Slug Forum and its successor databases, though the most actively maintained platform as of recent years is iNaturalist, where a large community of experts validates identifications and flags misidentifications. Terrence Gosliner and Gary Williams of the California Academy of Sciences have published extensively on nudibranch systematics and their work is the standard reference for systematic taxonomy. For field identification, the multi-volume Nudibranch and Sea Slug Identification series by Terrence Gosliner, Angel Valdes and David Behrens remains the most comprehensive regional guide available.

## Photographing Nudibranchs

Macro photography and nudibranchs are inseparable in the minds of many divers, and for good reason: a close-focus image of a Chromodoris annae or a Flabellina affinis reveals structural detail invisible to the naked eye and produces images that are genuinely striking to non-divers. The basic setup for nudibranch photography is a close-up or macro lens on a compact camera, or a dedicated macro port with a 60mm or 100mm lens on a mirrorless or DSLR system, paired with twin strobes positioned to provide even, shadow-free illumination from the sides.

Buoyancy control matters enormously at this scale. At 5x magnification, a millimetre of forward movement blurs the image. Hovering completely still while framing a moving subject on a current-swept slope is the practical challenge, and it is one that improves markedly with dive experience. The best macro photographers typically have excellent buoyancy control and a willingness to spend considerable time at a single small subject rather than covering ground.
