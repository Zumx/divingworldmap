Indonesia is the undisputed centre of global marine biodiversity. The archipelago of more than 17,000 islands sits at the apex of the Coral Triangle — the region spanning Indonesia, the Philippines, Papua New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands — and within that triangle, the waters of eastern Indonesia contain more coral species, fish species, and invertebrate diversity than anywhere else on Earth. Raja Ampat in West Papua is widely considered the richest reef system ever documented. Komodo, Bunaken, and the Banda Sea are names that appear on any serious diver's list. The USAT Liberty wreck in Tulamben, Bali, is one of the most visited wrecks in the world. Water temperatures range from 26°C to 30°C across most of the country, with cold upwellings to 20°C possible at thermocline-heavy sites in the east. The country demands multiple trips — a lifetime is not quite enough.

## 1. Raja Ampat, West Papua

The four main islands of Raja Ampat — Waigeo, Batanta, Salawati, and Misool — enclose a sea area that recorded over 600 coral species and more than 1,500 fish species in early surveys. The numbers have only increased since. Diving here ranges from the manta ray cleaning stations at Manta Sandy near Arborek to the powerful drift dives through the Cape Kri channel, where fish populations are so dense the water appears dark. Cape Kri held the record for the largest single-dive fish species count for many years. Visibility averages 15–25 metres; the best season is October to April. Liveaboard and resort-based diving available.

## 2. Komodo National Park, East Nusa Tenggara

The park protects the waters around the Komodo and Rinca islands, and the combination of cold, nutrient-rich upwelling from deeper water and a complex of current-swept channels creates conditions for extraordinary marine life density. Batu Bolong is a single seamount pinnacle where manta rays, sharks, turtles, and tightly packed reef fish schools create arguably the most action-packed dive in Indonesia. Manta Alley attracts reef manta rays year-round for cleaning and feeding. Currents at many sites are strong and unpredictable — intermediate to advanced experience is recommended, and local dive guides who know the site-specific current patterns are essential.

## 3. Bunaken Marine Park, North Sulawesi

Bunaken was one of Indonesia's first gazetted marine parks and remains one of the best-managed. The reef walls surrounding Bunaken Island drop vertically from a few metres below the surface to beyond recreational limits, with gorgonian fans beginning at 20 metres and a succession of hard coral formations throughout the shallower section. Dugong have been recorded in the seagrass beds of adjacent Mantehage Island, and the reef is exceptional for night diving, with lobster, flatworms, and uncommon nudibranchs emerging after dark. Water temperature: 27–29°C. Best season: May to October; the park is accessible year-round.

## 4. Lembeh Strait, North Sulawesi

Lembeh is the world capital of muck diving — the shallow, black-sand slopes of the strait between Bitung and Lembeh Island have produced more new species descriptions and first sightings of rare critters than any comparable stretch of water. Mimic octopus, hairy frogfish, rhinopias scorpionfish, and dozens of nudibranch species appear with regularity on these apparently featureless slopes. It is slow, close-focus, macro-intensive diving that bears no resemblance to a conventional reef dive. Visibility is modest — 5–12 metres — and the best divers here are those who move least. Dedicated muck dive guides are essential for getting the most from this site.

## 5. USAT Liberty Wreck, Tulamben, Bali

The USAT Liberty, an American army cargo ship torpedoed in 1942 and finally beached at Tulamben, was pushed into the water by the 1963 eruption of Gunung Agung and now lies 30 metres from shore at a depth of 3–29 metres. It is the most accessible major wreck dive in the world — a shore entry from a black sand beach leads directly to the bow. The hull is covered in spectacular soft coral growth and hosts one of the densest concentrations of reef fish in Bali, including a permanent school of bumphead parrotfish that circulates the wreck in the early morning. Night dives reveal sleeping fish and hunting crustaceans. Open Water suitable for the shallower sections.

## 6. Nusa Penida, Bali

The island southeast of Bali has earned a reputation for a specific and remarkable encounter: mola mola (ocean sunfish), among the largest bony fish in the ocean, visit the cleaning stations at Crystal Bay and Manta Point between July and October. At Crystal Bay, these bizarre, flattened giants ascend from the cold deep water to the cleaning stations at 15–20 metres, and divers descend to meet them. Temperatures at depth can drop sharply to 18°C or below when the thermocline is present, and currents at Crystal Bay require experience and strong navigation skills. Manta rays are reliably present at Manta Point year-round in gentler, shallower conditions.

## 7. Banda Sea, Maluku

The Banda Sea is one of the least visited major dive destinations in Indonesia and rewards those who accept the logistical challenge of reaching it. The waters surrounding the Banda Islands and the outer atolls contain spectacular coral gardens, large pelagics including thresher sharks and hammerheads, and World War II wreck material scattered through the surrounding islands. The volcanic cone of Gunung Api last erupted in 1988, and the fresh lava flows to the sea on the southern side have created unusual terrain where pioneer coral species colonise bare rock. Liveaboard access from Ambon or Sorong; best season April to November.

## 8. Alor Archipelago, East Nusa Tenggara

Alor, east of Flores, is the undersea equivalent of a find that appears on no map. The currents sweeping through the island channels deliver nutrients that support dense hard coral coverage and huge fish aggregations. The species count per dive at sites like Kal's Dream and The Cathedral rivals Raja Ampat, but with a fraction of the visitor numbers. Mandarin fish — small, spectacularly patterned dragonets that perform a courtship display at dusk — can be observed at several sites in calm shallows. Water temperature: 26–28°C. Access via liveaboard from Maumere or Labuan Bajo, or a direct flight from Kupang.

## 9. Wakatobi National Park, Southeast Sulawesi

Wakatobi — the name is an acronym for the four main islands: Wangi-Wangi, Kaledupa, Tomia, and Binongko — encompasses one of Indonesia's larger marine protected areas and is notable for consistent, high-quality reef diving accessible from the resort jetties. Roma Wall, Table Coral City, and the Banda Sea Wall outside the atoll provide a gradient from shallow training dives to advanced wall dives. The most celebrated resident dive operation here maintains a local no-fishing zone around its house reef that has produced some of the densest fish populations in the country. Water temperature: 27–29°C. Year-round access with best conditions May to November.

## 10. Derawan Islands, East Kalimantan

The Derawan archipelago off the coast of Borneo offers an unusual combination: manta rays, whale sharks (seasonal), one of the world's largest sea turtle nesting beaches on Derawan Island, and the spectacular Kakaban Lake — a marine lake similar to Palau's Jellyfish Lake but with four species of stingless jellyfish rather than one. The reef diving around Sangalaki and Kakaban ranges from 8 to 30 metres and is in good condition. Whale shark sightings peak between November and January around fish farm platforms at Berau. Access via charter from Tarakan or Berau. A drysuit is not needed; 3mm wetsuit is sufficient year-round.

## Planning your Indonesia dive trip

Indonesia is a year-round destination, but specific sites have optimal windows. Bali, Lombok, and Komodo dive best from April to November; the Banda Sea and Raja Ampat from October to April. The country is large enough that when one region is in monsoon, another is in calm. Liveaboards are the best way to access the outer island groups; resort-based diving suits Bali, Bunaken, and Wakatobi. Indonesia requires PADI, SSI, or equivalent certification for all diving — no certification, no dive, regardless of the dive centre.
