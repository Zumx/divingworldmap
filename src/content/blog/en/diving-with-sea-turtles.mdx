## Seven Species, Seven Contexts

Seven species of sea turtle exist and their distributions, depths, and habitats differ enough that knowing which species you are likely to encounter tells you a great deal about the dive. The green turtle, Chelonia mydas, is the most reliably seen in tropical reef habitats — it is the large, rounded-shelled turtle resting on sandy ledges at sites from the Great Barrier Reef to the Red Sea and Caribbean. Adults are almost exclusively herbivorous, grazing on seagrass beds in shallow water, which explains why you often see them near seagrass meadows at 3 to 8 metres depth.

The hawksbill, Eretmochelys imbricata, is the reef turtle par excellence — its narrow head and bird-like beak are adapted for extracting sponges from reef crevices, and it is most commonly seen working the reef face at 10 to 30 metres. The hawksbill's carapace has the overlapping scute pattern prized by the shell trade that drove it to critically endangered status; the IUCN listing remains critical endangered despite recovery efforts.

The loggerhead, Caretta caretta, is a powerful, heavy-headed turtle of temperate as well as tropical seas. Divers encounter it at rocky reef sites in the Mediterranean — Zakynthos, Kefalonia, and the bays around Dalyan in Turkey — and along the US Eastern Seaboard, South Africa, and Australia. Loggerheads eat crustaceans, molluscs, and jellyfish, and their crushing jaw musculature is formidable.

The leatherback, Dermochelys coriacea, is the largest living reptile and entirely unlike the hard-shelled species. It lacks a bony carapace, instead carrying a flexible mosaic of cartilaginous platelets covered by oily, leathery skin. Leatherbacks dive to extraordinary depths — beyond 1,000 metres in pursuit of jellyfish — and are found in temperate and even sub-polar seas. Divers occasionally encounter them in the water column in open ocean conditions, but this is an exceptional rather than planned encounter.

The remaining three species — the Kemp's ridley, the olive ridley, and the flatback — are less commonly encountered by recreational divers. Ridleys are the smallest sea turtles and primarily pelagic or found near estuarine shallows. The flatback is endemic to Australian and Papua New Guinean waters and relatively understudied.

## Where to Find Turtles: Reliable Sites

Some dive sites have acquired well-earned reputations for consistent turtle encounters, which reflects a combination of protected habitat, cleaning station activity, and resident turtle populations rather than lucky sightings.

Apo Island in the Philippines, a no-take marine reserve established in 1982, has one of the densest green and hawksbill turtle populations in Southeast Asia. The shallow reef slope at 8 to 20 metres is regularly patrolled by turtles resting on the coral, and the reserve's long protection history means individuals here show comparatively little fear of divers.

Sipadan in Malaysian Borneo is frequently cited as the most turtle-dense dive site in the world. The island is surrounded by deep water — the wall drops from the reef edge at 3 metres to over 600 metres — and the surface currents that wash around the point create rich feeding conditions. Green turtles sleep in crevices in the wall at 10 to 25 metres. On any given dive it is not unusual to pass more than twenty individuals.

Akumal in Mexico is a seagrass bay where green turtles graze in 3 to 5 metres of water and have become so habituated to snorkellers and divers that encounters are essentially guaranteed. The site is extremely popular and management of visitor numbers has been a long-running challenge — the turtle behaviour at busy periods is noticeably more agitated than at quieter times.

In Egypt's Red Sea, the lagoon at Ras Abu Galum and the reefs around Shark Bay on the Sinai peninsula provide reliable hawksbill encounters at moderate depths. The turtles at these sites are accustomed to divers but not habituated to the point of distress.

## Cleaning Stations and Turtle Behaviour

Like manta rays, sea turtles use cleaning stations. Green turtles particularly seek out surgeonfish and tang that pick algae from their carapaces, and hawksbills visit reef areas where cleaner wrasse service their shells and skin folds. Observing a turtle at a cleaning station is one of the most intimate encounters in reef diving — the animal holds position with slow flipper strokes while fish work systematically across its shell and neck.

When resting, turtles tuck themselves under ledges or into coral channels where they wedge their shells firmly. Turtles resting this way are not asleep in the human sense but are in a low-metabolism state. Disturbing a resting turtle causes it to abandon the site and burn energy in an unnecessary ascent, which is harmful particularly to animals in cooler water where metabolic costs are higher.

## Behaviour Underwater: What Works and What Harms

The guidance on diver behaviour around turtles is consistent across all responsible operators. Approach slowly and from the side, not from directly in front where the field of vision is poorest. Do not position yourself above a resting turtle and block its path to the surface — turtles breathe air and must ascend regularly. Do not touch or ride, which is illegal in most jurisdictions and physiologically harmful. Flash photography from close range affects turtles' vision and orientation.

Some of the most damaging interactions are well-intentioned. A diver who approaches a feeding turtle slowly and stays still is doing the right thing; a diver who shadows a turtle for ten minutes is creating a sustained stressor even if no contact is made. Five minutes of calm observation at three metres distance causes measurably less disturbance than continuous pursuit at any range.

Feeding turtles by hand is prohibited at virtually every managed site and for good reason: it alters foraging behaviour, creates dependency, and causes aggregations of turtles near diver entry points that increase contact risk. Sites where feeding has been practised extensively show markedly more aggressive and bold turtle behaviour, which looks impressive but represents a genuine welfare problem.

## Nesting Season and Terrestrial Encounters

Many dive destinations sit alongside significant turtle nesting beaches, and the overlap between diving and nesting season is worth understanding. Green and loggerhead turtles nest on sandy beaches above the high-tide line, typically at night, and hatchlings emerge forty to sixty days later in nocturnal mass emergences.

In Kefalonia and Zakynthos the loggerhead nesting beaches are within walking distance of excellent dive sites, and local operators run structured night-watch programmes that allow visitors to observe nesting without disturbing it. Similar programmes operate in Cyprus, Costa Rica, and along the eastern coast of Malaysia. The turtle conservation organisations at these sites are almost universally funded in part by dive tourism, creating a direct link between recreational diving and protection funding.

## Conservation Status and What It Means for Diving

All seven sea turtle species are listed by the IUCN as either endangered or critically endangered. The pressures are well-documented: bycatch in longline and trawl fisheries, plastic ingestion, coastal development destroying nesting beaches, and harvest for meat, eggs, and shell in parts of Asia, Central America, and the Pacific. Climate change is emerging as an additional stressor through its effect on beach temperatures — since the sex of turtle hatchlings is determined by sand temperature during incubation, warming beaches produce female-skewed populations.

The dive community has been an effective advocate for turtle protection. The scientific documentation of site-faithful individuals, the political constituency created by millions of divers who have experienced meaningful encounters with wild turtles, and the economic value that live turtles represent to dive tourism have all contributed to legal protection in jurisdiction after jurisdiction. Keeping that protection meaningful requires divers who understand what sustainable interaction looks like — and who hold operators accountable when they fall short.
