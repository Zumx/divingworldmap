## The Cost of Neglect

A complete set of scuba equipment — regulator, BCD, wetsuit, computer, mask, fins, SMB — represents a significant investment, and most of it will outlast its owner's interest in diving if looked after properly. The same equipment, neglected, can fail in the first few years, and some failure modes are life-threatening. Salt and chlorine are relentless. They corrode metal fittings, degrade rubber, stiffen fabric, and work their way into moving parts that need to move freely when you are 30 metres underwater.

The basic rhythm of gear care is not complicated: rinse after every dive, dry thoroughly before storage, and have critical life-support equipment serviced on a regular schedule. What makes the practice slightly involved is that each piece of equipment has different needs, and skipping steps because you are tired at the end of a dive day creates problems that compound invisibly until they surface as an equipment failure.

## The Regulator

The regulator deserves the most attention because it keeps you alive. After every dive in salt water, the regulator must be rinsed with fresh water — but only with the dust cap seated firmly over the first-stage inlet. Pushing water into an open first stage damages internal components and introduces moisture into the mechanism. Soak the regulator in a fresh-water rinse tank for several minutes rather than rinsing under a tap, which may force water through ports under pressure.

After rinsing, hang the regulator with the second stages lower than the first stage so any residual water drains out. Do not breathe or blow through the second stage while the first stage is unpressurised — drawing air without pressure can allow water to enter the mechanism through the downstream valve.

Service intervals vary by manufacturer and usage. As a general rule, annual servicing or after every 200 dives is appropriate for regulators in regular use. This means disassembly by a qualified technician, replacement of O-rings, seats, and springs as scheduled by the manufacturer, and flow testing before return. A regulator that has not been serviced in three years should be professionally inspected before use regardless of how it feels. Intermediate pressure creep — where the IP climbs slowly due to a degraded high-pressure seat — is not something you will detect until the regulator free-flows or, in a worst case, fails to deliver adequate gas at depth.

## BCD and Bladder

The BCD bladder must be rinsed internally as well as externally. After diving, add a small amount of fresh water through the oral inflation valve, swish it around to coat the interior, and then drain it completely. Saltwater residue left inside the bladder encourages mildew and can degrade the material over time. Inflate the BCD slightly before hanging to store — keeping the bladder slightly pressurised prevents it from folding and developing creases that can become weak points.

The dump valves and corrugated hose should be operated during the rinse process to ensure they move freely and the rubber seats are not stuck. Dump valves that stick in the open position are a real hazard, as is an oral inflation button that does not release cleanly. If any valve feels sticky after rinsing, soak and repeat. If the problem persists, the valve assembly needs replacement.

Annual servicing of a BCD by a qualified technician covers bladder integrity testing, valve inspection and replacement, and inspection of the harness webbing and buckles.

## Wetsuit and Drysuit

A wetsuit should be rinsed in fresh water, turned inside out and rinsed again, and hung to dry on a wide hanger — not a thin wire hanger, which creates pressure points and permanent creases. Store the wetsuit hung or loosely rolled in a cool, dark location. Folding it repeatedly along the same lines creates delamination. Avoid storing in direct sunlight; UV radiation degrades neoprene within months.

Neoprene conditioners are available and can extend the life of an aging wetsuit. A zip lubricant — beeswax-based products are standard — applied to the zip before and after every dive prevents corrosion and keeps the mechanism moving easily.

Drysuits require more involved care. The seals — latex or neoprene — must be inspected for tears and cracks after every dive, and latex seals benefit from occasional treatment with a silicone conditioner. The zip requires the specific lubricant recommended by the manufacturer; using the wrong product can degrade the seal. Valves should be tested before every dive and serviced annually. Store drysuits loosely hung, never folded under pressure — a pinched valve or crushed neck seal will be discovered at the worst possible moment.

## Dive Computer

Rinse your computer in fresh water and operate the buttons underwater during the rinse to ensure the button gaskets are clean. Most modern computers are rated to depths well beyond recreational range, but the weak points are the contacts around buttons and, particularly, the battery compartment. If your computer uses replaceable batteries, inspect the O-ring in the battery cap annually and replace it on schedule — a flooded computer is usually unrepairable.

Battery life in modern computers is measured in years under normal use, but aggressive use of the backlight, wireless transmission, and vibration alerts drains batteries faster. A computer that was reading full battery at the end of last season may be critically low when you pull it out for the next season's first dive. Replace batteries well before a trip, not at the airport.

## Mask and Fins

Masks accumulate salt deposits around the frame seal and nose pocket that can cause the silicone to degrade prematurely. A thorough fresh-water rinse after every dive is sufficient for daily maintenance. Periodically, clean the lens with a small amount of toothpaste to remove the silicone film that manufacturers apply during production — an essential step on new masks that causes persistent fogging if not removed.

Store masks in their original protective box or in a padded bag away from direct sunlight. A deformed or crimped mask skirt will not seal correctly. Fin straps — particularly spring straps — should be rinsed and inspected; a snapped strap mid-dive is an annoyance, but a broken strap just before entering the water wastes time.

## Cylinders

Steel and aluminium cylinders require annual visual inspection and periodic hydrostatic (pressure) testing according to national regulations — typically every two to five years depending on jurisdiction. Visual inspection checks the interior for corrosion, pitting, and moisture. A cylinder that fails visual inspection should not be filled. Boots and valve O-rings should be inspected and the valve serviced as part of the annual cycle.

## The Bag and the Travel Case

Even pristine gear can be damaged in transit. A padded regulator bag and a dedicated cylinder valve protector are not luxuries if you travel frequently. For longer trips, a semi-hard case for your computer and a roll-top waterproof bag for your wetsuit prevent most damage. Travelling with gear that you have invested in is substantially cheaper than renting at destination, and gear that you know performs reliably removes one variable from every dive.
