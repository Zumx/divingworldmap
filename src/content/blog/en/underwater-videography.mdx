Underwater videography and underwater photography share equipment, share water, and share the challenge of capturing subjects that move fast and live in a medium that destroys light and colour within a few metres of depth. But they differ in one fundamental respect that shapes every other decision: video requires you to hold steady, move slowly, and sustain a shot for seconds or minutes, while photography rewards the decisive moment and forgives everything else. Divers who are good at one skill often struggle with the other, at least initially.

The discipline that makes underwater video compelling — and distinguishes it from mere documentation — is storytelling. A sequence of clips showing coral, a turtle, more coral, a fish, and a diver is not a video; it is a slideshow with motion. A video is a composed sequence with a beginning, development, and resolution, even if the entire thing runs two minutes. Learning to see in sequences rather than in still frames is the central cognitive shift that underwater videography demands.

## Camera Systems and Housings

The camera landscape for underwater video divides cleanly between compact systems and mirrorless/DSLR systems, with action cameras as a third category.

Action cameras — GoPro and its competitors — are the entry point and are more capable than their low price suggests. Modern 4K action cameras resolve excellent detail, handle motion well, and fit into unusually small housings. Their limitations are in sensor size (small sensors perform poorly in dim or dark conditions), lens distortion (the wide-angle optics can make subjects look round when they are not), and audio (irrelevant underwater but important for topside narration). For reef overviews, wide shots, and reef-mounted perspectives, an action camera is entirely adequate.

Compact cameras — Sony RX100 series, Canon G-series, Olympus TG models — in dedicated housings offer larger sensors than action cameras and manual control over aperture, shutter speed, and white balance. The Sony RX100 VII has become a favourite in the underwater photography and video community because of its fast autofocus, good low-light performance, and a housing ecosystem that supports wet lenses. These systems are significantly more expensive than action camera rigs but produce noticeably better footage in the ambient-light conditions typical of tropical reef diving.

Mirrorless and DSLR systems in aluminium or polycarbonate housings are used by professionals and serious enthusiasts. Sony A7 and A6000-series cameras, Canon R-series, and Nikon Z-series all have established housing options from Nauticam, Ikelite, Subal, and other manufacturers. The image quality ceiling is much higher, and the ability to use full-frame sensors with fast prime lenses produces footage in very low light — deep wrecks, night dives, cave passages — that compact systems cannot match. The trade-off is size, weight, and cost.

## Lighting for Video

Water absorbs colour selectively. Red disappears within about three metres of depth, orange follows by five metres, and yellow by ten; below 15 metres most naturally lit footage appears as a palette of blues and greens. Video lights restore the absorbed colours, but their effect is limited to a cone of illumination that typically extends two to three metres from the camera.

The key parameters for video lights are lumen output, beam angle, and burn time. Wide beam angles — 120 degrees and above — are preferred for wide shots of reef scenes because they illuminate a broad area evenly. Narrow beams are used for lighting specific subjects at close range, similar to a spotlight. Most serious underwater videographers use two lights, one on each arm of a tray system, to eliminate the harsh central shadow cast by a single light source.

Video draws far more power than photography strobes because it must maintain continuous output rather than firing a flash. A 5,000-lumen video light might burn for 90–120 minutes on full power, which is adequate for a typical 60-minute dive but requires discipline: turning lights off between subjects extends burn time significantly. Charging protocols matter; most lights use lithium cells that must be charged in fresh water, and cells exposed to salt water without rinsing can fail before their rated service life.

## Settings and White Balance

Shooting in a log or flat picture profile preserves more dynamic range for post-processing, but footage shot flat looks washed-out and grey straight out of the camera and requires colour grading to look correct. For divers who intend to edit properly, log profiles are worthwhile. For those who want usable footage directly from the camera, shooting with a standard or vivid picture profile with manual white balance applied produces more immediately pleasing results.

Manual white balance calibrated to the specific depth and ambient light of each dive is more accurate than auto white balance for ambient-light shooting. The technique involves pointing the camera at a white or grey reference — a grey card, a white fin, the palm of a gloved hand — and setting custom white balance. This corrects for the blue-green cast without the need for video lights in the shallower sections. At depth, white balance correction cannot restore colours that have been physically absorbed by the water column; video lights become necessary.

Frame rates have practical implications for underwater footage. Shooting at 60fps or 120fps allows footage to be slowed dramatically in post to create slow-motion sequences — an effective technique for capturing quick fish behaviour or the motion of a passing manta ray. The trade-off is reduced low-light performance at higher frame rates. The industry standard for delivery in most editing contexts is 24fps or 25fps; footage shot at higher rates must be conformed or interpreted as slow motion.

## Stabilisation and Movement

Shaky footage is the most common problem in new underwater videographers' work, and it is directly related to buoyancy control and body positioning. A diver who is poorly weighted and compensating with fin kicks cannot hold a camera steady; the micro-adjustments of good buoyancy management translate directly into steady footage. Before attempting to film anything, establish neutral buoyancy, come to rest if hovering against a coral head is possible without contact, and breathe in a slow, controlled rhythm.

Camera movement — pan shots, tilts, slow approaches — requires deliberate, slow execution. The rule in video is that any camera movement should take twice as long as you think it should. A pan that feels slow to the diver will still look too fast on a monitor. The slow approach to a subject — starting wide to show context, moving gradually closer to show detail — is a fundamental technique that produces footage that can be edited as a sequence. The same subject filmed only at close range has no establishing context.

Stable platforms improve consistency. A tray system with handles on both sides reduces hand shake and allows both hands to steady the rig. Staying low and using a sandy bottom or rock as a physical resting point removes all motion from held shots. A reef hook used for fish behaviour photography — though incompatible with most reef surfaces — has an underwater video equivalent in a sand anchor pin that allows a stationary position in current.

## Editing and Storytelling

Raw underwater footage requires colour grading to remove the blue-green water cast and restore the coral and fish colours that the video lights and white balance could not fully compensate for. In DaVinci Resolve, Premiere Pro, or Final Cut Pro, the standard first step is to add an orange/warm correction using the Lumetri Color or Color Balance tools, then lift shadows and reduce green channel to taste. Footage shot at depth with no video lights often cannot be salvaged simply by correcting white balance; a full-spectrum video light is not a post-processing problem.

The structure of a complete underwater video has an opening establishing shot — usually a wide reef scene or boat descent sequence — a middle section of subject footage with varied angles and distances, and a closing shot that resolves the sequence. The minimum clip length that holds attention in a finished edit is approximately three seconds; clips shorter than this cut before the viewer has registered the subject. Clips longer than eight to ten seconds lose attention unless the subject is doing something that develops through the clip. Selecting footage for editing, rather than including everything that was filmed, is the craft of video production.

Music selection transforms underwater footage more than any other post-processing decision. Match tempo to the environment: fast cuts and upbeat music suits current dives and pelagic encounters; slow, ambient music suits macro subjects and night dives.
