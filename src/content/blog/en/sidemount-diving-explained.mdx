## From Cave Diving to the Open Ocean

Sidemount configuration originated in the flooded cave systems of the United Kingdom and later became the dominant equipment setup for cave diving in the restrictions of the Yucatan Peninsula. The motivation was practical: the narrow passages of Mexican cenote systems — sections of underwater cave where the roof and floor converge to leave a space measured in tens of centimetres — are physically impassable with tanks on your back. Mounting the cylinders at your sides, clipped to the harness with bungee cords at the valve and a cam-band clip at the bottom, allows a diver to detach and push cylinders ahead through constrictions too tight to pass otherwise.

From that specialist origin, sidemount has expanded into recreational and technical open-water diving at a pace that would have surprised the cave pioneers who developed it. It is now a mainstream configuration with training programmes offered by PADI, SSI, TDI, and every cave-specialised agency. The reasons for its adoption beyond caves are partly about the physical advantages of the configuration, partly about redundancy, and in some measure about the cultural gravity of a setup associated with serious, skilled divers.

## The Physical Setup

A sidemount harness differs substantially from a standard backmount BCD. The defining feature is the absence of a cylinder band at the back. Instead, the harness has a bladder — usually a wing-style bladder worn on the back — and a system of attachment points on the lower back and hips for the cylinder clips, and bungee loops at the shoulder for holding the cylinder valves under the diver's armpits.

The cylinders hang horizontally along the diver's sides when the diver is in a horizontal trim position, with the valves at the shoulder and the cylinder bases at or behind the hips. Each cylinder has its own first stage and second stage. The right cylinder's regulator is typically breathed from first, and breathing is alternated between cylinders to keep the pressures balanced — usually switching at each 30-bar drop or at a defined pressure interval. This keeps the cylinders equalised and ensures that if one first stage or regulator fails, the diver still has a complete independent breathing source on the other side.

The buoyancy control for most sidemount divers comes primarily from the wing and secondarily from the drysuit if one is worn. Some sidemount configurations include small pockets of buoyancy in the shoulder straps, but the core principle is that the cylinders themselves contribute buoyancy at the beginning of the dive and gradually become less buoyant as they empty — a factor the diver manages with the wing throughout the dive.

## Advantages in Non-Cave Diving

The case for sidemount outside caves rests on several genuine advantages. The first is the spinal load. Picking up and donning two back-mounted steel cylinders — 12 litres each at 200 bar weighs roughly 30 kg in air — requires significant physical effort and places mechanical stress on the back and knees during the entry. With sidemount, the cylinders are typically attached in the water after the diver has entered, or attached one at a time while the diver is seated on the boat, with the weight distributed differently and the spine not required to carry it upright.

For divers with back injuries, this is sometimes not merely a preference but a functional requirement. Several experienced divers who have continued to dive after spinal surgery or with chronic lumbar conditions report that sidemount made it possible for them to continue diving when backmount had become prohibitively painful.

The second advantage is trim. A diver with two cylinders hanging at their sides, properly trimmed, achieves a horizontal body position almost automatically. The weight of the cylinders is distributed along the length of the body rather than concentrated on the back, and there is no tank top extending above the shoulders to create drag or interfere with passage through restrictions.

The third is redundancy. Two independent breathing systems mean that a complete first-stage failure, which would end a backmount dive immediately, instead ends the use of one cylinder while the diver calmly switches to the other and plans an ascent with a full redundant gas supply. This is the same redundancy that technical backmount divers achieve with manifolded doubles and an isolator valve, but in a configuration that does not require modifying the cylinders.

## Gas Planning with Sidemount

The standard recreational sidemount configuration uses two single cylinders, typically 10 or 12 litres each. This provides 4,800 to 6,000 litres of gas for a diver starting at 200 bar — roughly equivalent to a set of standard backmount doubles, and considerably more than a single recreational cylinder.

Gas planning for sidemount mirrors technical diving practice. The diver plans the dive based on the smaller cylinder (in case they are slightly different sizes), tracks consumption across both cylinders, and maintains balance between them by breathing alternately. Turn pressures should be calculated to leave enough gas in each cylinder for both the planned ascent and an emergency ascent with the buddy breathing from one cylinder.

The practical result is that a sidemount recreational diver has considerably more gas available than a single-cylinder backmount diver, and the redundancy means that minor equipment issues do not require immediate surfacing. For divers making longer dives — photography dives, muck dives in Lembeh or Anilao where the interesting part of the dive is often the last 30 minutes — this can translate to meaningfully more time in the water.

## Who Should Consider Sidemount

Sidemount is not for everyone, and the configuration comes with its own complications. Setting up the harness correctly requires more time and familiarity than clipping into a standard BCD. The cylinder attachment system — typically two bungee loops at the shoulder and a clip at the lower hip — must be adjusted for each cylinder size and for different suits and underlayers. A poorly adjusted sidemount setup handles badly and is frustrating to dive; a correctly adjusted setup becomes intuitive after several dives.

The ideal candidate for sidemount training is a diver who has solid buoyancy control already — at minimum PADI Advanced Open Water or equivalent, with genuine comfort in the horizontal trim position. Attempting to learn sidemount while simultaneously developing fundamental buoyancy skills creates too many variables to manage at once. Most instructors recommend a minimum of 50 logged dives before starting sidemount training, and a meaningful amount of actual in-water experience in trimmed horizontal position specifically.

Cave divers typically use sidemount with stage cylinders carried in addition to the two main cylinders — a configuration called 'full cave sidemount' that can involve four or more cylinders and requires specific training to manage safely. The principles are the same but the complexity is substantially greater.

## Training and Certification

The PADI Sidemount Diver course and the SSI Sidemount Diving specialty are the most accessible entry points, designed for recreational open-water use with two cylinders in open water. Technical dive agencies including TDI, IANTD and GUE offer sidemount training integrated into their technical curricula, oriented toward cave and decompression diving applications. Cave agencies such as NSS-CDS, NACD and CMAS cave sections offer advanced cave-sidemount training for divers working in actual overhead environments.

A sidemount course typically runs over two to three days with four to six pool and open-water dives. The skills covered include assembling and adjusting the harness, cylinder attachment and detachment both at the surface and underwater, buoyancy control and trim with side cylinders, alternate-cylinder breathing procedures, and the fundamentals of managing two independent regulators. The investment in time and equipment is real, but for the right diver in the right context, sidemount opens possibilities that backmount simply cannot.
