Panama occupies a unique position in the diving world: two coasts, two very different oceans, and one of the most biologically productive seascapes on the planet. The Pacific side, cooled by upwellings from the Humboldt and Panama currents, delivers the big animals — whale sharks, hammerheads, humpback whales, and vast schools of fish that gather around offshore seamounts. The Caribbean side offers the classic tropical tableau of warm, clear water, healthy coral reefs, and colourful invertebrate life. Both are accessible from Panama City within a day's travel. Coiba National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, represents some of the most pristine tropical marine habitat anywhere in the Americas, and the Bocas del Toro archipelago on the Caribbean coast provides relaxed, accessible reef diving. ## 1. Coiba National Park, Pacific

Coiba is the cornerstone of Panamanian diving, a 270,125-hectare marine park protecting what may be the last major intact Pacific reef system in the eastern tropical Pacific. The island was a penal colony until 2004, and that isolation preserved its ecology spectacularly. Dives here regularly produce whale sharks, bull sharks, tiger sharks, Pacific spotted dolphins, and enormous schools of bigeye trevally and dog-tooth tuna. Sites like Granito de Oro offer shallow coral gardens teeming with reef fish; the outer walls descend to technical depths with visibility sometimes exceeding 30 metres. Water temperature averages 27°C, dropping to 22°C in January–March upwelling season. Advanced Open Water recommended.

## 2. Hannibal Bank, Pacific

Hannibal Bank is a submerged seamount about 50 kilometres offshore from Coiba, rising to around 18 metres below the surface. The productivity here is extraordinary: schooling hammerhead sharks gather in large aggregations from June through November, while humpback whales pass through on their southern migration from July to October. Whale sharks appear from June onward. The pelagic fish biomass — yellowfin tuna, wahoo, mahi-mahi — is exceptional. Visibility ranges from 15 to 30 metres depending on upwelling intensity. This is blue-water diving requiring advanced skills and a reliable surface marker buoy. Current can be strong and unpredictable. Advanced Open Water minimum; Divemaster guidance essential.

## 3. Isla Coiba, Shark Point

Within the Coiba park, Shark Point lives up to its name. The site concentrates whitetip reef sharks in numbers that would be extraordinary elsewhere; more than 30 on a single dive is routine. Nurse sharks rest along the base of the wall. The reef itself is dominated by massive coral heads with good coverage of elkhorn and brain corals. Creole wrasse and snapper school in the hundreds. Depths range from the reef crest at 5 metres to the wall base at around 25 metres. The site produces consistent encounters year-round. Water temperature stays above 25°C from May through December. Open Water certification sufficient for the shallower sections; Advanced for the wall.

## 4. Bocas del Toro, Caribbean

The Bocas del Toro archipelago on Panama's Caribbean coast offers a totally different dive experience — warmer, clearer water, living coral in good health, and a relaxed dive culture centred on the small town of Bocas del Toro on Isla Colón. Sites like Polo Beach and Hospital Point feature extensive coral gardens at 6–15 metres with abundant sea turtles, spotted eagle rays, and nurse sharks resting under coral heads. Visibility averages 15–20 metres in the dry season. Water temperature is a consistent 28–29°C. Suitable for all certification levels. The best season runs February through April; September and October can be rainy with reduced visibility. Multiple dive operators on Isla Colón offer day trips and certification courses.

## 5. Isla Iguana Wildlife Refuge, Pacific

Isla Iguana, off the Azuero Peninsula on Panama's Pacific coast, shelters one of the best-preserved coral reefs on the Panamanian Pacific. The extensive shallow reef crest at 5–8 metres supports impressive coral coverage, and the outer slope drops to 20 metres over a mix of rock, sand, and coral. Visibility can reach 20 metres in the dry season. Humpback whales are present from July to October, and it's possible to hear them vocalising during dives in the bay. Spotted eagle rays, sea turtles, and large schools of surgeonfish are regular sightings. Accessibility is straightforward from the town of Pedasi. Suitable for Open Water divers; surge can be significant in the wet season.

## 6. Isla de los Pájaros, Bocas del Toro

Isla de los Pájaros sits in the protected inner waters of the Bocas del Toro lagoon and provides some of the most luxuriant soft coral growth in the entire archipelago. The sloping reef runs from about 5 to 22 metres and is covered in orange and yellow tube sponges, sea fans, and sea rods that create a three-dimensional structure sheltering dense populations of reef fish. Frogfish in various colour morphs have been reliably found here. Currents are gentle; the site is suitable for Open Water divers and photographers who want calm conditions. Best visibility occurs in the dry months of February through April. Night dives here produce spectacular activity from octopus, lobster, and hunting moray eels.

## 7. Coiba: La Catedral

La Catedral is a dramatic underwater rock formation within the Coiba park, a series of massive boulders that create canyons and caverns accessible from around 10 metres down to 30. The structures are heavily encrusted with red, orange, and purple sponge. Schools of almaco jack circle overhead; moray eels occupy every crack. Nurse sharks and Pacific guitar sharks rest in the sand-floored caverns. The name derives from the cathedral-like light that filters between the rock pillars into the chambers below, giving the site its photographic appeal. Conditions are typically calm inside the park. Advanced Open Water recommended for the deeper sections. Dive with a Coiba National Park-licensed operator.

## 8. Portobelo, Caribbean

Portobelo on the Caribbean coast is a historic Spanish colonial port, and its bay hides several dive sites worth the drive from Panama City. The most interesting is the C-59 wreck, a deliberately scuttled Panamanian Navy vessel that lies upright at around 15 metres in the bay. It's completely penetrable and heavily colonised with encrusting life; barracuda hover over the bow and a large school of grunts resides in the engine room. The bay also has coral gardens along its western wall. Visibility is often 10–15 metres, less than Bocas, but the wreck more than compensates. Open Water with wreck specialty recommended. Year-round diving; the dry season (January–April) offers best conditions.

## 9. Isla Pacheca, Pearl Islands

The Pearl Islands in the Gulf of Panama are a 90-minute boat ride from Panama City and offer a diving experience that ranges from accessible reef dives to serious offshore pelagic encounters. Isla Pacheca's seamount rises to about 12 metres and attracts large shoals of jack and tuna, with hammerheads a real possibility from June through November. The surrounding rocky reefs host massive schools of fish that rarely see divers. Visibility is variable — 10–25 metres depending on upwelling — and water temperature can drop to 21°C in the January–March cold season. This is advanced territory; strong currents and open-water swells are the norm rather than the exception. Carry a surface marker buoy and dive with an experienced local guide.

## 10. Cayo Zapatilla, Bocas del Toro

The two Zapatilla Cayes within the Bastimentos Marine National Park host some of the most colourful shallow-water reef in Panama's Caribbean, but the real appeal for divers is the wall on the seaward side of Zapatilla Dos, which drops cleanly from 5 metres to beyond 40. The upper section is a coral-encrusted slope full of trunkfish, spotted drums, and juvenile hawksbill turtles. At around 20 metres the wall becomes more vertical, and large black grouper begin to appear. Visibility is typically 15–20 metres; water temperature 28°C. Accessible from Bocas del Toro town by boat. Open Water for the slope section; Advanced for the deeper wall. Dive within the park requires a park entry fee.

## Planning Your Panama Dive Trip

Panama's two coasts require different planning approaches. Pacific diving — Coiba and Hannibal Bank — is best from May through November, when visibility improves after the upwelling season and big pelagic concentrations are at their peak. Whale shark sightings at Coiba are most reliable from June to August. Caribbean diving in Bocas del Toro runs best from February through April and September through October, between the two rainy seasons. Panama City serves as the hub for both coasts; Coiba is best reached via Santa Catalina on the Pacific, which has a small but capable set of dive operators offering day trips and liveaboard services to the national park. Bocas del Toro has its own airport with regular connections from Panama City. Most operators carry PADI or SSI certifications, and rental equipment is widely available in both regions.
