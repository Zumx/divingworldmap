Sardinia sits in the heart of the western Mediterranean, a granite-and-limestone island ringed by some of Italy's clearest and most protected sea. Its diving divides naturally between the dramatic walls and pinnacles of the north-east — Tavolara, the Costa Smeralda and the Maddalena archipelago — and the protected reefs of the south around Capo Carbonara. Marine parks cover much of the coastline, and the result is fish life that has all but vanished from less protected corners of the Mediterranean: meagre, dentex and groupers the size of dustbins. Water reaches 25 to 26°C in August, visibility commonly passes 30 metres, and the granite topography gives the underwater terrain a sculptural drama found nowhere else in Italy.

## 1. Secca del Papa, Tavolara

The Secca del Papa, off the great limestone monolith of Tavolara near Olbia, is widely regarded as the finest dive in Sardinia. It is a series of granite pinnacles rising from beyond 50 metres to within 18 of the surface, draped in vivid red and yellow gorgonian fans and patrolled by enormous dusky groupers, dentex and barracuda. The site lies within the Tavolara-Capo Coda Cavallo marine protected area, which accounts for the density of large fish. Depths and currents make it an Advanced dive. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres. Best from May to October.

## 2. Capo Carbonara Marine Park, Villasimius

The marine protected area off Villasimius at Sardinia's south-east tip is one of the Mediterranean's success stories. Sites like Secca di Santa Caterina and the reefs around Isola dei Cavoli hold groupers grown so tame they hover at eye level, alongside meagre, amberjack and shoals of barracuda. The granite reef runs from 8 to 35 metres, suiting everyone from Open Water divers to those wanting deeper walls. Posidonia meadows and protected sand flats complete the picture. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres, and the season runs May through October.

## 3. The Nasello and the KT Wreck, Capo Carbonara

Off Villasimius, the wreck of a Second World War German cargo ship — usually called the KT, sunk in 1943 — lies broken across the seabed at 28 to 40 metres. Munitions, the hull plating and machinery are scattered and colonised by congers, groupers and shoaling fish, making it a compelling Advanced and technical dive within the marine park. The nearby Nasello reef offers a shallower alternative at 12 to 25 metres. Currents are usually mild but the depth demands good gas management. Visibility runs 15 to 30 metres, best in the calm summer months.

## 4. Grouper City, Capo Caccia, Alghero

The limestone cliffs of Capo Caccia near Alghero, on Sardinia's north-west coast, plunge straight into deep water and are riddled with caves. Among them, the site nicknamed Grouper City lives up to its name, with dozens of dusky groupers gathered on a reef at 18 to 30 metres. Nearby, the famous Nereo Cave is one of the largest underwater caves in the Mediterranean, a network of tunnels and chambers for trained cavern and cave divers. Reef sites suit Advanced divers. Visibility is excellent, often 30 metres. Dive May to October.

## 5. Nereo Cave, Capo Caccia

Grotta di Nereo, beneath the towering cliffs of Capo Caccia, is the largest sea cave in the Mediterranean open to divers — a vast system with two main entrances, multiple chambers and tunnels carpeted in red coral and sponge. Sections of the cave can be dived by experienced Open Water divers with a guide, following well-lit galleries between 12 and 30 metres, while the deeper passages are cave-diving territory. The play of light through the openings is spectacular. Boats run from Alghero and Porto Conte. Best conditions are the settled summer months.

## 6. Isola di Mortorio, Costa Smeralda

Off the glamorous Costa Smeralda near Porto Cervo, the granite island of Mortorio offers some of north-east Sardinia's most photogenic diving. Boulder fields and reefs descend from 10 to 35 metres through clear, bright water, with groupers, octopus, moray eels and shoals of bream. The granite sculpting — arches, gullies and giant rounded boulders — is the signature of the Costa Smeralda underwater. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility frequently passes 30 metres in summer, and the area is busiest, and warmest, from June to September.

## 7. Secca di Tre Frati, La Maddalena

The Maddalena archipelago, a national park between Sardinia and Corsica, holds dozens of granite reefs and pinnacles. The Secca di Tre Frati is a standout, with three rocky peaks rising to around 12 metres and dropping past 40, washed by current that brings in dentex, amberjack and barracuda. Gorgonians and red coral cling to the deeper rock. The site suits Advanced divers comfortable with depth and current; shallower reefs in the park welcome Open Water divers. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres. The park is dived May through October from La Maddalena and Palau.

## 8. Punta Coda Cavallo, San Teodoro

Within the Tavolara marine park near San Teodoro, Punta Coda Cavallo offers gentler, shallower diving than the offshore seccas — granite reef and posidonia from 6 to 20 metres, busy with octopus, cuttlefish, scorpionfish and young groupers. The protected status means even these shallow sites teem with life, and the calm, accessible conditions make them ideal for Open Water divers and relaxed second dives. Visibility runs 15 to 25 metres. The sheltered bays here are diveable across a long season from April to November.

## 9. Relitto dell'Angelika, Capo Testa

Off Capo Testa at Sardinia's northern tip near Santa Teresa Gallura, the Angelika is a steel cargo wreck lying in 20 to 35 metres among the dramatic wind-sculpted granite for which the cape is famous. The hull is intact enough to penetrate in places for trained divers, and congers, groupers and shoaling fish have made it home. The surrounding reefs of Capo Testa offer granite arches and swim-throughs in clear water. The wreck suits Advanced divers; the reefs welcome all levels. Visibility is typically 20 to 30 metres, best in summer.

## 10. Cala Gonone and the Gulf of Orosei

On Sardinia's wild east coast, the limestone cliffs of the Gulf of Orosei near Cala Gonone plunge into deep blue water riddled with caves — including the famous Bue Marino, once home to Mediterranean monk seals. Diving here ranges from cavern entrances and reefs at 10 to 25 metres to deeper walls, all in exceptional clarity beneath some of Italy's most dramatic coastal scenery. Groupers, lobster and shoaling fish are abundant. Sites suit Open Water through Advanced divers. Visibility frequently exceeds 30 metres. Dive May through October when the sea is calm.

## Explore on the map

Sardinia rewards choosing a coast: the north-east — Tavolara, the Costa Smeralda and the Maddalena — for granite pinnacles and big fish; the south around Capo Carbonara for protected reefs and wrecks; the west at Capo Caccia for the great Nereo Cave; and the east at Cala Gonone for caves beneath limestone cliffs. The season runs roughly May to October, warmest in August.
