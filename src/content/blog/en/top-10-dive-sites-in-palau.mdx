Palau sits at the western edge of Micronesia, and its reputation among serious divers borders on the reverential. The archipelago's roughly 340 islands are the remnants of ancient volcanic formations now eaten by erosion and coral growth into a labyrinth of channels, tunnels, lagoons and open-ocean corners. The diving is not gentle. Many of Palau's signature sites are driven by powerful oceanic currents that funnel through narrow passes and along exposed walls, concentrating pelagic life in ways that are difficult to find elsewhere. Grey reef sharks, white-tip sharks, Napoleon wrasse, barracuda, bumphead parrotfish, manta rays and the occasional hammerhead are not rare sightings — at the right sites and the right time of day, they are expected. WWII left the lagoon littered with Japanese and American wrecks that have spent eight decades developing into mature artificial reefs. Palau's government has been proactive about marine protection for decades, establishing shark sanctuaries and no-take zones that are genuinely enforced. Water temperature hovers between 27°C and 30°C year-round, visibility regularly reaches 30 metres, and the main diving season runs from October through April. ## 1. Blue Corner, Ngemelis

Blue Corner is arguably the most famous dive site in the Pacific and one of the ten best in the world by any serious reckoning. The site is a flat coral plateau that juts out from the wall of Ngemelis Island into the full force of the open-ocean current. When the current runs — which it does most of the time — the corner acts as a current aggregator, piling fish and predators on its exposed lip. Hundreds of grey reef sharks circle at mid-water while schools of barracuda, jacks and Napoleon wrasse hold station against the flow. Divers hook into the reef with reef hooks — small anchor devices that clip to an exposed coral head — and hang in the current watching the spectacle without fin-kicking. Depths range from 6 metres at the plateau to a wall dropping well past 40 metres. Advanced Open Water is the minimum; current experience is essential.

## 2. German Channel, Ngemelis

The German Channel is a cut dredged through the reef in the early twentieth century during Germany's colonial period in Palau, and it has become one of the most reliable manta ray cleaning stations in the Pacific. Manta rays arrive year-round — some resident individuals have been documented at the site for years — and hover over the sand at depths between 15 and 20 metres while cleaner wrasse work their gills and skin. Drift diving into the channel from the ocean side on an incoming tide brings the full current experience; diving it from the lagoon side on a more settled flow gives better visibility and steadier positioning for photography. Hammerhead sharks are occasionally sighted in the deeper water at the channel entrance. Advanced Open Water recommended; operators time the dive to the manta cleaning activity, which peaks in the afternoon.

## 3. Jellyfish Lake, Eil Malk Island

Jellyfish Lake is not a dive site in the conventional sense — scuba equipment is prohibited, so it must be snorkelled — but no honest account of Palau's underwater world can leave it out. The marine lake on Eil Malk Island is a meromictic basin whose lower layers are permanently anoxic and disconnected from the ocean. The golden jellyfish (Mastigias papua etpisoni) that inhabit it have evolved in near-isolation, and their stinging cells have atrophied to the point that they are harmless to humans. Millions of them migrate across the lake surface each day, following the sunlight to feed on photosynthetic algae. Swimming through a pulsing cloud of softball-sized jellyfish in flat calm water is one of the strangest and most memorable experiences in all of diving. The lake requires a separate Rock Islands entry permit on top of the standard dive fee.

## 4. Ulong Channel, Ulong Island

Ulong Channel is a narrow cut through the reef near Ulong Island that produces some of the most dramatic drift diving in Palau. On an incoming tide, the current funnels through the channel at considerable speed, carrying divers through a corridor of hard corals and sea fans while grey reef sharks and white-tip sharks patrol the channel floor. Bumphead parrotfish feed on the coral heads at the shallower sections of the channel in the early morning — their collective chomping and the cracking sound of their beak-like teeth on coral is audible from several metres away. Depths in the channel proper run from 10 to 22 metres; the outer reef wall drops much deeper. Advanced Open Water with current experience is appropriate; operators time entries to coincide with the peak flow.

## 5. Chandelier Cave, Koror

Chandelier Cave is one of the more unusual sites in Palau — a series of interconnected underwater caverns with multiple air pockets that extend back into the limestone above the waterline. The cave system flooded when sea levels rose at the end of the last ice age, and its air pockets still contain breathable air, giving the system its name: cave divers can surface inside and look up at stalactites and crystal formations on the ceiling. The entry is at 3 metres; the main chambers range from 5 to 15 metres. Salt and freshwater layers produce a halocline effect in sections, temporarily blurring vision as divers pass through the thermocline. Cavern-trained divers or confident Open Water divers with experienced guides can access the main chambers; full cave certification is required for the deeper extensions.

## 6. Blue Holes, Ngemelis

Four vertical shafts open at the surface of the Ngemelis reef at 5 metres of depth and drop together into a single chamber at around 30 metres before exiting through windows in the outer reef wall and dropping to open water. The shafts are wide enough to swim through comfortably; sunlight pours down through the openings in shafts of blue and white that give the site its name. The chamber at 30 metres holds an abundance of soft corals — yellow, orange and purple — and the openings in the reef wall look out into the blue with fish cruising past the frames. This is a decompression-safe site dived as a recreational penetration; operators spend only a few minutes in the deeper chamber before exiting through the wall. Advanced Open Water is recommended, more for comfort with depth and buoyancy than for any particular technical requirement.

## 7. Peleliu Corner

Peleliu, the southernmost major island of Palau, is a two-hour boat ride from Koror, and the commitment involved means it sees far fewer divers than the Ngemelis sites. The reward is some of the most intense shark action in the archipelago. The corner at the southwestern tip of Peleliu takes the full brunt of oceanic swells and currents, and the grey reef shark population is substantial — counts in the hundreds during peak current are not unusual. White-tip reef sharks rest on the sand along the slope. Occasionally, ocean white-tip sharks — a deep-water species — come up along the wall. Sea conditions at Peleliu can be rough; the boat ride back after a deep drift dive in swell requires a strong stomach. Advanced Open Water with current experience at minimum; strong current diver at best.

## 8. Iro Maru, Malakal Harbor

The Iro Maru is a Japanese naval tanker sunk during Operation Desecrate One in March 1944, resting on its port side in Malakal Harbor at depths from 12 to 40 metres. At 145 metres long, it is one of the largest and most intact wrecks in Palau, and the coral encrustation on its hull is among the thickest of any Palauan wreck after eight decades of growth. The engine room, holds and upper superstructure are accessible to recreational divers with wreck specialities; the deeper sections of the hull require advanced or technical training. Lionfish, scorpionfish, and large grouper inhabit the dark interior spaces. Visibility in the harbour is typically lower than the open reef sites — expect 10 to 20 metres — but the density of marine growth on the wreck makes it arguably better for close-up photography than any reef site.

## 9. Siaes Tunnel, Babeldaob

Siaes Tunnel is one of Palau's longer cave and tunnel structures, beginning at 18 metres on a reef slope and emerging on the open wall at 35 metres. The tunnel walls are covered in soft corals, cup corals and crinoids; the passage is wide enough for two divers side by side. At the exit, which opens onto the outer reef wall, grey reef sharks are frequently found hovering in the current below. The topside reef at Siaes is also excellent — a garden of hard and soft coral extending across a broad plateau from 5 to 18 metres — making this a site that rewards a full dive from the shallow plateau through the tunnel to the wall. Advanced Open Water and comfort with enclosed spaces are appropriate; the site does not require cave certification.

## 10. Ngemelis Wall

The outer wall of Ngemelis Island extends south from Blue Corner in a continuous drop from 3 metres at the crest to depths well below recreational limits. The wall is draped in a profusion of sea fans, sea whips and sponges of every colour, and the fish life is as dense as anywhere in Palau. Turtles are common along the upper wall; Napoleon wrasse patrol the crest looking for cleaning stations. Without the intense current of the corner itself, the Ngemelis Wall is a gentler dive — suitable for Advanced Open Water divers who want the visual spectacle of a great Pacific wall without the shark aggregation at the corner. It makes a natural second dive after Blue Corner, when the current has eased and divers are low on gas and high on nitrogen.

## Planning Your Palau Dive Trip

The best diving season is generally October through April, when visibility is clearest and conditions are most settled. The months of December through February offer the calmest seas. Between May and September, the southwest monsoon brings increased rainfall and reduced visibility on some sites, though diving continues year-round. Koror is the operational hub, with international flights connecting via Guam, Manila, Seoul and Taipei. Most diving is done from purpose-built speedboats with experienced local guides who know the current cycles at each site; liveaboard vessels also operate from Koror and allow access to the more remote sites like Peleliu. Entry fees, including the Palau Pristine Paradise Environmental Fee and the Rock Islands permit for Jellyfish Lake, add meaningfully to trip costs — budget accordingly.
