Jacques Cousteau famously called the Sea of Cortez the aquarium of the world, and the long arm of the Baja California peninsula that shelters it remains one of the most rewarding diving regions in the Americas. This is big-animal country: schooling hammerheads, whale sharks, mobula rays leaping in their thousands, and the boisterous sea lion colonies that turn every dive into a wrestling match with the curious young bulls. The water warms dramatically through the year, from a cool 18°C in winter to a balmy 29°C by late summer, and the diving ranges from the gentle reefs of the Sea of Cortez to the wild, current-swept pinnacles of the open Pacific. Above all, Baja is a story of recovery, and nowhere tells it better than Cabo Pulmo.

## Cabo Pulmo Marine Park

Cabo Pulmo, on the East Cape near the tip of the peninsula, is home to the oldest and largest hard-coral reef in the Sea of Cortez and one of the great conservation success stories on Earth. In 1995 the local fishing community, watching their catches collapse, persuaded the Mexican government to declare a no-take marine reserve. Within a decade the reef's total biomass had increased by more than 400 percent, the most dramatic recovery ever documented for a marine reserve. Today divers drift over coral heads swarming with jacks in tornado-like schools, encounter bull sharks, groupers and turtles, and witness firsthand what a protected reef can become.

## Los Islotes, La Paz

Los Islotes, a rocky islet at the northern end of Espiritu Santo island near La Paz, is home to a thriving California sea lion colony and the most beloved sea lion dive in Baja. The young animals are relentlessly playful, spiralling around divers, tugging at fins and blowing bubbles, while the bulls bark warnings from the rocks. The shallow, sunlit water makes it suitable for all levels, and the surrounding reef adds reef fish, eels and rays. It is one of the most joyous encounters in diving.

## El Bajo Seamount, La Paz

El Bajo, a submerged seamount in the open water off La Paz, was once world-famous for its schools of scalloped hammerhead sharks that gathered over the pinnacle in their hundreds. While the great aggregations have diminished, El Bajo remains a thrilling advanced dive where pelagic life — manta rays, schooling jacks, tuna and the chance of hammerheads — patrols the blue around the seamount. The depth and current demand experience, but the sense of being suspended in open ocean over a hidden mountain is unforgettable.

## The Whale Sharks of La Paz Bay

From autumn through spring, the sheltered waters of La Paz Bay host one of the most reliable whale shark aggregations in the world. Juvenile whale sharks gather to feed on the rich plankton in the shallow bay, and snorkellers — diving with them is restricted to protect the animals — can swim alongside these gentle giants in warm, accessible water. The encounter is strictly regulated, with limited swimmers and no touching, ensuring the world's largest fish continues to return to these waters each year.

## Espiritu Santo Island

The island of Espiritu Santo, a UNESCO World Heritage site near La Paz, is fringed with dive sites along its desert coastline. Beyond Los Islotes, the island offers reef dives, walls and the chance of encountering mobula rays, which gather in vast numbers in the Sea of Cortez and leap spectacularly from the surface. The contrast of stark red desert cliffs above and vibrant reef below epitomises Baja diving, and the calm leeward sites suit divers of all levels.

## The Sea Lions and Reefs of Loreto

Further north, the Loreto Bay National Marine Park protects a stretch of the Sea of Cortez studded with islands — Coronado, Carmen and Danzante — and rich reefs. The diving here is quieter than the busier southern hubs, offering healthy reef fish, sea lions, rays and the occasional pelagic visitor in clear, warm water. The combination of dramatic island scenery and protected reefs makes Loreto a rewarding and less-crowded alternative for divers exploring the central peninsula.

## Cabo San Lucas and the Sand Falls

At the very tip of the peninsula, the Cabo San Lucas Marine Park protects the meeting point of the Sea of Cortez and the Pacific. The site's most famous feature is the underwater sand falls, where streams of sand cascade over the edge of the Cabo San Lucas submarine canyon like an underwater waterfall, a phenomenon first filmed by Cousteau. The nearby Pelican Rock and Neptune's Finger offer easy reef and wall dives teeming with tropical fish, rays and the occasional sea lion, all within sight of the iconic El Arco rock arch.

## The Pacific Pinnacles of the East Cape

Along the East Cape between Cabo Pulmo and Los Cabos, a series of reefs and pinnacles offer rich diving in the warm, productive water where the Sea of Cortez meets the Pacific influence. Sites teem with reef fish, schooling jacks, turtles and rays, and the area is a corridor for the seasonal passage of larger pelagics. The reefs here benefit from the spillover of the protected Cabo Pulmo ecosystem, demonstrating how a single well-managed reserve enriches the wider region.

## Magdalena Bay and the Pacific Coast

On the peninsula's Pacific side, Magdalena Bay is renowned less for reef diving than for its extraordinary seasonal wildlife. From late summer into autumn, the open water off the bay becomes a feeding arena where striped marlin, sea lions, mobula rays and dolphins corral bait balls of sardines in spectacular hunting events, drawing snorkellers and freedivers from around the world. The Pacific water is cooler and wilder than the Sea of Cortez, and the spectacle of a coordinated marlin hunt is one of the ocean's greatest shows.

## Gorda Banks, San Jose del Cabo

The Gorda Banks, a pair of seamounts in the open water off San Jose del Cabo, rise from the deep to within recreational range and attract the big animals of the Sea of Cortez. Mobula rays, schooling fish, hammerheads in season and the occasional whale shark patrol the pinnacles, and humpback whales pass overhead during their winter migration. This is an advanced dive in blue water with current, where the reward is the open-ocean drama that defines diving at the tip of Baja.
