Cuba's restricted access to foreign investment for decades has produced an inadvertent conservation success: reefs that elsewhere in the Caribbean have been depleted by overfishing and coastal development remain substantially intact around the island's more remote coastlines. Jardines de la Reina, an archipelago of mangrove cayes and offshore reefs 80 kilometres south of the main island, is the benchmark — shark populations, fish biomass and coral coverage here are what Caribbean reefs looked like before widespread exploitation. On the western tip, María la Gorda offers wall diving with visibility that can exceed 40 metres. In the south, the Bay of Pigs adds WWII-era wrecks and pristine inland sea access. Water temperature holds at 24–29°C throughout the year; a 3mm wetsuit is comfortable year-round. The island is a liveaboard-primary destination for the best sites.

## 1. Jardines de la Reina (Gardens of the Queen)

Jardines de la Reina is the largest marine protected area in the Caribbean, encompassing nearly 840,000 hectares of mangroves, seagrass beds, sand flats and coral reefs. Access is strictly controlled and requires a liveaboard booking through one of the two authorised operators. The reward is remarkable: Caribbean reef sharks in numbers rarely encountered elsewhere, silky sharks in the blue water, the occasional bull shark at cleaning stations, and large schools of jacks and snappers on the reef crests. Crocodiles inhabit the mangrove channels. Dive depths run 10 to 25 metres for most sites; visibility averages 25 to 35 metres. Open Water certification covers the reef sites; the liveaboard format means all dives are guided. Best season is November through May.

## 2. María la Gorda, Pinar del Río

María la Gorda sits on Cuba's western tip at the entrance to the Gulf of Mexico, facing deep open water to the west and south. The wall drops from the reef crest at 5 metres to depths beyond recreational limits, and the upper sections of the wall between 10 and 30 metres are covered in black coral, sea fans and large barrel sponges. Visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres, and on exceptional days approaches 40 metres — the water here benefits from the circulation of the Gulf Stream and sees minimal runoff from the mainland coast. Whale sharks pass during their seasonal migrations, and eagle rays are common on the deeper sections of the wall. The Centro International de Buceo at María la Gorda is the primary operator; accommodation is on-site. Advanced Open Water is recommended for deeper wall diving.

## 3. Bay of Pigs (Bahía de Cochinos), Playa Girón

The Bay of Pigs, remembered internationally for the 1961 invasion, sits on Cuba's southern coast and has an entirely different character: a limestone shelf with vertical underwater walls starting in extremely shallow water. At Playa Girón and Playa Larga, the wall begins at 1 to 2 metres of depth — directly accessible from the beach — and drops to 35 metres or more in brilliant visibility. The site is notable for its absolute ease of access and for the density of the reef life on the wall. Spotted eagle rays, hawksbill turtles, queen angelfish and large grouper are regular sightings. The Centro de Buceo Punta Perdiz operates here and runs daily boat and shore dives. Open Water certification is sufficient; the ease of shore access makes this one of the most accessible wall dives in the Caribbean.

## 4. Cueva de los Peces (Cave of the Fish), Bay of Pigs

Cueva de los Peces is a flooded cenote — a solution hole in the limestone — connected to the sea at a depth of around 40 metres, sitting just beside the coast road near Playa Girón. Snorkelling and diving in the upper chamber, which is 70 metres in diameter and 70 metres deep, reveals a vertical wall inside the cave with thousands of fish suspended at different depths. The mix of freshwater lens at the top and saltwater below creates a halocline visible as a shimmering refraction layer at around 4 metres. The connected sea outlet means marine fish — including silversides in clouds, barracuda and grouper — pass through. Open Water divers can access the upper 18 metres of the cenote. Entrance fee applies; it is visited on day trips from the Bay of Pigs dive centres.

## 5. Cayo Largo del Sur

Cayo Largo is a small resort island 175 kilometres south of the main island, serving as one of the few Cuban dive destinations easily reached by international visitors without a liveaboard. The reefs surrounding the cay are in good condition — the remoteness and the marine reserve status of the surrounding waters limit fishing pressure — and sea turtles, in particular hawksbill and loggerhead, are almost guaranteed encounters. Wall diving on the southern face of the cay drops to 30 metres in visibility regularly exceeding 25 metres. Nurse sharks and Caribbean reef sharks patrol the reef edges. Dive operators at the hotel complex run daily boat trips. Water temperature stays above 26°C year-round. Open Water certification sufficient.

## 6. Playa Santa Lucía, Camaguey Province

Playa Santa Lucía on the northern coast is Cuba's most extensive resort beach and hosts a dive scene built around one signature experience: bull shark feeding dives. Local divemasters have been hand-feeding large bull sharks at a site called 'Shark's Friend' since the late 1980s, and the dives involve divers kneeling on the sand at 12 metres while sharks take bait from handlers. The ethical dimensions of shark feeding remain debated in the diving community, but as wildlife encounters go, watching bull sharks at close range in clear water is unambiguously powerful. Beyond the sharks, the reefs around Santa Lucía hold healthy coral systems and good fish life. Club Náutico Santa Lucía is the main operator.

## 7. Varadero Reefs, Matanzas Province

Varadero is Cuba's largest tourist resort, and the nearby reefs, while not as remote or pristine as the southern sites, provide solid accessible diving for visitors based on the peninsula. The nearby Cayo Piedras reef system at 10 to 20 metres holds sea fans, barrel sponges and the usual Caribbean reef assemblage: queen triggerfish, French and grey angelfish, schools of blue tang. The relatively high visitor traffic has affected some shallower sites, but the outer reef areas retain good condition. Dive centres operate from the hotel zone and can accommodate Open Water divers with no advance planning. Water temperature is 26–29°C from May through November. This is convenient rather than outstanding diving, but it works well as a supplement to a beach holiday.

## 8. Isla de la Juventud (Isle of Youth)

The Isle of Youth, 100 kilometres south of Havana, has historically been considered Cuba's premier dive destination before Jardines de la Reina was fully developed. The southern coast, particularly the Punta Frances marine park, holds extensive wall diving at 15 to 40 metres with sponge communities, gorgonians and regular pelagic passes. The site has somewhat reduced in prominence as access to more remote areas improved, but the quality of the diving remains high and the infrastructure — the Hotel Colony with its dedicated dive centre — is one of the most established in the country. Advanced Open Water divers will find the deeper wall sections worthwhile. Getting here requires an internal flight or a long ferry from Havana.

## 9. Trinidad — Costa Sur

The stretch of reef off Cuba's south coast near Trinidad, the UNESCO-listed colonial city in central Cuba, is accessible by day boat and offers a mix of shallow coral gardens and moderate-depth wall sections at 10 to 25 metres. The site is lightly visited and the reef is in noticeably good condition by the standards of accessible Caribbean diving — the combination of south-coast exposure and limited coastal development has reduced sedimentation and nutrient loading. Nurse sharks, hawksbill turtles and morays are the standard residents; schools of horse-eye jacks are seasonal visitors. Trinidad-based operators run half-day dive trips that can be combined with cultural visits to the city, making this a practical option for travellers not exclusively focused on diving.

## 10. Octopus Club, Havana Coast

The Octopus Club site accessible from Havana represents the capital's closest diving, suitable for those spending time in the city between trips to more distant sites. The reef here is at 10 to 20 metres in visibility of 12 to 15 metres — not spectacular, but honest urban-edge Caribbean diving with nurse sharks common on the sand, lobster in the crevices, and the reliably interesting octopus population that gives the site its name. The Club Náutico harbour provides the operator base. This is recreational rather than destination diving: useful for refreshing skills, logging dives or introducing non-diving companions to snorkelling before a more ambitious program.

## Planning Your Trip

Cuba's best diving season is November through April, the dry winter period when visibility is highest and weather most settled. The summer months — June through August — bring occasional tropical depressions but also warm water and fewer visiting divers. Jardines de la Reina liveaboard bookings must be made months in advance through authorised operators; the limited number of boats means spaces fill early in the peak season. For independent visiting divers, the Bay of Pigs, María la Gorda and Cayo Largo are the most accessible high-quality sites. A current Cuba tourist card and standard travel documentation are required; regulations on independent travel change periodically.
