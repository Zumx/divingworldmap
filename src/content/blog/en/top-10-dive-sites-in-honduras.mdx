Honduras contains some of the Caribbean's most rewarding dive destinations, concentrated on the Bay Islands — a chain of eight islands and roughly 60 cays sitting on the edge of the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, the world's second largest coral reef system. Roatan is the largest and most developed island, with a mature dive industry and direct international flights. Utila, smaller and quieter, has built a reputation as one of the most affordable places in the world to learn to dive and for the whale shark aggregations that visit its northern coast. Guanaja, the third main island, is the least developed and arguably the most pristine. Water temperature ranges from 26°C to 30°C year-round, and visibility across the bay islands averages 20–30 metres, often considerably more on the outer reef.

## 1. Mary's Place, Roatan

The most famous dive site in Honduras, Mary's Place is a series of parallel, vertical crevices cutting through the reef wall in the southwest of Roatan near French Harbour. The crevices are wide enough to swim through with arms outstretched, and the walls of the fissures are covered in orange and yellow encrusting sponges, tube sponges, and deepwater sea fans. The crack descends from 15 metres at the surface entry to around 40 metres at the base of the deeper fissures. Intermediate divers can explore the upper sections; the deeper penetrations require Advanced certification and experience with tight spaces. Best season: year-round.

## 2. West End Wall, Roatan

The wall that runs along the western end of Roatan from West End village to Sandy Bay is among the most consistently good reef dives in the Caribbean. The reef flat begins at 5–12 metres and breaks away into a vertical drop reaching recreational limits and beyond. Eagle rays glide along the wall face, Nassau grouper and black grouper hold station over the coral heads, and large schools of creole wrasse and blue chromis fill the water column above the drop-off. Multiple entries from the beach or by boat. Open Water divers can make excellent dives in the 10–20 metre range on the reef flat. Year-round diving with peak clarity April to September.

## 3. Whale Shark Encounters, Utila

Between March and May, and again in October and November, whale sharks aggregate in the warm water off Utila's north coast. The sightings are not guaranteed — these are wild animals following bait fish — but Utila has one of the highest year-round whale shark encounter rates of any dive destination in the Caribbean. The PADI and SSI whale shark snorkel encounter protocol is standard here; dive operators also run scuba dives during aggregations when the sharks are working deeper. The rest of Utila's dive sites are also first-rate, with the Pinnacles, Black Hills, and the northern channel walls among the best.

## 4. Cayos Cochinos, Bay Islands

The Cayos Cochinos (Hog Islands) are a protected marine reserve about 20 km northeast of La Ceiba on the mainland and are far less visited than the Bay Islands. Access requires chartering a boat from Roatan or La Ceiba, but the reward is some of the most undisturbed reef in Honduras. The fish populations here — particularly large snapper, barracuda, and the remnant grouper populations — reflect what the Bay Islands must have looked like before intensive fishing pressure and dive tourism. Visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres. No permanent dive infrastructure; liveaboard or day-charter access only.

## 5. CoCo View Wall, Roatan

The house reef of the long-established CoCo View Resort on Roatan's south shore is more than just a training ground. The wall drops cleanly from 12 metres to beyond 30 metres with a diverse coral community including brain corals, massive star corals, and an abundance of sponges. The resort has also sunk several vessel hulls to create artificial reef structures at recreational depths — the most visited is the Prince Albert, a cargo vessel sitting at 25–30 metres. The combination of natural wall and structured wrecks makes it a full day's diving without leaving the immediate area. Year-round accessibility.

## 6. The Pinnacles, Utila

A group of coral spires rising from 30 metres to within 8 metres of the surface on Utila's northern reef edge, the Pinnacles concentrate fish life the way seamounts do in the open ocean. Nurse sharks rest on the sand between the structures, Caribbean reef sharks circle the outer edges, and the densest shoals of grunt and snapper in the area crowd the spaces between the pinnacles at dawn and dusk. The dive works best in a gentle current that pushes bait fish through the site and activates the feeding behavior of the larger predators. Advanced certification recommended for the deeper sections.

## 7. Jade Trader Wreck, Roatan

This cargo vessel was sunk deliberately near Punta Gorda on Roatan's northeast coast in the early 1970s and now sits at 27–34 metres. The hull has had decades to accumulate marine growth, and the superstructure is colonised by barrel sponges, tube sponges, and black coral fans. A large resident green moray eel has occupied the same section of the hull for years and is approachable with patience. The wreck is far enough from the main dive centres that it sees fewer visitors than the West End sites, giving it a quieter atmosphere. Advanced certification appropriate for the depth.

## 8. Texas, Roatan

A broad, gently sloping reef platform southwest of West End, Texas is an excellent shallow dive that delivers more fish per square metre than many of Roatan's deeper sites. Flamingo tongue cowries dot the sea fans, cleaning stations on the coral heads see a constant queue of parrotfish and surgeonfish, and the sandy gullies between coral formations shelter spotted eagle rays resting on the bottom. Maximum depth around 16 metres makes it perfectly suited to Open Water divers and an ideal site for new divers making their first post-certification dives. Visibility is typically 20–25 metres.

## 9. Halliburton Wreck, Roatan

Deliberately sunk in 1999 to provide an artificial reef, the Halliburton is a large tugboat sitting upright at 24 metres on a sandy bottom on Roatan's south shore. The ship is the most popular wreck dive for beginners because the depth is manageable and the open interior is navigable without dedicated wreck training — though a torch is useful. The deck coral growth after 25 years is substantial, and a colony of black durgon triggerfish patrols the superstructure. Bottom time at this depth is generous for divers using air or nitrox, and there is enough to see for two relaxed dives.

## 10. Guanaja Wrecks and Walls, Bay Islands

The least visited of the three main Bay Islands, Guanaja has a steeper, more dramatic reef profile than Roatan or Utila. The walls on the island's north side begin shallower and drop harder, and the isolation of the island means fish populations are noticeably healthier — large grouper, dog snappers, and big-eye trevally are common where they have been fished out elsewhere. Several small wrecks lie in shallow water around the island. Accommodation and dive operations are sparse, so access typically requires staying at one of the small dive resorts that operate on the island. Best season: March to September.

## Planning your Honduras dive trip

The Bay Islands dive year-round, with the driest and calmest weather from February through April and September through October. Hurricane season peaks August to October and can interrupt operations for short periods, though direct hits are infrequent. Utila is the budget diver's destination, with very affordable certification courses and a lively backpacker scene. Roatan offers full resort infrastructure. The whale shark season around Utila from March to May and October to November is a specific draw worth planning around.
