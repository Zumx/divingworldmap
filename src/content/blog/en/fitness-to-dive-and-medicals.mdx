## Why a Medical Assessment Matters

Scuba diving places the human body under conditions that are physiologically unusual: increased ambient pressure, breathed gas at higher-than-surface partial pressures, physical exertion in a hostile environment, and the cognitive demands of managing equipment and navigation simultaneously. The consequences of certain medical conditions in this environment are more severe than they would be at the surface, and several conditions that cause no meaningful limitation in everyday life create genuine risk underwater.

The purpose of a dive medical is not to exclude people from diving. The vast majority of people who present for a dive medical are cleared to dive, including many with managed chronic conditions. The purpose is to identify the minority of conditions that require monitoring, equipment modification, or specific restrictions, and to give the diver and their physician the information needed to dive safely rather than guessing.

Most recreational certification agencies require candidates to complete a medical self-declaration form before beginning training. This is a questionnaire covering cardiovascular, respiratory, neurological, and other systems. If the candidate answers 'no' to all items, no further medical assessment is required. If they answer 'yes' to any item, they are directed to a physician for review before proceeding.

## Cardiovascular Health and the Diver

Cardiovascular disease is the most significant category of medical concern in diving because immersion in cold or cool water produces an immediate cardiovascular response — heart rate drops through the diving reflex while peripheral vasoconstriction increases cardiac work. The combination of cold-water immersion and physical exertion in a diver with compromised coronary circulation is the circumstance most frequently associated with cardiac events in diving fatalities.

Hypertension — high blood pressure — is probably the most common managed cardiovascular condition in the diving population. Well-controlled hypertension with no target organ damage, managed with medication that does not itself contraindicate diving (beta-blockers are tolerated by most divers; diuretics can be more problematic due to dehydration effects on decompression sickness risk), is generally compatible with recreational diving. The annual review of a hypertensive diver's fitness is sensible practice, because blood pressure control changes with age, lifestyle, and medication.

A patent foramen ovale — PFO — is a hole between the two upper chambers of the heart that fails to close at birth in roughly a quarter of the population. In most people this has no consequence at the surface. In divers, a PFO creates a pathway through which nitrogen bubbles in venous blood can pass directly into the arterial circulation, bypassing the lungs. This mechanism is associated with a subset of decompression sickness cases and is particularly implicated in neurological and cutaneous DCS. The evidence for PFO closure surgery improving diving outcomes remains debated, but divers with a known PFO and a history of unexpected DCS events should have the question reviewed by a dive medicine specialist.

## Respiratory Fitness

The lungs are the primary organ at risk from pulmonary barotrauma — the potentially fatal injury caused by gas expansion in the lungs during ascent when breath-holding is involuntary or when gas trapping occurs. Any condition that creates pulmonary air trapping — areas of lung that fill with air but fail to empty normally during exhalation — is a concern because the trapped gas expands on ascent even if the diver is breathing continuously.

Active asthma, particularly exercise-induced or cold-air-triggered asthma, is the most discussed respiratory contraindication. Many divers with well-controlled asthma, no recent exacerbations, normal lung function tests, and symptoms that are not triggered by exercise or cold air dive safely. The British Thoracic Society guidelines allow diving for asthmatics who meet specific criteria, including normal pre-dive spirometry and absence of symptoms requiring emergency bronchodilator use in the preceding six months. The decision should involve both a respiratory physician and a dive medicine specialist.

Spontaneous pneumothorax — a collapsed lung — has historically been considered an absolute contraindication because it implies a tendency to lung bleb rupture, and a pneumothorax at depth would be catastrophic. A single spontaneous pneumothorax following surgical repair, with CT confirmation that the affected lung has no residual blebs, may be compatible with diving under specialised advice.

## Diabetes and Diving

Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes are managed conditions that have been the subject of significant dive medicine research over the past two decades. The historical blanket contraindication to diving for insulin-dependent diabetics has been substantially revised by evidence that well-controlled diabetics can dive safely with appropriate precautions.

The DAN (Divers Alert Network) and UHMS (Undersea and Hyperbaric Medical Society) guidelines for diabetic divers are detailed. Key requirements include blood glucose stability, no history of hypoglycaemia unawareness, testing before and after dives, target blood glucose ranges for entry (typically 6 to 10 mmol/L with a stable or rising trend), a dive buddy who knows the diver's condition and the signs of hypoglycaemia, and a glucose source accessible at the surface. Depths above 30 metres are typically advised against due to narcosis effects on hypoglycaemia recognition.

An insulin-pumping diver faces the additional consideration of how to manage the pump underwater. Continuous glucose monitoring devices are generally not rated for dive depths and should be discussed with both the device manufacturer and a dive medicine physician.

## Ear and Sinus Conditions

Chronic ear problems — recurrent otitis externa, a history of perforations, or Eustachian tube dysfunction causing consistent equalisation failure — are among the most common reasons divers seek medical advice. An ENT review before beginning diving is advisable for anyone with a history of ear surgery, chronic ear infections, or significant hearing asymmetry.

Grommets — ventilation tubes — are placed in the eardrum to manage chronic middle ear effusion and do not allow equalisation in the normal way. Grommets allow pressure to equalise directly through the tube, which means descent is possible, but the continuous opening also allows water to enter the middle ear, creating infection risk. Diving with grommets is generally not recommended without specific medical clearance.

## The Practical Medical: What to Expect

A formal dive medical conducted by a physician trained in diving medicine — available through DAN-accredited providers, the British Diving Medical Committee, or specialist hyperbaric and dive medicine clinics — typically takes 30 to 60 minutes and includes cardiovascular examination, spirometry, resting ECG for older divers, and detailed history review. The outcome is a signed declaration of fitness or a recommended restriction.

The cost in the UK runs approximately 60 to 100 pounds; in Australia and the US prices vary widely. The expense is modest relative to the cost of a dive holiday and the risk of an undetected condition. Many dive resorts in popular locations have relationships with local dive medicine physicians, and same-week appointments are common in high-volume diving areas.

## Staying Dive-Fit

Fitness for diving is not the same as fitness in the general sense, though there is overlap. The physiological demands of a dive are often modest for a recreational diver in calm conditions — a 45-minute dive at 18 metres on a tropical reef with no current requires no more aerobic output than a brisk walk. The demand profile changes sharply with current, distance swimming, lifting heavy equipment, and multiple dives per day.

Aerobic fitness reduces the work of breathing at depth, which reduces air consumption and extends the dive. It also reduces the tendency to panic under stress and improves the ability to respond to unexpected conditions calmly. Swimming training is the most transferable, though any cardiovascular conditioning helps.
