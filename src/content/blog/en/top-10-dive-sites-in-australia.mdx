Australia holds one of the most diverse underwater landscapes on the planet. From the sun-bleached Coral Sea of Queensland to the kelp cathedrals of Victoria and the whale sharks of Ningaloo, the continent's diving spans tropical, temperate and pelagic realms within a few hours of flying. Water temperatures range from a bracing 10°C in winter Tasmania to a bathlike 29°C on the Great Barrier Reef, meaning the exposure protection you pack determines where you can comfortably dive as much as your certification level. Australia rewards returning divers — each region has its own character, its own seasonal windows, and its own suite of species found nowhere else on Earth.

## 1. Great Barrier Reef, Queensland

The world's largest coral structure stretches more than 2,300 kilometres along the Queensland coast and encompasses roughly 3,000 individual reef systems. Iconic sites like Cod Hole on Ribbon Reef No. 10 put enormous potato cod — fish well over a metre long — at recreational depths of around 18 metres. Osprey Reef in the Coral Sea pushes deeper, with walls descending past 40 metres and grey reef sharks patrolling the current-washed corners. Water temperature hovers between 24°C and 28°C. Best visibility and calmest conditions run June through October. Open Water certification covers most reef-top dives; some wall and outer-reef excursions benefit from Advanced Open Water.

## 2. SS Yongala, Queensland

The Yongala is widely considered the finest wreck dive in the Southern Hemisphere. The steel passenger vessel sank in a cyclone in March 1911 with 122 people aboard and was not discovered until 1958. It lies in 14 to 30 metres of water off Cape Bowling Green, roughly 90 kilometres from Townsville, intact enough that the hull form is fully recognisable. Bull sharks, sea snakes, giant trevally, marble rays and Queensland grouper are permanent residents. Currents can run hard — timed entries are essential — and the minimum certification requirement is Advanced Open Water with logged open-sea experience. Visibility is typically 10 to 20 metres. Dive it October through May before cyclone season peaks.

## 3. Ningaloo Reef, Western Australia

Ningaloo sits just off the Exmouth Peninsula in Western Australia and is unusual in that the fringing reef starts within metres of the beach at places like Navy Pier, which regularly appears on world top-ten lists for shore diving. Schooling fish, moray eels, turtles and reef sharks crowd the pier pilings at 6 to 20 metres in visibility that frequently exceeds 20 metres. The real draw, however, is whale shark season: from March through July, juvenile whale sharks aggregate at the surface after mass coral spawning events and snorkelling or diving alongside them is a controlled, permitted encounter. Water temperature ranges from 22°C in winter to 27°C in summer. Open Water certification is sufficient for most sites.

## 4. Fish Rock, New South Wales

Fish Rock is a sea cave that passes entirely through a sea stack off South West Rocks, roughly 440 kilometres north of Sydney. The 125-metre swim-through reaches depths of 24 metres and hosts grey nurse sharks — critically endangered in the eastern Australian population — in significant numbers between April and August. Wobbegong sharks rest on ledges, giant cuttlefish linger in the shallows, and loggerhead turtles are a reliable sighting year-round. The cave itself is dramatic enough that experienced Open Water divers can pass through with a guide, though the site benefits from an Advanced certification. Water temperature sits around 18–24°C depending on season.

## 5. Fly Point, Port Stephens, New South Wales

Fly Point is a protected marine reserve inside Port Stephens that is accessible directly from the beach — one of the most consistent and rewarding shore dives on the New South Wales coast. Depths range from 5 to 18 metres over rocky reef and sand, with leafy sea dragons, seahorses, blue-ringed octopus and Port Jackson sharks all documented regularly. Current is minimal on most tides, visibility runs 8 to 15 metres, and the protected bay means surface conditions are manageable even in light onshore winds. An ideal site for Open Water divers or those wanting a relaxed second dive after Seal Rocks nearby. Best months are December through May.

## 6. Blue Fish Wall, Byron Bay, New South Wales

Julian Rocks marine reserve at Byron Bay sits three kilometres offshore in waters where tropical and temperate currents meet, creating unusual species diversity. The Blue Fish Wall is a steep reef structure descending to around 24 metres where leopard sharks rest on sand between the boulders, loggerhead turtles graze on sponge, and pelagic species — including manta rays in summer — cruise the blue water edges. Visibility ranges from 10 to 25 metres. Dive operators run regular boat trips from Byron Bay township. Conditions suit Open Water divers from November through April; the rest of the year can bring surge and cooler water around 19–21°C.

## 7. The Wall, Merimbula, New South Wales

The Wall is a vertical reef structure south of Merimbula Wharf dropping to around 40 metres, festooned with orange cup corals and black coral trees at depth. Wobbegongs, blue grouper, cuttlefish and schools of sweep make regular appearances. Merimbula sits within a recognised aggregation area for grey nurse sharks, which appear here in the cooler months from May through August. The site is accessible by local dive boats; the Merimbula Wharf itself provides a gentler alternative for newer divers wanting calm water and pier fauna at 5 to 8 metres. Water temperature ranges from 14°C in winter to 22°C in summer.

## 8. SS Duckenfield, Hunter River, New South Wales

The Duckenfield was an iron paddle steamer that sank in the Hunter River in 1898 and has settled into roughly 10 metres of often-murky fresh and salt-mixed water. Though visibility can be limited — sometimes as little as 3 to 5 metres — this is a fascinating piece of colonial maritime history with significant structural integrity. The site rewards patient, observant diving: the boiler, machinery and hull plating are clearly identifiable. Unusual freshwater-marine transition fauna inhabit the wreck, including bull sharks which occasionally move upriver. Dive it in the warmer months and treat it as a navigation and wreck-appreciation dive rather than a visibility showcase.

## 9. Looking Glass, Shelly Beach, Port Macquarie, New South Wales

Shelly Beach at Port Macquarie is one of the north coast's most accessible marine parks, and Looking Glass is its standout dive — a reef system with swim-throughs and crevices at 12 to 20 metres that regularly hosts grey nurse sharks in winter. The site is a five-minute boat ride from the beach. Leopard sharks rest on sand patches, eastern blue grouper investigate divers with remarkable boldness, and the invertebrate life on the reef walls — nudibranchs, sponges, sea pens — is exceptional for macro photography. Water temperature ranges from 18°C in winter to 25°C in summer. Open Water divers are comfortable here in calm conditions.

## 10. North Rock, Montague Island, New South Wales

Montague Island sits eight kilometres off Narooma and is home to a large Australian fur seal colony whose members turn every dive into an encounter with acrobatic, endlessly curious animals. North Rock is the island's best-known dive site, descending over kelp-covered boulders to around 28 metres. Visibility can reach 20 to 30 metres when the East Australian Current pushes south. Beyond the seals — which come to investigate lights, tug fins and blow bubbles — the site holds blue grouper, moray eels and occasional school sharks. Dive season runs October through April for the best chance of warm water around 18–22°C and calm swell. Advanced Open Water is recommended.

## Planning Your Trip

Australia's east coast runs dive operators from Cairns and the Whitsundays in the north down through Brisbane, Sydney and Merimbula to the south. Western Australia's Ningaloo Coast is served by operators based in Exmouth and Coral Bay. The Great Barrier Reef's best conditions align with the dry season from June to October; New South Wales grey nurse shark aggregations peak May through August; Ningaloo whale shark season runs March to July. Liveaboard trips offer access to Osprey Reef and the outer Coral Sea. A 3mm wetsuit covers Queensland year-round; NSW diving from May to October warrants at least a 5mm suit, and Victorian waters demand a 7mm or drysuit.
