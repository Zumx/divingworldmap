Spain's coastline spans two entirely different seas and one ocean, producing diving conditions that vary more than almost any other European country. The Mediterranean coast from Catalunya to Murcia offers warm, clear water, protected marine reserves, and a dive infrastructure that is among the best-organised in Europe. The Atlantic-facing northwest and the Bay of Biscay bring colder, more current-driven conditions with far fewer divers and genuinely impressive pelagic encounters. Then, out in the Atlantic, the Canary Islands and the outermost island of El Hierro represent a subtropical environment that belongs more to the world of Macaronesian diving than to European diving — volcanic walls, manta rays, whale sharks and visibility that regularly exceeds 30 metres. Water temperatures range from 13°C in the Cantabrian coast in winter to 26°C in the Canaries in summer. [Open the map](/map) to locate the full spread of Spanish dive sites.

## 1. Cabo de Palos, Murcia

Cabo de Palos, the rocky headland at the eastern end of the Mar Menor lagoon in Murcia, is the best dive site on the Spanish Mediterranean coast by many reckoning. The Islas Hormigas marine reserve, which extends offshore from the cape, protects a series of rocky pinnacles rising from 40 metres to near the surface, draped in yellow and orange gorgonian sea fans that have developed to exceptional size under protected status. Grouper here are the largest on the Spanish coast, with individuals exceeding 40 kg resting beside the sea fans without retreating from divers. Barracuda and amberjack school at the surface of the pinnacles. The main sites are Bajo de las Amoladeras and the Isla Grosa channel, both requiring Advanced Open Water due to the 25 to 35 metre depths and occasional current. The dive centre density in the town of La Manga serves the site well; access is by short boat trip.

## 2. Medes Islands, Costa Brava

The Medes Islands marine reserve off the town of L'Estartit on the Costa Brava is the most heavily regulated and most studied dive site in Spain. The reserve was established in 1983, and the decades of protection are visible in the fish life: enormous grouper, large dentex and dusky grouper that are practically tame, and a density of marine life that makes Medes one of the outstanding Mediterranean dives regardless of European competition. The underwater terrain is a series of tunnels, caves and walls running between the seven small islands; the Cova del Dofí tunnel system at 18 to 22 metres is the most celebrated, threading through the rock with groups of grouper waiting in the blue at each exit. Visibility averages 15 to 25 metres. Water temperature reaches 24°C in August and drops to 13°C in February. Dive operators in L'Estartit run multiple daily trips from April through November.

## 3. El Hierro, Canary Islands

El Hierro is the smallest and westernmost of the Canary Islands, and its underwater park — the first marine reserve in the Canary Islands — has been protected since 1996. The diving is defined by the island's volcanic character: sheer black basalt walls dropping from the surface to beyond 40 metres, lava arches, lava tunnels and the occasional fumarole producing small bubbles from the seafloor. The marine reserve around La Restinga in the south holds large numbers of large pelagic species that have responded to decades of protection: amberjack in shoals, barracuda, moray eels of impressive size, and the stingrays and manta rays that cruise the deeper sections. Water temperature at El Hierro sits between 19°C and 22°C year-round — warm enough for a 5mm wetsuit. The Canarian diving schools in La Restinga cater to both beginners and advanced divers. Best season is June through November.

## 4. Canary Islands, Lanzarote and Fuerteventura

Lanzarote and Fuerteventura share the shallow straits between them, the Bocaina Strait, where Atlantic current pushes through and concentrates plankton, feeding manta rays that cruise the area seasonally and angel sharks that rest on the sandy bottom. Lanzarote's dive sites on the north and east coasts include the Museo Atlántico — the first underwater contemporary art museum in Europe, sited at 12 to 14 metres off the coast of Playa Blanca, with submerged sculptures that have colonised with sea life. Fuerteventura's eastern coast has cleaner Atlantic visibility and some of the best muck diving in the Canaries. Both islands have extensive dive infrastructure catering to the substantial tourist populations. Water temperature ranges from 18°C in February to 23°C in September.

## 5. Cabo Tiñoso, Murcia

A few kilometres west of Cabo de Palos, Cabo Tiñoso is a wild, barely-accessible headland with no road access and therefore almost no dive operators explicitly selling it. The dive sites around the cape are reached by boat from La Azohía or from the nearest marina, and they offer rocky reef diving at 10 to 30 metres with far less diver pressure than the Medes Islands or even Cabo de Palos itself. Cala Cerrada, accessible from the south coast of the cape, is a sheltered bay with a rocky reef descending to 25 metres that holds good-sized grouper and consistent octopus encounters. The site requires a boat for access, which the dive centres in La Azohía provide. Open Water divers can comfortably manage most of the site; the deeper extension toward 30 metres suits Advanced Open Water.

## 6. Tenerife, Gran Canaria

Tenerife is the dive-hub of the Canaries in terms of infrastructure, with the highest concentration of dive centres in the archipelago. The underwater landscape is dominated by volcanic structure — black basalt, lava tubes and open walls — and the resident fish population includes large angel sharks resting motionless on sand patches at 15 to 20 metres. Tenerife has reliable year-round conditions; the eastern and southern coasts are sheltered from the northeast trade winds that can make northern and western coasts difficult. Gran Canaria to the east has a comparable volcanic dive character with somewhat clearer visibility on the southern coasts and the additional draw of the Cathedral site near Mogan — a lava arch at 25 metres that frames the blue beyond it and is consistently occupied by barracuda and amberjack. Both islands are served by major international airports.

## 7. Cap de Creus, Costa Brava

At the extreme northeastern tip of Spain, where the Pyrenees meet the sea, Cap de Creus is a raw Atlantic-influenced cape that generates stronger currents and colder water than the sheltered Medes Islands to its south. The diving is consequently more demanding but the reward is greater: grouper, dentex and the large wrasse species that characterise the Western Mediterranean in their most unspoiled condition, because the difficult access limits recreational diver traffic significantly. The best sites are the outer points and underwater ridges at 15 to 25 metres that catch the current and accumulate fish. A natural park protects the terrestrial and marine environment. Dive centres in the town of El Port de la Selva run trips to the better sites; the crossing to the exposed points can be rough. Advanced Open Water appropriate for the current-affected outer sites.

## 8. Valencia and Costa Blanca Wrecks

The waters off Valencia and the Costa Blanca hold several diveable wrecks from the Spanish Civil War and WWII periods, the most accessible of which is the wreck Cala Campello off the coast of Campello, at 22 to 28 metres. The wreck is encrusted with gorgonians and populated by a resident congregation of large grouper and bream. CB Costa Campello and several other local operators based in the Alicante area run trips to this and other wrecks in the area. The visibility on Costa Blanca sites is typically better than in the northern Costa Brava, averaging 15 to 20 metres with peaks in October through November. Water temperature reaches 26°C in August and drops to 14°C in winter; a 5mm wetsuit covers the summer season comfortably.

## 9. Illes Columbretes, Castellon

The Columbretes Islands are a volcanic archipelago 56 kilometres offshore from Castellon, declared a nature reserve in 1988. The isolation and protected status have produced fish life of a scale rare on the Spanish Mediterranean coast: enormous dusky grouper in numbers, large schools of amberjack and barracuda, and a loggerhead turtle population that uses the islands as a waypoint on their Atlantic migrations. Diving is strictly managed and requires advance reservation through the reserve administration; day trips by fast boat from Castellon or Vinaros run from April through October. The central island, Grossa, has walls dropping to 40 metres with dense gorgonian coverage. The isolation means that any deterioration in weather turns the return journey into an adventure; most operators watch conditions carefully and will not run the trip in questionable forecasts.

## 10. Nerja Reef, Costa del Sol

Buceo Costa Nerja and other operators based in Nerja serve a section of the Andalusian coast where the mountains drop steeply into the Mediterranean and the underwater topography mirrors the land. Rocky walls and ridges run down to 25 metres in good visibility — the western Mediterranean here averages 15 to 20 metres clarity — with a mixed fauna that combines Atlantic and Mediterranean species in proportions that shift depending on season. The Cueva del Mar site near Nerja, distinct from the famous land cave, is a partially submerged cavern accessible by snorkel and scuba from the water level, giving it an unusual topside-subsea character. Water temperature peaks at 24°C in August; a 5mm wetsuit is sufficient from May to November.

## Planning Your Spain Dive Trip

The Mediterranean season runs from April through November, with the best conditions and visibility typically in October and November after summer algae bloom subsides. The Canaries are diveable year-round, with the best manta ray encounters around Lanzarote from May through September. The Costa Brava and Costa Blanca have established dive centres in most resort towns; the Canary Islands have the highest operator density of anywhere in Spain. Nitrox is widely available in the Canaries and at the larger Mediterranean centres. For the marine reserves at Medes, Columbretes and El Hierro, advance booking is essential in summer.
