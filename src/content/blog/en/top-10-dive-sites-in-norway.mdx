Norway is not a destination for divers seeking warm, clear, tropical water. What it offers instead is something categorically different: walls of enormous cold-water coral, fjord-channelled currents that sweep past with genuine power, an extraordinarily diverse temperate marine fauna, and encounters with marine mammals that would be headline sightings anywhere else in the world. In winter, the fjords of Troms and Finnmark fill with humpback and orca following herring, and divers can enter the water a few metres from surfaces churned by bubble-net feeding. The visibility in Norwegian waters is often better than its latitude suggests, and a drysuit extends the diving season to effectively year-round for motivated divers. This is a country that rewards people who come specifically to dive, rather than those who add diving as an afterthought. [Open the map](/map) to explore what is out there.

## 1. Gulen, Western Norway

Gulen Dive Resort has built its reputation on reliable encounters with wolf fish, Greenland sharks, cuttlefish, and a remarkable variety of temperate invertebrates. The resort sits at the confluence of several fjord arms, and the sites around it range from 5 metres to technical depths. The most celebrated is the resident population of large spotted wolffish that have been habituated to divers over many years — remarkably photogenic animals that sit in rock crevices at 15–25 metres. Norway's only dedicated dive resort, Gulen offers guided diving with local knowledge that transforms what would otherwise be a difficult site to navigate. Drysuit experience strongly recommended. Best season: year-round; summer for best visibility, winter for marine mammals.

## 2. Saltstraumen Maelstrom, Nordland

Saltstraumen generates the strongest tidal current in the world — a 400-metre-wide channel where the tidal exchange of the Skjerstadfjord creates currents reaching 28 knots during spring tides. Diving Saltstraumen is done at slack water and for a strictly limited window of perhaps 30–45 minutes. During that window, the underwater concentration of marine life is extraordinary: centuries of nutrient flow through the narrow channel has built reef structures covered in anemone fields, large kelp, and schools of fish too dense to see through. The visual impact is one of the most powerful in European diving. Advanced certification; experience with tidal diving is essential. Best season: June to September for best visibility at slack.

## 3. Gudrun Wreck, Oslofjord

The Gudrun is a merchant vessel lying in the Oslofjord at around 35 metres, largely intact and now hosting a community of cold-water species typical of the outer fjord: common wolf fish, lumpfish, large cod, and the sponge and anemone encrustation that accumulates on submerged structures over decades. The Oslofjord holds many wrecks from World War II and earlier maritime traffic; the Gudrun is one of the most accessible for day-trip divers from Oslo. Advanced certification recommended. Best season: April to October.

## 4. Hunden Reef, Rogaland

Hunden ('the dog'), off the Jæren coast south of Stavanger, is a submerged rocky reef rising from about 35 metres to within 10 metres of the surface. The pinnacle's exposure to open-sea swells and current brings nutrients that have built an extraordinarily rich community of cold-water corals, sea fans and filter feeders. Large Atlantic halibut have been seen on the sand at its base. The site requires good sea conditions for safe boat access and is typically diveable only during settled summer weather. Advanced certification. Best season: June to August.

## 5. Møkkalassa, Vestland

The Møkkalassa is an island group off the coast north of Bergen, where a series of walls and channels run to 30–40 metres in water that receives open-sea influence. The walls are covered in Lophelia pertusa cold-water coral formations — the same species that forms the great deep reefs of the Norwegian continental shelf, here accessible at recreational depth. Massive anemone fields in purple, orange and white cascade down the rock. Seals are regular visitors. Drysuit experience required; water temperature rarely exceeds 14°C even in summer. Best season: May to September.

## 6. Nykorallene, Northern Norway

Nykorallene ('the new coral reefs') was discovered by local divers relatively recently and represents a younger cold-water coral structure compared to the ancient reefs of the Nordfjord. What makes it compelling is its accessibility and the density of marine life associated with the growing reef: redfish, wolffish, large ling and various species of rockfish inhabit the coral structure. The site is located in northern Norway where winter brings darkness and operational challenges, but summer diving in the midnight sun is a genuinely unique experience. Advanced certification. Best season: June to August.

## 7. Lektern, Trøndelag

Lektern is a popular recreational dive site in central Norway that delivers excellent temperate marine biology within reasonable reach of Trondheim. Rocky walls descend to about 25 metres, covered in soft corals, plumose anemones and kelp to the photic zone. Sea trout are seen in the shallower water alongside various wrasse species. The site serves as a benchmark for Norwegian recreational diving — accessible enough for Open Water certified divers with drysuit experience, rewarding enough to bring back experienced local divers repeatedly. Best season: May to October.

## 8. Trolla, Møre og Romsdal

Trolla is a rock formation in the Møre region where the wall descends steeply to around 40 metres in water that receives good oceanic influence. The most dramatic feature is the undercut wall at 20–30 metres, where large plumose anemones form dense curtains and Atlantic wolffish occupy virtually every crevice. Edible crabs of impressive size move across the rock face. The site can be diveable in relatively poor surface conditions as the terrain provides shelter from the prevalent southwest swell. Advanced certification. Best season: April to September.

## 9. Texacobryggen, Stavanger Area

Texacobryggen is an artificial structure — an old oil platform terminal jetty — that has become one of the most productive urban dive sites in Norway. The pilings descend to about 20 metres and are covered top-to-bottom in mussels, sea squirts and anemones that form habitat for an extraordinary invertebrate community: nudibranchs in variety, hermit crabs, large common starfish and the occasional octopus. Juvenile cod use the structure as a nursery. The site's proximity to Stavanger makes it ideal as an add-on to boat dives further offshore. Open Water certification with drysuit. Best season: year-round; spring offers best nudibranch diversity.

## 10. ND31 (Oder Wreck), North Sea

The ND31, sometimes listed as the Oder, is a North Sea freighter lying at around 38 metres off the Norwegian coast. The wreck is intact enough for external exploration and the deeper sections allow guided penetration. Cold, productive North Sea water has built a heavy biofouling community on the hull, and large ling, cod and wrasse inhabit the structure. This is a proper cold-water wreck dive: drysuit required, limited visibility common, but exceptional reward in marine life density. Advanced certification; drysuit certification required. Best season: May to September.

## Planning Your Dive Trip to Norway

Norway is a drysuit destination. While open-water recreational diving in summer (June to August) is possible in a semi-dry or thick wetsuit in the south, any serious engagement with Norwegian diving requires drysuit certification and appropriate thermal underwear. Water temperature ranges from 3–4°C in winter to a maximum of 16–18°C in shallow summer water in the south. Stavanger, Bergen and Trondheim all have established dive operations. The Gulen Dive Resort near Bergen is the most comprehensive single base for visiting divers. For the orca and humpback encounters of the northern fjords in winter (November to January), operations typically run from Tromsø or Alta.
