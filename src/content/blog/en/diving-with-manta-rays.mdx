## Two Species, One Extraordinary Experience

There are two species of manta ray, and understanding which one you are likely to encounter shapes your dive planning considerably. The reef manta, Mobula alfredi, is the smaller of the two — disc widths typically between 3 and 4.5 metres — and it is strongly site-faithful, returning to the same cleaning stations and feeding grounds for years. Individual reef mantas have been identified by their unique ventral spot patterns and followed by researchers for over a decade at sites like Hanifaru Bay in the Maldives and Lady Elliot Island in Queensland.

The oceanic manta, Mobula birostris, is the world's largest ray, with disc widths regularly exceeding 5 metres and exceptional individuals approaching 7 metres. It is more pelagic and less predictable, though reliable aggregations occur at a handful of sites — the Revillagigedo Islands off Baja California, the Maldivian atolls, and Socorro Island — typically linked to seasonal productivity cycles.

Both species are now classified as vulnerable by the IUCN, principally due to targeted fisheries and bycatch in Asian and Pacific fishing grounds. Their low reproductive rate — one pup every two to three years — makes recovery from population crashes extremely slow.

## Cleaning Stations: The Fundamental Encounter

The manta encounter that most divers describe as transformative happens at a cleaning station — a fixed point on the reef where small wrasse, particularly the bluestreak cleaner wrasse, remove parasites and dead tissue from the manta's skin, gills, and spiracles. Mantas visit these stations repeatedly, sometimes queuing in loose spirals above the reef as they wait their turn.

The protocol for approaching a cleaning station is well-established and worth understanding before you enter the water. Divers should descend slowly and position themselves at least three to four metres from the station, lying flat against the reef or hovering motionless as low as possible. Standing on coral is unacceptable for obvious reasons, but equally problematic is hovering above the station — the shadow or silhouette of a diver directly between the manta and the surface is enough to abort the visit. Mantas approach from depth and require an unobstructed flight path to the station.

At Manta Point in the Komodo National Park, Indonesia, at depths of around 15 to 18 metres, the station etiquette has been standardised by local operators and most guides brief divers rigorously before descent. Encounters of ten to twenty minutes with multiple individuals are common during the peak dry season from May to October, when upwelling creates plankton-rich water and mantas aggregate in numbers.

## Hanifaru Bay: The World's Greatest Aggregation

Hanifaru Bay in the Baa Atoll of the Maldives is a seasonal phenomenon with few parallels in diving. During the southwest monsoon from June through November, plankton blooms concentrate inside the shallow bay to the point where reef mantas feed in cyclone formations — dozens of individuals looping in tight vortices, each following the one ahead to maximise the filtration efficiency of their wide cephalic fins. In exceptional years, more than 200 reef mantas have been counted in Hanifaru simultaneously, accompanied by whale sharks drawn by the same plankton bloom.

The site is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and diving is tightly regulated. Maximum group sizes, no-fin zones, and prohibition on touching or riding mantas are strictly enforced. The visibility during peak plankton is often less than five metres — murky green water thick with zooplankton — but the closeness of the encounter more than compensates. You are surrounded rather than observing.

Water temperature in Hanifaru during peak season runs around 27 to 29 degrees Celsius, and a 3mm wetsuit is comfortable. The dives are shallow, rarely exceeding 10 metres, and the current can be strong on the incoming tide when the bay fills.

## Yap, Micronesia: Manta Cathedral

The island of Yap in Micronesia has been a pilgrimage site for manta divers since the 1980s. The site known as Mi'l Channel, and particularly the cleaning station called Manta Ridge at around 15 metres, offers perhaps the most reliable close-range encounters with reef mantas of any dive site on earth. The mantas here have been interacting with divers for decades and show little behavioural change in the presence of well-mannered groups — they glide within centimetres of stationary divers, their wing tips brushing the water column.

Best season is roughly October through April, when the channel currents run consistently and the plankton supply is predictable. Visibility is typically excellent — 20 to 30 metres of blue water with the reef dropping away to the channel floor at 25 to 30 metres. The combination of clear water, reliable manta presence, and the cultural protocols around diving in Yap — where divers are briefed on local customs including respect for women's swimming areas — makes it a singularly well-organised destination.

## Revillagigedo: Giant Encounters in Blue Water

The Revillagigedo Archipelago, a UNESCO World Heritage Site roughly 400 kilometres southwest of Cabo San Lucas in Mexico, is one of the few places where encounters with oceanic mantas are reliable. At Socorro Island in particular, pelagic mantas approach divers in open water in a behaviour that marine biologists believe may be investigative or social — the animals seem to seek out divers actively rather than simply tolerating their presence.

These are big animals in big water. Depths at the cleaning stations around the pinnacles and seamounts are 15 to 30 metres, and the background is blue rather than reef. Visibility typically runs 20 to 30 metres. Currents can be strong and unpredictable. Most divers reach the Revillagigedos by liveaboard from Cabo, with crossings of around 24 hours.

## Behaviour, Etiquette and the Law

Chasing mantas, blocking their path, touching them, or approaching from directly below are behaviours that cause genuine harm. The cephalic fins at the front of the head contain sensory tissue and the animals find direct approach alarming. Mantas that are regularly harassed at cleaning stations abandon them and move elsewhere, depriving both the reef ecosystem of parasite control and the dive community of the encounter entirely.

In the Maldives, New Zealand, the Philippines, and several other jurisdictions, mantas are fully protected by law. In the Maldives, even a deliberate feeding attempt carries a fine. Beyond the legal framework, the ethical case for treating manta encounters with genuine restraint is straightforward: these are long-lived, slowly reproducing animals whose continued presence at any dive site is a function of how they are treated there.

## Photography at Cleaning Stations

Shooting mantas at a cleaning station rewards patience more than equipment. A wide-angle lens — anything equivalent to 12 to 16mm full-frame — captures the full disc width at reasonable distances. Strobes pointed directly at the animal wash out the subtle patterning of the dorsal surface and can disturb the cleaning behaviour. Natural light shooting, particularly in well-lit shallow water, produces compelling results without intrusion.

The ventral spot patterns are the key to individual identification and contribute to research databases maintained by organisations like the Manta Trust. A clear shot of the underside can be uploaded to Wildbook for Whale Sharks and related platforms, making recreational photography a genuine contribution to population monitoring.
