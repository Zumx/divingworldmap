A dive trip is governed by water — its temperature, current, clarity, and the
marine season. Plan around your certification and the conditions, and a dive
trip becomes one of the most rewarding journeys there is.

## Step 1: Match the trip to your certification and experience

Be honest: open-water, advanced, or experienced with logged dives. Many top
sites require an advanced card, drift-diving skill, or a minimum dive count.
Booking a trip beyond your level is the most common — and most dangerous —
diving-trip mistake. Build the trip to your real ability.

## Step 2: Decide resort or liveaboard

Two very different trips. Resort/day-boat: flexible, shore time, good for
mixed groups and learners. Liveaboard: reaches remote, pristine sites (Raja
Ampat, the Banda Sea), more dives per day, for committed divers. Choose by
ability and ambition.

## Step 3: Get the season right

Marine seasons are specific: visibility windows, calm periods, and animal
aggregations (mantas, mola, whale sharks, hammerheads) all have months.
Research the exact window for your destination and target species and build
the trip inside it — a month off can change everything.

## Step 4: Vet the operator hard

This is the decisive safety and ethics step. Choose operators with strong
safety records, well-maintained gear, oxygen and evacuation plans, small
groups, conservation practice (mooring buoys, briefings, no-touch policy), and
recent specific reviews. The cheapest boat is often the unsafe one.

## Step 5: Map and cluster the sites

Open the [map](/map), filter to your region, and group dive sites reachable
from one base or liveaboard route. Note depth, current, and exposure on each so
the plan matches the group's level.

## Step 6: Sort certification and refreshers early

Certify at home, not in a rushed resort course. If you have not dived recently,
book a refresher. Carry your card and logbook — top sites and operators check.
Consider advanced or specialty training before, not during, the trip.

## Step 7: Plan the diving rhythm and flights

Two to three dives a day with proper surface intervals is typical; respect
no-fly times (usually 18–24 h) when scheduling departure flights. Build a
non-diving buffer day before flying — this is a safety rule, not optional.

## Step 8: Dive responsibly

Dive your plan and your limits, never hold your breath, slow ascents and
safety stops, stay with buddy and guide, perfect buoyancy, touch nothing, and
follow marine-park rules. Reef-safe sun protection always.

## Put it together

Ability, then resort/liveaboard, then season, then a vetted operator, then a
clustered plan. Open the [map](/map), pin the sites, lock the season, and a
dive trip becomes safe, ethical, and unforgettable.
